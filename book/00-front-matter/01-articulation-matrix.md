# Articulation Matrix: The 8-Week Camp and the NextGen FinTech Scholars Program

## How the two fit together

This eight-week camp is a methodological feeder for the **NextGen FinTech Scholars / Data Science Young Scholars Research Program**, a twelve-week research program at George Mason University's Schar School of Policy and Government (June 26 – September 11, 2026), directed by Dr. Lei Gao. NextGen is organized in three phases: **Phase 1**, seven weekly Friday meetings (12:00–2:00 pm EST) that introduce FinTech as the meeting point of artificial intelligence, machine learning, and data analytics; **Phase 2**, a conference presentation around August 15; and **Phase 3**, four weeks refining a team research paper for publication in the Schar School *Young Scholars Journal* and deposit in GMU's MARS research repository, with scholarship awards up to $2,000. NextGen scholars work with professional-grade data — CRSP, Compustat, SEC EDGAR, and Bloomberg — and use AI platforms including Claude and ChatGPT.

The two programs are deliberately complementary rather than redundant. **NextGen leads on context, application, and deliverables:** the FinTech business cases, crypto and blockchain basics, policy framing, mock-trading exercises, and the team-and-journal logistics that turn analysis into a published paper. **This camp deepens the methodological core that NextGen assumes but cannot fully teach in seven Friday afternoons:** the econometric foundations beneath the regression black box, the causal-inference toolkit that separates a finding from a coincidence, the fair-lending and identification rigor that lets a result survive a skeptic, and the reproducibility discipline that makes any of it trustworthy. A student who completes the camp arrives at NextGen already able to do the hard part well; a student in both gets the rare combination of a real research question *and* the methods to answer it honestly.

## Week-by-week mapping

| Camp Week | Camp focus | Corresponding NextGen touchpoint | Who leads / who deepens |
|-----------|-----------|----------------------------------|-------------------------|
| **1** | Probability, sampling, and the logic of inference; what a "finding" is and what could have been seen instead; loading real public data (yfinance, FRED, EDGAR) reproducibly; mentor session on return predictability anchored to *Market Intraday Momentum* (Gao, Han, Li & Zhou, 2018, *JFE*) | Phase 1 onboarding; introduction to FinTech, the AI/ML/data-analytics frame, and the CRSP/Compustat/EDGAR/Bloomberg data stack | NextGen leads the FinTech framing and data access; **camp deepens** the statistical foundations and reproducible-workflow habits |
| **2** | The OLS engine: regression in matrix form, Gauss–Markov and the Frisch–Waugh–Lovell theorem, and the standard-error flavors that keep inference honest (classical, HC1–HC3, HAC/Newey–West, clustered); how misspecification and omitted-variable bias break the textbook story | Phase 1 early Friday sessions; first hands-on data work with AI assistants (Claude, ChatGPT) | **Camp deepens** the OLS mechanics and inference foundations; NextGen leads on using AI tools as a working partner |
| **3** | The causal question: correlation vs. causation, omitted-variable bias, the potential-outcomes framework; why "controlling for things" is not enough | Phase 1 FinTech business cases; framing a question that matters to a market or a policy | **Camp leads and deepens** causal-inference foundations; NextGen leads on sourcing a business-relevant question |
| **4** | Research designs that identify: difference-in-differences, natural experiments, fixed effects, panel data; fair-lending discrimination detection as the worked case (after Gao & Sun, 2019, *PNAS*) | Phase 1 policy framing; crypto/blockchain basics; mock trading | **Camp deepens** identification rigor and the fair-lending case study; NextGen leads on crypto/blockchain and trading context |
| **5** | Reading the frontier I: dissecting published empirical papers — data, design, identifying assumption, threats; guided reads of the asset-pricing and panel-inference classics (Fama–French 1992/1993, Jegadeesh–Titman 1993, Petersen 2009, Bertrand–Duflo–Mullainathan 2004); mentor session on *The Rainbow of Credits* municipal-borrowing pipeline (Gao, Liu & Wang, AEA 2025) | Phase 1 late sessions; selecting and scoping the team project | **Camp deepens** critical reading and design appraisal; NextGen leads on team formation and project scoping |
| **6** | Reading the frontier II: text-as-data and AI-assisted empirics; replication, robustness, and turning a frontier paper into your own testable extension; fair-lending in the algorithmic era ties back to Gao & Sun (2019, *PNAS*), and the mentor session anchors on supply-chain common-ownership disclosure (Gao, Han, Kim & Pan, 2024, *JCF*) | Transition Phase 1 → Phase 2; preparing analysis for presentation | **Camp deepens** replication, robustness, and responsible-AI discipline; NextGen leads on shaping findings for an audience |
| **7** | Your research project I: from question to specification (outcome · treatment · controls · fixed effects · clustering · sample · identifying assumption); building the replicable repo | **Phase 2 conference presentation (~Aug 15)** | NextGen leads the presentation milestone; **camp deepens** the underlying specification and reproducibility |
| **8** | Your research project II: writing the 12–20 page paper in LaTeX; honest empirical writing; the replication package | **Phase 3** four-week paper refinement; *Young Scholars Journal* submission; MARS deposit | NextGen leads the publication and deposit logistics; **camp deepens** the writing standards and the replication package |

## The handshake: from camp to NextGen deliverables

The path from this camp to a finished NextGen deliverable is meant to be short and concrete. By the end of Week 8 you already have the two things NextGen's later phases are organized around: a defensible research design stated in the camp's specification discipline, and a replication package — code that runs end to end and reproduces every number — in a GitHub repository. When you enter NextGen's team-project work, you bring that backbone to a group, adapt your question to the FinTech and policy framing NextGen provides, and run the analysis against the professional CRSP/Compustat/EDGAR/Bloomberg data the program opens up. Phase 2's conference presentation (around August 15) becomes a chance to defend a design you already understand cold, rather than one you are still assembling. Phase 3's four weeks of refinement are then spent polishing prose and robustness for the *Young Scholars Journal* and the MARS deposit — the part of the work that is genuinely about communication, because the part that is about rigor is already behind you. In short: the camp gets your science right; NextGen gets it published.
