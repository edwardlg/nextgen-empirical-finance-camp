# Articulation Matrix: The 12-Week Camp and the NextGen FinTech Scholars Program

## How the two fit together

This twelve-week camp is the methodological and operational spine of the **NextGen FinTech Scholars / Data Science Young Scholars Research Program**, a twelve-week summer research program at George Mason University's Schar School of Policy and Government that runs from **June 26 through September 11, 2026** and is directed by Dr. Lei Gao. NextGen is organized in three phases. **Phase 1** is seven weekly Friday meetings, 12:00–2:00 p.m. EST, from June 26 through August 7, that introduce FinTech as the meeting point of artificial intelligence, machine learning, and data analytics and that walk scholars through the professional data stack — CRSP, Compustat, SEC EDGAR, Bloomberg — alongside AI working partners (Claude, ChatGPT). **Phase 2** is the conference presentation on August 14, where each team defends a research design before a faculty and industry audience. **Phase 3** is four weeks of paper refinement, from August 21 through September 11, that ends with a final paper submitted to the Schar School *Young Scholars Journal* and deposited in GMU's MARS research repository. Scholarship awards up to $2,000 are tied to the strongest finished work.

The camp and NextGen are deliberately complementary rather than redundant. NextGen leads on cohort, context, faculty access, and the published-paper venue. The camp leads on what a serious empirical paper actually needs underneath: a defensible research design, an honest standard-error story, a replication package that runs end-to-end, and the reading habits that let a student tell a finding from a coincidence. A student who completes the camp arrives in NextGen already able to do the hard part well. A student in both gets the rare combination of a real research question, the methods to answer it honestly, and a venue that publishes the answer.

## What NextGen brings that the camp does not

The camp can take a student a long way alone, but five things only NextGen provides, and all five are load-bearing. The **cohort** matters most: NextGen places a scholar in a team of similarly motivated high-school students who must argue about a research question, divide labor, and defend the result together — an experience no textbook delivers. The **live faculty mentorship** is second: seven Friday afternoons of guided discussion with Dr. Gao and invited speakers, plus the question-and-answer around the August 14 presentation, give feedback on a student's own work that no static curriculum can substitute for. The **$2,000 scholarship awards**, tied to the strongest finished work, create real stakes that turn "summer project" into "submitted paper." The **publication and archival venue** is fourth: a finished NextGen paper appears in the Schar School *Young Scholars Journal* and is deposited in GMU's MARS research repository, giving the work a permanent, citable home that follows the student into college applications and beyond. **Conference networking** — with faculty, industry guests, and the cohort itself — is the fifth.

## What the camp brings that NextGen by itself does not

NextGen's seven Friday meetings, conference, and four-week refinement window are an excellent scaffold, but they assume a methodological maturity that high-school students rarely arrive with. The camp builds that maturity, in full. It provides a **structured twelve-week curriculum** mapped one-to-one onto the NextGen calendar, with roughly **sixty chapters and reading guides** that take a student from probability and the logic of inference through OLS in matrix form, causal inference, panel-data and natural-experiment designs, text-as-data, replication, and honest empirical writing. Each chapter ships a **runnable notebook** — roughly sixty in total — that enforces the camp's reproducibility discipline (`python=3.11`, pinned `pandas>=2.2`, `numpy`, `scipy`, `statsmodels`, `linearmodels`, `pyfixest`, `matplotlib`, `sklearn`; seeded RNG; `matplotlib.use("Agg")`; no chained indexing without `.copy()`). It provides roughly **sixty problem sets with full solution manuals** in Appendix E, so a student can actually practice the moves rather than admire them. It includes **five capstone exemplars** in Appendix D — full, faculty-grade research write-ups in the camp's house style, one for each member of the recurring student cast — that show what a finished NextGen paper should look like. It teaches the **AI co-pilot module**, an explicit, citation-disciplined protocol for using Claude and ChatGPT in empirical research without surrendering authorship or rigor. And it embeds **bias-and-robustness drills** — placebo tests, leave-one-out re-estimation, alternative clustering, alternative samples, and the threats-to-validity audit — into every empirical chapter, so robustness is a habit rather than a one-time appendix exercise.

## The handshake

A student moves through both programs the way a serious researcher moves through a real project. The first seven camp weeks march in lockstep with the seven Phase 1 Fridays: each Friday at NextGen is the live application of what the camp covered Monday through Thursday, anchored on a published Gao paper that models the move. By Week 7 the student already has a written specification, a populated GitHub repository, and a first-draft results table; the NextGen team adopts that backbone and extends it on professional data. Week 8 is the symposium-prep transition: the analysis is compressed into a fifteen-minute talk with an anchor figure and a defensible threats slide. Week 9 is the conference itself — Friday, August 14 — and the rest of that week is a structured debrief that converts feedback into a written revision plan. Weeks 10 through 12 then track Phase 3: a tightened identification section in Week 10, a full LaTeX draft and audited replication package in Week 11, and a final submission to the *Young Scholars Journal* with a MARS deposit and a signed reproducibility statement on **Friday, September 11, 2026**. The camp gets the science right; NextGen gets the science published; together they produce a high-school researcher whose first paper is genuinely defensible and genuinely citable, in a venue that will still be there in ten years.
