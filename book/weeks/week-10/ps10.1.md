# PS 10.1 — Apply the Cost / Value / Threat 3×3 to Your Own Feedback Ledger and Produce the Revision Plan

**Course:** 8-Week Empirical Finance Camp · Week 10 · Problem Set 10.1
**Covers:** Ch 10.1 (Triaging Conference Feedback — the 3×3 and the Required / Welcome / Decline memo), building on Ch 9.5 (the 24-hour feedback ledger), Ch 7.5 (the identification memo and threats table), and Ch 8.2 (the robustness battery the comments will land against). Uses no notebook code in the strict sense — this is a planning artifact — but the deliverable is a CSV plus a Markdown memo, both committed to your capstone repository, both regenerated deterministically by `make triage`.
**Type:** Project deliverable with embedded numeric problems. You produce two artifacts — `triage/ledger.csv` (the 3×3 grid applied to *your* full feedback ledger) and `triage/revision-plan.md` (the four-week work plan that follows from the grid) — and you also answer six embedded numeric questions about the *arithmetic* of triage (expected hours, marginal-value calculus, deadline feasibility). The numeric questions force you to confront the constraint Ch 10.1 §2 made conceptual: there are only 80 productive hours in four weeks, you cannot do everything, and the grid is what tells you what to drop.

**Total: 100 points.** Point values are stated per part. A model deliverable — Maya's full 47-row triage applied to her fair-lending staggered-DiD revision, with the embedded numeric answers worked end-to-end and instructor grading notes keyed to the rubric — is in Appendix E (`E-w10-ps10.1-solutions.md`). Read it *after* you draft your own.

A boundary to hold throughout. PS 10.1 is **not** about producing analysis — no regressions are run, no figures are drawn, no robustness tests are executed in this problem set. PS 10.1 is about producing the *plan* that the next four problem sets and the next three weeks of work will execute *against*. The failure mode this assignment exists to prevent is the student who, on Day 1 of revision, opens the laptop and starts running whichever robustness check feels most urgent — usually the loudest comment, usually the wrong one — and emerges on Day 28 with a sprawling appendix and a deck slide that no longer matches the headline. The 3×3 grid is the spec for what to do *and what to refuse to do*; the memo is the public commitment that prevents drift. You write both, in this problem set, before you write any code in PS 10.2 onward.

## What you submit

A single folder, `triage/`, committed to your capstone repository, containing **two artifacts** plus a **work log**:

## Part 1 — Ingest the ledger and assign the 3×3 cells (25 points)

The single sin this part prevents: **letting a forty-seven-row gut-feel CSV stay a gut-feel CSV.**

**(a) Ingest your Ch 9.5 ledger and verify completeness (5 pts).** Start from `notes/feedback-ledger.csv` (the artifact Ch 9.5 produced within 24 hours of your conference). Verify that every comment from the conference appears as a row — count the rows, count the voice memos, count the index cards or sticky notes from the poster session, and reconcile. If you find a comment in your voice memos that is not in the CSV, add it now; if you find a row that you cannot remember the context for, remove it and log the removal. Report two numbers in the log: the *initial* row count (from Ch 9.5) and the *post-reconciliation* row count. The post-reconciliation count is your $N$; everything that follows is per-row work on these $N$ rows.

**(b) Cost assignment with the calibrated four-week clock (8 pts).** For each of the $N$ rows, assign `cost` ∈ {L, M, H} using the Ch 10.1 §2.1 calibration: L = ≤ 4 hours, M = ½ day to 2 days (4–16 hours), H = ≥ 3 days (≥ 24 hours). The Hofstadter rule applies: double your first instinct. A *task* that "feels like an hour" is a Low cost assignment only if you have *already* done that exact task on this exact dataset; otherwise it is Medium. A *task* that requires you to read a method paper, install a new package, or build a new merge key is High by default. Also assign an integer `hours_estimate` (the midpoint of the bucket: L → 3, M → 10, H → 30) — this is the column the Part 2 arithmetic will sum over. Record any row where your cost assignment moved between buckets after a 24-hour pause in `triage/log.md`; the second-thought assignment is the one that goes in the CSV.

**(c) Value assignment from the paper's perspective (7 pts).** For each row, assign `value` ∈ {L, M, H} using the Ch 10.1 §2.2 calibration. The rule that catches the most students: **value is to the *paper*, not to the commenter.** A senior person's request to cite their own paper is L-value unless their paper genuinely closes a threat on your table. A junior peer's question about a subgroup is H-value if the subgroup is theoretically motivated by your design. Write the assignment honestly, even when the commenter has institutional authority over you — the memo in Part 3 is where you handle the political cost of declining, not the grid. If you cannot decide between two adjacent buckets, the conservative default for triage is to assign the *higher* value bucket and let the cost bucket force the trade-off — this prevents systematic under-rating of comments that are easy to dismiss.

**(d) Threat assignment, with translation from spoken-comment to threats-table vocabulary (5 pts).** For each row, assign `threat` ∈ {none, bounded, central}. The translation step from Ch 10.1 §3 is mandatory: a comment phrased as "have you thought about endogeneity?" gets *unranked* until you can map it to one of the four canonical threats (omitted variable, reverse causality, measurement error, selection). If you cannot do the translation in two sentences in the `verdict_reason` column, the comment is a *presentation* comment (threat = none) by default, *or* you mark it `unranked` and follow up with the commenter — never assign a threat level you cannot defend in writing.

## Part 2 — The arithmetic of triage (20 points)

This part is the embedded numeric problem set. The grid is a planning device only if the arithmetic forces a real budget. Six short calculations, each defensible from your `ledger.csv`.

**(a) Total hours-if-you-did-everything (3 pts).** Sum the `hours_estimate` column across all $N$ rows. Call this $H_{\text{all}}$. Report $H_{\text{all}}$ in the log. For a typical conference ledger ($N \approx 30$–50, mixed cost), this number lands between 200 and 500 hours. Your available budget is **80 hours** (4 weeks × 20 hours/week of revision-eligible time, which is what Ch 10.1's calibration assumed for a working PhD student or motivated camp student; adjust if your context is different and state the adjustment in the log). Report the ratio $H_{\text{all}} / 80$. This is the *triage compression factor*: the number by which the grid must reduce your work.

**(b) Required-only hours (3 pts).** Sum the `hours_estimate` column over rows whose `verdict` = Required. Call this $H_{\text{req}}$. Report $H_{\text{req}}$. You must have $H_{\text{req}} \le 80$ — if not, the Required list is over-committed and you must downgrade at least one row (the lowest-marginal-value Required item) to Welcome. The grader's first sanity check: does $H_{\text{req}}$ fit in 80 hours? If not, the grid has failed its purpose.

**(c) Welcome-pool slack (3 pts).** Compute the slack $S = 80 - H_{\text{req}}$. The Welcome-pool is the set of rows you will do *if* slack permits. Sum the `hours_estimate` over Welcome rows; call this $H_{\text{wel}}$. Report both $S$ and $H_{\text{wel}}$. The relationship that determines your sequencing: if $H_{\text{wel}} \le S$, you can do all Welcome items in the slack; if $H_{\text{wel}} > S$, you must rank Welcome items by *value-per-hour* (the next part) and do them in order until slack runs out.

**(d) Value-per-hour ranking for Welcome (4 pts).** For each Welcome row, compute the marginal value per hour: $\text{vph} = v / h$, where $v$ ∈ {1, 2, 3} for L, M, H value, and $h$ is `hours_estimate`. Sort the Welcome rows by `vph` descending. The Welcome lane will execute in that order until slack is exhausted. Report the top three Welcome rows by `vph` in the log, with their numerical `vph` values. The pedagogical point: a Welcome item with $v=2, h=4$ has $\text{vph} = 0.50$ and outranks a Welcome item with $v=3, h=24$ which has $\text{vph} = 0.125$ — the smaller, faster win is the higher-priority one *within the Welcome lane*.

**(e) Decline-list total (3 pts).** Sum `hours_estimate` over Decline rows; call this $H_{\text{dec}}$. Report $H_{\text{dec}}$ and the percentage of total hours declined: $100 \cdot H_{\text{dec}} / H_{\text{all}}$. This percentage is what the memo's Decline section is *justifying*: it is the work you chose to refuse, and you owe the reader (your advisor, the referee who reads the response letter) a defense. A healthy decline percentage for a typical ledger is 30–60%; below 30% suggests you are doing too much (the over-committing student); above 60% suggests you are dismissing real comments (the dismissive student). State which side of that band you sit on in the log.

**(f) Sanity check: required-hours-per-week and the calendar reality (4 pts).** Now divide $H_{\text{req}}$ into the four weeks of Phase 3. Week-1 (this week, the planning + Romano-Wolf + CATE work in PS 10.2 and 10.3) typically carries 25–30 hours; Week-2 (mechanism + external validity, PS 10.4 and 10.5) typically carries 20–25; Week-3 (writing the revision + response memo) carries 15–20; Week-4 (polishing + the response letter — Week 11 in the book) carries 10–15. Allocate your Required items to weeks such that no week exceeds its hour budget, and write the allocation as a four-row table in the log. **If you cannot fit the Required items into the calendar, the grid is over-committed and you go back to Part 1(c) and downgrade further — there is no other option.**

## Part 3 — The Required / Welcome / Decline memo (25 points)

The grid is the private artifact; the memo is the public commitment. The memo is what you share with your advisor, what your team commits to, and what becomes the spine of the response letter you will write in Week 12.

**(a) Required section (10 pts).** Write one paragraph per Required item — typically 3 to 6 items. Each paragraph has exactly three sentences:

1. *What the comment was*, paraphrased in one sentence in the commenter's voice (not yours), with the commenter's name or role if known ("the discussant", "Senior Colleague A", "the audience member from the back").
2. *What you will do about it*, in one sentence naming the analysis (e.g., "I will estimate a causal forest CATE on the four pre-specified moderators per PS 10.3, with honest best-linear-projection reporting"), and naming the artifact the work will produce (a new robustness row in Table 5; a new Figure 4; a new paragraph in §5.3).
3. *What you will report*, in one sentence — the *result* you will commit to reporting *whether or not it favors the headline*. The third sentence is the honesty-pre-commitment: a referee who reads your response letter will see that you committed to reporting the answer before you knew it.

The three-sentence constraint is the discipline. A four-sentence paragraph is hedging; a two-sentence paragraph is missing the pre-commitment to report. Stick to three.

**(b) Welcome section (8 pts).** Write one *line* per Welcome item — not a paragraph. Format: `- [ ] (h hrs, vph=X.XX) one-sentence description; if-time output is Y.` This is a checklist; it gets executed if and only if slack permits. The square brackets become a literal checkbox during revision — you tick them as you do them in weeks 2–3.

**(c) Decline section (7 pts).** Write one *sentence* per Decline item — not a paragraph, not a line, a sentence. Format: `[Comment paraphrase] — declined because [reason] — alternative is [the cheaper thing you did or will do instead, if any].` The "alternative" clause is the courtesy: a senior person whose comment you decline is owed a sentence explaining what you did *instead*. The Decline section is what the response letter cites when a referee re-raises one of these comments: *"we considered this and chose not to pursue it because X; we did do Y instead, which addresses the underlying concern in Z way."* A Decline without a defensible "because" is a Decline that will come back as a referee comment.

## Part 4 — Per-week sequencing and the deadline-collision check (20 points)

**(a) Map Required items to PS 10.2–10.5 and Week 11–12 deliverables (8 pts).** Three to five of your Required items should map *directly* to the problem sets in Weeks 10–11: a heterogeneity item belongs to PS 10.3 (CATE); a multiple-testing-required item belongs to PS 10.2 (Romano-Wolf); a mechanism item belongs to PS 10.4 (mediation); an external-validity item belongs to PS 10.5 (transport). Items that do *not* fit a PS slot belong in Week 11 (extra robustness, response-letter content, draft-revision work). Write the map as a table:

| Required item | PS slot (10.X) or Week | Output artifact |
|---|---|---|
| ... | ... | ... |

If you have a Required item that *cannot* be slotted, you have either mis-classified its cost (it's High and won't fit in PS-week timing) or mis-classified its value (it's not actually Required). Reconcile.

**(b) The deadline-collision check (6 pts).** Identify any pair of Required items whose dependencies collide. The two most common collisions: a heterogeneity item depends on an updated sample that a mechanism item also depends on; a robustness re-cluster depends on a re-run that takes overnight and blocks the morning's work. List collisions in the log and write the resolution (which item runs first, what the second waits for). The cost of a missed collision is a wasted week 3.

**(c) Reserve buffer (6 pts).** Allocate 20% of your $H_{\text{req}}$ as buffer — work that you *do not schedule* on the per-week table, kept as slack for the inevitable analysis surprise (a coefficient that flips sign on a robustness re-run; a CATE that doesn't converge; a mediator that turns out to be a bad control). Report the buffer hours and confirm they are not allocated to a specific week. **If your buffer plus your Required allocation exceeds 80 hours, the schedule is over-committed and you return to Part 2(b) and trim Required by the overage amount.** The buffer is not optional — every empirical revision has surprises, and an unbuffered schedule converts the first surprise into a missed deadline.

## Part 5 — Tie the memo to the threats table from Ch 7.5 (10 points)

**(a) Threats-table reconciliation (6 pts).** Open the threats table you wrote in Ch 7.5 — the one with named threats, their severity, and the design feature or robustness check that addresses each. For each Required item with `threat = central` or `threat = bounded`, write in the memo which row of the threats table the item *closes* or *strengthens*. The output is one column added to the threats table: `addressed_by` = the Required item number from your ledger. After this part, every central-threat row of your threats table must have at least one Required item pointing at it, *or* you have a central threat with no planned response — and that is a paper that will not survive review. Report any unresolved central threats in the log and write the rescue plan as a new Required item.

**(b) The pre-commitment line (4 pts).** Add one sentence at the top of `revision-plan.md`: *"The three (or four) items in the Required list below are pre-committed: I will report the results of these analyses whether or not they support the headline, by [calendar date one week before submission]."* The sentence is short. It is also the most important sentence in the file: it is the bright line that distinguishes pre-registered revision work from post-hoc fishing, and it is what your response letter in Week 12 will cite when a referee asks why you ran the analyses you ran.

## How this is graded

Part 1 (cell assignment, 25) on whether every row has a defensible threat/cost/value triple — graders spot-check 10 random rows and check the `verdict_reason` is consistent with the cell. Part 2 (arithmetic, 20) on whether the numbers reconcile — $H_{\text{req}} \le 80$, the value-per-hour ranking is correct, the buffer fits. Part 3 (memo, 25) on whether the three-sentence Required paragraphs include the pre-commitment-to-report sentence, and whether the Decline justifications are defensible to a hypothetical referee. Part 4 (sequencing, 20) on whether the per-week allocation actually fits and the collisions are surfaced. Part 5 (threats-table tie-in, 10) on whether every central threat has a Required item.

The single highest-signal check: **does $H_{\text{req}}$ plus buffer fit in 80 hours, and does every central threat have a Required item?** A `Y` on both is the load-bearing pair; if either is `N`, the plan is either over-committed or under-committed and you go back to Part 1.

## The bridge to PS 10.2 through PS 10.5

PS 10.1 produces a plan; PS 10.2 through PS 10.5 execute parts of it. Specifically: any Required item involving heterogeneity-with-multiple-testing rolls into PS 10.2 (Romano-Wolf); any Required item involving CATE or treatment heterogeneity rolls into PS 10.3; any Required item involving mechanism or "why does this work" rolls into PS 10.4 (mediation); any Required item involving generalizability or "would this hold in a different sample" rolls into PS 10.5 (external-validity transport). If after PS 10.1 you do not have at least one Required item that fits each of PS 10.2 and PS 10.3, your plan is *under-committed* in the dimensions the camp's Phase 3 is designed to teach — and the right response is to revisit the comment ledger, find the heterogeneity question the discussant asked (every discussant asks one) and the multiple-testing question that should have come up (and write yourself the comment that did not come up out loud but should have, because *you* have read this chapter and the discussant has not).
