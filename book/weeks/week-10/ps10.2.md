A boundary worth holding before you start. PS 10.2 is **not** "compute Bonferroni" — Bonferroni is a one-line calculation and you would not need a problem set for it. The pedagogical point of this problem set is the gap between Bonferroni's worst-case-correlation conservatism and Romano-Wolf's actual-correlation-respecting power. You will see that gap quantitatively in Part 2, on your own data; the rows that survive RW but die under Bonferroni are the rows where the conservatism mattered.

## Part 1 — Define the family of tests (15 points)

The single sin this part prevents: **applying multiple-testing correction to the wrong family.** A family that is too small is dishonest (you got more freedom than you admit); a family that is too large is wasteful (you lose power on tests you didn't actually conduct).

**(a) Name the family with one sentence (4 pts).** Write, in `memo.md`, one sentence of the form: *"The family of tests adjusted in this analysis is the set of $m$ tests of the form [test description] across [moderator/outcome/subgroup dimension], conducted on the [sample] used in the paper's headline regression."* For Maya, the sentence is: *"The family is the four per-subgroup ATTs from the staggered DiD on county-year denial gaps, across the four pre-specified moderators (intensity-3, minority-tercile, MSA-binary, CRA-overlap), conducted on the 2015–2024 panel used in Table 3."* Specificity is the point: a family defined as "all heterogeneity tests in the paper" is not specific enough to defend.

**(b) State $m$, the family size (3 pts).** Count the tests. If your moderator has $k$ levels and you test the per-subgroup ATT against zero, $m = k$ for that moderator. If you have four moderators, $m$ is the sum of $k_i$ across moderators (so the four-moderator example in (a) has $m = 3 + 3 + 1 + 1 = 8$ if each binary cut is counted as two tests, or $m = 4$ if you count one test of "the per-subgroup ATTs are jointly zero" per moderator). The convention this textbook adopts: count *the number of separate hypothesis tests you would naively report stars on*, which is usually the number of point estimates × the number of moderators ÷ (any merged outcome). State your $m$ explicitly and justify the count in two sentences.

**(c) Pre-registration check (4 pts).** Report whether your family of tests was pre-specified in a pre-analysis plan (PAP) or in a public commitment (a previous version of the paper, a working-paper draft, a registered report). If yes, cite the PAP and the date. If no, write the sentence that goes in the memo: *"The family was not pre-registered; we adopt a conservative Romano-Wolf adjustment across all subgroup-tests reported in this version of the paper to bound the family-wise error rate, and we will report only those subgroups whose adjusted p falls below $\alpha = 0.05$ as statistically distinguishable."* A non-pre-registered family is not the end of the world, but it shifts the burden of proof to the correction; you compensate with a more conservative procedure (Romano-Wolf rather than BH-FDR, in most cases).

**(d) Sanity check $m$ against $1 - (1-\alpha)^m$ (4 pts).** Compute the family-wise error rate under independence: $\text{FWER}_{\text{ind}} = 1 - (1 - 0.05)^m$. For $m = 4$, $\text{FWER}_{\text{ind}} \approx 0.186$; for $m = 8$, $\approx 0.337$; for $m = 20$, $\approx 0.642$. Report your $\text{FWER}_{\text{ind}}$. If $\text{FWER}_{\text{ind}} > 0.30$, the family is large enough that *some* adjustment is mandatory at any referee; if $\text{FWER}_{\text{ind}} > 0.60$, Bonferroni alone will leave you with low power and Romano-Wolf becomes essential to detect anything. The numerator on which the rest of the problem set's payoff depends.

## Part 2 — The four adjustments on a worked example (30 points)

Before you adjust *your* p-values, compute all four adjustments on a stated four-test example. The numbers are deliberately small so you can verify by hand. Adapt the procedures to your own family in Part 3.

**The worked example.** Four heterogeneity tests on Maya's fair-lending DiD produce the following raw two-sided p-values:

| Subgroup | $\widehat{\text{ATT}}$ (pp) | SE | $|t|$ | $p_{\text{raw}}$ |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| High intensity | $-2.6$ | 0.97 | 2.68 | 0.008 |
| Mid intensity  | $-1.3$ | 0.64 | 2.03 | 0.042 |
| High minority share | $-1.9$ | 0.80 | 2.38 | 0.018 |
| MSA | $-1.4$ | 0.67 | 2.09 | 0.035 |

The $|t|$ column is computed from the two-sided $p_{\text{raw}}$ as $|t| = \Phi^{-1}(1 - p/2)$ where $\Phi^{-1}$ is the inverse standard-normal CDF.

**(a) Bonferroni-adjust by hand (5 pts).** Compute $p_{\text{bonf},j} = \min(1, m \cdot p_{\text{raw},j})$ for each $j$, $m = 4$. Show your arithmetic. Verify with `python3`:

```python
import numpy as np
p_raw = np.array([0.008, 0.042, 0.018, 0.035])
p_bonf = np.minimum(1, 4 * p_raw)
print(p_bonf)  # [0.032, 0.168, 0.072, 0.140]
```

Report which rows reject the null at $\alpha = 0.05$ under Bonferroni. Only the High-intensity row (0.032) survives.

**(b) Holm-Bonferroni step-down by hand (8 pts).** Order $p_{\text{raw}}$ from smallest to largest: $p_{(1)} = 0.008$, $p_{(2)} = 0.018$, $p_{(3)} = 0.035$, $p_{(4)} = 0.042$. Compute adjusted p-values: $p_{\text{holm},(k)} = (m - k + 1) \cdot p_{(k)}$, with monotonicity enforced (each adjusted p is at least the previous): $p_{\text{holm},(1)} = 4 \cdot 0.008 = 0.032$; $p_{\text{holm},(2)} = \max(0.032, 3 \cdot 0.018) = \max(0.032, 0.054) = 0.054$; $p_{\text{holm},(3)} = \max(0.054, 2 \cdot 0.035) = \max(0.054, 0.070) = 0.070$; $p_{\text{holm},(4)} = \max(0.070, 1 \cdot 0.042) = 0.070$ (since 1·0.042 = 0.042 < 0.070, the monotonicity holds the running max). Restore the original order: `[0.032, 0.070, 0.054, 0.070]`. Verify:

```python
order = np.argsort(p_raw)
sorted_p = p_raw[order]
holm_raw = sorted_p * (4 - np.arange(4))
holm_mono = np.maximum.accumulate(holm_raw)
p_holm = np.empty_like(holm_mono)
p_holm[order] = np.minimum(1, holm_mono)
print(p_holm)  # [0.032, 0.070, 0.054, 0.070]
```

Holm rejects only High-intensity at $\alpha = 0.05$. Same rejection set as Bonferroni *in this example* but Holm is uniformly more powerful in general (the monotone-running-max can be smaller than $m \cdot p_{(k)}$).

**(c) Benjamini-Hochberg false-discovery-rate adjustment by hand (8 pts).** Order $p_{\text{raw}}$ from smallest to largest as in (b). Compute the BH-adjusted p as $p_{\text{bh},(k)} = (m / k) \cdot p_{(k)}$, with monotonicity enforced *from the largest down* (each adjusted p is at most the next-largest's): $p_{\text{bh},(4)} = (4/4) \cdot 0.042 = 0.042$; $p_{\text{bh},(3)} = \min(0.042, (4/3) \cdot 0.035) = \min(0.042, 0.0467) = 0.042$; $p_{\text{bh},(2)} = \min(0.042, (4/2) \cdot 0.018) = \min(0.042, 0.036) = 0.036$; $p_{\text{bh},(1)} = \min(0.036, (4/1) \cdot 0.008) = \min(0.036, 0.032) = 0.032$. Restore original order: `[0.032, 0.042, 0.036, 0.042]`. Verify:

```python
ranks = np.arange(1, 5)
bh_raw = sorted_p * 4 / ranks
bh_mono = np.minimum.accumulate(bh_raw[::-1])[::-1]
p_bh = np.empty_like(bh_mono)
p_bh[order] = np.minimum(1, bh_mono)
print(p_bh)  # [0.032, 0.042, 0.036, 0.042]
```

Under BH-FDR at $q = 0.05$, *all four rows* are discoveries: the smallest adjusted p is 0.032, the largest is 0.042, all below 0.05. BH-FDR's interpretation: "we expect at most 5% of these four discoveries to be false," i.e., 0.2 false discoveries among the 4 — in expectation, fewer than one. The BH and FWER procedures answer *different* questions, and the answer changes.

**(d) Romano-Wolf step-down via bootstrap (9 pts).** Romano-Wolf adjusts $p$ by simulating the joint distribution of the test statistics under the null *while preserving their correlation*. The procedure:

1. Bootstrap-resample the original sample (or residual-bootstrap if you have a clean residual structure) $B$ times, holding the null distribution by recentering: $t^{*}_{j,b} = (\hat\theta_{j,b} - \hat\theta_j)/\widehat{\text{se}}(\hat\theta_{j,b})$ for each test $j$ and bootstrap $b$.
2. For each bootstrap $b$, compute the vector of absolute t-stats $|t^{*}_{1,b}|, \dots, |t^{*}_{m,b}|$.
3. Order the *observed* absolute t-stats from largest to smallest: $|t_{(1)}| \ge |t_{(2)}| \ge \dots \ge |t_{(m)}|$.
4. For $k = 1, 2, \dots, m$, compute the RW adjusted p as $p_{\text{rw},(k)} = (1/B) \sum_{b=1}^{B} \mathbb{1}\{\max_{j \in S_k} |t^{*}_{j,b}| \ge |t_{(k)}|\}$, where $S_k$ is the set of tests not yet rejected (in step-down order). Enforce monotonicity: $p_{\text{rw},(k)} \ge p_{\text{rw},(k-1)}$.

For the worked example, simulate the joint null assuming the four t-stats are correlated at $\rho = 0.5$ (a defensible benchmark for shared-sample subgroup tests). With $B = 100{,}000$ and `rng = np.random.default_rng(42)`:

```python
import numpy as np
from scipy import stats
rng = np.random.default_rng(42)
m, rho, B = 4, 0.5, 100_000
Sigma = np.full((m, m), rho); np.fill_diagonal(Sigma, 1.0)
draws = rng.multivariate_normal(np.zeros(m), Sigma, size=B)
abs_draws = np.abs(draws)
p_raw = np.array([0.008, 0.042, 0.018, 0.035])
t_obs = np.abs(stats.norm.ppf(1 - p_raw/2))
order = np.argsort(-t_obs)  # descending
t_sorted = t_obs[order]
p_rw_sorted = np.zeros(m)
for j in range(m):
    remaining = order[j:]
    max_remaining = abs_draws[:, remaining].max(axis=1)
    p_rw_sorted[j] = np.mean(max_remaining >= t_sorted[j])
p_rw_mono = np.maximum.accumulate(p_rw_sorted)
p_rw = np.empty_like(p_rw_mono); p_rw[order] = p_rw_mono
print(p_rw)  # [0.0288, 0.0649, 0.0485, 0.0649]
```

Report all four $p_{\text{rw}}$ values. Compare side-by-side with Bonferroni: the High-intensity row goes from 0.032 (Bonferroni) to 0.029 (Romano-Wolf, $\rho = 0.5$) — a sharper rejection because the procedure exploited the correlation. The High-minority-share row goes from 0.072 (Bonferroni, not significant) to 0.049 (Romano-Wolf, just-significant) — the row that flipped from non-discovery to discovery because of the correlation accounting. *This is the pedagogical payoff of Romano-Wolf*; write it as one sentence in `memo.md`.

## Part 3 — Run Romano-Wolf on your paper's family (25 points)

Now apply the procedure to your own paper.

**(a) Extract raw test results into `raw-tests.csv` (5 pts).** From your heterogeneity or robustness table, extract the per-test point estimate, standard error, t-statistic, and raw two-sided p-value. One row per test in the family you defined in Part 1. If your t-statistics come from cluster-robust standard errors, state the cluster level in the memo; the bootstrap in (c) must preserve the cluster structure.

**(b) Implement the bootstrap with cluster preservation (10 pts).** The Romano-Wolf bootstrap on a panel with cluster-correlated residuals must resample *whole clusters*, not individual observations — otherwise the bootstrap underestimates the correlation among the t-stats and Romano-Wolf becomes Bonferroni-like (over-conservative for a different reason). Use the cluster-bootstrap:

```python
# nb10.2 cell 4 — cluster bootstrap for Romano-Wolf
def cluster_bootstrap_indices(clusters, rng):
    """Resample cluster IDs with replacement; return row indices."""
    unique_clusters = np.unique(clusters)
    sampled = rng.choice(unique_clusters, size=len(unique_clusters), replace=True)
    idx = np.concatenate([np.where(clusters == c)[0] for c in sampled])
    return idx
```

For each of $B = 9{,}999$ bootstrap iterations, draw a cluster-bootstrap sample, re-estimate the $m$ per-subgroup tests, and store the absolute t-statistics. The recentering step replaces $\hat\theta_{j,b}$ with $\hat\theta_{j,b} - \hat\theta_j$ to impose the null. *Do not skip this recentering* — without it, the bootstrap measures sampling variability around the *observed* estimate, not the null distribution, and Romano-Wolf gives nonsense.

**(c) Compute $p_{\text{rw}}$ and write to `adjusted-tests.csv` (5 pts).** Apply the step-down loop from Part 2(d) on the bootstrap matrix. Report the adjusted p-values rounded to 4 decimal places. Verify monotonicity in the sorted order.

**(d) Diagnostic: bootstrap correlation matrix (5 pts).** Compute the sample correlation among the bootstrap-t-statistic vectors:

```python
corr = np.corrcoef(boot_t_stats.T)  # m x m correlation matrix
```

Report the off-diagonal mean correlation $\bar\rho$. If $\bar\rho < 0.10$, your tests are nearly independent and Romano-Wolf will give results close to Holm; if $\bar\rho > 0.50$, your tests are highly correlated and Romano-Wolf will be substantially more powerful than Bonferroni. State which regime your tests fall in. The diagnostic is what makes the choice of Romano-Wolf *defensible* — without it, a referee can ask "why didn't you use Holm?" and you have no answer.

## Part 4 — Side-by-side comparison and the rejection-set diff (15 points)

**(a) Four-column comparison table (8 pts).** In `memo.md`, present the family in a single table with columns: `subgroup`, `p_raw`, `p_bonf`, `p_holm`, `p_bh`, `p_rw`. Bold the cells where the adjusted p is below 0.05 (or your chosen $\alpha$). Count the discoveries under each method. The expected pattern, in order of conservativeness: Bonferroni ≤ Holm ≤ Romano-Wolf (when tests are correlated) ≤ BH-FDR. *Discoveries under BH-FDR ≥ discoveries under Romano-Wolf ≥ discoveries under Holm ≥ discoveries under Bonferroni*, where the inequalities can be equalities. Verify your table respects this ordering. If it doesn't, you have a coding bug — the ordering is mathematically guaranteed.

**(b) Rejection-set diff (4 pts).** Identify rows that change rejection status across the four methods. The *most informative* row in your table is the one that survives Romano-Wolf but dies under Bonferroni — that row is what Romano-Wolf was for. Name it. If no row changes, your tests were not correlated enough for Romano-Wolf to help — and Bonferroni or Holm is the more parsimonious choice for the published paper. State this honestly.

**(c) Choose the method you will report in the paper (3 pts).** Defensible defaults: Romano-Wolf if $m \ge 4$ and $\bar\rho > 0.20$ (i.e., correlated tests where the power gain is real); Holm if $m$ is small and $\bar\rho$ is unclear (the more-powerful-than-Bonferroni baseline); BH-FDR if your *audience* is comfortable with FDR control (most empirical-finance audiences are not yet, but the genomics-adjacent and the program-evaluation audiences are). State your choice in the memo and one sentence justifying it. *Do not pick the most-favorable method post-hoc* — that is itself a multiple-testing violation of a different kind, and a referee who reads your code will spot it.

## Part 5 — The published-paper sentence (15 points)

The most important output of this problem set is a single sentence — the one you put in the paper next to your heterogeneity table.

**(a) Draft the sentence (8 pts).** Format: *"Across the $m = X$ pre-specified subgroup tests in Table 5, [Y] subgroups remain statistically distinguishable from zero at $\alpha = 0.05$ under [Method] step-down family-wise error-rate control; the surviving subgroups are [list]; the non-surviving subgroups had raw p-values that would be marginally significant under naive single-test rules but are not after multiple-testing adjustment."* Write the sentence with your specific numbers. The honest version names both what survives *and* what doesn't.

**(b) The accompanying footnote (4 pts).** Add a footnote: *"Romano-Wolf [or other] step-down adjustment computed via cluster-bootstrap at the [cluster] level with $B = 9{,}999$ bootstrap replications, seeded at SEED = [your seed]. The procedure recenters each bootstrap test statistic to impose the null and respects the empirical correlation among test statistics (mean off-diagonal $\bar\rho = [\text{value}]$). Procedure follows Romano and Wolf (2005a, 2005b); see [Clarke, Romano, and Wolf (2020)] for the Stata implementation we benchmarked against."* The footnote is what makes the sentence defensible — a referee reading the footnote should be able to reproduce the bootstrap on your code.

**(c) The robustness-vs-headline reconciliation (3 pts).** If your headline ATT survives multiple-testing correction (a single test of $\widehat{\text{ATT}} = 0$ on the full sample) but the per-subgroup tests do not all survive, write one sentence: *"The headline ATT in Table 3 is not subject to the multiple-testing correction reported here, because it tests a single pre-specified hypothesis; the heterogeneity table tests $m$ hypotheses and is corrected accordingly."* This sentence is what prevents a sophisticated referee from claiming you double-corrected — the headline is its own family of $m = 1$, and the per-subgroup correction is a separate family.
