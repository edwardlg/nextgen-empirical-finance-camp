PS 10.5 — External-Validity Memo: Who Is Your Sample, Who Is Your Target, and What Is the Transport Error?

Course: 8-Week Empirical Finance Camp · Week 10 · Problem Set 10.5 Covers: the external-validity question implicit in every empirical-finance paper and explicit in almost none of them. Builds on Ch 10.1 (the conference comment “does this generalize?” — the question every paper gets in the Q&A and most papers handle with arm-waving), Ch 7.5 (the identification memo whose threats-table is internal validity; external validity is a separate ledger), and PS 10.3 (the CATE distribution, which is the within-sample version of the heterogeneity that external validity extrapolates across samples). Uses the transportability framework of Pearl and Bareinboim (2014), Journal of Causal Inference 2(1):107–134, and the practical inverse-probability-weighting approach of Stuart, Cole, Bradshaw, and Leaf (2011), JRSSA 174(2):369–386. Type: Mixed: ~40% computational (one transport estimate from your sample to a stated target population) and ~60% writing — the external-validity memo that names the sample, names the target, identifies the moderators that differ between them, and bounds the transport error. The memo is what a reader uses to decide whether your paper’s finding is relevant to their sample.

Total: 100 points. Point values stated per part. A model deliverable — Devon’s external-validity memo for his crypto-fork event-study (sample: Ethereum 2016–2024 mainnet forks; target: hypothetical Bitcoin or Solana fork populations, with a worked IPW transport using on-chain feature distributions), instructor grading notes — is in Appendix E (E-w10-ps10.5-solutions.md).

A boundary worth holding before you start. PS 10.5 is not “the discussion section of your paper.” Many papers handle external validity in two sentences in the discussion: “Our results may not generalize to [other settings] because…” — a hedge that absorbs the question without answering it. PS 10.5 is a separate, structured memo that names the sample and the target as populations with distributions of moderators, computes the transport error — the bias from estimating in your sample but applying to the target — and writes the bound. The pedagogical point is that “generalizability” has a quantitative content: under what assumptions does the ATE in your sample equal the ATE in the target, and how far apart are they when the assumptions are violated by realistic amounts? The memo is the answer.

What you submit

A single folder, external-validity/, committed to your capstone repository, containing:

  1. external-validity/sample-target.md — the structured description (Part 1): who is in your sample, who is in your target, what is the unit, what is the time window, what is the inclusion/exclusion rule.
  2. external-validity/moderator-distributions.csv — the table of moderator distributions in sample vs. target (Part 2), with the standardized mean differences (SMDs) for each moderator.
  3. external-validity/transport.py — the script that computes the transported ATE (Part 3) using either inverse-probability-of-sampling weighting (IPSW) or g-computation against the target’s moderator distribution.
  4. external-validity/transport-results.txt — the printed output: sample ATE, transported ATE, transport error, 95% bootstrap CI on the transported estimate.
  5. external-validity/bound.md — the sensitivity-bound (Part 4): the magnitude of unobserved-moderator-shift that would invalidate the transport.
  6. external-validity/memo.md — the one-page external-validity memo (Part 5) that goes in §7 of the paper, structured as Pearl-Bareinboim (2014) prescribes.

Part 1 — Name the sample and the target as populations (15 points)

The single sin this part prevents: treating “external validity” as a vague hedge in the discussion section. A paper that says “results may not generalize” without naming the target is making no claim at all; a paper that names the target and computes the transport is making a bounded claim that a reader can audit.

(a) Name the sample (5 pts). Write, in sample-target.md, a paragraph (~150 words) describing the population from which your sample is drawn:

  • Unit of analysis (county, firm, transaction, fork event).
  • Inclusion criteria — what defines being in the sample (e.g., “U.S. counties with at least 50 home-purchase loan applications per year, 2015–2024” — Maya).
  • Exclusion criteria — what is left out (e.g., “Puerto Rico, Guam, and the U.S. Virgin Islands; counties with fewer than 50 applications in any year”).
  • Sample size N_S — the number of units actually in the sample.
  • Time window — the period over which the data was collected.
  • Selection mechanism — how did units end up in the sample (random, observational, opportunistic)? Was inclusion endogenous to the outcome (e.g., delisted firms drop out of CRSP — that is endogenous selection that distorts external validity beyond what the IPW correction in Part 3 can fix).

The selection-mechanism question is the load-bearing one. Random selection implies the sample is a random subset of the target (and transport is just averaging weights). Observational selection on observables implies IPW can correct for the difference. Observational selection on unobservables implies IPW cannot correct fully, and Part 4’s sensitivity is mandatory.

(b) Name the target (5 pts). Write a parallel paragraph describing the target population — the population to which the paper implicitly or explicitly claims its findings apply. The target is not “everyone everywhere”; it is the narrowest population that the paper’s policy or implication addresses. For Maya, the target might be “all U.S. counties subject to CRA examinations under the Federal Reserve, including those in the Puerto Rico-and-Pacific exclusions” — a superset of the sample by extending across the inclusion-exclusion line. Or the target might be “U.S. counties from 2025–2030” — extending the time window forward. Or the target might be “Canadian census divisions under the parallel Canadian fair-lending regime” — extending across institutional jurisdiction.

The target’s size N_T is reported, the time window is reported, and the inclusion-exclusion difference relative to the sample is named explicitly. A paper whose target is identical to its sample has no external-validity content; a paper whose target is much larger than its sample has more external-validity content but also more transport error.

(c) The implicit-target audit (5 pts). Read your paper’s abstract and conclusion sentences. Do they make general statements (e.g., “policies that increase examination intensity reduce denial gaps”) or specific statements (“the policy in U.S. counties from 2015–2024 reduced denial gaps by 1.4pp”)? Most papers slide between the two unintentionally — the abstract reads general while the data is specific. List in the memo the sentences that imply a target broader than your sample, and confirm the target you named in (b) is consistent with those sentences. If you find an abstract sentence that implies a target your transport in Part 3 cannot defend, rewrite the abstract sentence to match the target you can defend, and note the rewrite in the memo. The output is alignment between what the paper claims and what the data supports.

Part 2 — Compute moderator distributions in sample vs. target (20 points)

For the transport in Part 3 to work, you need to characterize how the sample differs from the target on the moderators that matter.

(a) Choose the moderators (5 pts). Use the moderators you pre-specified in PS 10.3’s pre-analysis plan (the variables you said could drive treatment heterogeneity). If those are the variables whose distribution differs between sample and target, then heterogeneity in CATE × moderator-distribution-shift is what creates transport error — exactly what we need to compute. If your PS 10.3 moderator set is wrong for the transport (e.g., you pre-specified moderators that don’t differ between sample and target, but other variables do differ), state so and add the additional moderators here for the transport analysis only — and document the addition in the memo as a transport-specific extension of the PS 10.3 PAP.

(b) Get the target’s moderator distribution (8 pts). You need to know the moderator distribution in the target population — and this is the hardest practical step. Three common approaches:

  1. Population reference dataset. For most U.S.-county targets, the American Community Survey provides population moments. For firm targets, CRSP-Compustat full-universe samples. For transaction targets, exchange-published totals. Use the reference dataset to compute the target’s mean and variance of each moderator.
  2. Published moments from a survey or report. If the target has been characterized in a published source (a regulatory report, an industry survey), use those moments — but note in the memo the source and the precision of the published moments.
  3. Hypothetical / counterfactual target. If the target is a future population (Maya’s 2025–2030 counties), you need to either assume its distribution equals the latest year of the sample or project the distribution forward using a stated model. Stating the assumption is mandatory; a transport against a hypothetical target with no stated moderator distribution is not a transport.

Report in moderator-distributions.csv a table with columns: moderator, sample_mean, sample_sd, target_mean, target_sd, smd (standardized mean difference), source (the reference for the target distribution).

(c) Compute the standardized mean difference (SMD) for each moderator (4 pts). SMD is the standard one-number measure of distribution difference:

\text{SMD}_j = \frac{\bar{X}^S_j - \bar{X}^T_j}{\sqrt{(s^{S,2}_j + s^{T,2}_j)/2}}

Compute it for each moderator. The conventional thresholds: |\text{SMD}| < 0.10 = negligible difference (transport error is small for this moderator); 0.10 \le |\text{SMD}| < 0.25 = moderate; |\text{SMD}| \ge 0.25 = substantial (transport error is large; IPW correction is essential). Report which moderators fall in each bucket. The moderators with |\text{SMD}| \ge 0.25 are the ones doing the transport-error work — they are where the sample and target most differ.

(d) Construct the IPSW weights (3 pts). Inverse-probability-of-sampling weighting (IPSW) reweights the sample to match the target’s moderator distribution. The weight for unit i in the sample is:

w_i = \frac{\Pr(S = 1 \mid \mathbf{X} = \mathbf{x}_i)^{-1}}{\sum_{i \in \text{sample}} \Pr(S = 1 \mid \mathbf{X} = \mathbf{x}_i)^{-1}} \cdot \Pr(\text{target}|S = 0)

In practice, this is implemented by fitting a logistic regression of “is this unit in the sample (S=1) vs. the target (S=0)?” on the moderators, predicting \hat{p}_i = \Pr(S = 1 \mid \mathbf{x}_i), and computing w_i \propto (1 - \hat{p}_i) / \hat{p}_i. Verified arithmetic from the textbook:

# nb10.5 cell 1 — IPSW weights via logistic regression
from sklearn.linear_model import LogisticRegression
import numpy as np
rng = np.random.default_rng(42)
# Sample: 1000 obs centered at X_mean = 0.5; target: 5000 obs centered at 0.
X_s = rng.normal(0.5, 1.0, 1000).reshape(-1, 1)
X_t = rng.normal(0.0, 1.0, 5000).reshape(-1, 1)
X_all = np.concatenate([X_s, X_t])
S = np.concatenate([np.ones(1000), np.zeros(5000)])
lr = LogisticRegression().fit(X_all, S)
ps = lr.predict_proba(X_s)[:, 1]
w = (1 - ps) / ps
print(f"Weight range: [{w.min():.3f}, {w.max():.3f}]; effective sample size: {(w.sum())**2 / (w**2).sum():.0f}")

Report the weight range and the effective sample size (ESS) = (\sum w_i)^2 / \sum w_i^2. If ESS < N_S / 4, the weights are extreme and the IPW transport is unstable; you have substantial moderator-shift and the transport error in Part 3 will be large.

Part 3 — Estimate the transported ATE (20 points)

(a) Sample ATE (3 pts). Report the ATE estimated on your sample — this is your headline number from Chapter 4. Call this \widehat{\text{ATE}}_S.

(b) Transported ATE via IPSW (10 pts). Re-estimate the ATE on the sample, weighted by the IPSW weights w_i from Part 2(d). This produces \widehat{\text{ATE}}_T, the transported ATE — the estimate of what the ATE would be in the target population, assuming the conditional ATE function \tau(\mathbf{x}) is invariant across sample and target (the exchangeability assumption).

# nb10.5 cell 2 — transported ATE via IPSW
import statsmodels.api as sm
# y, treat, X, w defined from your data
sample_ate_mod = sm.WLS(y, sm.add_constant(treat), weights=w).fit(cov_type="HC1")
transported_ate = sample_ate_mod.params[1]
print(f"Sample ATE: {sample_ate:.3f}")
print(f"Transported ATE (IPSW): {transported_ate:.3f}")
print(f"Transport correction: {transported_ate - sample_ate:.3f}")

The transport correction \widehat{\text{ATE}}_T - \widehat{\text{ATE}}_S is the magnitude of the IPSW reweighting. A small correction (< 10% of the sample ATE) means the sample is close to the target on the moderators; a large correction (> 30% of the sample ATE) means the IPSW is doing serious work and the sensitivity in Part 4 matters a great deal.

(c) Bootstrap the transported ATE (4 pts). Bootstrap the transport with cluster preservation (same protocol as PS 10.2 and PS 10.4): in each bootstrap draw, resample whole clusters from the sample, refit the IPSW logistic regression, recompute the weights, and re-estimate the weighted ATE. With B = 9{,}999 bootstrap replications, report the bootstrap point estimate (median of the bootstrap distribution) and the percentile 95% CI.

(d) Compare to g-computation (3 pts). An alternative to IPSW is g-computation: estimate the CATE function \hat\tau(\mathbf{x}) on the sample (using your PS 10.3 causal forest), then integrate it over the target’s moderator distribution:

\widehat{\text{ATE}}_T^{\text{g}} = \int \hat\tau(\mathbf{x}) \, dF_T(\mathbf{x}) \approx \frac{1}{N_T} \sum_{i \in \text{target}} \hat\tau(\mathbf{x}_i)

If you have the target’s individual-level moderator data, you can compute this directly. If you only have target moments, you use the integrated form. The g-computation estimate and the IPSW estimate should agree to within bootstrap SE; if they disagree substantially, one of the two has a misspecification problem and the disagreement is a diagnostic finding worth reporting in the memo.

Part 4 — Bound the transport error under unobserved moderator shift (20 points)

The transport in Part 3 corrects for observed moderator shift. There may be unobserved moderators that differ between sample and target, in which case the transport is incomplete and biased.

(a) Sensitivity to unobserved moderator (10 pts). Following the practical approach of Nguyen, Ebnesajjad, Cole, and Stuart (2018) — “Sensitivity Analysis for an Unmeasured Moderator in Meta-Analysis of Sub-Group Treatment Effects,” Statistical Methods in Medical Research 27(10): 2987–3001 — bound the transport error from an unmeasured moderator by parameterizing:

  • \Delta_U: the SMD on the unmeasured moderator U between sample and target (analogous to your observed SMDs).
  • \beta_U: the BLP coefficient on U if it were in your PS 10.3 BLP regression (i.e., the strength of the heterogeneity along U).

The bias from omitting U from the transport is approximately:

\text{Bias}_U \approx \beta_U \cdot s_U \cdot \Delta_U

where s_U is the sample SD of U. For plausible values of |\Delta_U| \in \{0.10, 0.25, 0.50\} and |\beta_U \cdot s_U| scaled to a typical observed moderator’s effect (use the largest BLP coefficient from PS 10.3 as a benchmark), report the implied bias. At what combination of \Delta_U and \beta_U does the bias exceed the transported ATE itself? That is the breakdown point.

(b) Name plausible unobserved moderators (5 pts). Sensitivity is only as useful as the named unobserved moderators it bounds. List two or three plausible candidate U variables for your transport. For Maya transporting from U.S. counties 2015–2024 to U.S. counties 2025–2030, candidate U’s include future regulatory environment (CFPB enforcement intensity), interest-rate regime (the post-pandemic rate cycle), and demographic shift (changes in racial composition of borrower pools). For each, state whether the SMD between sample and target is plausibly large (> 0.25), moderate (0.10–0.25), or small.

(c) Bound the transport error in human units (5 pts). Combining Part 4(a) and (b), write a sentence: “Under the plausible unobserved-moderator shifts described above, the transport error from sample to target is bounded by \pm [value] pp; the transported ATE of [value] pp falls within this band [/falls outside / partially overlaps with zero]; we judge the transported estimate [robust / fragile] to unobserved moderator shift.” This is the publishable bound.

Part 5 — The §7 external-validity memo (25 points)

(a) Draft the external-validity paragraph (15 pts). Six to eight sentences in memo.md:

  1. Frame. “The paper’s headline ATE of [value] is estimated on a sample of [sample description, ~one phrase]; we now ask whether it transports to [target description, ~one phrase].”
  2. Observable moderator-shift. “On the moderators we pre-specified in §5 (PS 10.3), the sample and target differ as follows: [list the moderators with |\text{SMD}| \ge 0.25 and the direction of difference]. The largest single-moderator shift is on [moderator] (SMD = [value]).”
  3. Transport correction. “Reweighting the sample via inverse-probability-of-sampling-weighting (IPSW) on the pre-specified moderators yields a transported ATE of [value] (95% bootstrap CI [low, high]) — a [correction percentage] adjustment from the sample ATE.”
  4. Robustness to unobserved moderator-shift. “Under plausible unobserved-moderator shifts (Part 4 sensitivity analysis), the transported estimate is robust to bias up to \pm [value] pp; the lower bound of the sensitivity range [does/does not] cross zero.”
  5. What we claim. “We claim our findings apply to [the target population, with the assumptions stated above]; we do not claim they apply to [populations the analysis excluded — name them].”
  6. What we don’t claim. “The IPSW transport assumes the CATE function \tau(\mathbf{x}) is invariant across sample and target on \mathbf{x}; we cannot rule out CATE shifts driven by unobserved structural change between the two populations.”
  7. Future work. “A direct test of external validity — replicating the analysis on the target’s data once available — would refine the bound; we encourage [the relevant follow-up].”

The fifth and sixth sentences are the load-bearing pair: they bound the claim the paper makes externally to match the evidence the transport produced. A paper whose abstract claims generality beyond what the transport supports is making a claim it cannot defend at a sophisticated referee.

(b) Cite the transportability literature (5 pts). Include the four citations:

  • Pearl, J., & Bareinboim, E. (2014). “External Validity: From Do-Calculus to Transportability Across Populations.” Statistical Science 29(4): 579–595.
  • Stuart, E. A., Cole, S. R., Bradshaw, C. P., & Leaf, P. J. (2011). “The Use of Propensity Scores to Assess the Generalizability of Results from Randomized Trials.” Journal of the Royal Statistical Society: Series A 174(2): 369–386.
  • Nguyen, T. Q., Ebnesajjad, C., Cole, S. R., & Stuart, E. A. (2018). “Sensitivity Analysis for an Unmeasured Moderator in Meta-Analysis.” Statistical Methods in Medical Research 27(10): 2987–3001.
  • Egami, N., & Hartman, E. (2023). “Elements of External Validity: Framework, Design, and Analysis.” American Political Science Review 117(3): 1070–1088.

(c) The abstract-ready summary sentence (5 pts). Write the one sentence that, in the abstract, would correctly summarize the external-validity finding: “The ATE we estimate is [value] pp in our [sample]; transported to [target] via IPSW on the pre-specified moderators, the implied ATE is [value] pp (95% CI [low, high]), and this estimate is robust to plausible unobserved-moderator shifts of magnitude |\Delta_U| \le [\text{value}].” This sentence tells a reader, in one breath, what populations the finding applies to and how confident the paper is in extrapolation. It is short. It is also rare in published papers — most papers do not compute the transport — and is a credibility marker worth committing to.

How this is graded

Part 1 (sample-target naming, 15) on whether the sample and the target are specified as populations with selection mechanisms, and whether the implicit-target audit aligns the abstract to the evidence. Part 2 (moderator distributions, 20) on whether the SMDs are computed correctly and the IPSW weights’ ESS is reported. Part 3 (transport estimation, 20) on whether the IPSW and g-computation transports agree and the bootstrap CI is cluster-respecting. Part 4 (unobserved-moderator sensitivity, 20) on whether the bound is computed with named candidate U’s. Part 5 (memo, 25) on whether the paragraph includes the “what we don’t claim” sentence and the abstract-ready summary.

The single highest-signal check: does the abstract of your paper claim a generality that the IPSW transport defends — or does it overclaim? If overclaiming, the audit in Part 1(c) caught it and the rewrite is documented. If under-claiming (the transport supports more than the paper claims), note in the memo and consider strengthening the abstract.

- *Sample size* $N_S$ — the number of units actually in the sample.
- *Time window* — the period over which the data was collected.
- *Selection mechanism* — *how* did units end up in the sample (random, observational, opportunistic)? Was inclusion endogenous to the outcome (e.g., delisted firms drop out of CRSP — that is endogenous selection that distorts external validity beyond what the IPW correction in Part 3 can fix).

The selection-mechanism question is the load-bearing one. *Random selection* implies the sample is a random subset of the target (and transport is just averaging weights). *Observational selection on observables* implies IPW can correct for the difference. *Observational selection on unobservables* implies IPW cannot correct fully, and Part 4's sensitivity is mandatory.

**(b) Name the target (5 pts).** Write a parallel paragraph describing the *target population* — the population to which the paper *implicitly or explicitly* claims its findings apply. The target is not "everyone everywhere"; it is the *narrowest* population that the paper's policy or implication addresses. For Maya, the target might be "all U.S. counties subject to CRA examinations under the Federal Reserve, including those in the Puerto Rico-and-Pacific exclusions" — a *superset* of the sample by extending across the inclusion-exclusion line. Or the target might be "U.S. counties from 2025–2030" — extending the time window forward. Or the target might be "Canadian census divisions under the parallel Canadian fair-lending regime" — extending across institutional jurisdiction.

The target's size $N_T$ is reported, the time window is reported, and the *inclusion-exclusion difference* relative to the sample is named explicitly. A paper whose target is identical to its sample has no external-validity content; a paper whose target is much larger than its sample has more external-validity content but also more transport error.

**(c) The implicit-target audit (5 pts).** Read your paper's abstract and conclusion sentences. Do they make *general* statements (e.g., "policies that increase examination intensity reduce denial gaps") or *specific* statements ("the policy in U.S. counties from 2015–2024 reduced denial gaps by 1.4pp")? Most papers slide between the two unintentionally — the abstract reads general while the data is specific. List in the memo the sentences that *imply* a target broader than your sample, and confirm the target you named in (b) is consistent with those sentences. If you find an abstract sentence that implies a target your transport in Part 3 cannot defend, *rewrite the abstract sentence to match the target you can defend*, and note the rewrite in the memo. The output is alignment between what the paper claims and what the data supports.

## Part 2 — Compute moderator distributions in sample vs. target (20 points)

For the transport in Part 3 to work, you need to characterize *how the sample differs from the target on the moderators that matter*.

**(a) Choose the moderators (5 pts).** Use the moderators you pre-specified in PS 10.3's pre-analysis plan (the variables you said could drive treatment heterogeneity). If those are the variables whose distribution differs between sample and target, then heterogeneity in CATE × moderator-distribution-shift is what creates transport error — exactly what we need to compute. If your PS 10.3 moderator set is wrong for the transport (e.g., you pre-specified moderators that don't differ between sample and target, but other variables do differ), state so and add the additional moderators here for the transport analysis only — and document the addition in the memo as a transport-specific extension of the PS 10.3 PAP.

**(b) Get the target's moderator distribution (8 pts).** You need to know the moderator distribution in the target population — and this is the hardest practical step. Three common approaches:

1. *Population reference dataset.* For most U.S.-county targets, the American Community Survey provides population moments. For firm targets, CRSP-Compustat full-universe samples. For transaction targets, exchange-published totals. Use the reference dataset to compute the target's mean and variance of each moderator.
2. *Published moments from a survey or report.* If the target has been characterized in a published source (a regulatory report, an industry survey), use those moments — but note in the memo the source and the precision of the published moments.
3. *Hypothetical / counterfactual target.* If the target is a future population (Maya's 2025–2030 counties), you need to either *assume* its distribution equals the latest year of the sample or *project* the distribution forward using a stated model. Stating the assumption is mandatory; a transport against a hypothetical target with no stated moderator distribution is not a transport.

Report in `moderator-distributions.csv` a table with columns: `moderator`, `sample_mean`, `sample_sd`, `target_mean`, `target_sd`, `smd` (standardized mean difference), `source` (the reference for the target distribution).

**(c) Compute the standardized mean difference (SMD) for each moderator (4 pts).** SMD is the standard one-number measure of distribution difference:

$$ \text{SMD}_j = \frac{\bar{X}^S_j - \bar{X}^T_j}{\sqrt{(s^{S,2}_j + s^{T,2}_j)/2}} $$

Compute it for each moderator. The conventional thresholds: $|\text{SMD}| < 0.10$ = negligible difference (transport error is small for this moderator); $0.10 \le |\text{SMD}| < 0.25$ = moderate; $|\text{SMD}| \ge 0.25$ = substantial (transport error is large; IPW correction is essential). Report which moderators fall in each bucket. *The moderators with $|\text{SMD}| \ge 0.25$ are the ones doing the transport-error work* — they are where the sample and target most differ.

**(d) Construct the IPSW weights (3 pts).** Inverse-probability-of-sampling weighting (IPSW) reweights the sample to match the target's moderator distribution. The weight for unit $i$ in the sample is:

$$ w_i = \frac{\Pr(S = 1 \mid \mathbf{X} = \mathbf{x}_i)^{-1}}{\sum_{i \in \text{sample}} \Pr(S = 1 \mid \mathbf{X} = \mathbf{x}_i)^{-1}} \cdot \Pr(\text{target}|S = 0) $$

In practice, this is implemented by fitting a logistic regression of "is this unit in the sample (S=1) vs. the target (S=0)?" on the moderators, predicting $\hat{p}_i = \Pr(S = 1 \mid \mathbf{x}_i)$, and computing $w_i \propto (1 - \hat{p}_i) / \hat{p}_i$. Verified arithmetic from the textbook:

```python
# nb10.5 cell 1 — IPSW weights via logistic regression
from sklearn.linear_model import LogisticRegression
import numpy as np
rng = np.random.default_rng(42)
# Sample: 1000 obs centered at X_mean = 0.5; target: 5000 obs centered at 0.
X_s = rng.normal(0.5, 1.0, 1000).reshape(-1, 1)
X_t = rng.normal(0.0, 1.0, 5000).reshape(-1, 1)
X_all = np.concatenate([X_s, X_t])
S = np.concatenate([np.ones(1000), np.zeros(5000)])
lr = LogisticRegression().fit(X_all, S)
ps = lr.predict_proba(X_s)[:, 1]
w = (1 - ps) / ps
print(f"Weight range: [{w.min():.3f}, {w.max():.3f}]; effective sample size: {(w.sum())**2 / (w**2).sum():.0f}")
```

Report the weight range and the *effective sample size* (ESS) $= (\sum w_i)^2 / \sum w_i^2$. If ESS < $N_S / 4$, the weights are extreme and the IPW transport is unstable; you have substantial moderator-shift and the transport error in Part 3 will be large.

## Part 3 — Estimate the transported ATE (20 points)

**(a) Sample ATE (3 pts).** Report the ATE estimated on your sample — this is your headline number from Chapter 4. Call this $\widehat{\text{ATE}}_S$.

**(b) Transported ATE via IPSW (10 pts).** Re-estimate the ATE on the sample, *weighted* by the IPSW weights $w_i$ from Part 2(d). This produces $\widehat{\text{ATE}}_T$, the transported ATE — the estimate of what the ATE *would be* in the target population, assuming the conditional ATE function $\tau(\mathbf{x})$ is invariant across sample and target (the *exchangeability assumption*).

```python
# nb10.5 cell 2 — transported ATE via IPSW
import statsmodels.api as sm
# y, treat, X, w defined from your data
sample_ate_mod = sm.WLS(y, sm.add_constant(treat), weights=w).fit(cov_type="HC1")
transported_ate = sample_ate_mod.params[1]
print(f"Sample ATE: {sample_ate:.3f}")
print(f"Transported ATE (IPSW): {transported_ate:.3f}")
print(f"Transport correction: {transported_ate - sample_ate:.3f}")
```

The *transport correction* $\widehat{\text{ATE}}_T - \widehat{\text{ATE}}_S$ is the magnitude of the IPSW reweighting. A small correction (< 10% of the sample ATE) means the sample is close to the target on the moderators; a large correction (> 30% of the sample ATE) means the IPSW is doing serious work and the sensitivity in Part 4 matters a great deal.

**(c) Bootstrap the transported ATE (4 pts).** Bootstrap the transport with cluster preservation (same protocol as PS 10.2 and PS 10.4): in each bootstrap draw, resample whole clusters from the sample, refit the IPSW logistic regression, recompute the weights, and re-estimate the weighted ATE. With $B = 9{,}999$ bootstrap replications, report the bootstrap point estimate (median of the bootstrap distribution) and the percentile 95% CI.

**(d) Compare to g-computation (3 pts).** An alternative to IPSW is *g-computation*: estimate the CATE function $\hat\tau(\mathbf{x})$ on the sample (using your PS 10.3 causal forest), then integrate it over the target's moderator distribution:

$$ \widehat{\text{ATE}}_T^{\text{g}} = \int \hat\tau(\mathbf{x}) \, dF_T(\mathbf{x}) \approx \frac{1}{N_T} \sum_{i \in \text{target}} \hat\tau(\mathbf{x}_i) $$

If you have the target's individual-level moderator data, you can compute this directly. If you only have target moments, you use the integrated form. The g-computation estimate and the IPSW estimate should agree to within bootstrap SE; if they disagree substantially, one of the two has a misspecification problem and the disagreement is a diagnostic finding worth reporting in the memo.

## Part 4 — Bound the transport error under unobserved moderator shift (20 points)

The transport in Part 3 corrects for *observed* moderator shift. There may be *unobserved* moderators that differ between sample and target, in which case the transport is incomplete and biased.

**(a) Sensitivity to unobserved moderator (10 pts).** Following the practical approach of Nguyen, Ebnesajjad, Cole, and Stuart (2018) — "Sensitivity Analysis for an Unmeasured Moderator in Meta-Analysis of Sub-Group Treatment Effects," *Statistical Methods in Medical Research* 27(10): 2987–3001 — bound the transport error from an unmeasured moderator by parameterizing:

- $\Delta_U$: the SMD on the unmeasured moderator $U$ between sample and target (analogous to your observed SMDs).
- $\beta_U$: the BLP coefficient on $U$ if it were in your PS 10.3 BLP regression (i.e., the strength of the heterogeneity along $U$).

The bias from omitting $U$ from the transport is approximately:

$$ \text{Bias}_U \approx \beta_U \cdot s_U \cdot \Delta_U $$

where $s_U$ is the sample SD of $U$. For plausible values of $|\Delta_U| \in \{0.10, 0.25, 0.50\}$ and $|\beta_U \cdot s_U|$ scaled to a typical observed moderator's effect (use the largest BLP coefficient from PS 10.3 as a benchmark), report the implied bias. *At what combination of $\Delta_U$ and $\beta_U$ does the bias exceed the transported ATE itself?* That is the breakdown point.

**(b) Name plausible unobserved moderators (5 pts).** Sensitivity is only as useful as the *named* unobserved moderators it bounds. List two or three plausible candidate $U$ variables for your transport. For Maya transporting from U.S. counties 2015–2024 to U.S. counties 2025–2030, candidate $U$'s include *future regulatory environment* (CFPB enforcement intensity), *interest-rate regime* (the post-pandemic rate cycle), and *demographic shift* (changes in racial composition of borrower pools). For each, state whether the SMD between sample and target is plausibly large (> 0.25), moderate (0.10–0.25), or small.

**(c) Bound the transport error in human units (5 pts).** Combining Part 4(a) and (b), write a sentence: *"Under the plausible unobserved-moderator shifts described above, the transport error from sample to target is bounded by $\pm$ [value] pp; the transported ATE of [value] pp falls within this band [/falls outside / partially overlaps with zero]; we judge the transported estimate [robust / fragile] to unobserved moderator shift."* This is the publishable bound.

## Part 5 — The §7 external-validity memo (25 points)

**(a) Draft the external-validity paragraph (15 pts).** Six to eight sentences in `memo.md`:

1. *Frame.* "The paper's headline ATE of [value] is estimated on a sample of [sample description, ~one phrase]; we now ask whether it transports to [target description, ~one phrase]."
2. *Observable moderator-shift.* "On the moderators we pre-specified in §5 (PS 10.3), the sample and target differ as follows: [list the moderators with $|\text{SMD}| \ge 0.25$ and the direction of difference]. The largest single-moderator shift is on [moderator] (SMD = [value])."
3. *Transport correction.* "Reweighting the sample via inverse-probability-of-sampling-weighting (IPSW) on the pre-specified moderators yields a transported ATE of [value] (95% bootstrap CI [low, high]) — a [correction percentage] adjustment from the sample ATE."
4. *Robustness to unobserved moderator-shift.* "Under plausible unobserved-moderator shifts (Part 4 sensitivity analysis), the transported estimate is robust to bias up to $\pm$ [value] pp; the lower bound of the sensitivity range [does/does not] cross zero."
5. *What we claim.* "We claim our findings apply to [the target population, with the assumptions stated above]; we do not claim they apply to [populations the analysis excluded — name them]."
6. *What we don't claim.* "The IPSW transport assumes the CATE function $\tau(\mathbf{x})$ is invariant across sample and target on $\mathbf{x}$; we cannot rule out CATE shifts driven by unobserved structural change between the two populations."
7. *Future work.* "A direct test of external validity — replicating the analysis on the target's data once available — would refine the bound; we encourage [the relevant follow-up]."

The fifth and sixth sentences are the load-bearing pair: they bound the *claim* the paper makes externally to match the *evidence* the transport produced. A paper whose abstract claims generality beyond what the transport supports is making a claim it cannot defend at a sophisticated referee.

**(b) Cite the transportability literature (5 pts).** Include the four citations:

- Pearl, J., & Bareinboim, E. (2014). "External Validity: From Do-Calculus to Transportability Across Populations." *Statistical Science* 29(4): 579–595.
- Stuart, E. A., Cole, S. R., Bradshaw, C. P., & Leaf, P. J. (2011). "The Use of Propensity Scores to Assess the Generalizability of Results from Randomized Trials." *Journal of the Royal Statistical Society: Series A* 174(2): 369–386.
- Nguyen, T. Q., Ebnesajjad, C., Cole, S. R., & Stuart, E. A. (2018). "Sensitivity Analysis for an Unmeasured Moderator in Meta-Analysis." *Statistical Methods in Medical Research* 27(10): 2987–3001.
- Egami, N., & Hartman, E. (2023). "Elements of External Validity: Framework, Design, and Analysis." *American Political Science Review* 117(3): 1070–1088.

**(c) The abstract-ready summary sentence (5 pts).** Write the one sentence that, in the abstract, would correctly summarize the external-validity finding: *"The ATE we estimate is [value] pp in our [sample]; transported to [target] via IPSW on the pre-specified moderators, the implied ATE is [value] pp (95% CI [low, high]), and this estimate is robust to plausible unobserved-moderator shifts of magnitude $|\Delta_U| \le [\text{value}]$."* This sentence tells a reader, in one breath, what populations the finding applies to and how confident the paper is in extrapolation. It is short. It is also rare in published papers — most papers do not compute the transport — and is a credibility marker worth committing to.
