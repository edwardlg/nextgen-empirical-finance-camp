PS 10.4 — Mechanism Section: One Honest Mediator, with the Bad-Controls Audit

Course: 8-Week Empirical Finance Camp · Week 10 · Problem Set 10.4 Covers: the mechanism question that PS 10.3’s CATE analysis sets up. Builds directly on Ch 10.1 (the conference comment “what’s the mechanism?” — the question every paper gets and most papers handle badly), Ch 7.5 (the identification memo whose threats-table named the unobserved-confounder threat that mediation can either close or expose), and Ch 4 (the headline ATE the mediator decomposes). Draws on the directed-acyclic-graph (DAG) reasoning from Pearl (2009) and the bad-controls taxonomy of Cinelli, Forney, and Pearl (2024), Sociological Methods & Research 53(3):1071–1104. Uses nb10.4 — the mediation-and-bad-controls notebook. Type: Mixed: ~50% computational (one mediation estimation on your data with bootstrap SEs and a Sobel test) and ~50% conceptual-with-diagrams (the bad-controls DAG audit, which is the part most students get wrong and the part most papers get wrong).

Total: 100 points. Point values stated per part. A model deliverable — Priya’s mediation analysis of climate-risk disclosure (effect → analyst attention → forecast accuracy), with the bad-controls audit of three plausible mediators (one good, one bad, one ambiguous), instructor grading notes — is in Appendix E (E-w10-ps10.4-solutions.md).

A boundary worth holding before you start. PS 10.4 is not “find a mediator and report the indirect effect.” Mediation, done badly, is the single most common source of bad-controls bias in empirical finance — authors add a “mechanism” variable to the regression, watch the treatment coefficient shrink, and report the shrinkage as evidence the mediator carries the effect. That is wrong when the mediator is itself caused by the treatment and also caused by an unobserved confounder, because conditioning on the mediator opens a collider path from the treatment to the confounder to the outcome — and the bias can go in either direction. The discipline that makes mediation honest is drawing the DAG before running the regression, auditing the mediator against the four bad-control patterns (M-bias, butterfly, collider, post-treatment), and bounding the indirect effect with a sensitivity analysis when the audit cannot rule out a problem. This problem set teaches the audit and the bound, and forces you to commit — in writing — to one mediator and exactly one indirect-effect estimate.

What you submit

A single folder, mechanism/, committed to your capstone repository, containing:

  1. mechanism/dag.md — the directed-acyclic-graph description (Part 1): the treatment, outcome, mediator, and any auxiliary nodes with the arrows between them, written in dagitty syntax or as an ASCII diagram. The DAG is the artifact that makes the bad-controls audit auditable.
  2. mechanism/mediator-audit.md — the bad-controls audit (Part 2): a three-row table testing your candidate mediator against the four bad-control patterns.
  3. mechanism/mediation.py — the estimation script (Part 3): the three regressions (total, mediator-on-treatment, direct-with-mediator) plus the bootstrap for the indirect effect.
  4. mechanism/mediation-results.txt — the printed table: total effect, direct effect, indirect effect, Sobel z, bootstrap 95% CI on the indirect effect.
  5. mechanism/sensitivity.md — the omitted-mediator sensitivity analysis (Part 4): the bound on the indirect effect under plausible unobserved-confounder strengths.
  6. mechanism/memo.md — the one-page memo (Part 5) that interprets the mediation, defends or qualifies the bad-controls audit, and writes the paragraph that goes in §6 of the paper.

Part 1 — Draw the DAG before you run any regression (15 points)

The single sin this part prevents: adding a control to a regression because it “made the coefficient go in the right direction” rather than because the DAG says it belongs.

(a) Name the nodes (5 pts). List in dag.md:

  • T: the treatment (e.g., Maya’s exam-adoption indicator; Priya’s mandated-climate-disclosure indicator; Devon’s hard-fork event).
  • Y: the outcome (e.g., Maya’s denial gap; Priya’s forecast-accuracy error; Devon’s price-discovery time-to-fair-value).
  • M: the proposed mediator — the one channel you will estimate. Just one. Naming three mediators is fishing across the mediator space, which is the multiple-testing problem from Ch 10.2 in mediator clothing. Commit to one in this commit; you can have a second mediator as a robustness in Part 5, but the primary mediator is one variable.
  • U: any unobserved confounder you cannot rule out. Name it specifically — not “endogeneity” but, e.g., “local-mortgage-market intensity” (Maya), “firm-level analyst capacity” (Priya), or “miner political-coordination” (Devon).
  • Any observed confounders \mathbf{W} that affect both T and Y, or that affect M in a way that matters for the audit.

(b) Draw the arrows (5 pts). Write the arrows in dagitty syntax (or as an ASCII diagram). The canonical mediator DAG is:

T -> M -> Y
T -> Y           (the "direct effect" path, not through M)
W -> T
W -> Y
U -> M
U -> Y

The arrows you draw answer the questions: Does T cause M? Does M cause Y? Does anything else cause both M and Y? Each arrow needs a one-sentence justification of the form: “X \to Y because [theoretical/institutional reason].” For Priya, “M \to Y because the FCC-mandated climate-disclosure increases analyst attention to physical-risk content (M), which then improves forecast accuracy (Y) by providing analysts with structured data they previously had to scrape from news reports.” Eight to ten such sentences total.

(c) Identify the bad-control patterns before the audit in Part 2 (5 pts). Look at your DAG and find any of the four bad-control patterns from Cinelli, Forney, and Pearl (2024):

  • Pattern A — Post-treatment, on the causal path (M-mediation): T \to M \to Y. Conditioning on M blocks the indirect effect and isolates the direct effect. This is the mediation analysis itself; it is not “bad” if you want to decompose, but it destroys the total-effect estimate. Note: if your paper’s headline is the total effect, do not control for M in the headline regression — estimate M in a separate regression.
  • Pattern B — Post-treatment collider (M-bias): T \to M \leftarrow U \to Y. Conditioning on M opens a collider path T \to M \leftarrow U \to Y and biases the direct effect estimate in an unsigned direction. This is the bad-control pattern most likely to be present in an observational mediator.
  • Pattern C — Butterfly: T \to M and a separate T' \to M with T' \to Y — conditioning on M adjusts for the wrong variation.
  • Pattern D — Outcome of the outcome: Y \to M (reverse causality in the supposed mediator), so M is not actually a mediator at all.

Identify which pattern your candidate mediator falls into. Most observational mediators in finance papers are Pattern B: there is an unobserved U that causes both M and Y, and the textbook three-regression mediation (which assumes U does not exist) is biased. This is the question the audit in Part 2 confronts head-on.

Part 2 — The bad-controls audit (20 points)

For your candidate mediator M, fill out a three-column audit table.

Pattern Does it apply to your M? Evidence / argument
A: Post-treatment, on the path Yes / No …
B: Post-treatment collider (M-bias) Yes / No / Unsure …
C: Butterfly Yes / No …
D: Reverse causality (Y → M) Yes / No …

(a) Pattern A audit (4 pts). If M is post-treatment (measured after T), Pattern A applies by design — you are doing mediation, so T \to M \to Y is what you want. Confirm this is the case by checking the timing: M must be measured strictly between the treatment date and the outcome date. If M is measured before the treatment, it is not a mediator at all (it is at best a moderator, which is PS 10.3’s job). Report in mediator-audit.md the timing: T measured at date t_T, M at t_M, Y at t_Y, with t_T < t_M < t_Y. This is the minimum requirement; meeting it does not yet license the mediation interpretation.

(b) Pattern B audit — the central one (8 pts). This is the pattern that kills most mediator papers. The question: is there an unobserved U that causes both M and Y? You answer it in three steps.

  1. Name U specifically. Not “unobservables” but the specific omitted factor your DAG flagged. For Priya, U might be “firm-level analyst capacity” — firms with more analyst capacity both produce more accurate forecasts (U \to Y) and attract more analyst attention after disclosure (U \to M). If you cannot name U, you have not done the audit; the act of naming it forces you to think about whether it is plausible.
  2. Argue U does or does not exist. If you can credibly argue U does not exist (because the literature has ruled it out, because your design holds U fixed via a fixed effect, because your sample is selected to make U constant), then Pattern B does not apply and you can proceed with the textbook mediation. If you cannot rule U out, write so explicitly and proceed to Part 4’s sensitivity analysis to bound the bias.
  3. Optionally: an instrument for M. If you have a clean instrument Z that affects M but not Y directly except through M, you can estimate the mediation by 2SLS and avoid Pattern B entirely. This is rare in empirical finance and difficult to defend; do not invent an instrument to escape the audit. State whether you have one and, if so, describe it; if not, proceed to sensitivity in Part 4.

(c) Pattern C audit (3 pts). Butterfly bias is when a second variable T' affects both M and Y but is not the treatment. This is unusual; in most empirical-finance designs, the treatment is well-identified (a policy change, an event) and there are not competing pseudo-treatments. If your design is “everyone gets treated at the same time and there is a comparison group,” Pattern C is unlikely. If your design is more complex (e.g., two staggered policies), pattern C requires you to control for the second policy in both the M and Y regressions. State whether Pattern C applies.

(d) Pattern D audit (3 pts). Reverse causality in the mediator: does Y cause M rather than the other way around? For Priya, this would be “improved forecast accuracy attracts more analyst attention” — a plausible reverse-causality story. The way to rule it out is timing (which Pattern A’s check already covered) and theory (does the mechanism story require M \to Y or could it equally be Y \to M?). State your case.

(e) The verdict (2 pts). One sentence: “Of the four patterns, [X] applies/doesn’t apply to our mediator. We proceed [with the textbook mediation / with the sensitivity bound in Part 4 / with an alternative non-mediator interpretation in Part 5].” The verdict is what determines what Part 3 estimates.

Part 3 — Estimate the mediation (25 points)

If your audit cleared Pattern B (or you have an instrument), you estimate the textbook three-regression mediation. If it did not, you estimate the same regressions but interpret them as the upper bound (or lower bound, depending on the sign of the unobserved-confounder bias) on the indirect effect — see Part 4.

(a) The three regressions (10 pts). With T the treatment, M the mediator, Y the outcome, \mathbf{W} the observed controls:

Regression 1: Total effect. Y_i = \alpha_1 + \tau \cdot T_i + \mathbf{W}_i' \boldsymbol\gamma_1 + \varepsilon_{1i} The coefficient \hat\tau is the total effect of treatment on outcome. This is your headline — if you have a different headline regression (e.g., a DiD), state how it relates to this regression.

Regression 2: Treatment-on-mediator. M_i = \alpha_2 + \delta \cdot T_i + \mathbf{W}_i' \boldsymbol\gamma_2 + \varepsilon_{2i} The coefficient \hat\delta is the effect of treatment on the mediator. This is where the audit really matters: if your design identifies \tau in regression 1, it identifies \delta in regression 2 by the same argument.

Regression 3: Direct effect with mediator. Y_i = \alpha_3 + \tau^{\text{direct}} \cdot T_i + \gamma \cdot M_i + \mathbf{W}_i' \boldsymbol\gamma_3 + \varepsilon_{3i} The coefficient \hat\tau^{\text{direct}} is the direct effect; \hat\gamma is the mediator-to-outcome effect. The decomposition: indirect = \hat\gamma \cdot \hat\delta; total = \hat\tau^{\text{direct}} + \hat\gamma \cdot \hat\delta.

Implement these three regressions in mechanism/mediation.py. Verify the decomposition identity by computing both sides and comparing — they should match to within rounding error. With rng = np.random.default_rng(42):

# nb10.4 cell 2 — three-regression mediation, verified arithmetic
import numpy as np
import statsmodels.api as sm
rng = np.random.default_rng(42)

# Simulated example matching the chapter's numbers
N = 5000
D = rng.binomial(1, 0.5, N)
M = 0.5 + 0.8 * D + rng.standard_normal(N) * 1.0     # delta = 0.8
Y = 1.0 + 0.4 * D + 0.5 * M + rng.standard_normal(N) * 1.0  # tau_direct = 0.4, gamma = 0.5

X_t = sm.add_constant(D)
total = sm.OLS(Y, X_t).fit()
print(f"Total effect: {total.params[1]:.3f}")          # expect 0.816

X_d = sm.add_constant(np.column_stack([D, M]))
direct = sm.OLS(Y, X_d).fit()
print(f"Direct effect: {direct.params[1]:.3f}")        # expect 0.399
print(f"Mediator coef gamma: {direct.params[2]:.3f}")  # expect 0.513

X_m = sm.add_constant(D)
med = sm.OLS(M, X_m).fit()
print(f"Effect on mediator delta: {med.params[1]:.3f}")  # expect 0.813

indirect = direct.params[2] * med.params[1]
print(f"Indirect effect (gamma*delta): {indirect:.3f}")   # expect 0.417
print(f"Direct + indirect = {direct.params[1] + indirect:.3f}; total = {total.params[1]:.3f}")

Confirm the printed values match (the simulation seed 42 gives the numbers in the comments). On your own data, the three coefficients are whatever they are; the identity direct + indirect = total is what your code must verify.

(b) Sobel test (5 pts). The classical Sobel z for the indirect effect:

z = \frac{\hat\gamma \cdot \hat\delta}{\sqrt{\hat\gamma^2 \cdot \text{SE}(\hat\delta)^2 + \hat\delta^2 \cdot \text{SE}(\hat\gamma)^2}}

Report z and its two-sided p-value. The Sobel test is the baseline test of “is the indirect effect statistically distinguishable from zero?” but it assumes the indirect effect is normally distributed, which is wrong when either coefficient is small or noisy. Do not stop at the Sobel test — implement the bootstrap in (c) and report both.

(c) Bootstrap the indirect effect (10 pts). The defensible test of the indirect effect is the percentile-bootstrap CI from a cluster-bootstrap of the original data:

# nb10.4 cell 3 — bootstrap CI on the indirect effect, cluster-respecting
B = 9999
indirect_boot = np.zeros(B)
clusters = df["cluster_id"].to_numpy()
unique_clusters = np.unique(clusters)

for b in range(B):
    sampled = rng.choice(unique_clusters, size=len(unique_clusters), replace=True)
    idx = np.concatenate([np.where(clusters == c)[0] for c in sampled])
    Db, Mb, Yb = D[idx], M[idx], Y[idx]
    med_b = sm.OLS(Mb, sm.add_constant(Db)).fit()
    dir_b = sm.OLS(Yb, sm.add_constant(np.column_stack([Db, Mb]))).fit()
    indirect_boot[b] = dir_b.params[2] * med_b.params[1]

ci_lo, ci_hi = np.percentile(indirect_boot, [2.5, 97.5])
print(f"Indirect effect: {indirect:.3f}, 95% CI [{ci_lo:.3f}, {ci_hi:.3f}]")

Report the bootstrap point estimate, the 95% CI, and the proportion of bootstrap draws above zero (the bootstrap p-value). Compare to the Sobel p in (b): they may differ when the indirect effect is small or the distribution is skewed. The bootstrap is the report-in-the-paper number.

Part 4 — Sensitivity to omitted-mediator confounding (20 points)

If your Pattern B audit could not rule out an unobserved U that causes both M and Y, the textbook mediation estimate is biased and you must bound the bias. The standard tools: Imai, Keele, and Yamamoto (2010) sensitivity, or the simpler Oster (2019) delta applied to the mediator.

(a) The Imai-Keele-Yamamoto sensitivity parameter \rho (10 pts). The Imai-Keele-Yamamoto framework parameterizes unobserved confounding by the correlation \rho between the error in the mediator regression (\varepsilon_2) and the error in the outcome regression (\varepsilon_3). Under the null of no unobserved confounder, \rho = 0; under positive confounding (unobserved factors that raise both M and Y), \rho > 0. The indirect effect as a function of \rho is:

\widehat{ACME}(\rho) = \widehat{ACME}(0) - \frac{\rho \cdot \sigma_2 \cdot \sigma_3}{\sigma_M^2} \cdot \hat\delta

Compute \widehat{ACME}(\rho) for \rho \in \{-0.5, -0.3, -0.1, 0, 0.1, 0.3, 0.5\} and report the range of indirect effects. The pedagogical question: at what value of \rho does the indirect effect cross zero? That is the “breakdown” sensitivity value; if it occurs at |\rho| < 0.10, the indirect effect is fragile to unobserved confounding and your mechanism story is weak. If breakdown requires |\rho| > 0.40, the mechanism is robust.

(b) Plain-language sensitivity (5 pts). Convert the breakdown \rho into a plain-language statement: “For the indirect effect to be zero, an unobserved confounder would have to induce a correlation of [value] between the mediator’s residual and the outcome’s residual — equivalent to a confounder explaining [%] of the variation in M and [%] of the variation in Y simultaneously. We judge this [plausible / implausible] because [argument from institutional knowledge].”

(c) Bound the indirect effect (5 pts). Report a bounded indirect effect of the form: “Under the plausible range of unobserved confounding (|\rho| \le [\text{value}]), the indirect effect ranges from [low] to [high]; the textbook point estimate [value] sits in the middle of this range.” This is the honest report. If the bound straddles zero (low < 0 < high), the indirect effect is not robust and your paragraph in Part 5 must say so.

Part 5 — The §6 paragraph and the publishable mechanism statement (20 points)

(a) Draft the mechanism paragraph (12 pts). Four to six sentences in memo.md:

  1. The mechanism question. “A natural follow-up to the [headline] effect documented in §4 is whether [proposed mechanism] mediates the relationship.”
  2. The DAG and the audit. “We model the mechanism as T \to M \to Y and audit the mediator against the four bad-control patterns of Cinelli, Forney, and Pearl (2024). The audit finds Pattern [X] applies/does not apply [with specifics].”
  3. The decomposition. “The total effect of T on Y is [value] (HC1 SE = [value]); decomposing via the three-regression mediation, the direct effect is [value] and the indirect effect through M is [value] (95% bootstrap CI [low, high]).”
  4. The sensitivity. “The indirect effect is robust to unobserved confounding of strength |\rho| \le [\text{value}] (Imai, Keele, and Yamamoto 2010 sensitivity); we judge this plausible because [argument].”
  5. What we claim. “We interpret the mediation as evidence consistent with [mechanism story] without claiming a clean causal decomposition; the residual unobserved-confounder concern is bounded but not eliminated.”
  6. What we don’t claim. “We do not claim M is the unique mediator; the residual direct effect ([value]) is the share of the total effect through other channels we do not identify here.”

The fifth and sixth sentences are the load-bearing ones; they are the discipline that distinguishes a mediation paragraph from a causal-decomposition claim.

(b) Cite the bad-controls literature (3 pts). Include the citations:

  • Pearl, J. (2009). Causality: Models, Reasoning, and Inference, 2nd ed. Cambridge University Press.
  • Cinelli, C., Forney, A., & Pearl, J. (2024). “A Crash Course in Good and Bad Controls.” Sociological Methods & Research 53(3): 1071–1104.
  • Imai, K., Keele, L., & Yamamoto, T. (2010). “Identification, Inference, and Sensitivity Analysis for Causal Mediation Effects.” Statistical Science 25(1): 51–71.

The citation list is short but mandatory; a mediation paragraph without these three citations reads as if the authors are unaware of the literature, and a referee will say so.

(c) The single-sentence summary, for the abstract (5 pts). Write the one sentence that, if you put it in the abstract, would correctly summarize the mechanism finding: “The effect appears to operate through [mediator], which accounts for [X]% of the total effect (\widehat{\text{ACME}} = [value], 95% CI [low, high]); this share is robust to unobserved confounding of strength |\rho| \le [\text{value}].” The percentage is the indirect-as-share-of-total: \hat\gamma \cdot \hat\delta / \hat\tau. State the percentage and the conditional. A mechanism that “operates” through a channel that explains 10% of the total effect is a partial mediator; a 90% mediator is the dominant channel. State the share honestly.

The bridge to PS 10.5

PS 10.4 asked why the effect works. The mediation analysis here is internal to the sample (the indirect effect is the indirect effect in your data); external validity in PS 10.5 is the question of whether that same indirect effect would hold in a different sample (a different country, a different time period, a different sub-population). The two are complementary: PS 10.4 closes the within-sample-mechanism question, PS 10.5 opens the across-sample-generalizability question.

## Part 1 — Draw the DAG before you run any regression (15 points)

The single sin this part prevents: **adding a control to a regression because it "made the coefficient go in the right direction" rather than because the DAG says it belongs.**

**(a) Name the nodes (5 pts).** List in `dag.md`:

- $T$: the treatment (e.g., Maya's exam-adoption indicator; Priya's mandated-climate-disclosure indicator; Devon's hard-fork event).
- $Y$: the outcome (e.g., Maya's denial gap; Priya's forecast-accuracy error; Devon's price-discovery time-to-fair-value).
- $M$: the proposed mediator — the one channel you will estimate. Just one. Naming three mediators is fishing across the mediator space, which is the multiple-testing problem from Ch 10.2 in mediator clothing. Commit to one in this commit; you can have a second mediator as a robustness in Part 5, but the *primary* mediator is one variable.
- $U$: any *unobserved* confounder you cannot rule out. Name it specifically — not "endogeneity" but, e.g., "local-mortgage-market intensity" (Maya), "firm-level analyst capacity" (Priya), or "miner political-coordination" (Devon).
- Any *observed* confounders $\mathbf{W}$ that affect both $T$ and $Y$, or that affect $M$ in a way that matters for the audit.

**(b) Draw the arrows (5 pts).** Write the arrows in `dagitty` syntax (or as an ASCII diagram). The canonical mediator DAG is:

```
T -> M -> Y
T -> Y           (the "direct effect" path, not through M)
W -> T
W -> Y
U -> M
U -> Y
```

The arrows you draw answer the questions: *Does $T$ cause $M$? Does $M$ cause $Y$? Does anything else cause both $M$ and $Y$?* Each arrow needs a one-sentence justification of the form: *"$X \to Y$ because [theoretical/institutional reason]."* For Priya, *"$M \to Y$ because the FCC-mandated climate-disclosure increases analyst attention to physical-risk content (M), which then improves forecast accuracy (Y) by providing analysts with structured data they previously had to scrape from news reports."* Eight to ten such sentences total.

**(c) Identify the bad-control patterns *before* the audit in Part 2 (5 pts).** Look at your DAG and find any of the four bad-control patterns from Cinelli, Forney, and Pearl (2024):

- **Pattern A — Post-treatment, on the causal path (M-mediation):** $T \to M \to Y$. Conditioning on $M$ blocks the indirect effect and isolates the direct effect. *This is the mediation analysis itself*; it is not "bad" if you want to decompose, but it *destroys* the total-effect estimate. Note: if your paper's headline is the total effect, do not control for $M$ in the headline regression — estimate $M$ in a separate regression.
- **Pattern B — Post-treatment collider (M-bias):** $T \to M \leftarrow U \to Y$. Conditioning on $M$ opens a collider path $T \to M \leftarrow U \to Y$ and biases the *direct effect* estimate in an unsigned direction. *This is the bad-control pattern most likely to be present in an observational mediator.*
- **Pattern C — Butterfly:** $T \to M$ and a separate $T' \to M$ with $T' \to Y$ — conditioning on $M$ adjusts for the wrong variation.
- **Pattern D — Outcome of the outcome:** $Y \to M$ (reverse causality in the supposed mediator), so $M$ is not actually a mediator at all.

Identify which pattern your candidate mediator falls into. *Most observational mediators in finance papers are Pattern B*: there is an unobserved $U$ that causes both $M$ and $Y$, and the textbook three-regression mediation (which assumes $U$ does not exist) is biased. This is the question the audit in Part 2 confronts head-on.

## Part 2 — The bad-controls audit (20 points)

For your candidate mediator $M$, fill out a three-column audit table.

| Pattern | Does it apply to your $M$? | Evidence / argument |
|---|---|---|
| A: Post-treatment, on the path | Yes / No | ... |
| B: Post-treatment collider (M-bias) | Yes / No / Unsure | ... |
| C: Butterfly | Yes / No | ... |
| D: Reverse causality (Y → M) | Yes / No | ... |

**(a) Pattern A audit (4 pts).** If $M$ is post-treatment (measured after $T$), Pattern A applies *by design* — you are doing mediation, so $T \to M \to Y$ is what you want. Confirm this is the case by checking the *timing*: $M$ must be measured strictly between the treatment date and the outcome date. If $M$ is measured before the treatment, it is not a mediator at all (it is at best a moderator, which is PS 10.3's job). Report in `mediator-audit.md` the timing: $T$ measured at date $t_T$, $M$ at $t_M$, $Y$ at $t_Y$, with $t_T < t_M < t_Y$. This is the *minimum* requirement; meeting it does not yet license the mediation interpretation.

**(b) Pattern B audit — the central one (8 pts).** This is the pattern that kills most mediator papers. The question: *is there an unobserved $U$ that causes both $M$ and $Y$?* You answer it in three steps.

1. *Name $U$ specifically.* Not "unobservables" but the specific omitted factor your DAG flagged. For Priya, $U$ might be "firm-level analyst capacity" — firms with more analyst capacity both produce more accurate forecasts ($U \to Y$) and attract more analyst attention after disclosure ($U \to M$). If you cannot name $U$, you have not done the audit; the act of naming it forces you to think about whether it is plausible.
2. *Argue $U$ does or does not exist.* If you can credibly argue $U$ does not exist (because the literature has ruled it out, because your design holds $U$ fixed via a fixed effect, because your sample is selected to make $U$ constant), then Pattern B does not apply and you can proceed with the textbook mediation. If you cannot rule $U$ out, *write so explicitly* and proceed to Part 4's sensitivity analysis to bound the bias.
3. *Optionally: an instrument for $M$.* If you have a clean instrument $Z$ that affects $M$ but not $Y$ directly except through $M$, you can estimate the mediation by 2SLS and avoid Pattern B entirely. This is rare in empirical finance and difficult to defend; do not invent an instrument to escape the audit. State whether you have one and, if so, describe it; if not, proceed to sensitivity in Part 4.

**(c) Pattern C audit (3 pts).** Butterfly bias is when a second variable $T'$ affects both $M$ and $Y$ but is not the treatment. This is unusual; in most empirical-finance designs, the treatment is well-identified (a policy change, an event) and there are not competing pseudo-treatments. If your design is "everyone gets treated at the same time and there is a comparison group," Pattern C is unlikely. If your design is more complex (e.g., two staggered policies), pattern C requires you to control for the second policy in both the $M$ and $Y$ regressions. State whether Pattern C applies.

**(d) Pattern D audit (3 pts).** Reverse causality in the mediator: does $Y$ cause $M$ rather than the other way around? For Priya, this would be "improved forecast accuracy attracts more analyst attention" — a plausible reverse-causality story. The way to rule it out is *timing* (which Pattern A's check already covered) and *theory* (does the mechanism story require $M \to Y$ or could it equally be $Y \to M$?). State your case.

**(e) The verdict (2 pts).** One sentence: *"Of the four patterns, [X] applies/doesn't apply to our mediator. We proceed [with the textbook mediation / with the sensitivity bound in Part 4 / with an alternative non-mediator interpretation in Part 5]."* The verdict is what determines what Part 3 estimates.

## Part 3 — Estimate the mediation (25 points)

If your audit cleared Pattern B (or you have an instrument), you estimate the textbook three-regression mediation. If it did not, you estimate the same regressions but interpret them as *the upper bound* (or lower bound, depending on the sign of the unobserved-confounder bias) on the indirect effect — see Part 4.

**(a) The three regressions (10 pts).** With $T$ the treatment, $M$ the mediator, $Y$ the outcome, $\mathbf{W}$ the observed controls:

**Regression 1: Total effect.**
$$ Y_i = \alpha_1 + \tau \cdot T_i + \mathbf{W}_i' \boldsymbol\gamma_1 + \varepsilon_{1i} $$
The coefficient $\hat\tau$ is the total effect of treatment on outcome. This is your *headline* — if you have a different headline regression (e.g., a DiD), state how it relates to this regression.

**Regression 2: Treatment-on-mediator.**
$$ M_i = \alpha_2 + \delta \cdot T_i + \mathbf{W}_i' \boldsymbol\gamma_2 + \varepsilon_{2i} $$
The coefficient $\hat\delta$ is the effect of treatment on the mediator. *This is where the audit really matters*: if your design identifies $\tau$ in regression 1, it identifies $\delta$ in regression 2 by the same argument.

**Regression 3: Direct effect with mediator.**
$$ Y_i = \alpha_3 + \tau^{\text{direct}} \cdot T_i + \gamma \cdot M_i + \mathbf{W}_i' \boldsymbol\gamma_3 + \varepsilon_{3i} $$
The coefficient $\hat\tau^{\text{direct}}$ is the direct effect; $\hat\gamma$ is the mediator-to-outcome effect. *The decomposition*: indirect = $\hat\gamma \cdot \hat\delta$; total = $\hat\tau^{\text{direct}} + \hat\gamma \cdot \hat\delta$.

Implement these three regressions in `mechanism/mediation.py`. Verify the decomposition identity by computing both sides and comparing — they should match to within rounding error. With `rng = np.random.default_rng(42)`:

```python
# nb10.4 cell 2 — three-regression mediation, verified arithmetic
import numpy as np
import statsmodels.api as sm
rng = np.random.default_rng(42)

# Simulated example matching the chapter's numbers
N = 5000
D = rng.binomial(1, 0.5, N)
M = 0.5 + 0.8 * D + rng.standard_normal(N) * 1.0     # delta = 0.8
Y = 1.0 + 0.4 * D + 0.5 * M + rng.standard_normal(N) * 1.0  # tau_direct = 0.4, gamma = 0.5

X_t = sm.add_constant(D)
total = sm.OLS(Y, X_t).fit()
print(f"Total effect: {total.params[1]:.3f}")          # expect 0.816

X_d = sm.add_constant(np.column_stack([D, M]))
direct = sm.OLS(Y, X_d).fit()
print(f"Direct effect: {direct.params[1]:.3f}")        # expect 0.399
print(f"Mediator coef gamma: {direct.params[2]:.3f}")  # expect 0.513

X_m = sm.add_constant(D)
med = sm.OLS(M, X_m).fit()
print(f"Effect on mediator delta: {med.params[1]:.3f}")  # expect 0.813

indirect = direct.params[2] * med.params[1]
print(f"Indirect effect (gamma*delta): {indirect:.3f}")   # expect 0.417
print(f"Direct + indirect = {direct.params[1] + indirect:.3f}; total = {total.params[1]:.3f}")
```

Confirm the printed values match (the simulation seed 42 gives the numbers in the comments). On your own data, the three coefficients are whatever they are; the *identity* direct + indirect = total is what your code must verify.

**(b) Sobel test (5 pts).** The classical Sobel z for the indirect effect:

$$ z = \frac{\hat\gamma \cdot \hat\delta}{\sqrt{\hat\gamma^2 \cdot \text{SE}(\hat\delta)^2 + \hat\delta^2 \cdot \text{SE}(\hat\gamma)^2}} $$

Report $z$ and its two-sided p-value. The Sobel test is the *baseline* test of "is the indirect effect statistically distinguishable from zero?" but it assumes the indirect effect is normally distributed, which is wrong when either coefficient is small or noisy. *Do not stop at the Sobel test* — implement the bootstrap in (c) and report both.

**(c) Bootstrap the indirect effect (10 pts).** The defensible test of the indirect effect is the percentile-bootstrap CI from a cluster-bootstrap of the original data:

```python
# nb10.4 cell 3 — bootstrap CI on the indirect effect, cluster-respecting
B = 9999
indirect_boot = np.zeros(B)
clusters = df["cluster_id"].to_numpy()
unique_clusters = np.unique(clusters)

for b in range(B):
    sampled = rng.choice(unique_clusters, size=len(unique_clusters), replace=True)
    idx = np.concatenate([np.where(clusters == c)[0] for c in sampled])
    Db, Mb, Yb = D[idx], M[idx], Y[idx]
    med_b = sm.OLS(Mb, sm.add_constant(Db)).fit()
    dir_b = sm.OLS(Yb, sm.add_constant(np.column_stack([Db, Mb]))).fit()
    indirect_boot[b] = dir_b.params[2] * med_b.params[1]

ci_lo, ci_hi = np.percentile(indirect_boot, [2.5, 97.5])
print(f"Indirect effect: {indirect:.3f}, 95% CI [{ci_lo:.3f}, {ci_hi:.3f}]")
```

Report the bootstrap point estimate, the 95% CI, and the proportion of bootstrap draws above zero (the bootstrap p-value). Compare to the Sobel p in (b): they may differ when the indirect effect is small or the distribution is skewed. The bootstrap is the report-in-the-paper number.

## Part 4 — Sensitivity to omitted-mediator confounding (20 points)

If your Pattern B audit could not rule out an unobserved $U$ that causes both $M$ and $Y$, the textbook mediation estimate is *biased* and you must bound the bias. The standard tools: Imai, Keele, and Yamamoto (2010) sensitivity, or the simpler Oster (2019) delta applied to the mediator.

**(a) The Imai-Keele-Yamamoto sensitivity parameter $\rho$ (10 pts).** The Imai-Keele-Yamamoto framework parameterizes unobserved confounding by the correlation $\rho$ between the error in the mediator regression ($\varepsilon_2$) and the error in the outcome regression ($\varepsilon_3$). Under the null of no unobserved confounder, $\rho = 0$; under positive confounding (unobserved factors that raise both $M$ and $Y$), $\rho > 0$. The indirect effect as a function of $\rho$ is:

$$ \widehat{ACME}(\rho) = \widehat{ACME}(0) - \frac{\rho \cdot \sigma_2 \cdot \sigma_3}{\sigma_M^2} \cdot \hat\delta $$

Compute $\widehat{ACME}(\rho)$ for $\rho \in \{-0.5, -0.3, -0.1, 0, 0.1, 0.3, 0.5\}$ and report the range of indirect effects. The pedagogical question: *at what value of $\rho$ does the indirect effect cross zero?* That is the "breakdown" sensitivity value; if it occurs at $|\rho| < 0.10$, the indirect effect is fragile to unobserved confounding and your mechanism story is weak. If breakdown requires $|\rho| > 0.40$, the mechanism is robust.

**(b) Plain-language sensitivity (5 pts).** Convert the breakdown $\rho$ into a plain-language statement: *"For the indirect effect to be zero, an unobserved confounder would have to induce a correlation of [value] between the mediator's residual and the outcome's residual — equivalent to a confounder explaining [%] of the variation in $M$ and [%] of the variation in $Y$ simultaneously. We judge this [plausible / implausible] because [argument from institutional knowledge]."*

**(c) Bound the indirect effect (5 pts).** Report a *bounded* indirect effect of the form: *"Under the plausible range of unobserved confounding ($|\rho| \le [\text{value}]$), the indirect effect ranges from [low] to [high]; the textbook point estimate [value] sits in the middle of this range."* This is the honest report. If the bound straddles zero (low < 0 < high), the indirect effect is *not robust* and your paragraph in Part 5 must say so.

## Part 5 — The §6 paragraph and the publishable mechanism statement (20 points)

**(a) Draft the mechanism paragraph (12 pts).** Four to six sentences in `memo.md`:

1. *The mechanism question.* "A natural follow-up to the [headline] effect documented in §4 is whether [proposed mechanism] mediates the relationship."
2. *The DAG and the audit.* "We model the mechanism as $T \to M \to Y$ and audit the mediator against the four bad-control patterns of Cinelli, Forney, and Pearl (2024). The audit finds Pattern [X] applies/does not apply [with specifics]."
3. *The decomposition.* "The total effect of $T$ on $Y$ is [value] (HC1 SE = [value]); decomposing via the three-regression mediation, the direct effect is [value] and the indirect effect through $M$ is [value] (95% bootstrap CI [low, high])."
4. *The sensitivity.* "The indirect effect is robust to unobserved confounding of strength $|\rho| \le [\text{value}]$ (Imai, Keele, and Yamamoto 2010 sensitivity); we judge this plausible because [argument]."
5. *What we claim.* "We interpret the mediation as evidence consistent with [mechanism story] without claiming a clean causal decomposition; the residual unobserved-confounder concern is bounded but not eliminated."
6. *What we don't claim.* "We do not claim $M$ is the unique mediator; the residual direct effect ([value]) is the share of the total effect through other channels we do not identify here."

The fifth and sixth sentences are the load-bearing ones; they are the discipline that distinguishes a mediation paragraph from a causal-decomposition claim.

**(b) Cite the bad-controls literature (3 pts).** Include the citations:

- Pearl, J. (2009). *Causality: Models, Reasoning, and Inference*, 2nd ed. Cambridge University Press.
- Cinelli, C., Forney, A., & Pearl, J. (2024). "A Crash Course in Good and Bad Controls." *Sociological Methods & Research* 53(3): 1071–1104.
- Imai, K., Keele, L., & Yamamoto, T. (2010). "Identification, Inference, and Sensitivity Analysis for Causal Mediation Effects." *Statistical Science* 25(1): 51–71.

The citation list is short but mandatory; a mediation paragraph without these three citations reads as if the authors are unaware of the literature, and a referee will say so.

**(c) The single-sentence summary, for the abstract (5 pts).** Write the one sentence that, if you put it in the abstract, would correctly summarize the mechanism finding: *"The effect appears to operate through [mediator], which accounts for [X]% of the total effect ($\widehat{\text{ACME}}$ = [value], 95% CI [low, high]); this share is robust to unobserved confounding of strength $|\rho| \le [\text{value}]$."* The percentage is the indirect-as-share-of-total: $\hat\gamma \cdot \hat\delta / \hat\tau$. State the percentage and the conditional. A mechanism that "operates" through a channel that explains 10% of the total effect is a *partial mediator*; a 90% mediator is the dominant channel. State the share honestly.

## The bridge to PS 10.5

PS 10.4 asked *why* the effect works. PS 10.5 asks *who is the effect for* — what is the sample on which you estimated the effect, what is the target population the paper implicitly claims to speak to, and what is the transport error between the two. The mediation analysis here is *internal* to the sample (the indirect effect is the indirect effect in *your* data); external validity in PS 10.5 is the question of whether that same indirect effect would hold in a different sample (a different country, a different time period, a different sub-population). The two are complementary: PS 10.4 closes the within-sample-mechanism question, PS 10.5 opens the across-sample-generalizability question.
