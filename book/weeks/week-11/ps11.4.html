Builds on the Week-8 first-draft introduction you wrote alongside the early manuscript, on PS 11.1’s abstract (whose five sentences must each map to a paragraph of the intro), on PS 11.2’s table (which the Findings paragraph references), on PS 11.3’s figure (which the Findings or What-We-Do paragraph references), and on PS 10.5’s external-validity memo (which the Contribution paragraph cannot overclaim against). A boundary worth holding before you start. PS 11.4 is not “expand your introduction.” Almost every student introduction needs to shrink, not grow — the eleven-paragraph February draft is the modal mistake, and the five-paragraph rewrite is a reduction, not an expansion. The tracked-changes file is the proof you cut; without it, an advisor reading the new draft cannot tell whether you actually reorganized or just added more paragraphs on top of the old structure. The deletion is the discipline.

A second boundary. The five paragraphs are not five mini-papers. Each paragraph has one job. Paragraph 1’s job is to hook; once it has hooked, it stops. Paragraph 2’s job is to ask; once it has asked, it stops. Resist the temptation to put findings in paragraph 2 (because they’re exciting), or design details in paragraph 4 (because the reader might be confused), or contributions in paragraph 3 (because you want to “place” the design). One job per paragraph. The funnel only works if each paragraph narrows cleanly.

Part 1 — Paragraph 1: the Hook (15 points)

The single sin this part prevents: a literature-review opening. The hook is not the place to say “a growing literature has studied …”; it is the place to give a concrete fact, a specific number, or a vivid institutional detail that locates the reader in the world the paper is about.

(a) Identify your specific anchor (5 pts). Write a single concrete sentence that anchors the reader in a specific year, place, magnitude, or institutional detail. For Maya: “In 2019, a Black applicant for a conventional mortgage in the Atlanta MSA was 1.7 times as likely to be denied as a White applicant with the same income, credit score, debt-to-income ratio, and loan-to-value ratio.” For Devon: “On May 7, 2022, the algorithmic stablecoin TerraUSD lost its dollar peg by 12 cents in three hours, triggering a $48 billion drawdown in the algorithmic-stablecoin segment over the following 72 hours.” For Priya: “In 2018, FEMA issued revised flood-zone designations for 1,247 U.S. census tracts; homeowners in those tracts faced an average $1,840 annual premium increase in the following insurance cycle.” For Sam: “Between 9:30 and 10:00 a.m. on the average S&P 500 trading day in 2022, prices moved by 0.18%; the persistence of that morning move into the close was 41 basis points conditional on a 50-bp morning move.” For Leah: “Of the 8.7 million U.S. utility patents granted between 1976 and 2022, the median patent’s textual similarity to its three nearest neighbors falls below the cosine threshold of 0.15.”

The anchor sentence is the hardest one to write because it requires you to know your own data well enough to extract one number that lives in the world a non-specialist reader can picture. If you cannot write the anchor sentence in 25 words with a concrete number and a recognizable noun, you do not yet know your data well enough to write the introduction.

(b) The four-to-seven-sentence paragraph (8 pts). Build out from the anchor:

  • Sentence 1: The anchor (concrete fact, specific number).
  • Sentence 2: The puzzle — what makes this fact worth caring about, in one sentence. (“Gaps of this magnitude persist across the United States despite decades of fair-housing legislation…”)
  • Sentence 3: The specific institutional intervention being studied. (“In 2017, the Office of the Comptroller of the Currency initiated a program of intensified fair-lending examinations…”)
  • Sentence 4: The mechanism the intervention operates through. (“The program did not change underwriting law; it changed the intensity of scrutiny that lenders faced…”)
  • Sentence 5: The bridge to the question. (“Whether — and where, and for whom — that change in scrutiny translated into a measurable change in mortgage denial outcomes is the question this paper answers.”)

Five sentences, ~150 words. Some hooks use six or seven sentences; that is fine if each sentence carries weight. Avoid the eight-sentence hook — by then you are running the question into the hook, and paragraph 2’s job is being eaten.

(c) Diagnose what your Week-8 hook did wrong (2 pts). In paragraph-diagnostic.md, write one sentence naming the hook’s failure mode. The four named in Chapter 11.4 §2 are: literature-review hook (“A growing literature in finance…”), definition-hook (“Fair lending refers to the principle that…”), chest-thumping hook (“This paper makes three contributions to the literature on…”), anecdote-too-long hook (400 words on a single applicant). Pick the one your Week-8 hook fell into, or write “Week-8 hook did not exist; the paper opened with the literature review” if that was the case (which it often is for student drafts).

Part 2 — Paragraph 2: the Question (15 points)

The single sin this part prevents: stating the question without locating it. The question paragraph names the specific causal or descriptive question against the relevant prior literature’s gap.

(a) The question sentence (5 pts). The first sentence of paragraph 2 names the question. It is the longer, more grounded twin of sentence 1 of your abstract. Where the abstract is forced to be terse, the introduction can be specific: “This paper estimates the average treatment effect of the 2017 intensified fair-lending examination program on the county-year-level Black-White conventional-mortgage denial-rate gap, in the population of 614 OCC-supervised counties that experienced the rollout between 2017 and 2020.” The sentence names the treatment, the outcome, the unit, the time window, the population, and the design family (implied by “average treatment effect”). The reader after that sentence knows exactly what the paper is asking.

(b) The gap in the prior literature (5 pts). Sentences 2–4 of paragraph 2 name the gap your question fills. Three sentences, each acknowledging a prior literature thread:

  • Sentence 2: The descriptive literature on the gap. (“A large empirical literature documents persistent racial gaps in mortgage approval (Munnell et al. 1996; Bayer, Ferreira, and Ross 2016; Bartlett, Morse, Stanton, and Wallace 2022; Gao and Sun 2019).”)
  • Sentence 3: The literature on supervisory tools. (“A separate literature examines the effect of regulatory supervision on bank behavior more broadly (Agarwal, Lucca, Seru, and Trebbi 2014; Bhattacharya, Gabrieli, and Nieves 2024) but rarely focuses on disparate-impact outcomes.”)
  • Sentence 4: The gap your paper fills. (“We bring these literatures together by estimating, for a specific supervisory program and a specific disparate-impact outcome, the causal effect that the prior work either documents in correlation or examines for a different outcome.”)

The discipline: each cited paper has done some of what you do, but not all of it. Naming the partial overlap is more credible than claiming an empty space.

(c) The bridge to design (5 pts). The last sentence of paragraph 2 bridges to paragraph 3 (What-We-Do). It is a single sentence — “Answering this question requires a research design that isolates the effect of the 2017 intensification from other policy and market changes affecting the same counties over the same period; we now describe such a design.” — and it sets up the reader for the design paragraph without yet revealing the design.

Part 3 — Paragraph 3: the What-We-Do (20 points)

The single sin this part prevents: listing methods without naming assumptions. Paragraph 3 is the introduction’s version of sentence 3 of the abstract, expanded to give the reader enough detail to grade the identification before they reach the technical sections.

(a) The design family and variant (4 pts). First sentence of paragraph 3 names the design family (“difference-in-differences”) and the specific variant (“staggered, with cohort-specific event-study leads and lags, in the Callaway and Sant’Anna 2021 framework”). The variant is what the reader uses to know whether the negative-weight problem is being handled, whether the reader will see two-way fixed effects (rejected in modern staggered designs), or a heterogeneity-robust estimator. Naming the variant is naming the technical position the paper occupies.

(b) The data source and sample (4 pts). Second sentence names the data, the time window, the unit, and the sample size — the same content as the abstract’s sentence 2 but with more room to be specific. “We use loan-application-level data from HMDA’s Loan Application Register, 2014–2020, covering 47 million conventional mortgage originations across 47,128 zip codes in 3,134 counties, aggregated to the county-year panel of 21,938 observations.”

(c) The identifying assumption and the test (8 pts). Sentences 3–5 of paragraph 3 are the load-bearing trio:

  • Sentence 3 — the assumption. “Identification rests on the assumption that, absent the 2017 intensification, the denial-rate gap in treated counties would have followed the same time path as the gap in untreated counties — the parallel-trends assumption.”
  • Sentence 4 — the test for the assumption. “We test this assumption with pre-period event-study leads (Figure ); the leads are statistically indistinguishable from zero and the F-statistic on their joint significance is 1.04 (p = 0.39).”
  • Sentence 5 — the bound on unobservable selection. “We probe robustness to unobservable selection using Oster’s (2019) δ; the resulting bound of 1.7 implies that the unobservable selection would have to be 70% stronger than the observable selection to explain away the headline estimate.”

These three sentences are what earn the referee’s vote. Without the test sentence and the bound sentence, the design paragraph reduces to “we use DiD” and the assumption-naming has no defense.

(d) The reference to the figure and table (4 pts). The What-We-Do paragraph is the natural place to reference Figure 1 (the event-study plot from PS 11.3) and to foreshadow Table 2 (from PS 11.2). The references should be by label — \ref{fig:event-study} and \ref{tab:main} — and should be brief, not exhaustive. The figure’s job here is to back the parallel-trends sentence; the table’s job is to prepare the reader for the findings paragraph that follows.

Part 4 — Paragraph 4: the Findings (20 points)

The single sin this part prevents: burying the headline. Paragraph 4’s first sentence is the headline number, in the introduction’s voice, with the same precision as the abstract’s sentence 4 and the same value as Table 2’s preferred-column cell.

(a) The headline sentence (5 pts). First sentence: “We find that the 2017 intensification reduced the county-year Black-White denial-rate gap by 1.4 percentage points (Table , column 3; 95% CI: 0.8 to 2.0), a 23% reduction relative to the pre-treatment mean of 6.1 points.” Sign, magnitude, precision, relative magnitude, table reference. One sentence.

The headline sentence is the same number as the abstract’s sentence 4. If the two differ, you have an internal-consistency bug. Check this before submitting.

(b) The dynamic interpretation (5 pts). Second and third sentences interpret the dynamics from the event-study figure: “Effects emerge in the year of treatment, grow in magnitude over the first three post-treatment years (Figure ; \hat{\delta}_3 = -1.8 pp, 95% CI: -2.4 to -1.2), and stabilize at roughly the magnitude of the pooled estimate. We see no evidence of pre-treatment trends in the leads (\hat{\delta}_{-3} = 0.06 pp, 95% CI: -0.42 to 0.54; \hat{\delta}_{-2} = -0.11 pp, 95% CI: -0.59 to 0.37).” The reader after these sentences knows the shape of the effect, not just the average.

(c) The heterogeneity that survives multiple-testing (5 pts). Sentence 4 reports the heterogeneity from PS 10.3, adjusted for multiple testing from PS 10.2. “Effects are concentrated in counties with above-median minority-population shares (interaction coefficient: -1.1 pp, BH-FDR-adjusted p = 0.012); two of the three other pre-specified heterogeneity cuts do not survive multiple-testing adjustment and are reported as exploratory.” The discipline of saying which cuts are confirmed and which are exploratory is what separates a credible heterogeneity paragraph from a fishing-expedition one.

(d) The robustness anchor (5 pts). Sentence 5 anchors the result to the robustness summary: “The headline estimate survives the staggered-DiD alternatives (Callaway-Sant’Anna, Sun-Abraham, de Chaisemartin-D’Haultfœuille), the saturation alternatives (county-by-year fixed effects, lender fixed effects), the sample restrictions (the ‘tight’ OCC-only subsample), and the placebo events (Table in §6).” The reader after this sentence knows the result is not knife-edge.

Part 5 — Paragraph 5: the Contribution (15 points)

The single sin this part prevents: the three-strand contribution paragraph that does not say what was added to each strand. Paragraph 5 is structured around the three literatures the paper engages, with a one-or-two-sentence contribution claim against each.

(a) Strand 1 — what literature, what added (5 pts). First two sentences. Name the first literature: “We contribute to the literature on racial disparities in mortgage lending (Munnell et al. 1996; Bayer, Ferreira, and Ross 2016; Bartlett et al. 2022; Gao and Sun 2019) by isolating the causal effect of a specific supervisory tool against the documented disparate-impact gap.” The contribution sentence names what is new relative to the cited works — not “we contribute” (everyone does), but what specifically was added.

(b) Strand 2 — what literature, what added (5 pts). Second two sentences. Name the second literature: “We contribute to the literature on supervisory intensity (Agarwal et al. 2014; Bhattacharya et al. 2024) by extending the analysis from prudential outcomes (capital ratios, lending growth) to disparate-impact outcomes (denial-rate gaps), and by exploiting the staggered-rollout variation that the supervisory-bank-pairing prevented in earlier work.”

(c) Strand 3 — what literature, what added, what to do with it (5 pts). Third two sentences. Name the third literature: “We contribute to the policy-evaluation literature on fair-lending interventions (Munnell et al. 1996; Ross, Turner, Godfrey, and Smith 2008) by quantifying the magnitude that supervisory intensity can deliver — roughly one-quarter of the persistent national gap — and by bounding the external validity (§7) to the OCC-supervised counties that experienced the 2017 rollout.” The closing sentence is the what-now — the consequence the paper’s reader will carry forward.

Part 6 — The tracked-changes file and the cut list (15 points)

This is the diagnostic that proves you cut rather than just added.

(a) The tracked-changes file (8 pts). In tracked-changes.md, paste the Week-8 introduction. Strike through every word, phrase, or paragraph you deleted. Bold (or underline) every word, phrase, or paragraph you added. Reordered text should be both struck through (in its original location) and bolded (in its new location). The file should look like a Word document with track-changes on, rendered in Markdown.

Most successful Week-8-to-Week-11 introduction rewrites delete 40-60% of the original text. If your tracked-changes file shows only ~10% deletions and ~50% additions, you have expanded rather than reorganized, and you have likely violated the five-paragraph discipline somewhere. Verify the paragraph count of the rewrite (it should be exactly 5).

(b) The cut list (5 pts). In cut-list.md, list every cut at the paragraph or major-clause level, with a one-line justification. Examples:

  • “Cut paragraph 3 of Week-8 (definition of HMDA): no longer needed; the rewrite mentions HMDA in paragraph 3 by name only, and the definition lives in the data section.”
  • “Cut sentence 4 of Week-8 paragraph 2 (‘In addition, the literature has long debated…’): this sentence is a tangent on a debate the paper does not enter; tangent removed.”
  • “Cut paragraph 7 of Week-8 (a 200-word stream on broader societal implications of fair lending): this is a discussion-section thought, not an introduction thought; moved to §7 or deleted entirely.”
  • “Cut paragraph 10 of Week-8 (the chest-thumping ‘we make three contributions’ paragraph): folded into the new paragraph 5, but the original phrasing is gone.”

Each cut sentence is its own diagnostic — by writing the justification, you are confirming the discipline of the rewrite.

(c) The shrink ratio (2 pts). Word-count both versions. Report: “Week-8 was 3,200 words across 11 paragraphs; Week-11 is 1,520 words across 5 paragraphs. Shrink ratio: 0.475 (52.5% reduction).” The shrink ratio for an honest rewrite is typically 0.40–0.55. If your shrink ratio is below 0.30, you have expanded rather than rewritten; revisit the cut list.

How this is graded

**(b) The gap in the prior literature (5 pts).** Sentences 2–4 of paragraph 2 name the gap your question fills. Three sentences, each acknowledging a prior literature thread:

- *Sentence 2:* The descriptive literature on the gap. ("A large empirical literature documents persistent racial gaps in mortgage approval (Munnell et al. 1996; Bayer, Ferreira, and Ross 2016; Bartlett, Morse, Stanton, and Wallace 2022; Gao and Sun 2019).")
- *Sentence 3:* The literature on supervisory tools. ("A separate literature examines the effect of regulatory supervision on bank behavior more broadly (Agarwal, Lucca, Seru, and Trebbi 2014; Bhattacharya, Gabrieli, and Nieves 2024) but rarely focuses on disparate-impact outcomes.")
- *Sentence 4:* The gap your paper fills. ("We bring these literatures together by estimating, for a specific supervisory program and a specific disparate-impact outcome, the causal effect that the prior work either documents in correlation or examines for a different outcome.")

The discipline: each cited paper has done *some* of what you do, but not *all* of it. Naming the partial overlap is more credible than claiming an empty space.

**(c) The bridge to design (5 pts).** The last sentence of paragraph 2 bridges to paragraph 3 (What-We-Do). It is a single sentence — *"Answering this question requires a research design that isolates the effect of the 2017 intensification from other policy and market changes affecting the same counties over the same period; we now describe such a design."* — and it sets up the reader for the design paragraph without yet revealing the design.

## Part 3 — Paragraph 3: the What-We-Do (20 points)

The single sin this part prevents: **listing methods without naming assumptions.** Paragraph 3 is the introduction's version of sentence 3 of the abstract, expanded to give the reader enough detail to grade the identification before they reach the technical sections.

**(a) The design family and variant (4 pts).** First sentence of paragraph 3 names the design family ("difference-in-differences") and the specific variant ("staggered, with cohort-specific event-study leads and lags, in the Callaway and Sant'Anna 2021 framework"). The variant is what the reader uses to know whether the negative-weight problem is being handled, whether the reader will see two-way fixed effects (rejected in modern staggered designs), or a heterogeneity-robust estimator. Naming the variant is naming the technical position the paper occupies.

**(b) The data source and sample (4 pts).** Second sentence names the data, the time window, the unit, and the sample size — the same content as the abstract's sentence 2 but with more room to be specific. *"We use loan-application-level data from HMDA's Loan Application Register, 2014–2020, covering 47 million conventional mortgage originations across 47,128 zip codes in 3,134 counties, aggregated to the county-year panel of 21,938 observations."*

**(c) The identifying assumption and the test (8 pts).** Sentences 3–5 of paragraph 3 are the load-bearing trio:

- *Sentence 3 — the assumption.* "Identification rests on the assumption that, absent the 2017 intensification, the denial-rate gap in treated counties would have followed the same time path as the gap in untreated counties — the parallel-trends assumption."
- *Sentence 4 — the test for the assumption.* "We test this assumption with pre-period event-study leads (Figure \ref{fig:event-study}); the leads are statistically indistinguishable from zero and the F-statistic on their joint significance is 1.04 ($p = 0.39$)."
- *Sentence 5 — the bound on unobservable selection.* "We probe robustness to unobservable selection using Oster's (2019) δ; the resulting bound of 1.7 implies that the unobservable selection would have to be 70% stronger than the observable selection to explain away the headline estimate."

These three sentences are what earn the referee's vote. Without the test sentence and the bound sentence, the design paragraph reduces to "we use DiD" and the assumption-naming has no defense.

**(d) The reference to the figure and table (4 pts).** The What-We-Do paragraph is the natural place to reference Figure 1 (the event-study plot from PS 11.3) and to foreshadow Table 2 (from PS 11.2). The references should be by label — `\ref{fig:event-study}` and `\ref{tab:main}` — and should be brief, not exhaustive. The figure's job here is to *back* the parallel-trends sentence; the table's job is to *prepare* the reader for the findings paragraph that follows.

## Part 4 — Paragraph 4: the Findings (20 points)

The single sin this part prevents: **burying the headline.** Paragraph 4's first sentence is the headline number, in the introduction's voice, with the same precision as the abstract's sentence 4 and the same value as Table 2's preferred-column cell.

**(a) The headline sentence (5 pts).** First sentence: *"We find that the 2017 intensification reduced the county-year Black-White denial-rate gap by 1.4 percentage points (Table \ref{tab:main}, column 3; 95% CI: 0.8 to 2.0), a 23% reduction relative to the pre-treatment mean of 6.1 points."* Sign, magnitude, precision, relative magnitude, table reference. One sentence.

The headline sentence is *the same number* as the abstract's sentence 4. If the two differ, you have an internal-consistency bug. Check this before submitting.

**(b) The dynamic interpretation (5 pts).** Second and third sentences interpret the dynamics from the event-study figure: *"Effects emerge in the year of treatment, grow in magnitude over the first three post-treatment years (Figure \ref{fig:event-study}; $\hat{\delta}_3 = -1.8$ pp, 95% CI: $-2.4$ to $-1.2$), and stabilize at roughly the magnitude of the pooled estimate. We see no evidence of pre-treatment trends in the leads ($\hat{\delta}_{-3} = 0.06$ pp, 95% CI: $-0.42$ to $0.54$; $\hat{\delta}_{-2} = -0.11$ pp, 95% CI: $-0.59$ to $0.37$)."* The reader after these sentences knows the *shape* of the effect, not just the average.

**(c) The heterogeneity that survives multiple-testing (5 pts).** Sentence 4 reports the heterogeneity from PS 10.3, adjusted for multiple testing from PS 10.2. *"Effects are concentrated in counties with above-median minority-population shares (interaction coefficient: $-1.1$ pp, BH-FDR-adjusted $p = 0.012$); two of the three other pre-specified heterogeneity cuts do not survive multiple-testing adjustment and are reported as exploratory."* The discipline of saying which cuts are *confirmed* and which are *exploratory* is what separates a credible heterogeneity paragraph from a fishing-expedition one.

**(d) The robustness anchor (5 pts).** Sentence 5 anchors the result to the robustness summary: *"The headline estimate survives the staggered-DiD alternatives (Callaway-Sant'Anna, Sun-Abraham, de Chaisemartin-D'Haultfœuille), the saturation alternatives (county-by-year fixed effects, lender fixed effects), the sample restrictions (the 'tight' OCC-only subsample), and the placebo events (Table \ref{tab:robustness} in §6)."* The reader after this sentence knows the result is not knife-edge.

## Part 5 — Paragraph 5: the Contribution (15 points)

The single sin this part prevents: **the three-strand contribution paragraph that does not say what was added to each strand.** Paragraph 5 is structured around the three literatures the paper engages, with a one-or-two-sentence contribution claim against each.

**(a) Strand 1 — what literature, what added (5 pts).** First two sentences. Name the first literature: *"We contribute to the literature on racial disparities in mortgage lending (Munnell et al. 1996; Bayer, Ferreira, and Ross 2016; Bartlett et al. 2022; Gao and Sun 2019) by isolating the causal effect of a specific supervisory tool against the documented disparate-impact gap."* The contribution sentence names *what is new* relative to the cited works — not "we contribute" (everyone does), but *what specifically was added*.

**(b) Strand 2 — what literature, what added (5 pts).** Second two sentences. Name the second literature: *"We contribute to the literature on supervisory intensity (Agarwal et al. 2014; Bhattacharya et al. 2024) by extending the analysis from prudential outcomes (capital ratios, lending growth) to disparate-impact outcomes (denial-rate gaps), and by exploiting the staggered-rollout variation that the supervisory-bank-pairing prevented in earlier work."*

**(c) Strand 3 — what literature, what added, what to do with it (5 pts).** Third two sentences. Name the third literature: *"We contribute to the policy-evaluation literature on fair-lending interventions (Munnell et al. 1996; Ross, Turner, Godfrey, and Smith 2008) by quantifying the magnitude that supervisory intensity can deliver — roughly one-quarter of the persistent national gap — and by bounding the external validity (§7) to the OCC-supervised counties that experienced the 2017 rollout."* The closing sentence is the *what-now* — the consequence the paper's reader will carry forward.

## Part 6 — The tracked-changes file and the cut list (15 points)

This is the diagnostic that proves you *cut* rather than just *added*.

**(a) The tracked-changes file (8 pts).** In `tracked-changes.md`, paste the Week-8 introduction. Strike through every word, phrase, or paragraph you deleted. Bold (or underline) every word, phrase, or paragraph you added. Reordered text should be both struck through (in its original location) and bolded (in its new location). The file should look like a Word document with track-changes on, rendered in Markdown.

Most successful Week-8-to-Week-11 introduction rewrites delete 40-60% of the original text. If your tracked-changes file shows only ~10% deletions and ~50% additions, you have *expanded* rather than *reorganized*, and you have likely violated the five-paragraph discipline somewhere. Verify the paragraph count of the rewrite (it should be exactly 5).

**(b) The cut list (5 pts).** In `cut-list.md`, list every cut at the paragraph or major-clause level, with a one-line justification. Examples:

- "Cut paragraph 3 of Week-8 (definition of HMDA): no longer needed; the rewrite mentions HMDA in paragraph 3 by name only, and the definition lives in the data section."
- "Cut sentence 4 of Week-8 paragraph 2 ('In addition, the literature has long debated…'): this sentence is a tangent on a debate the paper does not enter; tangent removed."
- "Cut paragraph 7 of Week-8 (a 200-word stream on broader societal implications of fair lending): this is a discussion-section thought, not an introduction thought; moved to §7 or deleted entirely."
- "Cut paragraph 10 of Week-8 (the chest-thumping 'we make three contributions' paragraph): folded into the new paragraph 5, but the original phrasing is gone."

Each cut sentence is its own diagnostic — by writing the justification, you are confirming the discipline of the rewrite.

**(c) The shrink ratio (2 pts).** Word-count both versions. Report: *"Week-8 was 3,200 words across 11 paragraphs; Week-11 is 1,520 words across 5 paragraphs. Shrink ratio: 0.475 (52.5% reduction)."* The shrink ratio for an honest rewrite is typically 0.40–0.55. If your shrink ratio is below 0.30, you have expanded rather than rewritten; revisit the cut list.

## How this is graded

Part 1 (Hook, 15) on whether the anchor sentence is concrete and specific (a vivid number, a recognizable place, a named institution) and on whether the hook bridges cleanly to the question. Part 2 (Question, 15) on whether the question is specific and whether the literature gap is named at the level of overlapping-and-incomplete works rather than empty space. Part 3 (What-We-Do, 20) on whether the design family, variant, assumption, test, and bound are each named in their own sentence with the figure and table referenced by label. Part 4 (Findings, 20) on whether the headline number is the same across abstract, intro paragraph 4, and Table 2 column 3, and on whether the heterogeneity is disciplined by multiple-testing adjustment. Part 5 (Contribution, 15) on whether each strand has an explicit *what-was-added* sentence rather than a generic "we contribute." Part 6 (tracked changes, 15) on whether the shrink ratio is in the 0.40–0.55 range and the cut list is honest.

The single highest-signal check: **is the rewrite exactly five paragraphs?** A six-paragraph introduction has not yet absorbed the chapter's discipline. A four-paragraph introduction has missed one of the five jobs (usually the hook or the contribution). Five paragraphs, no more, no less.
