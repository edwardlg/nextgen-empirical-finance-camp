Ch 11.5 — The Expanded Literature Review: from a Bullet List to a Three-Strand Argument that Earns Your Contribution Sentence

You stitched the five-paragraph introduction into Maya’s manuscript on Friday afternoon. The contribution paragraph at the end names three literatures — fair-lending disparities, real effects of bank supervision, methodological staggered-DiD — and claims one contribution in each. On Monday your advisor’s email reads: “The intro lands. Now Section 2 needs to earn the contribution sentence.” You scroll to Section 2 of the manuscript and find a five-page bulleted list of papers, organized roughly by topic, with one-sentence summaries that read like an annotated bibliography written for a different assignment. It is not a literature review; it is a literature enumeration. This chapter is the discipline that turns the enumeration into an argument.

Here is the result the chapter turns on. A publication-grade literature review is not a list of papers; it is an argument that, by the end of the section, has earned the contribution sentences in the introduction. The argument has the structure of a three-strand braid: each strand corresponds to one literature your paper engages, each strand traces the development of that literature into the gap your paper fills, and the three strands together make it inevitable that the paper you wrote should exist. By the end of the literature review, a reader should be able to anticipate the contribution paragraph without having read the introduction. The literature review is not “background”; it is the rhetorical machinery that makes the contribution feel earned rather than asserted. This chapter is how you build the machinery.

The chapter is structured as the reveal-the-trick of every other chapter in this week. We will look at what a literature review is for (§1), at the three-strand braid as the load-bearing structure (§2), at how each strand is built (§§3–5), at the synthesis-and-gap closing (§6), at the common failure modes (§7), and at the procedural mechanics — what to read, in what order, how much time to spend — (§8). Maya’s fair-lending paper carries the worked example. By the end of the chapter we will see her literature review move from a bulleted enumeration of forty-three papers to a tightly argued nine-page Section 2 with twenty-eight strategically chosen citations organized into three strands. The discipline is selection, then synthesis, then argument.

1. The reveal: what a literature review is for

The textbook definition of a literature review — “a survey of prior work on the topic” — is functionally wrong. It produces the bulleted enumeration that students draft on their first pass and that referees mark for revision. The correct functional definition has three parts: a literature review establishes that the question has not been answered, establishes that the question is worth asking, and establishes that the design we propose is appropriate to the question. Each of these is a rhetorical job, not a summary job, and rhetorical jobs require argument structure.

The literature review has three readers, and like the abstract’s three readers (Chapter 11.1), the structure must satisfy all three.

The first reader is the same referee who read the introduction. She is checking whether the contribution claim is defensible. If your paragraph 5 in the introduction says “the closest precedent is Bhutta, Hizmo, and Ringo (2024) but our setting differs in dimensions X, Y, Z,” she will read Section 2 looking for the substantive engagement with Bhutta et al. that makes the X-Y-Z differentiation credible. If she does not find it — if Bhutta et al. is cited once and not discussed — she marks the contribution claim as overstated and the paper as needing major revision.

The second reader is the scholar working on the closest neighboring problem. She is checking whether you have read her work and represented it fairly. If your paper purports to be the first credibly identified estimate of fair-lending-examination effects, and she has a 2023 working paper on a closely related question with a different design, she will be looking for it in your citations. If it is missing, she becomes the referee who writes “the author appears unaware of …” — a near-fatal review. If it is cited but characterized inaccurately (“Smith (2023) studies a similar question and finds null effects, but uses a less credible design”) she becomes the angrier referee who writes “the author has mischaracterized Smith (2023), which actually does the following …”

The third reader is the graduate student or junior scholar who finds your paper three years from now and uses your literature review as a map of the field. If your literature review is well-organized and well-cited, she uses it to find her own way into the literature and cites your paper in passing as the canonical entry-point synthesis. If it is poorly-organized or under-cited, she does not.

The three readers impose three discipline constraints. The literature review must (a) defend the contribution claim in detail, (b) cite and characterize accurately every paper a knowledgeable referee would expect to see, and (c) organize the field so a future reader can use the review as a map. The three-strand braid is the structure that satisfies all three.

2. The three-strand braid

The braid is the load-bearing structure. Each strand is one literature your paper engages; each strand has a beginning (the foundational work), a middle (the recent extensions), and an end (the gap your paper fills). The three strands are interwoven across the section so the reader sees the convergence of all three on your question; the strands are not just three separate mini-reviews followed by a “and we contribute to all three” sentence.

For Maya’s paper, the three strands are:

Strand A — the fair-lending disparities literature. Foundational: Munnell, Tootell, Browne, and McEneaney (1996) (the Boston Fed study). Recent extensions: Bayer, Ferreira, and Ross (2018) on high-cost lender concentration; Gao and Sun (2019) on race-match effects in the underwriter–borrower dyad; Bhutta, Hizmo, and Ringo (2024) on algorithmic vs. human credit decisions. Gap: no credibly identified estimate of the effect of regulatory intervention on the gap.

Strand B — the real effects of bank supervision literature. Foundational: Berger and Bouwman (2013) on supervisory ratings and risk-taking; Hirtle, Kovner, and Plosser (2020) on the impact of supervision on bank outcomes. Recent extensions: Agarwal, Ben-David, and Yao (2017) on consumer-finance supervisory effects; Granja and Leuz (2024) on supervisory attention’s allocative effects. Gap: the literature has focused on safety-and-soundness outcomes (credit allocation, risk-taking) rather than on distributional outcomes (the cross-group denial gap).

Strand C — the methodological literature on staggered DiD and heterogeneity-disciplined inference. Foundational: Bertrand, Duflo, and Mullainathan (2004) on serial correlation in DiD; Callaway and Sant’Anna (2021) and Sun and Abraham (2021) on heterogeneous-treatment-effect DiD. Recent extensions: Roth, Sant’Anna, Bilinski, and Poe (2023) on the assumptions defense; Simonsohn, Simmons, and Nelson (2020) on specification curve analysis. Gap: the application of the full methodological battery (staggered DiD + multiple-testing-disciplined heterogeneity + Oster sensitivity + spec curve) to a fair-lending regulatory intervention has not been done.

Three strands, three gaps, all converging on Maya’s question. The literature review’s job is to make the convergence inevitable.

The strands are unequal in length. Strand A (the substantive literature on disparities) is the deepest engagement and gets the most space — perhaps two to three pages of the nine-page Section 2. Strand B (the supervisory-effects literature) is the second deepest — perhaps two pages. Strand C (the methodological literature) is the briefest — perhaps a page and a half — because the methodological contribution is auxiliary to the substantive one, and the deep methodological discussion belongs in Section 3 (Empirical Strategy). The proportions track the proportion of the contribution paragraph each strand earns.

The strands are interwoven, not stacked. A naive structure has Sub-section 2.1 = Strand A, Sub-section 2.2 = Strand B, Sub-section 2.3 = Strand C, with no cross-references. This is the bulleted-list pathology dressed in sub-section headers. The better structure interleaves: Sub-section 2.1 introduces the disparities literature, ending with the unresolved question of regulatory intervention; Sub-section 2.2 introduces the supervisory-intensity literature, returning to the question of whether it has been applied to distributional outcomes; Sub-section 2.3 introduces the methodological literature, framed as the toolkit that the substantive question demands; Sub-section 2.4 (the synthesis-and-gap closer) ties the three together. The reader sees the three strands converge; she does not see three separate sub-reviews.

3. Building Strand A — the substantive disparities literature

We work through Strand A in detail; Strands B and C follow the same template more briefly.

The strand has a chronological-but-thematic structure. Chronological because the literature develops over time and the reader benefits from seeing the development; thematic because we group the citations into the intellectual moves the literature has made, not into a flat year-by-year list.

For Maya’s Strand A, three intellectual moves:

Move 1: documenting the gap. The Munnell, Tootell, Browne, and McEneaney (1996) Boston Fed study established that, in 1990 Boston-area mortgage applications, Black applicants were 2.4 times as likely to be denied as White applicants with similar applicant characteristics. The paper drew controversy at the time for methodological choices in handling censored applications, and the subsequent literature spent a decade arguing about whether the gap was real or an artifact of unobservables. The Munnell et al. controversy resolved into broad agreement — by the late 1990s and the 2000s — that the gap was real and persistent, though the magnitude varied across datasets and specifications. Move 1 ends with the acknowledgment that the gap is established.

Move 2: characterizing the gap’s mechanisms. Once the gap was established, the literature turned to characterizing how it arose. Bayer, Ferreira, and Ross (2008, 2018) showed that a substantial portion of the gap is explained by the concentration of high-cost, high-denial lenders in minority neighborhoods — a sorting story, not a same-lender-discrimination story. Hanson, Hawley, Martin, and Liu (2016) found evidence of differential treatment in lender response to similar applicant signals. Gao and Sun (2019) demonstrated the race-match effect — that Black applicants paired with Black loan officers had higher approval rates than Black applicants paired with non-Black officers, by 17 percentage points. The mechanism literature converged on the view that the gap arises from a combination of structural sorting and within-lender discretionary behavior, with both channels active.

Move 3: probing interventions. The most recent strand of the literature, primarily 2020 onward, has begun to ask what interventions reduce the gap. Bhutta, Hizmo, and Ringo (2024) compare algorithmic and human credit decisions and find that algorithms reduce but do not eliminate the gap; Fuster, Goldsmith-Pinkham, Ramadorai, and Walther (2022) study the distributional effects of algorithmic underwriting. The CRA-driven literature (Agarwal, Benmelech, Bergman, and Seru, 2012) studies a different regulatory intervention with mixed evidence on disparate-impact outcomes. The intervention literature is the closest to Maya’s question — but it has not directly studied the OCC’s targeted fair-lending examination program, and Move 3 ends with this gap.

The Strand A sub-section, roughly two and a half pages, reads like one continuous argument across these three moves. The transitions are explicit (“Once the gap was firmly established, the literature turned to …”; “More recently, attention has shifted to …”). Citations are clustered at the points where each move begins and where the move’s central claim is reinforced. Bullet lists do not appear; full paragraphs do.

Two principles govern how each citation is treated.

Cite what the paper finds, not just that it exists. The bad version: “Bayer, Ferreira, and Ross (2018) study mortgage disparities.” The good version: “Bayer, Ferreira, and Ross (2018) show that 30–40% of the Black–White origination gap is driven by the geographic concentration of high-cost lenders in minority neighborhoods, rather than by within-lender discrimination.” The good version makes the citation contribute to the argument: the reader learns something useful even if she does not click through to read Bayer et al. for herself. The bad version assigns the citation a role of merely existing, which wastes the citation.

Characterize accurately enough that the cited author would recognize the characterization. This is the second-reader (neighboring-scholar) constraint. If you cite a paper for a claim that the paper does not make, or if you cite it for a finding that is actually qualified in the paper, the cited author becomes the angry referee. The discipline: when you cite a finding, you should be able to point to the table, sentence, or finding in the cited paper from which the characterization comes. If you cannot, rewrite the citation more cautiously or pull a different one.

Here is the opening of Strand A as it appears in Maya’s revised Section 2 (about 420 words):

The persistence of a Black–White mortgage-denial gap, conditional on standard underwriting characteristics, has been documented across decades and datasets. The foundational empirical work, Munnell, Tootell, Browne, and McEneaney (1996), used 1990 Boston-area mortgage applications and found that Black applicants were 2.4 times as likely as observably similar White applicants to be denied conventional mortgages. The Munnell et al. result generated a decade of methodological debate concerning the treatment of censored applications and the handling of missing variables in the lender’s information set; that debate resolved into broad agreement, by the early 2000s, that the gap was real and substantial, though the precise magnitude varied with sample construction and outcome definition (Ross and Yinger, 2002).

Once the gap was firmly established, the literature turned to characterizing its mechanisms. Bayer, Ferreira, and Ross (2018), using a multi-decade panel of mortgage applications, showed that 30–40% of the Black–White origination gap is driven by the geographic concentration of high-cost lenders in minority neighborhoods rather than by within-lender disparate treatment — a sorting mechanism that the legal apparatus of fair-lending compliance does not directly address. Within-lender behaviors have nevertheless been shown to contribute. Gao and Sun (2019), using a sample of 47 million HMDA originations linked to lender-loan-officer demographic data, demonstrated a race-match effect: Black applicants paired with Black loan officers had origination rates 17 percentage points higher than Black applicants paired with non-Black officers, controlling for the full vector of HMDA-observable applicant characteristics. Bayer, Ferreira, and Ross (2008), examining subprime mortgage origination, found that, controlling for credit scores, race remained predictive of subprime placement, suggesting that the discretionary channel operates not only in the binary denial decision but also in the conditional-on-approval mortgage-type decision.

More recently, the literature has begun to ask what interventions reduce the gap. Bhutta, Hizmo, and Ringo (2024), comparing algorithmic and human credit decisions on a panel of mortgage applications, find that algorithmic underwriting reduces — but does not eliminate — the conditional gap, with the residual gap reflecting differences in the credit scores that the algorithms take as inputs. Fuster, Goldsmith-Pinkham, Ramadorai, and Walther (2022) extend this analysis to consider the distributional effects of machine-learning credit-risk models, finding that the gap-reducing benefits of algorithmic underwriting are concentrated in particular subsets of the applicant pool. The closest precedent to the present paper is the CRA-driven intervention literature — Agarwal, Benmelech, Bergman, and Seru (2012) and Bhutta, Skiba, and Tobacman (2015) — which studies the disparate-impact consequences of a related, but structurally distinct, regulatory tool. CRA examinations are scheduled and broad; OCC intensified fair-lending examinations are targeted, finding-history-driven, and rolled out in a staggered fashion that permits a credibly identified causal estimate of the kind not previously available in the literature.

Read the three paragraphs. They form one continuous argument across three intellectual moves; they cite eleven distinct papers; each citation makes a substantive contribution to the argument; the last sentence sets up Maya’s contribution claim by naming the gap that the OCC examination program’s specific design fills. The reader, after Strand A’s two and a half pages, knows the substantive literature well enough to anticipate why an OCC examination paper should exist.

4. Building Strand B — the methodologically-adjacent literature

Strand B follows the same template — chronological-but-thematic, with intellectual moves — but engages the supervisory-effects literature rather than the disparities literature.

The moves for Strand B:

Move 1: documenting that supervision matters. Berger and Bouwman (2013) establish that supervisory ratings (CAMELS) predict bank risk-taking; Hirtle, Kovner, and Plosser (2020) use a quasi-experimental design to show that supervision causes changes in bank lending and risk-taking, not merely correlates with them. The move establishes that supervisory intensity is a real lever.

Move 2: characterizing supervisory effects. Agarwal, Lucca, Seru, and Trebbi (2014) document inconsistencies in how the same banks are supervised by different agencies; Granja and Leuz (2024) study supervisory attention as a scarce resource and show that allocative-attention shifts have measurable effects on covered banks’ behavior. The move characterizes how supervision operates — through scarce attention, through finding-history-driven targeting, through follow-up examination intensity.

Move 3: extending to non-traditional outcomes. Agarwal, Ben-David, and Yao (2017) extend the supervisory-effects framework to consumer-finance outcomes (mortgage securitization decisions); Bhutta, Hizmo, and Ringo (2024) — also a Strand A citation, used here for its Strand B role — connect supervisory regimes to mortgage outcomes. The move points to the still-open question of distributional effects: supervision has been shown to affect aggregate credit allocation and risk-taking; whether it affects the gap in outcomes across applicant groups is the question Strand A defined.

The Strand B sub-section is about two pages. It is shorter than Strand A because the supervisory-effects literature is less central to the paper’s substantive contribution and because the reader will need to spend less time understanding it to anticipate the gap. It is structured as one continuous argument across the three moves, with citations clustered at the move openings.

The synthesis sentence at the end of Strand B is the bridge to Strand A’s question: “The supervisory-effects literature has demonstrated that targeted supervisory intensity can change bank behavior in measurable ways, but has focused on credit-allocation and risk-taking outcomes. Whether the supervisory tools can also affect the distributional outcomes Strand A’s literature identifies — the conditional Black–White denial gap — is the question this paper answers.”

5. Building Strand C — the methodological literature

Strand C is the briefest, about a page and a half. It engages the methodological literature on staggered DiD, multiple-testing-disciplined heterogeneity, sensitivity analysis, and specification-curve robustness. The motivation for the strand is that the paper’s methodological choices need defense, and the place to defend them is here (not in Section 3 — Section 3 is the application; Section 2 is the justification).

The moves for Strand C:

Move 1: the staggered-DiD revolution. Bertrand, Duflo, and Mullainathan (2004) established that standard DiD inference understates uncertainty in the presence of serial correlation; the cluster-robust standard-error literature (Bertrand et al. 2004; Cameron and Miller, 2015) responded with practical guidance. More recently, Callaway and Sant’Anna (2021), Sun and Abraham (2021), and de Chaisemartin and D’Haultfœuille (2020) have shown that standard two-way fixed-effects DiD estimators are biased in the presence of heterogeneous treatment effects across cohorts and that heterogeneity-robust estimators are required.

Move 2: defending the parallel-trends assumption. Oster (2019) provides a formal framework for bounding coefficient stability against unobservable confounding; Roth, Sant’Anna, Bilinski, and Poe (2023) develop the practical tools for parallel-trends defense, including event-study leads testing and sensitivity analysis. The move establishes that modern DiD applications require defenses of the assumption, not just statements of it.

Move 3: disciplining heterogeneity and robustness. Benjamini and Hochberg (1995) established the false-discovery-rate framework; Romano and Wolf (2005) extended it to step-down procedures with bootstrap inference. Simonsohn, Simmons, and Nelson (2020) developed specification-curve analysis as a disclosure-of-forks tool. The move establishes that heterogeneity analysis and robustness exercises must be subject to multiple-testing discipline and specification disclosure.

The synthesis sentence at the end of Strand C is: “The methodological toolkit assembled here — staggered DiD with heterogeneity-robust estimation, parallel-trends defenses (event-study leads, Oster bounds), multiple-testing-disciplined heterogeneity, and specification-curve robustness — is the toolkit a credible application to the staggered fair-lending-examination rollout demands.”

6. The synthesis-and-gap closer

The fourth sub-section, Section 2.4 of Maya’s manuscript, is about half a page. It ties the three strands together and states the gap that the paper fills.

The synthesis works by summarizing the three strands in a single paragraph, with the convergence being explicit. Then a gap-and-contribution paragraph names what is missing across all three strands and what this paper provides.

Here is the closer (about 250 words):

The three strands of literature reviewed in this section have each made substantial progress on adjacent problems. The disparities literature has established the persistence of a conditional Black–White denial gap and has characterized its mechanisms — geographic sorting of high-cost lenders, within-lender discretion, race-match effects in the loan-officer–borrower dyad — without offering a credibly identified estimate of any policy intervention’s effect on the gap. The supervisory-effects literature has established that supervisory intensity can change bank behavior in measurable ways through scarce-attention and finding-history-driven targeting mechanisms, without considering whether the targeting can affect distributional rather than allocative outcomes. The methodological literature has developed the heterogeneity-robust staggered-DiD estimators, the parallel-trends defense tools, and the multiple-testing discipline that a credible application would require.

The paper presented here joins the three strands. We provide the first credibly identified estimate of the causal effect of a targeted regulatory examination program on the Black–White conditional denial gap, using the staggered rollout of the 2017 OCC intensified fair-lending examination program. Methodologically, we deploy the full toolkit Strand C demands. Substantively, we connect Strand B’s documentation of supervisory-intensity effects to Strand A’s distributional question. The convergence is what makes the paper’s contribution claim — three contributions, one to each strand — defensible.

The synthesis paragraph names the three strands by their function (disparities, supervisory-effects, methodological) and the gap each has left. The contribution paragraph names what the present paper does for each. The reader, after Section 2.4, can recite the contribution paragraph from the introduction without having read the introduction. That is when the literature review has earned the contribution sentence.

7. Common failure modes, named

We have the positive template. The negative template — the patterns of sick literature reviews — is the focus of this section.

7.1 The Annotated Bibliography

Pathology. The literature review is a list of papers, each with a one-sentence summary, organized by topic but with no argument connecting them. (“Smith (2018) studies X and finds Y. Jones (2019) studies a related question and finds Z. Brown (2020) extends Jones’s analysis to …”) The reader leaves the section knowing which papers exist but not knowing what the literature argues.

Diagnostic. Read the section aloud. Does each paragraph end with a transition that connects to the next paragraph’s argumentative move? If transitions are missing — if paragraphs are just “Smith (2018) studies …” followed by “Jones (2019) studies …” — the section is an annotated bibliography.

Fix. Group citations into intellectual moves (as in §3 above). Use full paragraphs that argue, with citations woven in. Eliminate sentence-per-paper summaries.

7.2 The Kitchen-Sink Review

Pathology. The literature review cites every paper the author has read on the topic, including marginal and tangentially related work, in a single section of fifteen pages. The argument is buried under volume.

Diagnostic. Count distinct citations in Section 2. If more than 60, the kitchen-sink pathology has probably happened. If more than 100, definitely.

Fix. Triage the citations as you triaged conference comments in Chapter 10.1. Each citation earns its place by contributing to one of the three strands’ intellectual moves. Citations that contribute only “this paper also touches on the topic” — but not to any move — go. The target for Maya’s nine-page Section 2 is 25–35 distinct citations; she trimmed from 43 to 28.

7.3 The Self-Citation Inflation

Pathology. The author cites their own prior work disproportionately — five citations of “Smith (2017), Smith (2019), Smith (2020a), Smith (2020b), Smith and Brown (2021)” — and characterizes each self-citation more flatteringly than the surrounding citations. The reader notices.

Diagnostic. Count the citations to the author’s own work as a fraction of total citations. If above 15%, the inflation is visible.

Fix. Self-citations are fine when relevant; they are necessary when the author has prior work that is genuinely close to the present paper. The fix is not to remove relevant self-citations but to keep their characterization at the same neutral standard as other citations — describe what the paper found, not what was impressive about the author’s contribution.

7.4 The Stale Literature Review

Pathology. The literature review’s most recent citation is from three or four years before the submission date. The reader, especially the referee, sees that the author has not engaged with the most recent work and infers either that the author is unaware of the current literature or that the paper was drafted long ago and never updated.

Diagnostic. Sort the citations by publication year. What is the most recent citation? If it is more than two years before the submission date, the literature review is stale.

Fix. Update before submission. Run a literature search in the six months before submission and add three to seven recent citations across the three strands. The recent citations should each contribute to an intellectual move; do not pad with citations that exist but do not contribute.

7.5 The Mischaracterization

Pathology. A paper is cited for a finding it does not make, or its finding is described in a way the original author would reject. The angry-referee scenario from §1.

Diagnostic. For every citation that supports a substantive claim, can you point to the specific table, sentence, or figure in the cited paper that justifies the characterization? If no — if any citation is supported only by your memory of the abstract — re-read the cited paper before submitting.

Fix. Re-read closely. If your characterization does not match what the paper says, either change your characterization or remove the citation. Never cite a paper for a claim it does not make.

7.6 The Disconnected Methodological Section

Pathology. Strand C (the methodological literature) is treated as a standalone defense of method, with no connection to Strands A and B’s substantive content. The reader sees three independent reviews — disparities, supervision, method — with no synthesis.

Diagnostic. Read the section’s transitions between sub-sections. Does the transition from Strand B to Strand C connect them argumentatively, or does it just say “Methodologically, our paper relates to …”? If the latter, the disconnection has happened.

Fix. Frame Strand C as the toolkit demanded by the substantive question Strands A and B raise. The synthesis sentence at the end of Strand C is the explicit connection.

8. The procedural mechanics: what to read, in what order

The literature review you can write depends on the literature you have read. Most students under-read for the literature review, then try to write the review around their inadequate reading, and produce a sick section. The procedural fix is to read deliberately, in stages, before writing.

Stage 1 — Foundational pass. For each strand, identify the two or three foundational papers. These are typically the highly cited papers that the strand keeps coming back to. For Strand A: Munnell et al. (1996), Bayer, Ferreira, and Ross (2018), Gao and Sun (2019). For Strand B: Berger and Bouwman (2013), Hirtle, Kovner, and Plosser (2020). For Strand C: Callaway and Sant’Anna (2021), Sun and Abraham (2021), Oster (2019). Read the abstract, introduction, and headline results of each. Take notes on what the paper finds and what move it makes in the literature.

Stage 2 — Recency pass. For each strand, search Google Scholar and SSRN for the most-cited recent work (last two to three years). Sort by citation count within recent papers. Read the abstracts of the top ten; read the introduction and tables of the top three. The recency pass catches the working papers and recent publications that the foundational pass misses.

Stage 3 — Citation-chasing pass. For each foundational paper, pull its reference list and identify any cited paper you have not yet read that the foundational paper relies on heavily. Read the abstract; decide whether to read further. The citation-chasing pass catches the older work that the foundational paper is in conversation with.

Stage 4 — Closest-neighbor pass. Identify the two or three papers closest to your own contribution. These are the papers that, if your paper is published, will appear in the citing literature next to yours. Read them deeply (full read-through). Maya’s closest neighbors are Bhutta, Hizmo, and Ringo (2024) and Agarwal, Benmelech, Bergman, and Seru (2012); she should be able to recite each one’s design, identifying assumption, and finding.

The total time for the four passes is, for a paper Maya is writing, about three to four weeks of background reading distributed across the four months before Section 2 is drafted. For a student writing their first paper, this is an enormous lift, and it is the lift; without the reading, the literature review cannot be written.

A practical aid: maintain a literature spreadsheet. Each row is a paper. Columns: citation; one-sentence “what they find”; strand (A/B/C); move (Move 1/2/3); whether you have read deeply, abstractly, or just skimmed. The spreadsheet becomes the working memory of the literature review. When you draft Section 2, you do not flip through PDFs; you scan the spreadsheet and pull the citation-and-finding pairs into the strand they belong to. Maya’s spreadsheet is included in the chapter’s notebook (nb11.5) as a CSV template you can adopt.

9. Connecting to the rest of the manuscript

The literature review does not stand alone. Three connection points matter.

Connection to the introduction’s contribution paragraph. The contribution paragraph names three literatures; the literature review’s three strands match those three literatures by name. If the introduction says “we contribute to the fair-lending-disparities literature, the bank-supervision literature, and the staggered-DiD methodology literature,” Section 2’s three sub-sections should bear those names exactly (or very close). The matching is what makes the contribution claim defensible.

Connection to the empirical strategy section. Section 2’s Strand C (methodological) introduces the methods; Section 3 (Empirical Strategy) applies them. The Section 3 application should cite back to Section 2 (“As discussed in Section 2.3, we estimate a staggered DiD with cohort-specific event-study leads and lags following Callaway and Sant’Anna (2021) …”). The cross-reference makes the methods choice feel deliberate.

Connection to the discussion section. When the paper’s discussion section interprets the findings, it should refer back to the literature review’s gap statements (“These findings address the question raised in Section 2.4 …”). The cross-reference closes the rhetorical loop: the literature review identified a gap, the paper filled it, the discussion announces the fill.

These three cross-references are simple to add at the polishing stage but transform the manuscript’s rhetorical coherence. A reader following the references through the paper sees a tightly engineered argument structure where, without the references, she would have seen three loosely related sections. The discipline is in the cross-referencing.

draft_structure = (

11. The final checklist

Before you commit the literature review to the manuscript, run the following six-item checklist.

  1. Three strands, named. Are the three strands explicitly named in sub-section titles, and do they match the contribution paragraph’s three contributions? If no, restructure.
  2. Each strand has three intellectual moves. Can you identify, in each sub-section, what move is being made by each paragraph? If no, the section is a list.
  3. Each citation contributes. Is each citation in the section the source of a substantive claim used in the argument? If you have citations that exist but do not contribute, cut them.
  4. Synthesis closer. Does Section 2.4 (or the section’s final paragraph) name the three strands’ gaps and claim the contribution? If no, add.
  5. Cross-references in place. Does Section 3 cite back to Section 2? Does Section 6 (Discussion) cite back to Section 2’s gap statements? If no, add.
  6. No mischaracterization. For each citation, can you point to the specific source in the cited paper for the characterization you give? If no, re-read or remove.

The notebook nb11.5 runs the first three checks programmatically (it counts strand-headers, identifies move-transition language, counts substantive vs. ornamental citations). The remaining checks are human.

# Ch 11.5 — The Expanded Literature Review: from a Bullet List to a Three-Strand Argument that Earns Your Contribution Sentence

## 1. The reveal: what a literature review is *for*

The textbook definition of a literature review — "a survey of prior work on the topic" — is functionally wrong. It produces the bulleted enumeration that students draft on their first pass and that referees mark for revision. The correct functional definition has three parts: a literature review **establishes that the question has not been answered**, **establishes that the question is worth asking**, and **establishes that the design we propose is appropriate to the question**. Each of these is a *rhetorical* job, not a *summary* job, and rhetorical jobs require argument structure.

The literature review has three readers, and like the abstract's three readers (Chapter 11.1), the structure must satisfy all three.

The **first reader** is the same referee who read the introduction. She is checking whether the contribution claim is defensible. If your paragraph 5 in the introduction says "the closest precedent is Bhutta, Hizmo, and Ringo (2024) but our setting differs in dimensions X, Y, Z," she will read Section 2 looking for the substantive engagement with Bhutta et al. that makes the X-Y-Z differentiation credible. If she does not find it — if Bhutta et al. is cited once and not discussed — she marks the contribution claim as overstated and the paper as needing major revision.

The **second reader** is the scholar working on the closest neighboring problem. She is checking whether you have read *her* work and represented it fairly. If your paper purports to be the first credibly identified estimate of fair-lending-examination effects, and she has a 2023 working paper on a closely related question with a different design, she will be looking for it in your citations. If it is missing, she becomes the referee who writes "the author appears unaware of …" — a near-fatal review. If it is cited but characterized inaccurately ("Smith (2023) studies a similar question and finds null effects, but uses a less credible design") she becomes the angrier referee who writes "the author has mischaracterized Smith (2023), which actually does the following …"

The **third reader** is the graduate student or junior scholar who finds your paper three years from now and uses your literature review as a *map* of the field. If your literature review is well-organized and well-cited, she uses it to find her own way into the literature and *cites your paper* in passing as the canonical entry-point synthesis. If it is poorly-organized or under-cited, she does not.

The three readers impose three discipline constraints. The literature review must **(a) defend the contribution claim in detail**, **(b) cite and characterize accurately every paper a knowledgeable referee would expect to see**, and **(c) organize the field so a future reader can use the review as a map**. The three-strand braid is the structure that satisfies all three.

---

## 2. The three-strand braid

The braid is the load-bearing structure. Each strand is one literature your paper engages; each strand has a beginning (the foundational work), a middle (the recent extensions), and an end (the gap your paper fills). The three strands are interwoven across the section so the reader sees the convergence of all three on your question; the strands are not just three separate mini-reviews followed by a "and we contribute to all three" sentence.

For Maya's paper, the three strands are:

**Strand A — the fair-lending disparities literature.** Foundational: Munnell, Tootell, Browne, and McEneaney (1996) (the Boston Fed study). Recent extensions: Bayer, Ferreira, and Ross (2018) on high-cost lender concentration; Gao and Sun (2019) on race-match effects in the underwriter–borrower dyad; Bhutta, Hizmo, and Ringo (2024) on algorithmic vs. human credit decisions. Gap: no credibly identified estimate of the effect of *regulatory intervention* on the gap.

**Strand B — the real effects of bank supervision literature.** Foundational: Berger and Bouwman (2013) on supervisory ratings and risk-taking; Hirtle, Kovner, and Plosser (2020) on the impact of supervision on bank outcomes. Recent extensions: Agarwal, Ben-David, and Yao (2017) on consumer-finance supervisory effects; Granja and Leuz (2024) on supervisory attention's allocative effects. Gap: the literature has focused on safety-and-soundness outcomes (credit allocation, risk-taking) rather than on *distributional* outcomes (the cross-group denial gap).

**Strand C — the methodological literature on staggered DiD and heterogeneity-disciplined inference.** Foundational: Bertrand, Duflo, and Mullainathan (2004) on serial correlation in DiD; Callaway and Sant'Anna (2021) and Sun and Abraham (2021) on heterogeneous-treatment-effect DiD. Recent extensions: Roth, Sant'Anna, Bilinski, and Poe (2023) on the assumptions defense; Simonsohn, Simmons, and Nelson (2020) on specification curve analysis. Gap: the application of the full methodological battery (staggered DiD + multiple-testing-disciplined heterogeneity + Oster sensitivity + spec curve) to a fair-lending regulatory intervention has not been done.

Three strands, three gaps, all converging on Maya's question. The literature review's job is to make the convergence inevitable.

**The strands are unequal in length.** Strand A (the substantive literature on disparities) is the deepest engagement and gets the most space — perhaps two to three pages of the nine-page Section 2. Strand B (the supervisory-effects literature) is the second deepest — perhaps two pages. Strand C (the methodological literature) is the briefest — perhaps a page and a half — because the methodological contribution is auxiliary to the substantive one, and the deep methodological discussion belongs in Section 3 (Empirical Strategy). The proportions track the proportion of the contribution paragraph each strand earns.

**The strands are interwoven, not stacked.** A naive structure has Sub-section 2.1 = Strand A, Sub-section 2.2 = Strand B, Sub-section 2.3 = Strand C, with no cross-references. This is the bulleted-list pathology dressed in sub-section headers. The better structure interleaves: Sub-section 2.1 introduces the disparities literature, ending with the unresolved question of regulatory intervention; Sub-section 2.2 introduces the supervisory-intensity literature, returning to the question of whether it has been applied to distributional outcomes; Sub-section 2.3 introduces the methodological literature, framed as the toolkit that the substantive question demands; Sub-section 2.4 (the synthesis-and-gap closer) ties the three together. The reader sees the three strands converge; she does not see three separate sub-reviews.

## 3. Building Strand A — the substantive disparities literature

We work through Strand A in detail; Strands B and C follow the same template more briefly.

The strand has a **chronological-but-thematic** structure. Chronological because the literature develops over time and the reader benefits from seeing the development; thematic because we group the citations into the *intellectual moves* the literature has made, not into a flat year-by-year list.

For Maya's Strand A, three intellectual moves:

**Move 1: documenting the gap.** The Munnell, Tootell, Browne, and McEneaney (1996) Boston Fed study established that, in 1990 Boston-area mortgage applications, Black applicants were 2.4 times as likely to be denied as White applicants with similar applicant characteristics. The paper drew controversy at the time for methodological choices in handling censored applications, and the subsequent literature spent a decade arguing about whether the gap was real or an artifact of unobservables. The Munnell et al. controversy resolved into broad agreement — by the late 1990s and the 2000s — that the gap was real and persistent, though the magnitude varied across datasets and specifications. Move 1 ends with the acknowledgment that the gap is established.

**Move 2: characterizing the gap's mechanisms.** Once the gap was established, the literature turned to characterizing how it arose. Bayer, Ferreira, and Ross (2008, 2018) showed that a substantial portion of the gap is explained by the concentration of high-cost, high-denial lenders in minority neighborhoods — a sorting story, not a same-lender-discrimination story. Hanson, Hawley, Martin, and Liu (2016) found evidence of differential treatment in lender response to similar applicant signals. Gao and Sun (2019) demonstrated the race-match effect — that Black applicants paired with Black loan officers had higher approval rates than Black applicants paired with non-Black officers, by 17 percentage points. The mechanism literature converged on the view that the gap arises from a combination of structural sorting and within-lender discretionary behavior, with both channels active.

**Move 3: probing interventions.** The most recent strand of the literature, primarily 2020 onward, has begun to ask what interventions reduce the gap. Bhutta, Hizmo, and Ringo (2024) compare algorithmic and human credit decisions and find that algorithms reduce but do not eliminate the gap; Fuster, Goldsmith-Pinkham, Ramadorai, and Walther (2022) study the distributional effects of algorithmic underwriting. The CRA-driven literature (Agarwal, Benmelech, Bergman, and Seru, 2012) studies a different regulatory intervention with mixed evidence on disparate-impact outcomes. The intervention literature is the closest to Maya's question — but it has not directly studied the OCC's targeted fair-lending examination program, and Move 3 ends with this gap.

The Strand A sub-section, roughly two and a half pages, reads like one continuous argument across these three moves. The transitions are explicit ("Once the gap was firmly established, the literature turned to …"; "More recently, attention has shifted to …"). Citations are clustered at the points where each move begins and where the move's central claim is reinforced. Bullet lists do not appear; full paragraphs do.

Two principles govern how each citation is treated.

**Cite what the paper *finds*, not just *that* it exists.** The bad version: "Bayer, Ferreira, and Ross (2018) study mortgage disparities." The good version: "Bayer, Ferreira, and Ross (2018) show that 30–40% of the Black–White origination gap is driven by the geographic concentration of high-cost lenders in minority neighborhoods, rather than by within-lender discrimination." The good version makes the citation *contribute to the argument*: the reader learns something useful even if she does not click through to read Bayer et al. for herself. The bad version assigns the citation a role of merely existing, which wastes the citation.

**Characterize accurately enough that the cited author would recognize the characterization.** This is the second-reader (neighboring-scholar) constraint. If you cite a paper for a claim that the paper does not make, or if you cite it for a finding that is actually qualified in the paper, the cited author becomes the angry referee. The discipline: when you cite a finding, you should be able to point to the table, sentence, or finding in the cited paper from which the characterization comes. If you cannot, rewrite the citation more cautiously or pull a different one.

Here is the opening of Strand A as it appears in Maya's revised Section 2 (about 420 words):

> *The persistence of a Black–White mortgage-denial gap, conditional on standard underwriting characteristics, has been documented across decades and datasets. The foundational empirical work, Munnell, Tootell, Browne, and McEneaney (1996), used 1990 Boston-area mortgage applications and found that Black applicants were 2.4 times as likely as observably similar White applicants to be denied conventional mortgages. The Munnell et al. result generated a decade of methodological debate concerning the treatment of censored applications and the handling of missing variables in the lender's information set; that debate resolved into broad agreement, by the early 2000s, that the gap was real and substantial, though the precise magnitude varied with sample construction and outcome definition (Ross and Yinger, 2002).*
>
> *Once the gap was firmly established, the literature turned to characterizing its mechanisms. Bayer, Ferreira, and Ross (2018), using a multi-decade panel of mortgage applications, showed that 30–40% of the Black–White origination gap is driven by the geographic concentration of high-cost lenders in minority neighborhoods rather than by within-lender disparate treatment — a sorting mechanism that the legal apparatus of fair-lending compliance does not directly address. Within-lender behaviors have nevertheless been shown to contribute. Gao and Sun (2019), using a sample of 47 million HMDA originations linked to lender-loan-officer demographic data, demonstrated a race-match effect: Black applicants paired with Black loan officers had origination rates 17 percentage points higher than Black applicants paired with non-Black officers, controlling for the full vector of HMDA-observable applicant characteristics. Bayer, Ferreira, and Ross (2008), examining subprime mortgage origination, found that, controlling for credit scores, race remained predictive of subprime placement, suggesting that the discretionary channel operates not only in the binary denial decision but also in the conditional-on-approval mortgage-type decision.*
>
> *More recently, the literature has begun to ask what interventions reduce the gap. Bhutta, Hizmo, and Ringo (2024), comparing algorithmic and human credit decisions on a panel of mortgage applications, find that algorithmic underwriting reduces — but does not eliminate — the conditional gap, with the residual gap reflecting differences in the credit scores that the algorithms take as inputs. Fuster, Goldsmith-Pinkham, Ramadorai, and Walther (2022) extend this analysis to consider the distributional effects of machine-learning credit-risk models, finding that the gap-reducing benefits of algorithmic underwriting are concentrated in particular subsets of the applicant pool. The closest precedent to the present paper is the CRA-driven intervention literature — Agarwal, Benmelech, Bergman, and Seru (2012) and Bhutta, Skiba, and Tobacman (2015) — which studies the disparate-impact consequences of a related, but structurally distinct, regulatory tool. CRA examinations are scheduled and broad; OCC intensified fair-lending examinations are targeted, finding-history-driven, and rolled out in a staggered fashion that permits a credibly identified causal estimate of the kind not previously available in the literature.*

Read the three paragraphs. They form one continuous argument across three intellectual moves; they cite eleven distinct papers; each citation makes a substantive contribution to the argument; the last sentence sets up Maya's contribution claim by naming the *gap* that the OCC examination program's specific design fills. **The reader, after Strand A's two and a half pages, knows the substantive literature well enough to anticipate why an OCC examination paper should exist.**

## 4. Building Strand B — the methodologically-adjacent literature

Strand B follows the same template — chronological-but-thematic, with intellectual moves — but engages the supervisory-effects literature rather than the disparities literature.

The moves for Strand B:

**Move 1: documenting that supervision matters.** Berger and Bouwman (2013) establish that supervisory ratings (CAMELS) predict bank risk-taking; Hirtle, Kovner, and Plosser (2020) use a quasi-experimental design to show that supervision *causes* changes in bank lending and risk-taking, not merely correlates with them. The move establishes that supervisory intensity is a real lever.

**Move 2: characterizing supervisory effects.** Agarwal, Lucca, Seru, and Trebbi (2014) document inconsistencies in how the same banks are supervised by different agencies; Granja and Leuz (2024) study supervisory attention as a scarce resource and show that allocative-attention shifts have measurable effects on covered banks' behavior. The move characterizes *how* supervision operates — through scarce attention, through finding-history-driven targeting, through follow-up examination intensity.

**Move 3: extending to non-traditional outcomes.** Agarwal, Ben-David, and Yao (2017) extend the supervisory-effects framework to consumer-finance outcomes (mortgage securitization decisions); Bhutta, Hizmo, and Ringo (2024) — also a Strand A citation, used here for its Strand B role — connect supervisory regimes to mortgage outcomes. The move points to the still-open question of *distributional* effects: supervision has been shown to affect aggregate credit allocation and risk-taking; whether it affects the *gap* in outcomes across applicant groups is the question Strand A defined.

The Strand B sub-section is about two pages. It is shorter than Strand A because the supervisory-effects literature is less central to the paper's substantive contribution and because the reader will need to spend less time understanding it to anticipate the gap. It is structured as one continuous argument across the three moves, with citations clustered at the move openings.

The synthesis sentence at the end of Strand B is the bridge to Strand A's question: "The supervisory-effects literature has demonstrated that targeted supervisory intensity can change bank behavior in measurable ways, but has focused on credit-allocation and risk-taking outcomes. Whether the supervisory tools can also affect the distributional outcomes Strand A's literature identifies — the conditional Black–White denial gap — is the question this paper answers."

## 5. Building Strand C — the methodological literature

Strand C is the briefest, about a page and a half. It engages the methodological literature on staggered DiD, multiple-testing-disciplined heterogeneity, sensitivity analysis, and specification-curve robustness. The motivation for the strand is that the paper's methodological choices need defense, and the place to defend them is here (not in Section 3 — Section 3 is the application; Section 2 is the justification).

The moves for Strand C:

**Move 1: the staggered-DiD revolution.** Bertrand, Duflo, and Mullainathan (2004) established that standard DiD inference understates uncertainty in the presence of serial correlation; the cluster-robust standard-error literature (Bertrand et al. 2004; Cameron and Miller, 2015) responded with practical guidance. More recently, Callaway and Sant'Anna (2021), Sun and Abraham (2021), and de Chaisemartin and D'Haultfœuille (2020) have shown that standard two-way fixed-effects DiD estimators are biased in the presence of heterogeneous treatment effects across cohorts and that heterogeneity-robust estimators are required.

**Move 2: defending the parallel-trends assumption.** Oster (2019) provides a formal framework for bounding coefficient stability against unobservable confounding; Roth, Sant'Anna, Bilinski, and Poe (2023) develop the practical tools for parallel-trends defense, including event-study leads testing and sensitivity analysis. The move establishes that modern DiD applications require *defenses* of the assumption, not just statements of it.

**Move 3: disciplining heterogeneity and robustness.** Benjamini and Hochberg (1995) established the false-discovery-rate framework; Romano and Wolf (2005) extended it to step-down procedures with bootstrap inference. Simonsohn, Simmons, and Nelson (2020) developed specification-curve analysis as a disclosure-of-forks tool. The move establishes that heterogeneity analysis and robustness exercises must be subject to multiple-testing discipline and specification disclosure.

The synthesis sentence at the end of Strand C is: "The methodological toolkit assembled here — staggered DiD with heterogeneity-robust estimation, parallel-trends defenses (event-study leads, Oster bounds), multiple-testing-disciplined heterogeneity, and specification-curve robustness — is the toolkit a credible application to the staggered fair-lending-examination rollout demands."

## 6. The synthesis-and-gap closer

The fourth sub-section, Section 2.4 of Maya's manuscript, is about half a page. It ties the three strands together and states the gap that the paper fills.

The synthesis works by *summarizing the three strands in a single paragraph*, with the convergence being explicit. Then a *gap-and-contribution paragraph* names what is missing across all three strands and what this paper provides.

Here is the closer (about 250 words):

> *The three strands of literature reviewed in this section have each made substantial progress on adjacent problems. The disparities literature has established the persistence of a conditional Black–White denial gap and has characterized its mechanisms — geographic sorting of high-cost lenders, within-lender discretion, race-match effects in the loan-officer–borrower dyad — without offering a credibly identified estimate of any policy intervention's effect on the gap. The supervisory-effects literature has established that supervisory intensity can change bank behavior in measurable ways through scarce-attention and finding-history-driven targeting mechanisms, without considering whether the targeting can affect distributional rather than allocative outcomes. The methodological literature has developed the heterogeneity-robust staggered-DiD estimators, the parallel-trends defense tools, and the multiple-testing discipline that a credible application would require.*
>
> *The paper presented here joins the three strands. We provide the first credibly identified estimate of the causal effect of a targeted regulatory examination program on the Black–White conditional denial gap, using the staggered rollout of the 2017 OCC intensified fair-lending examination program. Methodologically, we deploy the full toolkit Strand C demands. Substantively, we connect Strand B's documentation of supervisory-intensity effects to Strand A's distributional question. The convergence is what makes the paper's contribution claim — three contributions, one to each strand — defensible.*

The synthesis paragraph names the three strands by their function (disparities, supervisory-effects, methodological) and the gap each has left. The contribution paragraph names what the present paper does for each. **The reader, after Section 2.4, can recite the contribution paragraph from the introduction without having read the introduction.** That is when the literature review has earned the contribution sentence.

## 7. Common failure modes, named

We have the positive template. The negative template — the patterns of sick literature reviews — is the focus of this section.

### 7.1 The Annotated Bibliography

**Pathology.** The literature review is a list of papers, each with a one-sentence summary, organized by topic but with no argument connecting them. ("Smith (2018) studies X and finds Y. Jones (2019) studies a related question and finds Z. Brown (2020) extends Jones's analysis to ...") The reader leaves the section knowing which papers exist but not knowing what the literature *argues*.

**Diagnostic.** Read the section aloud. Does each paragraph end with a transition that connects to the next paragraph's argumentative move? If transitions are missing — if paragraphs are just "Smith (2018) studies …" followed by "Jones (2019) studies …" — the section is an annotated bibliography.

**Fix.** Group citations into intellectual moves (as in §3 above). Use full paragraphs that argue, with citations woven in. Eliminate sentence-per-paper summaries.

### 7.2 The Kitchen-Sink Review

**Pathology.** The literature review cites every paper the author has read on the topic, including marginal and tangentially related work, in a single section of fifteen pages. The argument is buried under volume.

**Diagnostic.** Count distinct citations in Section 2. If more than 60, the kitchen-sink pathology has probably happened. If more than 100, definitely.

**Fix.** Triage the citations as you triaged conference comments in Chapter 10.1. Each citation earns its place by contributing to one of the three strands' intellectual moves. Citations that contribute only "this paper also touches on the topic" — but not to any move — go. The target for Maya's nine-page Section 2 is 25–35 distinct citations; she trimmed from 43 to 28.

### 7.3 The Self-Citation Inflation

**Pathology.** The author cites their own prior work disproportionately — five citations of "Smith (2017), Smith (2019), Smith (2020a), Smith (2020b), Smith and Brown (2021)" — and characterizes each self-citation more flatteringly than the surrounding citations. The reader notices.

**Diagnostic.** Count the citations to the author's own work as a fraction of total citations. If above 15%, the inflation is visible.

**Fix.** Self-citations are fine when relevant; they are *necessary* when the author has prior work that is genuinely close to the present paper. The fix is not to remove relevant self-citations but to keep their characterization at the same neutral standard as other citations — describe what the paper found, not what was impressive about the author's contribution.

### 7.4 The Stale Literature Review

**Pathology.** The literature review's most recent citation is from three or four years before the submission date. The reader, especially the referee, sees that the author has not engaged with the most recent work and infers either that the author is unaware of the current literature or that the paper was drafted long ago and never updated.

**Diagnostic.** Sort the citations by publication year. What is the *most recent* citation? If it is more than two years before the submission date, the literature review is stale.

**Fix.** Update before submission. Run a literature search in the six months before submission and add three to seven recent citations across the three strands. The recent citations should each contribute to an intellectual move; do not pad with citations that exist but do not contribute.

### 7.5 The Mischaracterization

**Pathology.** A paper is cited for a finding it does not make, or its finding is described in a way the original author would reject. The angry-referee scenario from §1.

**Diagnostic.** For every citation that supports a substantive claim, can you point to the specific table, sentence, or figure in the cited paper that justifies the characterization? If no — if any citation is supported only by your memory of the abstract — re-read the cited paper before submitting.

**Fix.** Re-read closely. If your characterization does not match what the paper says, either change your characterization or remove the citation. *Never* cite a paper for a claim it does not make.

### 7.6 The Disconnected Methodological Section

**Pathology.** Strand C (the methodological literature) is treated as a standalone defense of method, with no connection to Strands A and B's substantive content. The reader sees three independent reviews — disparities, supervision, method — with no synthesis.

**Diagnostic.** Read the section's transitions between sub-sections. Does the transition from Strand B to Strand C connect them argumentatively, or does it just say "Methodologically, our paper relates to …"? If the latter, the disconnection has happened.

**Fix.** Frame Strand C as the toolkit demanded by the substantive question Strands A and B raise. The synthesis sentence at the end of Strand C is the explicit connection.

## 8. The procedural mechanics: what to read, in what order

The literature review you can write depends on the literature you have read. Most students under-read for the literature review, then try to write the review around their inadequate reading, and produce a sick section. The procedural fix is to read deliberately, in stages, before writing.

**Stage 1 — Foundational pass.** For each strand, identify the two or three foundational papers. These are typically the highly cited papers that the strand keeps coming back to. For Strand A: Munnell et al. (1996), Bayer, Ferreira, and Ross (2018), Gao and Sun (2019). For Strand B: Berger and Bouwman (2013), Hirtle, Kovner, and Plosser (2020). For Strand C: Callaway and Sant'Anna (2021), Sun and Abraham (2021), Oster (2019). Read the abstract, introduction, and headline results of each. Take notes on what the paper finds and what move it makes in the literature.

**Stage 2 — Recency pass.** For each strand, search Google Scholar and SSRN for the most-cited recent work (last two to three years). Sort by citation count within recent papers. Read the abstracts of the top ten; read the introduction and tables of the top three. The recency pass catches the working papers and recent publications that the foundational pass misses.

**Stage 3 — Citation-chasing pass.** For each foundational paper, pull its reference list and identify any cited paper you have not yet read that the foundational paper relies on heavily. Read the abstract; decide whether to read further. The citation-chasing pass catches the older work that the foundational paper is in conversation with.

**Stage 4 — Closest-neighbor pass.** Identify the two or three papers *closest* to your own contribution. These are the papers that, if your paper is published, will appear in the citing literature next to yours. Read them deeply (full read-through). Maya's closest neighbors are Bhutta, Hizmo, and Ringo (2024) and Agarwal, Benmelech, Bergman, and Seru (2012); she should be able to recite each one's design, identifying assumption, and finding.

The total time for the four passes is, for a paper Maya is writing, about three to four weeks of background reading distributed across the four months before Section 2 is drafted. For a student writing their first paper, this is an enormous lift, and it is *the* lift; without the reading, the literature review cannot be written.

**A practical aid: maintain a literature spreadsheet.** Each row is a paper. Columns: citation; one-sentence "what they find"; strand (A/B/C); move (Move 1/2/3); whether you have read deeply, abstractly, or just skimmed. The spreadsheet becomes the working memory of the literature review. When you draft Section 2, you do not flip through PDFs; you scan the spreadsheet and pull the citation-and-finding pairs into the strand they belong to. Maya's spreadsheet is included in the chapter's notebook (`nb11.5`) as a CSV template you can adopt.

## 9. Connecting to the rest of the manuscript

The literature review does not stand alone. Three connection points matter.

**Connection to the introduction's contribution paragraph.** The contribution paragraph names three literatures; the literature review's three strands match those three literatures by name. If the introduction says "we contribute to the fair-lending-disparities literature, the bank-supervision literature, and the staggered-DiD methodology literature," Section 2's three sub-sections should bear those names exactly (or very close). The matching is what makes the contribution claim defensible.

**Connection to the empirical strategy section.** Section 2's Strand C (methodological) introduces the methods; Section 3 (Empirical Strategy) applies them. The Section 3 application should cite back to Section 2 ("As discussed in Section 2.3, we estimate a staggered DiD with cohort-specific event-study leads and lags following Callaway and Sant'Anna (2021) …"). The cross-reference makes the methods choice feel deliberate.

**Connection to the discussion section.** When the paper's discussion section interprets the findings, it should refer back to the literature review's gap statements ("These findings address the question raised in Section 2.4 …"). The cross-reference closes the rhetorical loop: the literature review identified a gap, the paper filled it, the discussion announces the fill.

These three cross-references are simple to add at the polishing stage but transform the manuscript's rhetorical coherence. A reader following the references through the paper sees a tightly engineered argument structure where, without the references, she would have seen three loosely related sections. The discipline is in the cross-referencing.

## 10. A worked example of the spreadsheet-to-section conversion

We close with a procedural walkthrough: how Maya converted her literature spreadsheet into the Section 2 draft.

```python
# nb11.5 cell 1 — literature-spreadsheet-to-section template
import pandas as pd

lit_df = pd.read_csv("literature_spreadsheet.csv")

# Columns: citation, finding, strand (A/B/C), move (1/2/3), depth (deep/abstract/skim).
# Each row is one paper; the spreadsheet has roughly 50 rows after Stages 1-4.

# Group by strand and move; the resulting structure is the draft outline.
draft_structure = (
    lit_df.sort_values(["strand", "move"])
          .groupby(["strand", "move"])
          .apply(lambda g: g[["citation", "finding", "depth"]].to_dict("records"))
)

for (strand, move), papers in draft_structure.items():
    print(f"\n=== Strand {strand}, Move {move} ===")
    for p in papers:
        print(f"  - {p['citation']}: {p['finding']} (depth={p['depth']})")
```

Running this on Maya's spreadsheet produces the outline for Section 2. Each strand-move cell becomes a paragraph; each paper in the cell becomes a citation-and-finding within the paragraph. The conversion from outline to prose is the writing step — turning bullet items into full sentences that argue rather than list — and is the place where the discipline of §§3–5 applies.

**A common pitfall here.** The spreadsheet-to-outline mechanics are easy; the prose-writing is hard. Students often produce drafts that look like the spreadsheet output formatted with periods at the end of each row. That is the annotated-bibliography pathology of §7.1. The fix is to write each paragraph in *argumentative* prose — the citations are *evidence* for argument moves, not items in a catalog.

## 11. The final checklist

Before you commit the literature review to the manuscript, run the following six-item checklist.

1. **Three strands, named.** Are the three strands explicitly named in sub-section titles, and do they match the contribution paragraph's three contributions? If no, restructure.
2. **Each strand has three intellectual moves.** Can you identify, in each sub-section, what move is being made by each paragraph? If no, the section is a list.
3. **Each citation contributes.** Is each citation in the section the source of a substantive claim used in the argument? If you have citations that exist but do not contribute, cut them.
4. **Synthesis closer.** Does Section 2.4 (or the section's final paragraph) name the three strands' gaps and claim the contribution? If no, add.
5. **Cross-references in place.** Does Section 3 cite back to Section 2? Does Section 6 (Discussion) cite back to Section 2's gap statements? If no, add.
6. **No mischaracterization.** For each citation, can you point to the specific source in the cited paper for the characterization you give? If no, re-read or remove.

The notebook `nb11.5` runs the first three checks programmatically (it counts strand-headers, identifies move-transition language, counts substantive vs. ornamental citations). The remaining checks are human.

A reader who reads through the literature review knows why the contract is credible. ## 13. Recap

The reveal: **a publication-grade literature review is not a list; it is an argument that earns the contribution sentence in the introduction.** The argument has the structure of a three-strand braid, with each strand corresponding to one literature, each strand making three intellectual moves, and the three strands converging on the gap the paper fills.

The six pathologies — annotated bibliography, kitchen-sink review, self-citation inflation, stale literature review, mischaracterization, disconnected methodological section — each have a diagnostic and a fix. The procedural mechanics — four reading stages, literature spreadsheet, six-item commit checklist — give a reproducible workflow.

The week's larger argument: every artifact (abstract, table, figure, introduction, literature review) is engineered to credibilize the same contribution at a different level of detail.

### Glossary

- **Three-strand braid**: the structure of a publication-grade literature review, with three sub-sections for three literatures and a synthesis closer.
- **Intellectual move**: a unit within a strand corresponding to one development in the literature (documenting the gap, characterizing the mechanism, probing interventions, etc.).
- **Synthesis-and-gap closer**: the final sub-section that names the three strands' gaps and claims the paper's contribution to each.
- **Annotated bibliography pathology**: the failure mode in which the literature review lists papers with one-sentence summaries instead of arguing.
- **Mischaracterization**: the failure mode in which a paper is cited for a finding it does not make or a claim it does not support.
