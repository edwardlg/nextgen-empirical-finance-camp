# PS 11.5 — Expanded Literature Review (~2500 Words) Organized as Three Strands Plus the Contribution-Sentence Reveal

**Course:** 8-Week Empirical Finance Camp · Week 11 · Problem Set 11.5
**Covers:** the literature-review section of the paper, the artifact that Chapter 11.4's introduction paragraph 5 references but does not contain. Builds on Week-2's annotated bibliography, Week-3's prior-evidence audit, PS 7.5's threats-to-identification table (which determines the third strand: identification-method literature), PS 10.5's external-validity memo (which constrains which papers a strand can credibly cite as comparable), and PS 11.4's introduction-paragraph-5 contribution claim (which the literature review's three strands must structurally support).
**Type:** Pure writing, with bibliographic verification. ~80% drafting the three strand-paragraphs, ~20% verifying every cited paper exists, is correctly cited, and is correctly described. The deliverable is a ~2500-word literature review with three strands, ending in a contribution-sentence reveal, plus a verified bibliography file with full citations.

**Total: 100 points.** Point values stated per part. A model deliverable — Maya's three-strand literature review (gap-documentation, supervisory-intensity, policy-evaluation), each strand running to ~750 words with the contribution-sentence reveal at the end, plus the verified bibliography with 24 entries — is in Appendix E (`E-w11-ps11.5-solutions.md`).

A boundary worth holding before you start. PS 11.5 is **not** "summarize every paper you read in Week 2." The Week-2 annotated bibliography is the *input*; the literature review is the *output* that selects, organizes, and discriminates among those papers. Most Week-2 bibliographies have 40–80 entries; most published literature reviews cite 20–35. Cutting is the discipline — choose the papers that bear most directly on your three strands, and cite each one *for the specific reason it bears on the strand*, not as a vague nod to "the literature."

A second boundary. **The "three strands" structure is a default, not a mandate.** Some papers have two strands; a few have four. Three is modal in empirical finance because three corresponds to (i) the descriptive literature that documents the phenomenon your paper studies, (ii) the methodological literature that develops the design you use, and (iii) the policy-or-application literature your paper's contribution speaks to. If your paper genuinely has a different number, write accordingly — but defend the choice in the opening paragraph of the literature review section.

A third boundary. **The contribution-sentence reveal at the end of each strand is the hardest move.** Most students write strand paragraphs that end on a generic "we contribute to this literature by…" sentence. The discipline of PS 11.5 is to write a contribution sentence that *names the specific paper or papers* your contribution most differs from, *names the specific dimension* (sample, design, mechanism, magnitude, sign) along which it differs, and *names the addition* in a way that a reader could verify against your Table 2. Writing twelve contribution-sentence candidates and choosing the strongest one is the apprenticeship work.

## What you submit

A single folder, `litreview/`, committed to your capstone repository, containing:

1. `litreview/strand-1.md` — the first strand-paragraph, ~750 words, ending in a contribution sentence.
2. `litreview/strand-2.md` — the second strand-paragraph, ~750 words, ending in a contribution sentence.
3. `litreview/strand-3.md` — the third strand-paragraph, ~750 words, ending in a contribution sentence.
4. `litreview/contribution-reveal.md` — the final three sentences (one per strand) collected into a single mini-paragraph, ~250 words, that closes the literature-review section and reveals the paper's overall contribution.
5. `litreview/refs.bib` — the BibTeX file with verified entries for every cited paper.
6. `litreview/verification-log.md` — the log of each citation's verification: title, year, journal, DOI (or `[CHECK]` if unverified).

## Part 1 — Choose and name your three strands (15 points)

The single sin this part prevents: **strands that overlap.** If your three strands are "racial gaps in mortgage approval," "discrimination in financial markets," and "fair-lending policy outcomes," you have one strand written three times. The three strands must be *distinct intellectual conversations*, each of which your paper enters from a different angle.

**(a) Name the three strands in one sentence each (9 pts, 3 each).** Write the opening paragraph of `strand-1.md`, `strand-2.md`, and `strand-3.md` to start with: *"The first/second/third strand of work this paper engages is the literature on [topic], which [describes / measures / debates / develops] [phenomenon or method]."*

For Maya:
- *Strand 1:* "the literature on racial gaps in mortgage approval, which documents persistent disparities in denial rates that are not fully explained by observable creditworthiness."
- *Strand 2:* "the literature on regulatory supervisory intensity, which examines how examination effort, examination scope, and examination consequences affect supervised institutions' behavior."
- *Strand 3:* "the literature on the causal evaluation of anti-discrimination interventions, which estimates the effect of laws, programs, and tools on disparate-impact outcomes."

For Devon:
- *Strand 1:* "the literature on cryptocurrency price formation and contagion, which documents how shocks propagate across linked assets."
- *Strand 2:* "the literature on event-study methodology in financial markets, which develops the abnormal-return estimation and inference tools."
- *Strand 3:* "the literature on stablecoin design and regulation, which examines the trade-offs between algorithmic and collateralized approaches to peg maintenance."

For Priya:
- *Strand 1:* "the literature on climate risk pricing in property markets, which documents how flood, wildfire, and other physical-risk exposures translate into property prices and insurance premiums."
- *Strand 2:* "the literature on regulatory disclosure of catastrophic-risk information, which examines how forced disclosure (FEMA maps, EPA registries, SEC climate disclosures) affects market behavior."
- *Strand 3:* "the literature on adaptation to revealed risk, which estimates whether disclosure leads to relocation, retrofitting, or insurance changes."

For Sam: the strands run as (1) intraday and short-horizon return predictability, (2) microstructure-cost models for converting gross to net returns, (3) out-of-sample testing and after-cost economic significance.

For Leah: the strands run as (1) measurement of technological proximity (citations, classifications, text), (2) knowledge spillovers and R&D externalities, (3) productivity estimation in panel data.

State your three strands in one sentence each. The strands should be intellectually distinct; if two of them are obviously the same field, merge them and add a third.

**(b) Defend the boundaries (6 pts).** In an opening paragraph (~150 words) at the top of the literature-review section, defend the boundary choices: *why* did you draw the line between strand 1 and strand 2 where you did, *why* are there three strands rather than two or four, and *why* are you not engaging with a fourth conversation a reader might expect.

For Maya: "We separate the literature on racial gaps (strand 1) from the literature on supervisory intensity (strand 2) because the two have largely run in parallel: the first documents the *phenomenon* without estimating *interventions*, and the second estimates *interventions* but rarely for *disparate-impact outcomes*. The novelty of our paper is in bridging these two conversations. We add the policy-evaluation strand (strand 3) because the contribution of the paper is ultimately policy-relevant — a regulator considering whether to deploy intensified examinations needs to know the magnitude — and the policy-evaluation literature is where that conversation happens. We do not engage with the broader literature on banking regulation generally, because our identification strategy is specific to one supervisory program and the broader debate (Basel, stress tests, capital requirements) is not where our contribution lies."

## Part 2 — Strand 1: the descriptive literature (20 points)

The single sin this part prevents: **a list of citations without an argument.** The strand-1 paragraph is not a chronological catalogue of papers; it is an argument that ends in a contribution sentence.

**(a) The strand-1 opening (3 pts).** One sentence naming the strand (from Part 1), one sentence stating what the strand collectively has established. For Maya: *"The strand of work documenting racial gaps in mortgage approval has, since Munnell, Tootell, Browne, and McEneaney (1996), established a series of robust findings: gaps persist across data sources (HMDA, proprietary lender data), gaps persist across time, gaps persist after controlling for an increasingly rich set of observable creditworthiness variables, and gaps vary across geography and lender type."*

**(b) Three or four detailed paper-by-paper engagements (12 pts, ~3 each).** The body of the strand paragraph engages with three or four specific papers, in approximate chronological order, each with one or two sentences explaining *what the paper added* and one sentence connecting that addition to your own paper.

For Maya, strand 1, the four engagement papers and example sentences:

1. *Munnell, Tootell, Browne, and McEneaney (1996), "Mortgage Lending in Boston: Interpreting HMDA Data," AER:* "Munnell et al. (1996) — the foundational paper of this literature — combined the publicly available HMDA application data with a richer survey of underwriting variables from Boston lenders, finding that the Black-White denial-rate gap shrank from roughly 60% to 30% after the full controls were added, but that a substantial residual gap remained. Our paper takes the post-control residual gap as the *phenomenon* and asks whether a specific supervisory tool can affect it."
2. *Bayer, Ferreira, and Ross (2016), "What Drives Racial and Ethnic Differences in High-Cost Mortgages?" REStat:* "Bayer, Ferreira, and Ross (2016) examined the high-cost-mortgage gap during the subprime period and decomposed it into between-lender and within-lender components, finding that within-lender variation explained the larger share. We follow them in clustering at the lender level for inference and in emphasizing that supervisory tools that target lender behavior — as ours does — operate on the dimension where the action is."
3. *Bartlett, Morse, Stanton, and Wallace (2022), "Consumer-Lending Discrimination in the FinTech Era," JFE:* "Bartlett, Morse, Stanton, and Wallace (2022) brought the literature into the algorithmic-underwriting era, finding that FinTech lenders display narrower but still substantial gaps relative to bank lenders. Our paper restricts attention to the bank lenders that are subject to OCC supervision; the FinTech segment is outside our identification (and outside our 2017 reform's scope), and we note in §7's external-validity memo that our estimates do not transport to the FinTech segment without further assumptions."
4. *Gao and Sun (2019), "Lender-Borrower Race-Match Effects on Mortgage Approval," PNAS:* "Gao and Sun (2019) demonstrated that race-matching between loan officer and borrower reduces denial-rate gaps by roughly half of the unadjusted disparity, suggesting that lender-level interventions can move the gap. Our paper differs in that we examine a *regulatory* lever (intensified examinations) rather than a *lender-staffing* lever (race-matching); the magnitudes are in the same ballpark, and we discuss the implication that the two channels may be complementary rather than substitutes."

Notice the discipline. Each engagement is one or two sentences per paper. Each names what the prior paper added. Each connects to your own paper through a specific design or content link (same data, same clustering, different lever, different segment). No paper is cited as a generic nod ("Smith 2024 also studied this"); every citation does specific work.

**(c) The strand-1 contribution sentence (5 pts).** The final sentence of strand 1 names what *your paper* adds to this conversation. It is the most-revised sentence of the literature review.

For Maya: *"Our paper adds to this strand by isolating the *causal* effect of a specific *regulatory* tool — the 2017 OCC examination intensification — on the documented gap, complementing the lender-staffing-level evidence of Gao and Sun (2019) and the algorithmic-vs.-bank evidence of Bartlett et al. (2022) with the first credibly causal estimate of a supervisory-program-level intervention on the disparate-impact outcome the literature has long documented."*

The contribution sentence: names the strand ("this literature"), names the contribution ("isolating the *causal* effect of a specific *regulatory* tool"), names the closest comparison points ("complementing… Gao and Sun (2019)… Bartlett et al. (2022)"), and names what is novel ("the first credibly causal estimate of a supervisory-program-level intervention"). One sentence does all four jobs. Most strand-1 paragraphs are rewritten three or four times before this sentence reads cleanly.

## Part 3 — Strand 2: the methodological or institutional literature (20 points)

The same structure as Part 2 — opening, three or four engagements, contribution sentence — but on the *second* strand. The dialectic between strand 1 (the phenomenon) and strand 2 (the design or institutional lever) is what motivates the paper's contribution.

**(a) The strand-2 opening (3 pts).** For Maya: *"The strand on regulatory supervisory intensity has produced a body of evidence on how examination effort affects prudential outcomes — capital ratios, lending growth, risk-taking — but rarely on disparate-impact outcomes."*

**(b) Three to four engagements (12 pts).** For Maya's strand 2, candidates include:

1. *Agarwal, Lucca, Seru, and Trebbi (2014), "Inconsistent Regulators," QJE:* engagement on the within-bank-across-supervisor identification, and on the finding that supervisory leniency affects loan classifications.
2. *Granja and Leuz (2024), "The Death of a Regulator: Strict Supervision, Bank Lending, and Business Activity," JFE:* engagement on the supervisory-strictness identification and on the lending-growth outcomes.
3. *Bhattacharya, Gabrieli, and Nieves (2024), or some specific paper from the recent supervisory-intensity literature [verify before citing]:* engagement on the closest disparate-impact-adjacent prior work.

**(c) The strand-2 contribution sentence (5 pts).** *"Our paper adds to this strand by extending the supervisory-intensity outcome variable from prudential measures to disparate-impact measures, exploiting the 2017 staggered rollout as a clean source of variation that is not confounded with the supervisor-pairing identification used by Agarwal et al. (2014) or with the agency-death event used by Granja and Leuz (2024)."*

The contribution sentence is structurally identical to strand 1's: names the strand, names the contribution, names the closest comparison, names what is novel.

## Part 4 — Strand 3: the policy-or-application literature (15 points)

The third strand is where the contribution paragraph (PS 11.4 paragraph 5) most closely connects. The third strand says "here is what policy-relevant question the paper's results inform."

**(a) The strand-3 opening (3 pts).** For Maya: *"The strand on the causal evaluation of anti-discrimination interventions is smaller than the first two, in part because credible identification of such interventions is rare."*

**(b) Two or three engagements (8 pts).** For Maya's strand 3, candidates include:

1. *Ross, Turner, Godfrey, and Smith (2008), "Mortgage Lending in Chicago and Los Angeles: A Paired-Testing Study," J. Urban Econ.:* engagement on the audit-study methodology and on what audit studies have established that observational designs have not.
2. *Munnell, Browne, McEneaney, and Tootell (1996), already cited in strand 1 but engaged here for its policy-implication discussion.*
3. *Recent work on the impact of CFPB, HMDA-rule, or other regulatory transitions [verify and cite specifically].*

**(c) The strand-3 contribution sentence (4 pts).** *"Our paper contributes to this strand by providing a magnitude estimate — roughly one-quarter of the persistent national gap, closed by the 2017 intensification in covered counties — that informs the design choice between supervisory tools and other anti-discrimination levers (community reinvestment requirements, lender-staffing interventions, audit-study disclosure)."*

The strand-3 contribution sentence is often the most policy-relevant one and the most readable to a non-academic audience. It is the sentence the press will quote if your paper gets covered.

## Part 5 — The contribution-sentence reveal (15 points)

The literature review's three contribution sentences, when stacked at the end of the section, form a mini-paragraph that previews the introduction's paragraph 5. This is the *reveal*: the moment where the paper's contribution is stated, fully, against the literature it engages.

**(a) Collect the three contribution sentences (5 pts).** In `contribution-reveal.md`, paste the final sentence of each strand (the contribution sentence from Parts 2c, 3c, 4c). The result is a three-sentence mini-paragraph. Read it aloud. It should be coherent — the three contributions should sound *related* (the paper makes one set of contributions to three literatures, not three unrelated contributions).

**(b) Write the unifying summary sentence (5 pts).** Add a fourth sentence at the end that unifies the three contributions: *"Taken together, our findings demonstrate that supervisory intensity is a tool that can move a long-documented disparate-impact outcome, that the magnitude is one-quarter of the persistent gap in covered counties, and that the result is robust to the multiple-testing, robustness-battery, and external-validity disciplines we apply (§§6–7)."* This sentence is the literature-review's bottom line.

**(c) Cross-reference to PS 11.4 paragraph 5 (5 pts).** Verify that the unifying summary sentence is consistent with — and a slight elaboration of — the introduction's paragraph 5 (the contribution paragraph from PS 11.4). The introduction's paragraph 5 is the abstract; the literature review's contribution reveal is the expansion. The two should not contradict; the literature review may legitimately add nuance, but the headline claim must match.

If the two contradict, fix the introduction first (paragraph 5 of PS 11.4 was written under less information than the literature review you now have) and update the tracked-changes file in PS 11.4.

## Part 6 — Bibliographic verification (15 points)

The single sin this part prevents: **citing a paper that does not exist or citing it incorrectly.** Modern referees Google citations; an incorrectly cited paper destroys credibility.

**(a) Build refs.bib (8 pts).** For every paper cited in the literature review, write a BibTeX entry. Include: author list (full first names where available), title (in title case), journal, year, volume, issue, pages, and DOI. Use the Crossref or journal-website canonical version, not Google Scholar (which sometimes has errors).

**(b) Verify each citation (4 pts).** In `verification-log.md`, write one line per cited paper: `Smith, J. (2024). "Title." Journal Vol(Issue): pages. DOI: 10.xxxx — VERIFIED via Crossref` or `... — [CHECK]: could not verify volume/issue from publisher page`. Mark unverified citations with `[CHECK]` rather than guessing. The book's convention (and modern publication norms) is that `[CHECK]` is acceptable for draft submission; fabricated citations are not.

**(c) Cross-check journal-name conventions (3 pts).** Use the canonical short forms: *Journal of Finance* (not *J. Fin.*), *Journal of Financial Economics* (not *JFE* in the bibliography, though *JFE* is acceptable in prose), *American Economic Review* (not *AER*). For finance, the *EconLit* canonical abbreviations are the safe choice. Verify in the verification log.

## How this is graded

Part 1 (strand selection, 15) on whether the three strands are intellectually distinct and the boundary defense is honest. Part 2 (strand 1, 20) on whether the three or four engagements are specific (each citation does work) and the contribution sentence names the closest comparison. Part 3 (strand 2, 20) on the same criteria for the second strand. Part 4 (strand 3, 15) on the same criteria for the third strand. Part 5 (contribution reveal, 15) on whether the three contribution sentences unify and on whether they are consistent with PS 11.4 paragraph 5. Part 6 (bibliographic verification, 15) on whether every citation is verified or honestly marked `[CHECK]`.

The single highest-signal check: **for each cited paper, can you state in one sentence what it added that your paper builds on or differs from?** If yes, the citation is doing work; if no, the citation is decoration and should be cut. A 24-citation literature review where every citation does work is more credible than a 60-citation review where half are decoration.

## The bridge to the rest of the manuscript

PS 11.5 closes Week 11's polish work on the *front* of the paper (abstract → intro → literature review). The *back* of the paper — methods, results, robustness, external-validity discussion, conclusion — was largely built in Weeks 7–10 and only needs minor polish in Week 12. Week 12 is the *integration* week: cover letter, response to referees (if you have one), final manuscript polish, version control, and submission. The artifacts from PS 11.1–11.5 (abstract, table, figure, intro, literature review) plus the artifacts from PS 10.1–10.5 (triage, multiple-testing, CATE, mechanism, external validity) plus the spec-menu robustness from PS 8.1 are the complete submission package. The book closes when the package is closed.
