# PS 11.1 — Three Drafts of Your Abstract, Each Under 250 Words, Each Implementing the Five-Sentence Skeleton Differently

**Course:** 8-Week Empirical Finance Camp · Week 11 · Problem Set 11.1
**Covers:** the move from Chapter 11.1 — the five-sentence skeleton (Question, Setting, Design, Finding, Why-It-Matters) — applied as a *deliberate-practice* exercise. You will write *three* abstracts of your own paper, not one. Each is under 250 words. Each implements the same five-sentence skeleton, but with a different lead — a Question-led version, a Finding-led variant, and a Setting-led variant — so that you can feel the difference each lead makes and pick the one your paper actually needs. The exercise builds on PS 10.5's external-validity memo (which constrains what Sentence 5 can claim) and on the publication-grade Table 2 you will produce in PS 11.2 (whose headline number Sentence 4 must quote verbatim).
**Type:** Pure writing, with a small diagnostic computation at the end. ~60% drafting the three abstracts, ~30% scoring them against the four-failure-mode diagnostic from Chapter 11.1 §4, ~10% running the `abstract_audit()` script from `nb11.1` on each draft and writing one paragraph defending your final choice. The deliverable is the three abstracts plus a short memo naming which one you would submit.

**Total: 100 points.** Point values stated per part. A model deliverable — Maya's three drafts of her fair-lending DiD abstract, scored against the diagnostic, with the Question-led variant chosen for submission and a one-paragraph defense — is in Appendix E (`E-w11-ps11.1-solutions.md`).

A boundary worth holding before you start. PS 11.1 is **not** "write the best abstract you can." It is "write three abstracts that demonstrate you can implement the skeleton three ways." The pedagogical move is *variation under constraint* — the constraint is the five-sentence skeleton and the 250-word cap; the variation is which sentence leads and which gets the most words. You learn the skeleton best when you have written it three times under three different framings, each of which forces a different tradeoff. The student who writes one good abstract has learned that one abstract; the student who writes three has learned the skeleton.

---

## What you submit

A single folder, `abstracts/`, committed to your capstone repository, containing:

1. `abstracts/draft-A-question-led.md` — Draft A: leads with the Question (sentence 1 is the longest), follows the skeleton in canonical 1→5 order. Word count target: 240–250.
2. `abstracts/draft-B-finding-led.md` — Draft B: leads with the Finding (sentence 1 is the headline, the rest reorders to support it). Word count target: 220–240.
3. `abstracts/draft-C-setting-led.md` — Draft C: leads with the Setting (sentence 1 establishes the empirical world before the question). Word count target: 230–250.
4. `abstracts/diagnostic.md` — the four-failure-mode scorecard for each draft (Disappearing Finding / Method-Heavy / Literature-Review Opening / Diffuse Conclusion), with a single Pass/Fail mark per cell.
5. `abstracts/audit-output.txt` — the printed output of `abstract_audit()` from `nb11.1` run on each of the three drafts.
6. `abstracts/choice-memo.md` — a one-paragraph memo (~200 words) defending which of the three drafts you would submit and why.

## Part 1 — Draft A: the Question-led abstract (25 points)

The single sin this part prevents: **leading with a hedge.** Draft A is the canonical implementation of the five-sentence skeleton. It is the safe choice; it is also the choice most published abstracts in *JF, JFE, RFS, JFQA, JFE,* and *AER* default to. You write it first to anchor the skeleton in your hand before you experiment.

**(a) Sentence 1 — Question (5 pts).** Write a Question sentence that names the causal or descriptive relationship being studied, the unit of analysis (implicit but recoverable), and the time period (implicit but recoverable). Use the template from Chapter 11.1 §2.1: *"This paper [estimates / measures / documents / quantifies] the [causal effect / association / pricing implication] of [treatment / variable] on [outcome] [in setting]."* The verb must match the design you will name in sentence 3 — *"estimates the causal effect"* requires a credible causal design; *"documents"* or *"quantifies"* is appropriate for descriptive or accounting work. If your verb mismatches your design, the abstract is *oversold* and a careful referee will mark it so on first reading.

**(b) Sentence 2 — Setting (5 pts).** Name the data source by its proper noun, the year range, the unit of observation, and the variation you will exploit. Avoid vague phrases like *"detailed administrative data"* — every empirical paper uses detailed administrative data. The specificity is the credential. Use the template: *"Using [data source(s)] from [year–year] on [unit of observation, sample size if striking], we [exploit / leverage / track] [the variation that will drive the design]."*

**(c) Sentence 3 — Design (8 pts).** Name the identification strategy *and* the assumption that makes it work, in one sentence. *"We use a difference-in-differences design"* is half a sentence; *"We use a staggered difference-in-differences design that identifies the average treatment effect under parallel trends, an assumption we test with pre-treatment event-study leads"* is the whole sentence. The assumption is the load-bearing noun phrase; the *evidence* for the assumption (the test, the placebo, the Oster δ) is the credibility marker.

This is the sentence most students underweight. It earns the referee's assignment-acceptance and is therefore worth twice the effort of any other sentence. Eight points reflects that weight.

**(d) Sentence 4 — Finding (5 pts).** Give the headline number with sign, magnitude in absolute units, magnitude in relative units, and precision (CI or t-statistic). The number must match — *verbatim* — the headline cell on the Table 2 you will produce in PS 11.2. If your Table 2 is not finalized yet, write the abstract against the *current best estimate* and mark the number with a `[verify against Table 2]` placeholder; do not invent a number, and do not write *"a statistically significant negative effect."* The unit must be one a non-specialist can interpret (percentage points, basis points, dollars, standard deviations — the kind of unit a policymaker would use, not log-points or coefficient-on-standardized-z).

**(e) Sentence 5 — Why-It-Matters (2 pts).** Name a consequence — a number a regulator can act on, a re-pricing a market should do, a forecast a forecaster should revise. Avoid the diffuse-conclusion failure mode: do not write *"our findings have implications for policy"* or *"future research should explore the mechanism."* The closing sentence is *what changes about the world* if your paper is right, in one declarative sentence.

The constraint: **Draft A is 240–250 words.** Use a word counter (the `nb11.1` script does this for you). Hitting 248 ± 2 is the discipline.

## Part 2 — Draft B: the Finding-led abstract (20 points)

The single sin this part prevents: **letting the headline number get lost behind the design.** Draft B inverts the canonical order: it leads with the finding. This is the structure used when the *result itself* is striking enough to earn the lead — when the magnitude or the direction or the precision is what the abstract should sell.

**(a) Reorder the five sentences (5 pts).** The order becomes: Finding → Setting → Design → Question (rephrased as "We ask whether…") → Why-It-Matters. The headline number is the first thing the reader sees. The Question sentence is demoted to the position where its job is now *to motivate the design that produced the finding* — it becomes a "we ask whether" sentence rather than a "this paper estimates" sentence.

Example reframe for Maya: instead of *"This paper estimates the causal effect of the 2017 intensified fair-lending examination program…"* (Question first), the Finding-led version opens *"The 2017 intensification of OCC fair-lending examinations reduced the Black–White denial-rate gap by 1.4 percentage points (95% CI: 0.8 to 2.0), or 23% of the pre-treatment gap, in covered U.S. counties."* The result is the lead; the rest of the abstract earns the credibility of the lead.

**(b) Write the reordered draft (10 pts).** 220–240 words. The discipline of Finding-led is *tighter* than Question-led — when the headline is first, the design and the assumption have to be re-introduced quickly and the abstract has less room to breathe. Most Finding-led abstracts in published finance run shorter than their Question-led counterparts for exactly this reason.

**(c) Identify when Finding-led wins (5 pts).** In a comment block at the end of `draft-B-finding-led.md`, write three sentences (~75 words) naming the conditions under which Finding-led would be the right submission choice for *your* paper. Three conditions to consider: (1) is the *magnitude* of your effect unusually large or unusually small for the literature (e.g., a 23% gap reduction is unusual; a 0.3% reduction is not — the unusual cases benefit from Finding-led)?  (2) is the *sign* unexpected — does your paper overturn a prior finding (Finding-led is *especially* effective when overturning, because the unexpected direction is the contribution)? (3) is the *precision* unusually high — do you have a tightly identified estimate with a CI an order of magnitude smaller than the prior literature (Finding-led broadcasts the precision)?

If none of the three apply, Finding-led is not your paper's lead. State so honestly in the comment; Draft B is then a useful exercise in the *negative* — you wrote it to discover that your paper does not need it.

## Part 3 — Draft C: the Setting-led abstract (20 points)

The single sin this part prevents: **assuming the reader already knows your empirical context.** Draft C leads with the Setting — the data, the institutional context, the period — before the question. This is the structure used when the *empirical setting* is itself novel or distinctive — when the data source is new, when the institutional context is unfamiliar to most readers, or when the time period is the key feature (a financial crisis, a regulatory transition, a pandemic).

**(a) Reorder the five sentences (5 pts).** The order becomes: Setting → Question → Design → Finding → Why-It-Matters. Sentence 1 establishes the empirical world; sentence 2 then asks the question *within* that world; the remaining three sentences proceed in canonical order.

Example reframe for Devon: the canonical opening would be *"This paper estimates the price impact of the May 2022 TerraUSD de-pegging event on a panel of 47 algorithmic and 12 collateralized stablecoins traded on centralized exchanges."* The Setting-led version opens with the world: *"Algorithmic stablecoins — cryptocurrencies that maintain a peg to fiat through arbitrage incentives rather than collateral — experienced a coordinated failure during the May 2022 TerraUSD collapse, with the affected market segment losing $48 billion of capitalization in 72 hours."* That sentence is two things at once: it teaches the reader what an algorithmic stablecoin is, *and* it locates them in the event. Sentence 2 then becomes *"We ask how the de-pegging event propagated through the algorithmic and collateralized segments of the broader stablecoin market…"* — the question, now anchored to a vivid setting.

**(b) Write the reordered draft (10 pts).** 230–250 words. Setting-led abstracts often run *longer* than Question-led because the setting paragraph has to teach the reader something — the institutional detail or the data feature that motivates the question. Resist the temptation to spend more than ~70 words on the setting; the reader is still waiting for the question.

**(c) Identify when Setting-led wins (5 pts).** In a comment block at the end of `draft-C-setting-led.md`, write three sentences (~75 words) naming the conditions under which Setting-led would be the right submission choice for *your* paper. Three conditions to consider: (1) is your data source new or proprietary — does the reader need to be told what HMDA-LAR, EDGAR full-text, FRED-MD, or a niche provider's data actually *is* (Setting-led wins)? (2) is your institutional context historically specific — does the reader need to understand the 2017 OCC fair-lending program, the EU MiCA regulation, the FEMA flood-zone process before they can grade the question (Setting-led wins)? (3) is the *event* itself the paper's contribution — is the paper an event study of a unique episode (the May 2022 de-peg, the March 2023 banking turmoil, the 2018 flood-map revision), where the Setting *is* the question (Setting-led wins by definition)?

## Part 4 — The diagnostic scorecard (15 points)

For each of the three drafts, score it on the four failure modes from Chapter 11.1 §4: Disappearing Finding, Method-Heavy, Literature-Review Opening, Diffuse Conclusion. Use the diagnostic questions from each subsection; a draft passes a diagnostic if you can answer the question "yes." Mark each cell P (pass) or F (fail) in `abstracts/diagnostic.md`.

The expected output:

| Diagnostic | Draft A | Draft B | Draft C |
|---|---|---|---|
| Disappearing Finding (can a reader quote a number?) | P/F | P/F | P/F |
| Method-Heavy (≤1 sentence mentions method)? | P/F | P/F | P/F |
| Literature-Review Opening (sentence 1 subject is not literature)? | P/F | P/F | P/F |
| Diffuse Conclusion (sentence 5 names a concrete consequence)? | P/F | P/F | P/F |

If any draft has more than one F, it is not submission-ready. Note in the scorecard which sentences are sick and what the rewrite would be.

**(a) Score the three drafts (8 pts).** Two points per draft for honestly applying the four diagnostics; two points for naming the sentence-level failure when a cell is marked F (do not just write "F" — write "F: sentence 5 ends on 'has implications for policy'").

**(b) Cross-draft pattern (4 pts).** Write a short paragraph (~100 words) naming any failure mode that recurs across drafts. If, for example, all three drafts fail Diffuse Conclusion, the diagnosis is that *the paper's why-it-matters is not yet sharp enough for the abstract* — and the fix is upstream of the abstract, in the paper itself. The cross-draft pattern is more informative than the per-draft score for spotting upstream problems.

**(c) Run the script (3 pts).** Run `abstract_audit()` from `nb11.1` on each of the three drafts. Capture the output in `audit-output.txt`. The script catches the obvious cases; the per-draft scorecard catches the subtle ones. Both should agree on the obvious failures and may disagree on the marginal ones — the script flags `disappearing_finding` if no `\d+\s*(pp|%|percent)` appears anywhere in the abstract, but a well-written abstract that quotes a *basis-point* number ("18 basis points") passes the diagnostic and fails the script. Note any such disagreements in the scorecard.

## Part 5 — The choice memo (20 points)

This is the deliverable that closes the exercise. You wrote three abstracts; one of them goes to your advisor on Friday. Defend the choice in a single paragraph (~200 words) in `choice-memo.md`.

**(a) Name your choice (3 pts).** State which of A, B, C you would submit. State it in the first sentence.

**(b) Defend the choice with three reasons (12 pts, 4 each).** Three reasons, each one sentence, each grounded in *this* paper's properties:

- *Reason 1 — what your paper's headline supports.* Is the magnitude or precision unusual (Finding-led wins)? Is the setting unfamiliar to your target audience (Setting-led wins)? Is the question the contribution (Question-led wins)? Reference the specific feature of your paper that points to the chosen lead.
- *Reason 2 — what your readers expect.* What journal are you targeting? Read three recent abstracts from that journal; what is the modal lead? In *Journal of Finance* and *Journal of Financial Economics* the modal lead is Question; in *Review of Financial Studies* and certain *AER* sections the modal lead can be Finding; in subfield journals (e.g., *Journal of Banking and Finance, Real Estate Economics*) the modal lead can be Setting. Your choice should be in dialogue with the target.
- *Reason 3 — what the audit caught.* Did one draft pass all four diagnostics while another failed two? If so, the audit picks for you and the choice is mechanical — choose the cleaner draft, regardless of which lead you initially preferred.

**(c) Name what you would change if your data changed (5 pts).** One sentence: *"If our preferred saturation in Table 2 instead delivered a finding of [hypothetical alternative magnitude], the abstract I would submit would shift from [chosen lead] to [other lead], because [reason]."* This sentence demonstrates that you understand the choice is *contingent* on the paper's empirical content — not an aesthetic preference about leads.

## How this is graded

Part 1 (Question-led draft, 25) on whether the five-sentence skeleton is implemented cleanly *and* whether sentence 3 names both design and assumption with evidence. Part 2 (Finding-led, 20) on whether the reorder works mechanically *and* whether the comment honestly identifies whether Finding-led is right for the paper. Part 3 (Setting-led, 20) on whether the setting paragraph teaches the reader something *and* whether the comment names the empirical-context novelty that motivates the choice. Part 4 (diagnostic, 15) on whether the scorecard is applied with the per-sentence rigor the diagnostic requires. Part 5 (choice memo, 20) on whether the choice is *defended* with three reasons grounded in the paper, not in a general preference about how abstracts should be written.

The single highest-signal check: **does the chosen draft's sentence 4 quote the same number, with the same precision, as the headline cell on your Table 2?** If the abstract says "1.4 percentage points (95% CI: 0.8 to 2.0)" and Table 2 says "1.43 (0.31)" — note that the CI implied by 0.31 is roughly 0.82 to 2.04, so the abstract's "0.8 to 2.0" is the correct one-decimal rounding of the table — that is correct. If the abstract says "1.4 pp" but Table 2 says "1.6 (0.31)," you have a number-discrepancy problem that the abstract revision did not catch and the table production in PS 11.2 will surface.

## The bridge to PS 11.2 and beyond

PS 11.1 produces the *abstract's number*. PS 11.2 produces the *table* that defends the number with full precision, panel structure, and footnote anatomy. PS 11.3 produces the *figure* that visualizes the dynamics behind the number. PS 11.4 produces the *introduction* whose five-paragraph funnel ends in the same number. PS 11.5 produces the *literature review* whose three-strand contribution paragraph closes with the abstract's sentence 5. The abstract is the smallest deliverable of Week 11 in word count and the most leveraged in attention — every reader sees it, and most readers see only it before deciding whether to read further. Get this right first; the rest of the week's work amplifies it.

*End of PS 11.1. Write Draft A first, against your own paper, before reading Maya's exemplar. Read the exemplar (Appendix E) to calibrate the Question-led discipline, the Finding-led reorder, the Setting-led teach-the-reader move, and the choice-memo defense. Do not copy Maya's fair-lending paper's content; the skeleton is the transferable artifact, not the example. By Friday your advisor's inbox will contain the version of your abstract that you chose against three alternatives — the kind of choice that survives careful editor reading.*
