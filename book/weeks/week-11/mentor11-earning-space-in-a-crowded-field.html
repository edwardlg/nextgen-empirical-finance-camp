Mentor Session 11 — “Writing a paper for a field that already has 200 papers on the topic.”

Week 11 live session · 60 minutes · led by Prof. Lei Gao · the Phase-3 manuscript-week mentor

You have a paper. The introduction is five paragraphs (Chapter 11.4), the tables are generated by etable (Chapter 11.2 and Lab 11), the figures share typography (Chapter 11.3), the bibliography validates against the verified list (Lab 11). The PDF compiles in one command. What you do not yet have — and what today’s session is about — is the part of the introduction that has nothing to do with discipline or build pipelines and everything to do with earning a paper’s place in a field that already has 200 papers on the topic.

Most of you are writing in crowded fields. Maya’s fair-lending literature has decades of denial-gap work behind it; Devon’s crypto-event-study literature has exploded since 2017; Sam is writing about momentum, a topic with thousands of papers; Leah is writing about patent-text-analysis, which had 50 papers in 2018 and 500 by 2024. A crowded field does not mean your paper does not belong. It means your paper has to earn its space, and the way it earns its space is in two specific sentences of the introduction — the contribution sentence and the literature-positioning move.

I anchor today on Gao, He & Wu (2023) in the Journal of Financial and Quantitative Analysis. Mentor Session 7 used this paper for its identification angle. Today I re-anchor on the positioning angle: how a paper writing into a literature with hundreds of CSR-and-firm-outcomes papers earned its space by naming a specific gap and filling it in a way no prior paper had. Bring the pre-read, your three Socratic warm-ups, and paragraph 5 of your draft introduction. We read yours in the room.

(a) Pre-read packet — “Why the contribution sentence is the hardest sentence to write.”

Read this once before the session. It is one page; read it slowly, because the disposition matters as much as the technique today.

Every introduction has a contribution paragraph, and every contribution paragraph has — in its first or second sentence — a single sentence that does the heaviest work in the entire paper: the contribution sentence. It is what the next researcher will paraphrase when they cite you, what the editor returns to when deciding whether to send out, what the referee returns to when deciding whether the paper merits the journal. Get it right and the paper has a chance. Get it wrong — overclaim, underclaim, or claim something the data cannot support — and the paper is in trouble even when the result is correct.

The hardest part is not writing the contribution sentence well. It is writing it honestly. Two failure modes. The overclaim: “This paper is the first to identify the causal effect of X on Y” — when the literature contains three close papers and the design has limitations the sentence does not name. The underclaim: “This paper contributes to the literature on X by providing new evidence on Y” — generic enough that no reader extracts a specific claim. The first costs you a referee who finds the un-cited paper; the second costs you the editor’s attention.

The third path is the specific-and-bounded contribution sentence: short, bounded, and positioned relative to the closest prior paper, not the broad literature. When I sat down to write the contribution sentence for Gao, He & Wu (2023), the CSR-and-firm-outcomes literature already had hundreds of papers. The honest move was not “this is the first paper on CSR” — that would have been laughable. The honest move was to ask: what does my design let me say that the closest existing paper does not?

Writing into a crowded field is not a defensive posture but a targeted one. The crowded field is the opportunity, not the obstacle: a well-positioned paper is more citable there, not less, because readers searching the field will find you.

(b) Three Socratic warm-up questions

Come with a written sentence or two for each. These have honest answers and evasive ones, not right and wrong ones.

  1. The closest-competitor audit. Open paragraph 5 of your draft introduction. Then open Google Scholar and search for the closest prior paper to yours — the one a referee would point to with “isn’t this just X?” Read its abstract. Now, in one or two sentences, write what your paper does that theirs does not — and what theirs does that yours does not. If you cannot honestly answer the second half, you have not found the right closest-competitor.

  2. The single-gap discipline. Look at paragraph 5 again. Count the distinct gaps your contribution paragraph claims to fill. If more than one — “new identification and new data and new heterogeneity” — pick the single most defensible and rewrite around it. Write the new contribution sentence. Why does claiming one gap make the paper more citable than claiming four? (Hint: a reader’s search returns papers by the gap they fill.)

  3. The “would Gao cite this?” test. Imagine I am writing a related-literature paragraph in my next paper. Write the exact sentence I would write about your paper: “Recent work by [your name] (2026) … documents/shows/identifies …”. If you cannot write it — if you do not know what your own paper’s one-sentence contribution is — your contribution paragraph has not done its job. What sentence did you write, and what does it tell you about your draft?

(c) Five-slide deck (speak-from scaffold)

Slide 1 — “The contribution sentence is the sentence the next paper will paraphrase.”

  • A paper in a crowded field earns its space in two sentences of the introduction: the contribution sentence (what the paper adds) and the literature-positioning move (how the addition sits relative to the closest prior work).
  • The contribution sentence is what the next researcher will paraphrase when they cite you, what the editor will look for when deciding to send out, what the referee will return to when deciding whether the paper merits the journal.
  • Two failure modes to avoid: the overclaim (promises more than the design delivers) and the underclaim (so generic the reader extracts no specific claim). Today’s discipline is the third path: specific-and-bounded.

Slide 2 — The 200-papers problem: why crowded fields reward positioning, not breadth.

  • A literature with 200 papers does not mean your paper does not belong. It means readers have well-developed mental maps of the gaps, and a paper that fills a named gap is more findable than one that tries to do everything.
  • The wrong instinct: “This field is crowded; I need to make my paper bigger to stand out.” The result is a paper that tries to do four things and does none well.
  • The right instinct: “This field is crowded; I need to make my paper more specific so the gap I fill is unique.” The result is a paper that does one thing well and earns its citations from the readers searching for that one thing.

Slide 3 — The literature-positioning move: name the closest competitor, then name the gap.

  • In paragraph 5 of the introduction, name the single closest prior paper to yours (not all 200 papers — just the one closest), then name the gap your paper closes relative to that paper.
  • The audit a referee runs: read paragraph 5, ask “who is the closest competitor?” If the answer is named, good; if not, the referee opens Scholar and finds the competitor themselves — which means the author missed an acknowledgment, and the review goes poorly.
  • The right closest-competitor is the paper a hostile referee would point to and say “isn’t this just X?” Naming X first is how you preempt the objection: “Closest to this paper is Bhutta, Hizmo & Ringo (2024) on CRA examinations; we differ in targeted-by-finding-history rather than scheduled-and-broad-based design, in staggered rather than cohort-once-per-cycle timing, and in focus on the racial gap rather than aggregate lending volume.”

Slide 4 — The contribution sentence: one gap, one sentence, one verb.

  • The contribution sentence is one sentence. Not a paragraph. If it is longer than 30 words, it is doing too much.
  • The verb must match the design, the same discipline as Chapter 11.1 sentence 1 of the abstract. Identifies / estimates the causal effect of requires a credible causal design. Documents / characterizes is appropriate for descriptive work. Quantifies is appropriate for calibration. Mismatch is the most common reason a referee marks a paper as “oversold.”
  • The contribution sentence names what and what gap. The “what” is the finding; the “what gap” is the part the closest-competitor did not have. A sentence that names what without the gap is descriptive; one that names the gap without what is meta-talk; the contribution sentence does both.

Slide 5 — The post-positioning check: would your paper appear in the field’s next survey?

  • A field with 200 papers produces a survey article every five to seven years. The survey author picks ~50 papers to discuss and groups them by sub-topic. A paper with a well-written contribution sentence appears in exactly one sub-topic of the survey, named for the gap it filled. A paper with a poorly written one appears either in too many sub-topics or in none.
  • The test: if a survey on your field came out in 2030, would your paper appear in it? If yes, which one paragraph? If no, your contribution sentence is not specific enough, and the rewrite is to claim a narrower territory.
  • The disposition: the contribution sentence is territorial. It claims a piece of the literature for your paper to occupy. The narrower the territory, the more defensible the claim — and the more findable the paper.

(d) Three stretch questions — Gao, He & Wu (2023) on CSR and price pressure, read for the positioning

These tie today’s theme to my own paper, used as a worked case from the literature-positioning angle (Mentor 7 used it from the identification angle). The citation, used verbatim:
