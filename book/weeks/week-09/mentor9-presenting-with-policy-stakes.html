Mentor Session 9 — “Presenting a paper that already has policy stakes.”

Week 9 live session · 60 minutes · led by Prof. Lei Gao · the conference-week mentor

Yesterday’s lab put you in a room and asked you to defend an estimate against a panel that had read your paper. That is the rehearsal. The rehearsal’s job is to make the conference day boring. But today I want to walk you through a room the lab cannot stage, because most academic rehearsals never see it either: the room you walk into after a paper has been published, when the audience is not three peers and a chair, but a senior staffer from HUD, a Federal Reserve economist with a portfolio you just touched, and a Congressional staffer who has to write a memo by Friday. The talk is the same eight minutes. The slide deck is the same six beats. And almost everything about how you deliver it changes — because for the first time, the people in the room are not deciding whether your paper is right. They are deciding what to do with it.

I have stood in that room. After our 2019 PNAS paper on lending fairness came out, the next two years included presentations at HUD, at the Federal Reserve, and to Congressional staff. The paper had been refereed and published, so the methodological fight was over; what remained was a different fight — about what the result means for policy, about what data was used and whose property it is, about which agency owns the question now. That is the room you may stand in if your capstone lands in the Schar Young Scholars Journal and gets cited, or if your fair-lending project gets a write-up that a community-development office reads. It is a room your camp is not done preparing you for until I tell you what changes. Bring the pre-read, your three Socratic warm-ups, and — this is the one that matters — your honest answer to the question on slide 5: what is the most irresponsible way someone could quote your result tomorrow?

(a) Pre-read packet — “When the audience includes the people who own the data.”

Read this once before the session. It is one page; read it slowly, because the mindset matters more than the technique today.

For eight weeks I have been teaching you to present to a referee — a hostile, intelligent reader whose job is to find the more boring explanation for your result and protect the literature from a wrong claim. The defense, against a referee, is to show you found the boring explanation first, looked at it, and either ruled it out or bounded what it could do. That is Mentor 8. That is the methodological room. It is the room your capstone defense lives in.

Today’s room is different in three concrete ways, and I want you to internalize all three before the session begins, because once a paper has policy stakes the failure mode is no longer “I got refereed.” The failure mode is “I said something a Congressional staffer quoted, and the quote was not wrong but it was irresponsible.”

The first change is about who is in the room. A regulator does not care whether your standard errors are clustered correctly — they care whether your finding applies to the population they have authority over. A Federal Reserve economist may know your method better than you do; what they want to hear is what you would do if you ran your own design on their internal data, the one with the variables your public sample is missing. A Congressional staffer is reading two papers a day for a memo with a deadline, and what they need from you is one sentence they can quote that will not embarrass them next month. The audience’s job, in other words, is no longer to evaluate your paper. It is to use it. That changes what they listen for, which changes what you must say.

The second change is about who collected the data you used. In academic seminars, the data are an input. In policy rooms, the data are very often the host’s data — HMDA was built by the agencies in the room, HUD has a more recent version of it on a server you cannot see, and the Federal Reserve has joined it to call-report variables no one outside the building has touched. Two consequences. First, the data’s limitations are limitations they already know; do not try to teach them. Second, your result implicitly tells the people who built that data whether they built it right — and that judgment lands harder than you think. The respectful posture is the one I learned in front of HUD: name the variable you wish you had, name the agency that has it, and ask the audience whether the result would survive on the internal version. That sentence turns the room from defending against you to thinking with you, which is the difference between a presentation that goes well and one that does not.

The third change is the one that matters most. Your result, in the policy room, has consequences. The agencies in the audience may, weeks or months later, draft a rule, fund an initiative, or pull back from one, partly because of the number you said out loud. That is the privilege of presenting research with stakes; it is also the duty. The duty is to say, in plain English and on the same slide as your headline, what your finding does NOT support — to draw the line between what your design can speak to and what it cannot, so the staffer who wants to write “Gao & Sun (2019) shows…” has both the true version of the sentence and the warning label about the cheaper, wronger version they would otherwise write. The strongest signal a junior researcher can send in a policy room is to volunteer, before being asked, where the over-reach line is. The audience trusts you more for naming it, not less.

Hold one reflex sharp as you read today’s questions — for my own capstone result, what is the irresponsible quote that someone could pull from it tomorrow, and have I built the slide that prevents that quote? If you have, the policy talk is honest. If you haven’t, that gap is the most important finding of the session.

(b) Three Socratic warm-up questions

Come with a written sentence or two for each. These have honest answers and evasive ones, not right and wrong ones.

  1. The “what should we do?” question, decoded. In the policy room, the question that ended five of my Q&A sessions and that no academic defense ever asks is: “OK — so what should we do?” The bad versions of your answer are obvious: a policy prescription your design cannot support, or an evasive “I’m just a researcher.” Write the good version in one or two sentences. (Hint: it has three parts — what your design can speak to, what it cannot, and a named follow-up question whose answer would actually inform the policy.) Why is a researcher who refuses to draw a direct prescription often more useful to a regulator than one who happily provides one?

  2. Talking about data the audience built. You are presenting a fair-lending result that used HMDA. In the room is a HUD staffer whose office publishes HMDA and whose colleague built the joined file you wish you had. You are about to put up a slide that says, in effect, “the public HMDA fields are missing a critical variable on applicant credit history, which limits what I can say about the channel.” (a) Why is this sentence dangerous if said the wrong way, and what is the wrong way? (b) Rewrite the same sentence so it credits the agency for building the data, names the limitation as your own honest constraint rather than their failure, and explicitly invites them — in the form of a research question — to extend the work on the internal version. (c) Why does this rewrite get you a better result than the wrong-way version, including for your future ability to access the internal data?

  3. The irresponsible quote. A reporter or a staffer is going to walk out of your talk with a single sentence. Write the honest sentence — the one you would be proud to see in a news story or a memo — and the irresponsible-but-defensible sentence — the one a journalist could write, partly quoting you, that you would not be proud of but could not legally call a misquote. What is the difference between the two, in terms of which clauses appear in your headline slide and which were dropped? And — the harder part — what is the slide change you could make today that would close the gap so the irresponsible quote becomes harder to extract?

(c) Five-slide deck (speak-from scaffold)

Slide 1 — “The room changed; the talk changed.” - Academic seminars judge whether your paper is right. Policy rooms decide what to do with your paper. The same eight minutes serve a different purpose, so emphasis shifts: less time on the design slide, more time on what the result does not support. - I learned this the hard way after Gao & Sun (2019) on lending fairness — the paper presented one way at finance conferences and another way at HUD, the Federal Reserve, and on the Hill, and the differences were not cosmetic. - The three differences you will internalize today: (1) the audience’s job is to use the work, not to evaluate it; (2) the data in the room often belong to people in the room; (3) your result has consequences, which means you owe the room a line between what your design can support and what it cannot.

Slide 2 — Audience as user, not evaluator. - A regulator listens for applicability to their authority — does your result generalize to the population they oversee, on the timeline they have to act on? Adjust your talk to answer that question explicitly: in the setup slide, name the population your sample maps to, and on the result slide, name the population it does not. - A Federal Reserve economist often knows your method better than you do. The respectful posture is not to teach the method but to invite them: “the version of this design I’d want to run on your internal call-report-joined file would add X — and I’d expect Y.” That is a conversation, not a defense. - A Congressional staffer needs one quotable sentence and a memo deadline. Give them the sentence on slide 3, in plain English, with the caveat baked into the same sentence rather than appended as a footnote. “In counties that adopted state-level fair-lending examination programs between 2015 and 2022, the gap narrowed by about 1.8 points on average, in a comparison that is credible but not airtight” is a memo-ready sentence; “the coefficient on Exam_st is −1.8 (p < 0.01), clustered at the state level” is not.

Slide 3 — When the data are the host’s. - Most policy datasets you will ever use were built by an agency that may be in the room. HMDA was built by the FFIEC agencies and HUD; the credit-bureau panels were built by the regulators or by data vendors the regulators contract with. The internal version almost always has a variable your public version does not. - The posture I learned in front of HUD: name the variable you wish you had, name the agency that has it, frame the limitation as your honest constraint rather than their publication failure, and ask the audience whether the result would survive on the internal version. “The public HMDA fields don’t carry applicant credit history; HUD’s internal join with credit-bureau data does. I’d love to know — and I think it’s the most important follow-up question — whether the gap survives once that channel is conditioned on.” - This single move converts the room from defending-against-you to thinking-with-you. It also, in three of my own experiences, opened the door to a data-access conversation that academic posturing would have closed.

Slide 4 — The “what should we do?” question. - This question will be asked. Refusing to engage it (“I’m just a researcher”) makes you useless to the room. Answering it with a policy prescription your design cannot support makes you irresponsible to the literature. Both are failure modes; the good answer threads between them. - The three-part shape: (a) what my design can speak to — usually a direction or a magnitude under stated assumptions; (b) what it cannot speak to — usually the mechanism, the long-run, or the welfare calculus; (c) the named follow-up — the question whose answer would actually inform the policy choice the room cares about. - Concretely: “My design says programs of the type adopted in 2015–2022 are associated with a narrower denial gap, under parallel trends. It does not say which feature of the program — the examination intensity, the publication of findings, the regulatory follow-up — is doing the work. The follow-up I’d most want funded is a within-program decomposition; that’s the question whose answer would change the design of the next program.” That is research talking to policy without overreaching. - The lesson I learned, repeatedly: the regulator who walks away saying “we can use this, but here’s what we still need to know” is the one who calls you back. The one who walks away with “this researcher told us to do X” is the one who never trusted you in the first place and never will.

Slide 5 — The irresponsible quote. - The single most useful exercise before any policy talk is to write the irresponsible-but-defensible quote — the sentence a journalist could extract from your work that you would not be proud of but could not call a misquote. Until you have written it, you do not know where your headline slide is leaking. - For our 2019 fair-lending paper, the irresponsible quote would have been a version that dropped the conditional clauses — the ones about which markets, which periods, and which segment of applicants the comparison was clean for — and left a sentence that sounded like a universal statement about discrimination. The defense was a slide whose headline sentence included the conditional clauses in the same breath as the magnitude, so that a quote that dropped them would have to be a quote out of context rather than a quote of the slide. - The duty is structural, not personal. You will not catch every misquote, and that is fine. The duty is to have written the slide such that the honest sentence is the easier sentence to extract — to make irresponsibility require effort. That is what publication-with-stakes asks of you, and it is what the rehearsal in Lab 9 did not have time to teach.

(d) Three stretch questions — defending a published fair-lending paper in front of HUD, the Fed, and Congress

These tie today’s theme to one of my own papers, used as a live worked case. The citation, used verbatim:

Gao, L., & Sun, R. (2019). Lending fairness in U.S. mortgage markets. Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences, 116(19), 9293–9302.

The paper studies the lending denial gap between minority and white applicants in U.S. mortgage markets, using HMDA loan-application records. It is the right case for today for three reasons. First, the data are HMDA — built by HUD and the FFIEC, the agencies most likely to be in the room. Second, the result has direct policy implications: any number about racial disparities in lending feeds, sooner or later, into a regulatory or Congressional conversation about fair-lending enforcement. Third, I gave versions of this talk at HUD, the Federal Reserve, and to Congressional staff in the two years after publication, so the lessons below are not hypothetical — they are what I learned by getting some things right and some things wrong.

Reason about how a published paper presents differently in policy rooms — not about what the paper specifically found. Do NOT invent or recite any specific reported magnitudes, coefficients, sample sizes, or named control variables from the paper. Frame every answer as “how I would present” or “what I would say”; mark anything you would need to confirm against the published version as [CHECK].

  1. The HUD room — credit them for building the data you used. Imagine you are presenting Gao & Sun (2019) in 2020 at HUD’s Office of Policy Development and Research, in a conference room of roughly twelve staff including one career economist who has worked on HMDA for fifteen years. The chair introduces you. You have thirty minutes. (a) Your first slide change relative to the academic version of the talk: identify the one sentence that you would add to slide 1 or slide 2 to credit the agency for building the dataset that made the research possible — and explain why that single sentence, said early, changes the tone of every question you will get afterward. (b) Now the data-limitation move: the public HMDA fields are famously missing some controls a fair-lending regulator would want — applicant credit characteristics, debt-to-income detail. Without citing any magnitude from the paper, draft the sentence you would use to name that limitation in the HUD room in a way that frames it as your constraint, not their failure, and that explicitly invites the agency’s internal version of the data as the follow-up. (c) After your talk, the senior career economist asks: “Are you saying our examination programs work?” In one or two sentences each, write the bluff answer (a clean “yes”), the evasive answer (“I’m just a researcher”), and the honest, useful answer that names what your design can speak to and what it cannot. Why does the honest answer, even though it is the least quotable, get you invited back?

  2. The Federal Reserve room — the audience may know your method better than you do. Now imagine you are at the Federal Reserve Board, presenting in a seminar room of ten people, three of whom have published their own fair-lending papers, one of whom built the call-report data you would have used if you had access. The clock is the same; the audience is sharper. (a) In the academic talk, the design slide is the spine and you spend the most time there. In the Fed room, you might spend less time on the design slide — not because the design matters less, but because of who is listening. Explain in one sentence why, and what you spend the extra minute on instead. (b) Draft the sentence you would use to invite the Fed economists to think about what your design would look like on their internal data — not as a request for access (which would be premature) but as a research framing. (Hint: the move is “the question I’d most want answered on the data you have is …” rather than “I wish I had your data.”) (c) One Fed economist asks the question that scares me most about presenting at the Fed: “Have you thought about how your design interacts with the differences in regulator behavior across the Fed districts?” You do not have a clean answer because the paper did not study district-level variation. Following Mentor 8’s located-“I-don’t-know” rule, draft your two-sentence answer that admits the gap, names the design feature you would need to add to address it, and turns the question into a follow-up rather than a wound.

  3. The Congressional-staff room — one quotable sentence, one deadline. Now the hardest room. You are presenting to four Congressional staffers from two committees in a thirty-minute briefing. They have read your abstract; they will not remember your method; they need one sentence each to put in a memo by Friday. None of them is hostile, but none of them has time. (a) Draft your headline sentence — the one you put on your headline slide and read out loud once — in a form that contains the conditional clauses (which markets, which years, which applicants) in the same breath as the magnitude, so that a memo that quotes the sentence cannot drop the conditionals without committing a visible truncation. Without using a fabricated magnitude, sketch the shape of that sentence — what its clauses are and in what order. (b) Now do the exercise from slide 5: write the irresponsible-but-defensible quote — the one a staffer could plausibly extract from your talk and put in a partisan memo, that you would not be proud of but could not legally call a misquote. Explain which clause they dropped, and what specific slide-design change would have made that drop visibly dishonest rather than merely selective. (c) A staffer asks the “what should we do?” question. Without prescribing a specific policy your design cannot support, draft your three-part answer (what the design speaks to / what it does not / the named follow-up) in the form of a 45-second spoken response — and explain why this is the answer that gets you cited correctly in their memo, when the prescriptive answer would have gotten you cited misleadingly.

(e) Post-session reflection prompt

Write ~250–350 words after the session. This is the one that goes with you into your conference talk on August 14 and — if your paper is good enough — into whatever policy room your work eventually reaches.

Write the policy-stakes brief for your own capstone. The lab in Week 9 had you build the bundle for the academic audience; this brief is the brief you would carry into the next room, the one that includes the people who own the data and the people who write the memos. Three paragraphs, in this order.

First, the audience-mapping paragraph. Name one specific real institution that, if your result is right, would have an interest in hearing it. For Maya’s fair-lending project, that is HUD’s Office of Policy Development and Research or the CFPB; for Priya’s climate-insurance work, FEMA or a state insurance commissioner; for Devon’s crypto project, the SEC’s Office of the Chief Economist or FinCEN; for Sam’s market work, a stock-exchange regulator like the SEC’s Division of Trading and Markets; for Leah’s patent-text work, the USPTO Office of the Chief Economist. Then write the sentence you would add to slide 1 of your conference talk to credit that institution for building the data you used, in the form of a real sentence you would actually say out loud.

Second, the irresponsible-quote paragraph. Following slide 5’s exercise, write your headline sentence with its conditional clauses in the same breath as the magnitude, then write the irresponsible-but-defensible quote a journalist or staffer could extract from a talk that omitted those clauses. Identify which slide in your current deck (from Lab 9) makes the dropping of those clauses easy, and describe in one sentence the specific design change you will make before the August 14 conference to make that drop visibly dishonest rather than merely selective.

Third, the “what should we do?” paragraph. Draft your three-part answer to that question on your own result: what your design can speak to, what it cannot, and the named follow-up question whose answer would actually inform the policy choice the audience cares about. Resist the temptation to either prescribe a policy or evade the question; thread the needle in three sentences, as someone whose paper has stakes and whose obligation is to be useful without overreaching.

Close with one sentence — not for the brief, but for yourself. Six years after Gao & Sun (2019), the sentence I would write here is “the irresponsible quote I worried about did get written, and the slide that made it harder to extract is the reason I am still happy with the paper.” Write the one-sentence version of that for yourself. If the version is honest, you have not just a paper — you have a result you would present to anyone, in any room, on any day, and trust to mean what you said.

## (c) Five-slide deck (speak-from scaffold)

**Slide 1 — "The room changed; the talk changed."**
- Academic seminars judge whether your *paper* is right. Policy rooms decide what to *do* with your paper. The same eight minutes serve a different purpose, so emphasis shifts: less time on the design slide, more time on what the result does *not* support.
- I learned this the hard way after Gao & Sun (2019) on lending fairness — the paper presented one way at finance conferences and another way at HUD, the Federal Reserve, and on the Hill, and the differences were not cosmetic.
- The three differences you will internalize today: (1) the audience's *job* is to use the work, not to evaluate it; (2) the *data* in the room often belong to people in the room; (3) your result has *consequences*, which means you owe the room a line between what your design can support and what it cannot.

**Slide 2 — Audience as user, not evaluator.**
- A regulator listens for *applicability to their authority* — does your result generalize to the population they oversee, on the timeline they have to act on? Adjust your talk to answer that question explicitly: in the setup slide, name the population your sample maps to, and on the result slide, name the population it does *not*.
- A Federal Reserve economist often knows your method better than you do. The respectful posture is not to teach the method but to invite them: "the version of this design I'd want to run on your internal call-report-joined file would add X — and I'd expect Y." That is a conversation, not a defense.
- A Congressional staffer needs one quotable sentence and a memo deadline. Give them the sentence on slide 3, in plain English, with the caveat baked into the same sentence rather than appended as a footnote. *"In counties that adopted state-level fair-lending examination programs between 2015 and 2022, the gap narrowed by about 1.8 points on average, in a comparison that is credible but not airtight"* is a memo-ready sentence; *"the coefficient on `Exam_st` is −1.8 (p < 0.01), clustered at the state level"* is not.

**Slide 3 — When the data are the host's.**
- Most policy datasets you will ever use were built by an agency that may be in the room. HMDA was built by the FFIEC agencies and HUD; the credit-bureau panels were built by the regulators or by data vendors the regulators contract with. The internal version almost always has a variable your public version does not.
- The posture I learned in front of HUD: name the variable you wish you had, name the agency that has it, frame the limitation as *your* honest constraint rather than *their* publication failure, and ask the audience whether the result would survive on the internal version. *"The public HMDA fields don't carry applicant credit history; HUD's internal join with credit-bureau data does. I'd love to know — and I think it's the most important follow-up question — whether the gap survives once that channel is conditioned on."*
- This single move converts the room from defending-against-you to thinking-with-you. It also, in three of my own experiences, opened the door to a data-access conversation that academic posturing would have closed.

**Slide 4 — The "what should we do?" question.**
- This question will be asked. Refusing to engage it ("I'm just a researcher") makes you useless to the room. Answering it with a policy prescription your design cannot support makes you irresponsible to the literature. Both are failure modes; the good answer threads between them.
- The three-part shape: *(a) what my design **can** speak to* — usually a direction or a magnitude under stated assumptions; *(b) what it **cannot** speak to* — usually the mechanism, the long-run, or the welfare calculus; *(c) the named follow-up* — the question whose answer would actually inform the policy choice the room cares about.
- Concretely: *"My design says programs of the type adopted in 2015–2022 are associated with a narrower denial gap, under parallel trends. It does not say which feature of the program — the examination intensity, the publication of findings, the regulatory follow-up — is doing the work. The follow-up I'd most want funded is a within-program decomposition; that's the question whose answer would change the design of the next program."* That is research talking to policy without overreaching.
- The lesson I learned, repeatedly: the regulator who walks away saying *"we can use this, but here's what we still need to know"* is the one who calls you back. The one who walks away with *"this researcher told us to do X"* is the one who never trusted you in the first place and never will.

**Slide 5 — The irresponsible quote.**
- The single most useful exercise before any policy talk is to write the irresponsible-but-defensible quote — the sentence a journalist could extract from your work that you would not be proud of but could not call a misquote. Until you have written it, you do not know where your headline slide is leaking.
- For our 2019 fair-lending paper, the irresponsible quote would have been a version that dropped the *conditional* clauses — the ones about which markets, which periods, and which segment of applicants the comparison was clean for — and left a sentence that sounded like a *universal* statement about discrimination. The defense was a slide whose headline sentence *included* the conditional clauses in the same breath as the magnitude, so that a quote that dropped them would have to be a quote *out of context* rather than a quote of the slide.
- The duty is structural, not personal. You will not catch every misquote, and that is fine. The duty is to have written the slide such that the *honest* sentence is the easier sentence to extract — to make irresponsibility require effort. That is what publication-with-stakes asks of you, and it is what the rehearsal in Lab 9 did not have time to teach.

## (d) Three stretch questions — defending a published fair-lending paper in front of HUD, the Fed, and Congress

These tie today's theme to one of my own papers, used as a live worked case. The citation, used verbatim:

> Gao, L., & Sun, R. (2019). Lending fairness in U.S. mortgage markets. *Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences*, 116(19), 9293–9302.

The paper studies the **lending denial gap** between minority and white applicants in U.S. mortgage markets, using HMDA loan-application records. It is the right case for today for three reasons. First, the data are HMDA — built by HUD and the FFIEC, the agencies most likely to be in the room. Second, the result has direct policy implications: any number about racial disparities in lending feeds, sooner or later, into a regulatory or Congressional conversation about fair-lending enforcement. Third, I gave versions of this talk at HUD, the Federal Reserve, and to Congressional staff in the two years after publication, so the lessons below are not hypothetical — they are what I learned by getting some things right and some things wrong.

**Reason about how a published paper presents differently in policy rooms — not about what the paper specifically found. Do NOT invent or recite any specific reported magnitudes, coefficients, sample sizes, or named control variables from the paper. Frame every answer as "how I would present" or "what I would say"; mark anything you would need to confirm against the published version as `[CHECK]`.**

1. **The HUD room — credit them for building the data you used.** Imagine you are presenting Gao & Sun (2019) in 2020 at HUD's Office of Policy Development and Research, in a conference room of roughly twelve staff including one career economist who has worked on HMDA for fifteen years. The chair introduces you. You have thirty minutes. (a) Your *first slide change* relative to the academic version of the talk: identify the one sentence that you would *add* to slide 1 or slide 2 to credit the agency for building the dataset that made the research possible — and explain why that single sentence, said early, changes the *tone* of every question you will get afterward. (b) Now the data-limitation move: the public HMDA fields are famously missing some controls a fair-lending regulator would want — applicant credit characteristics, debt-to-income detail. Without citing any magnitude from the paper, draft the *sentence* you would use to name that limitation in the HUD room in a way that frames it as your constraint, not their failure, and that explicitly invites the agency's internal version of the data as the follow-up. (c) After your talk, the senior career economist asks: *"Are you saying our examination programs work?"* In one or two sentences each, write the **bluff** answer (a clean "yes"), the **evasive** answer ("I'm just a researcher"), and the **honest, useful** answer that names what your design can speak to and what it cannot. Why does the honest answer, even though it is the least quotable, get you invited back?

2. **The Federal Reserve room — the audience may know your method better than you do.** Now imagine you are at the Federal Reserve Board, presenting in a seminar room of ten people, three of whom have published their own fair-lending papers, one of whom built the call-report data you would have used if you had access. The clock is the same; the audience is sharper. (a) In the *academic* talk, the design slide is the spine and you spend the most time there. In the Fed room, you might spend *less* time on the design slide — not because the design matters less, but because of *who is listening*. Explain in one sentence why, and what you spend the *extra* minute on instead. (b) Draft the sentence you would use to *invite* the Fed economists to think about what your design would look like on their internal data — not as a request for access (which would be premature) but as a research framing. (Hint: the move is "the question I'd most want answered on the data you have is …" rather than "I wish I had your data.") (c) One Fed economist asks the question that scares me most about presenting at the Fed: *"Have you thought about how your design interacts with the differences in regulator behavior across the Fed districts?"* You do not have a clean answer because the paper did not study district-level variation. Following Mentor 8's *located-"I-don't-know"* rule, draft your two-sentence answer that admits the gap, names the design feature you would need to add to address it, and turns the question into a follow-up rather than a wound.

3. **The Congressional-staff room — one quotable sentence, one deadline.** Now the hardest room. You are presenting to four Congressional staffers from two committees in a thirty-minute briefing. They have read your abstract; they will not remember your method; they need one sentence each to put in a memo by Friday. None of them is hostile, but none of them has time. (a) Draft your **headline sentence** — the one you put on your headline slide and read out loud once — in a form that contains the conditional clauses (which markets, which years, which applicants) in the *same breath* as the magnitude, so that a memo that quotes the sentence cannot drop the conditionals without committing a visible truncation. Without using a fabricated magnitude, sketch the *shape* of that sentence — what its clauses are and in what order. (b) Now do the exercise from slide 5: write the *irresponsible-but-defensible* quote — the one a staffer could plausibly extract from your talk and put in a partisan memo, that you would *not* be proud of but could not legally call a misquote. Explain which clause they dropped, and what specific slide-design change would have made that drop visibly dishonest rather than merely selective. (c) A staffer asks the *"what should we do?"* question. Without prescribing a specific policy your design cannot support, draft your three-part answer (what the design speaks to / what it does not / the named follow-up) in the form of a 45-second spoken response — and explain why this is the answer that gets you cited *correctly* in their memo, when the prescriptive answer would have gotten you cited *misleadingly*.

## (e) Post-session reflection prompt

*Write ~250–350 words after the session. This is the one that goes with you into your conference talk on August 14 and — if your paper is good enough — into whatever policy room your work eventually reaches.*

Write the **policy-stakes brief** for your own capstone. The lab in Week 9 had you build the bundle for the academic audience; this brief is the brief you would carry into the *next* room, the one that includes the people who own the data and the people who write the memos. Three paragraphs, in this order.

First, **the audience-mapping paragraph.** Name one specific real institution that, if your result is right, would have an interest in hearing it. For Maya's fair-lending project, that is HUD's Office of Policy Development and Research or the CFPB; for Priya's climate-insurance work, FEMA or a state insurance commissioner; for Devon's crypto project, the SEC's Office of the Chief Economist or FinCEN; for Sam's market work, a stock-exchange regulator like the SEC's Division of Trading and Markets; for Leah's patent-text work, the USPTO Office of the Chief Economist. Then write the sentence you would add to slide 1 of your conference talk to credit that institution for building the data you used, in the form of a real sentence you would actually say out loud.

Second, **the irresponsible-quote paragraph.** Following slide 5's exercise, write your headline sentence with its conditional clauses *in the same breath as the magnitude*, then write the irresponsible-but-defensible quote a journalist or staffer could extract from a talk that omitted those clauses. Identify which slide in your current deck (from Lab 9) makes the dropping of those clauses *easy*, and describe in one sentence the specific design change you will make before the August 14 conference to make that drop visibly dishonest rather than merely selective.

Third, **the "what should we do?" paragraph.** Draft your three-part answer to that question on your own result: what your design *can* speak to, what it *cannot*, and the named follow-up question whose answer would actually inform the policy choice the audience cares about. Resist the temptation to either prescribe a policy or evade the question; thread the needle in three sentences, as someone whose paper has stakes and whose obligation is to be useful without overreaching.

Close with one sentence — not for the brief, but for yourself. Six years after Gao & Sun (2019), the sentence I would write here is *"the irresponsible quote I worried about did get written, and the slide that made it harder to extract is the reason I am still happy with the paper."* Write the one-sentence version of that for yourself. If the version is honest, you have not just a paper — you have a result you would present to anyone, in any room, on any day, and trust to mean what you said.
