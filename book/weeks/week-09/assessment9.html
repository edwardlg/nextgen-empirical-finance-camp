Week 9 Assessment — The Symposium Performance

This is the conference-week grade. It is graded as a single 100-point exercise on the artifact bundle Lab 9 had you build — slides, poster, packet, talk recording, ledger — plus a written self-assessment that reads the ledger back to you and says, honestly, what landed, what didn’t, and what the Week-10 revision sprint will fix. The capstone defense in Week 8 graded whether your paper could survive a referee on paper. Week 9 grades whether your result survives a room — whether the eight minutes you spent in front of a panel that had read the paper produced a defense a stranger watching the recording would believe.

The standard is the one Week 8 set and Week 9 operationalizes: a modest result you defended honestly in a room outscores an ambitious one whose defense bluffed. A speaker who answered three of four hard questions from prepared threats-table rows, conceded the one fatal critique honestly, and wrote a self-assessment that named the bluff they noticed mid-sentence earns more than one who delivered a polished talk with no concession in the Q&A. Hold yourself to CONVENTIONS §4 (every spec stated in seven-slot form, in your deck and your poster) and §5 (the packet rebuilds on a fresh env, no secret anywhere) throughout — those are not extra credit, they are the floor.

Before you submit, watch one talk from the conference gallery in book/appendices/F-conference-gallery/ (or the placeholder in book/capstones/ if F has not been populated yet — [CHECK]): three finished student talks with their bundles and their ledgers. Watch one not for the result but for the seams — when the speaker stopped, when the panel landed, when the answer was prepared and when it was improvised but solid, when the speaker conceded and the room nodded. That is the bar you are clearing.

How you submit

The five artifacts of the bundle are one thing seen from five sides: the slides are the argument, the poster is the argument compressed to a glance, the packet is the proof, the recording is the moment the paper became a result, and the ledger is the audit trail. You submit them together as the bundle/ folder inside the capstone repository you have been committing into since Lab 7, plus a single bundle/SELF_ASSESSMENT.md file written after the symposium day. The bundle’s manifest.json is the bundle’s table of contents — a grader cloning the repo runs make clean && make bundle first, then verifies every artifact’s SHA-256 against the manifest, then opens SELF_ASSESSMENT.md last. There is no separate upload: the repository is the submission, the bundle/ is the deliverable inside it, and the artifacts in bundle/ were built by code, never copied by hand.

The five deliverables

D1 — bundle/slides.pdf (the 8-minute deck)

The same six-beat deck from Chapter 8.5 — question → why it matters → design/identification → headline result → robustness → contribution — exported to PDF, one idea per slide, every result spoken aloud rendered as a figure (point-with-error-bar, coefficient plot, event-study, RD binned scatter), with the design slide as the spine. The deck delivered in the symposium was timed to 8:00 or less with a pre-decided cut line. The PDF copy in the bundle is the same deck — no post-hoc edits to “polish” what was actually said in the room.

D2 — bundle/poster.pdf (the one-page parallel artifact)

A single-page poster on a 36”×48” sheet (or the single-column “billboard” equivalent — [CHECK] Morrison 2019 layout per Lab 9 §3.1), built by Markdown + pandoc or LaTeX + tikzposter, that pulls the same \input{}ed tables and \includegraphics{}ed figures the paper pulls. The headline figure dominates ~40% of the visual weight, the QR-code-to-repo is in the takeaway panel, and there is zero text the eye is supposed to read — every sentence is load-bearing or it is not on the poster. The seven-slot specification from CONVENTIONS §4 appears in the design panel, abbreviated to fit but complete.

D3 — bundle/packet.zip (the replication packet, frozen)

The Lab 8 packet zipped via git archive HEAD — never by hand-zipping a folder, because hand-zipping ships local junk. On unzip, the packet/ directory is the capstone repository at the commit you presented from; make clean && make all rebuilds the paper PDF from raw bytes through the seeded code, and make clean && make bundle rebuilds this same bundle. The packet’s commit hash matches the manifest.json’s commit hash; a grader who cloned a different commit would see the mismatch immediately.

D4 — bundle/talk.mp4 or bundle/talk_transcript.md (the recording)

The phone-on-a-chair recording of the 8-minute talk plus the 3-minute Q&A, or — if recording was infeasible — a peer-typed Markdown transcript of the same. The recording is not graded on production value; it is graded on whether a stranger watching it sees the talk you said you gave. The talk-delivery rubric row (below) is scored on this artifact, so a missing or unwatchable recording caps that row at Developing.

D5 — bundle/ledger.csv + bundle/SELF_ASSESSMENT.md (the audit and the reading)

The ledger CSV is your rows from the cohort’s ledger/ledger.csv — the timestamped checkpoints, the questions logged by family and fatal/survivable, the answers tagged prepared / improvised / concede / deflect / bluff. The self-assessment is a 600–900-word Markdown file you write after the symposium day, with four short sections fixed in advance:

  1. Three numbers, three sentences each. Total questions received; how many were fatal-class (identification or alternative-explanation); how many of your answers were tagged prepared. One sentence on what each number means about your defense, in plain English.
  2. One landed answer, one missed answer. Quote, in your own words, one question whose answer came from a threats-table row and landed — the panel visibly accepted it. Then quote one question where your answer was tagged improvised, deflect, or bluff, and write the prepared answer you wish you had given. Be specific; the grader is reading for whether you can see the difference.
  3. The Week-10 revision target. Name the one concrete change you will make to the paper, the packet, or the deck in the Week-10 revision sprint that responds directly to the panel’s most useful question. “Better motivation slide” is not concrete; “add a Callaway–Sant’Anna estimator as a robustness column, because Panelist B pointed out my staggered-treatment design assumes homogeneous effects” is concrete.
  4. The honest sentence. One sentence you can now say truthfully about your own result that you could not have said before the symposium. This is the sentence that closes the arc.

The self-assessment is what makes the bundle a graded artifact rather than a stack of files. Skipping it caps the writing-and-reflection portion at Developing on its own.

The self-assessment is graded inside two rows, not its own line: its honest reading of the ledger informs the Q&A handling row (specifically, whether a bluff was acknowledged), and its concrete Week-10 revision target informs the slides row (does the speaker see what their deck did not yet say?). Instructor guidance and grading key

This is an open-ended performance assessment on each student’s own project, so the key is a set of standards, anchor moves, and the bright lines that separate the levels — not an answer sheet.

The three highest-signal things to grade, in order:

  1. Watch the recording end-to-end. The single most objective row in the rubric is talk delivery, and it is graded on the recording, not on the deck. A speaker who hit the clock, paced to the §3.1 shape, and spoke the headline in plain English with conditional clauses intact is at Excellent before any other row is touched. A speaker who was cut off at 8:00 or read the slides aloud cannot be at Excellent regardless of how clean the bundle looks.

  2. Run make clean && make bundle on a fresh clone. This is the reproducibility row, and it is binary: the bundle either rebuilds or it does not. A bundle that rebuilds with a hand-tweak is Developing, not Proficient — the whole point of the manifest is that a stranger can verify the bundle without intervention. A bundle with a hard-coded secret is Missing on its own per CONVENTIONS §5, regardless of how good the talk was.

  3. Read the ledger CSV alongside the self-assessment. The defense row is graded on concordance between what the ledger logged and what the self-assessment says. A ledger that flagged two bluffs and a self-assessment that names zero is graded down for dishonesty (the speaker did not read their own ledger honestly), not for the bluffs themselves — a bluff named in the self-assessment is half-credit; a bluff hidden is no credit and signals a grading concern that carries into Week 10.

How to grade fairly. Grade the performance the student delivered, not the talk you would have given. A clean fair-lending talk that hit the clock, defended three of three questions from prepared threats-table rows, and named one concrete Week-10 revision is an A performance; a more ambitious talk that ran long, bluffed an alternative-explanation question, and wrote a self-assessment claiming “the talk went great” is not. Calibrate against the gallery: the three finished talks in book/appendices/F-conference-gallery/ ([CHECK] while gallery is populated) are worked anchors at the A line. Grade each row independently against the level descriptors, then sum.

The line between an A and a B performance. Both delivered the talk, ran the packet, fielded the questions. The difference is honest reading of the room. The A performance has a recording showing the speaker hit the clock and spoke the headline in memo-ready English; a packet that rebuilds the bundle byte-for-byte with the manifest hashes intact; a Q&A in which at least two answers came from prepared threats-table rows and any fatal critique was conceded with the four-beat located answer; and a self-assessment that names one bluff (because everyone has at least one in eight minutes of speech), one panelist question that landed in a way the deck did not yet anticipate, and one concrete Week-10 revision that responds to it. The B performance has a clean talk that ran 30 seconds long, a packet that needed one hand-tweak to rebuild, a Q&A with one prepared answer and two improvised-but-solid ones, and a self-assessment that is honest but vague about what Week 10 fixes. The B is not dishonest — it simply has not yet internalized that the symposium’s job is to find out what the next revision should fix, and the speaker who shows up to the conference already knowing has not used the conference.

Common failure modes, the classic Week-9 ways to lose points:

  1. Cut off by the chair — the talk was not delivered, it was interrupted. Caps talk delivery at Developing regardless of the rest. The fix is the pre-decided cut line from Lab 8 §3.1; if the speaker had no cut line, the recording will show why.

  2. The unread slide — a slide the speaker spoke past because it was running long. The recording shows this clearly. If the unread slide is the design slide or the caveat slide, the talk-delivery row caps at Developing because the spine of the argument was not in the room.

  3. The headline in jargon — a result slide that says “\hat\beta = -1.8, p < 0.01” instead of “the gap narrowed by about 1.8 percentage points after the program was adopted, under parallel trends.” This is the Mentor 9 §c slide 5 failure: the irresponsible quote is now the only quote available to extract, because the honest version was not on the slide.

  4. The hand-zipped packet — a packet.zip built by selecting files in the file manager and right-clicking “compress,” instead of by git archive HEAD. The file manager ships local junk (the .DS_Store, the editor backups, the half-finished notes.md) and misses git-tracked files in subtrees. The fix is in Lab 9 §4.2; the grader’s tell is a packet/ directory with no .git info and stray files.

  5. The ledger that lies — a ledger CSV with three prepared answers but a recording showing improvised answers, or a ledger with zero bluff rows but a recording where the speaker visibly invented a number. The grader is watching the recording with the ledger open; this is the easiest concordance to check. Caps the Q&A row at Missing because the audit trail itself was falsified — and that is the one row where falsification is a separate grading concern, not just a rubric category.

  6. The vague self-assessment — three pages of “I learned a lot, the experience was valuable, I will work harder in Week 10.” A self-assessment with no numbers from the ledger, no named question, and no concrete revision target earns zero credit toward the rows it informs and signals to Week 10 that the speaker has not yet learned to read the room.

  7. The missing caveat slide — the talk hit the clock and the headline was memo-ready, but the deck has no slide that says, in plain English, what the result does not support. This is the Mentor 9 §c slide 4 failure: the talk is technically correct but not yet responsible. Caps the slides row at Proficient even if every other slide is perfect.

  8. A hard-coded secret in the packet — caps reproducibility at Missing on its own per CONVENTIONS §5, no exceptions. A .env file in the zip, an API key in 01_pull_data.py, a database password in a notebook — any of these.

How the rubric maps to the publication standard. This assessment is calibrated to the real Phase-2 conference (NextGen, ~Aug 14) and the Schar / NextGen Young Scholars Journal symposium your bundle may be uploaded to. An Excellent across delivery, Q&A handling, and the bundle’s reproducibility is, concretely, a talk that would have survived at the Phase-2 symposium: a deck the chair did not have to cut off, a packet a follow-up reviewer could clone and rebuild, and a defense the panelists nodded along with on the answers that came from prepared threats-table rows. The MARS-deposit standard from Week 8 still applies to the packet inside the bundle: a stranger clones the repo, types make clean && make bundle, and the bundle they see matches the bundle the student submitted, manifest-hash for manifest-hash. Grade to that standard, because that is where the strongest of these bundles are actually headed.

What a top symposium performance looks like, in one paragraph

The speaker walks up, plugs in, takes a breath, and says the question — the same one the abstract says, but in plain English. The motivation lands in 90 seconds with a fact and a stake; the data slide credits the agency that built the dataset by name (Mentor 9’s HUD move, transposed to whatever institution owns the data); the design slide states the seven-slot specification and the identifying-assumption sentence in one breath; the headline slide says the result with its conditional clauses in the same sentence as the magnitude — memo-ready, irresponsible-quote-proof; the robustness slide shows a spec curve with the pre-registered point marked; the contribution slide makes the verb match the design, with no causal-language drift; the caveat slide says, in plain English, what the result does not support. The chair calls time at 7:50 by accident; the speaker had ten seconds of slack. The panel asks two questions; both come from the threats table; both answers are tagged prepared; the residual concern is named without flinching. The room asks the “what should we do?” question; the speaker answers in three parts (what the design speaks to, what it cannot, the named follow-up). The recording shows a talk; the ledger logs three prepared answers, zero bluffs, one concession. The bundle on disk rebuilds clean. The self-assessment names the one question whose answer was solid but improvised, and the one revision Week 10 will make in response, and ends with the sentence that closes the arc: “I presented an original empirical result in front of a panel that had read the paper, defended it without bluffing, and now know exactly what the next revision needs to fix.” That is the A performance. That is the conference. That is the room.

**Total: 100 points.** The rows sum to **25 + 20 + 20 + 20 + 15 = 100**. The two heaviest rows — slides (25) and the three reproducibility-or-defense rows tied at 20 — reflect what conference performance is: the artifact you *show*, the artifact you *give*, the artifact you *defend*. Q&A handling at 15 is weighted lower than delivery at 20 because the room can forgive a fumbled answer the speaker later locates honestly in the self-assessment; the room cannot forgive a deck that did not say the thing the speaker meant to say.

**The self-assessment is graded inside two rows**, not its own line: its honest reading of the ledger informs the Q&A handling row (specifically, whether a bluff was acknowledged), and its concrete Week-10 revision target informs the slides row (does the speaker see what their deck did not yet say?). A self-assessment that names a bluff the ledger flagged earns the speaker back part of the Q&A points; a self-assessment that lists vague revisions ("more robustness") rather than concrete ones ("the Callaway–Sant'Anna estimator for the staggered design") earns nothing extra.

## Instructor guidance and grading key

This is an open-ended performance assessment on each student's own project, so the key is a set of *standards*, anchor moves, and the bright lines that separate the levels — not an answer sheet.

**The three highest-signal things to grade**, in order:

(1) **Watch the recording end-to-end.** The single most objective row in the rubric is *talk delivery*, and it is graded on the recording, not on the deck. A speaker who hit the clock, paced to the §3.1 shape, and spoke the headline in plain English with conditional clauses intact is at Excellent before any other row is touched. A speaker who was cut off at 8:00 or read the slides aloud cannot be at Excellent regardless of how clean the bundle looks.

(2) **Run `make clean && make bundle` on a fresh clone.** This is the reproducibility row, and it is binary: the bundle either rebuilds or it does not. A bundle that rebuilds *with a hand-tweak* is Developing, not Proficient — the whole point of the manifest is that a stranger can verify the bundle without intervention. A bundle with a hard-coded secret is Missing on its own per CONVENTIONS §5, regardless of how good the talk was.

(3) **Read the ledger CSV alongside the self-assessment.** The defense row is graded on *concordance* between what the ledger logged and what the self-assessment says. A ledger that flagged two bluffs and a self-assessment that names zero is graded down for *dishonesty* (the speaker did not read their own ledger honestly), not for the bluffs themselves — a bluff named in the self-assessment is half-credit; a bluff hidden is no credit and signals a grading concern that carries into Week 10.

**How to grade fairly.** Grade the *performance* the student delivered, not the talk you would have given. A clean fair-lending talk that hit the clock, defended three of three questions from prepared threats-table rows, and named one concrete Week-10 revision is an A performance; a more *ambitious* talk that ran long, bluffed an alternative-explanation question, and wrote a self-assessment claiming "the talk went great" is not. Calibrate against the gallery: the three finished talks in `book/appendices/F-conference-gallery/` (`[CHECK]` while gallery is populated) are worked anchors at the A line. Grade each row independently against the level descriptors, then sum.

**The line between an A and a B performance.** Both delivered the talk, ran the packet, fielded the questions. The difference is *honest reading of the room*. The A performance has a recording showing the speaker hit the clock and spoke the headline in memo-ready English; a packet that rebuilds the bundle byte-for-byte with the manifest hashes intact; a Q&A in which at least two answers came from prepared threats-table rows and any fatal critique was conceded with the four-beat located answer; and a self-assessment that names one bluff (because everyone has at least one in eight minutes of speech), one panelist question that landed in a way the deck did not yet anticipate, and one *concrete* Week-10 revision that responds to it. The B performance has a clean talk that ran 30 seconds long, a packet that needed one hand-tweak to rebuild, a Q&A with one prepared answer and two improvised-but-solid ones, and a self-assessment that is honest but vague about what Week 10 fixes. The B is not dishonest — it simply has not yet internalized that the symposium's job is to *find out what the next revision should fix*, and the speaker who shows up to the conference already knowing has not used the conference.

**Common failure modes**, the classic Week-9 ways to lose points:

(a) *Cut off by the chair* — the talk was not delivered, it was interrupted. Caps *talk delivery* at Developing regardless of the rest. The fix is the pre-decided cut line from Lab 8 §3.1; if the speaker had no cut line, the recording will show why.

(b) *The unread slide* — a slide the speaker spoke *past* because it was running long. The recording shows this clearly. If the unread slide is the design slide or the caveat slide, the talk-delivery row caps at Developing because the spine of the argument was not in the room.

(c) *The headline in jargon* — a result slide that says "$\hat\beta = -1.8, p < 0.01$" instead of "the gap narrowed by about 1.8 percentage points after the program was adopted, under parallel trends." This is the Mentor 9 §c slide 5 failure: the irresponsible quote is now the *only* quote available to extract, because the honest version was not on the slide.

(d) *The hand-zipped packet* — a `packet.zip` built by selecting files in the file manager and right-clicking "compress," instead of by `git archive HEAD`. The file manager ships local junk (the `.DS_Store`, the editor backups, the half-finished `notes.md`) and misses git-tracked files in subtrees. The fix is in Lab 9 §4.2; the grader's tell is a `packet/` directory with no `.git` info and stray files.

(e) *The ledger that lies* — a ledger CSV with three `prepared` answers but a recording showing improvised answers, or a ledger with zero `bluff` rows but a recording where the speaker visibly invented a number. The grader is watching the recording with the ledger open; this is the easiest concordance to check. Caps the Q&A row at Missing because the audit trail itself was falsified — and that is the one row where falsification is a separate grading concern, not just a rubric category.

(f) *The vague self-assessment* — three pages of "I learned a lot, the experience was valuable, I will work harder in Week 10." A self-assessment with no numbers from the ledger, no named question, and no concrete revision target earns zero credit toward the rows it informs and signals to Week 10 that the speaker has not yet learned to read the room.

(g) *The missing caveat slide* — the talk hit the clock and the headline was memo-ready, but the deck has no slide that says, in plain English, *what the result does not support*. This is the Mentor 9 §c slide 4 failure: the talk is technically correct but not yet *responsible*. Caps the slides row at Proficient even if every other slide is perfect.

(h) *A hard-coded secret in the packet* — caps reproducibility at Missing on its own per CONVENTIONS §5, no exceptions. A `.env` file in the zip, an API key in `01_pull_data.py`, a database password in a notebook — any of these.

**How the rubric maps to the publication standard.** This assessment is calibrated to the real Phase-2 conference (NextGen, ~Aug 14) and the Schar / NextGen Young Scholars Journal symposium your bundle may be uploaded to. An Excellent across delivery, Q&A handling, and the bundle's reproducibility is, concretely, a talk that would have survived at the Phase-2 symposium: a deck the chair did not have to cut off, a packet a follow-up reviewer could clone and rebuild, and a defense the panelists nodded along with on the answers that came from prepared threats-table rows. The MARS-deposit standard from Week 8 still applies to the packet inside the bundle: a stranger clones the repo, types `make clean && make bundle`, and the bundle they see matches the bundle the student submitted, manifest-hash for manifest-hash. Grade to that standard, because that is where the strongest of these bundles are actually headed.

### What a top symposium performance looks like, in one paragraph

The speaker walks up, plugs in, takes a breath, and says the question — the same one the abstract says, but in plain English. The motivation lands in 90 seconds with a fact and a stake; the data slide credits the agency that built the dataset by name (Mentor 9's HUD move, transposed to whatever institution owns the data); the design slide states the seven-slot specification and the identifying-assumption sentence in one breath; the headline slide says the result with its conditional clauses in the same sentence as the magnitude — memo-ready, irresponsible-quote-proof; the robustness slide shows a spec curve with the pre-registered point marked; the contribution slide makes the verb match the design, with no causal-language drift; the caveat slide says, in plain English, what the result does *not* support. The chair calls time at 7:50 by accident; the speaker had ten seconds of slack. The panel asks two questions; both come from the threats table; both answers are tagged `prepared`; the residual concern is named without flinching. The room asks the *"what should we do?"* question; the speaker answers in three parts (what the design speaks to, what it cannot, the named follow-up). The recording shows a talk; the ledger logs three prepared answers, zero bluffs, one concession. The bundle on disk rebuilds clean. The self-assessment names the one question whose answer was solid but improvised, and the one revision Week 10 will make in response, and ends with the sentence that closes the arc: *"I presented an original empirical result in front of a panel that had read the paper, defended it without bluffing, and now know exactly what the next revision needs to fix."* That is the A performance. That is the conference. That is the room.
