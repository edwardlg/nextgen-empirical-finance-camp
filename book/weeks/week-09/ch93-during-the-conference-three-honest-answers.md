# Ch 9.3 — During the Conference: Reading the Room, Handling Hostile Questions, the Three Honest Answers

The talk ended forty-five seconds ago. The polite applause has finished. The
chair says, "*we have time for questions*," and a hand goes up in row three.
What you do in the next nine minutes — the question period — is where the
talk gets graded, and it is graded by a different standard than the talk
itself. The talk tested whether you could *present* the work; the questions
test whether you can *defend* it. And the defense is not a re-litigation of
what you already said. It is a sustained, in-public demonstration that you
know exactly where your design is strong, exactly where it is weak, and
exactly which is which. The reveal of this chapter — and you will need it
in the first thirty seconds — is that **the question period is not about
having all the answers; it is about having *honest, located, useful* answers
to the questions that matter, and a graceful way to handle the ones that
don't.**

Here is the move the chapter turns on. **There are three honest answers to
*any* hostile question, and a great defender uses all three on the same
afternoon: "I don't know," "It's in the paper," "Good point — and here's
what I did about it."** The amateur tries to use only the third, because
the third sounds smartest. The professional knows that the first two are
just as important, sometimes more so, because they are how you *budget your
credibility* across nine minutes of incoming fire. This chapter teaches you
to use all three, and to know — live, in the moment — which question
deserves which answer.

We organize the chapter around three skills, each of which improves
independently with practice: reading the room (so you know what *kind* of
question is coming before the question fully lands), the hostile-question
taxonomy (so you can classify each question into one of a small number of
buckets, each with a script), and the three honest answers (the scripts
themselves, with worked examples for each).

---

## 1. Reading the room: the signals that arrive before the question does

Most students treat the question period as an unbroken sequence of
surprises: a hand goes up, a question arrives, you respond, repeat. That
framing is wrong, and it is the source of the panicked-improv mode that
makes Q&A go badly. The truth is that **questions arrive with a half-second
of pre-context**, and reading the pre-context tells you most of what you
need to know about the question before you have to answer it. The skill is
not magical; it is the same skill a teacher uses to read a classroom or a
trader uses to read a tape. You learn it by being told what to look at, and
then by looking.

What to look at, before you take the question.

**Who is asking.** The chair acknowledges the questioner; you have
two-to-three seconds while they stand up or unmute. If you know the
questioner's research area — and at a conference you usually can recognize a
name tag, a face from yesterday's session, or the chair's introduction —
you have an immediate prior on the *kind* of question. A questioner who
works on the same data you used will ask about data construction; a
questioner who works on the same identification strategy will ask about
threats; a questioner from outside your field will ask about generalizability
or motivation. Knowing the prior is not the same as knowing the question,
but it puts you in the *right neighborhood* by the time the first sentence
lands, which buys you time you do not otherwise have.

**Tone before content.** The first half-second of a question is *vocal*, not
*verbal*. A genuinely curious questioner opens with rising intonation —
"*I'm just curious about…*"; a skeptical one opens with descending,
declarative intonation — "*I'm not sure I follow how…*"; a hostile one
opens *flat*, with no warm-up. The tone is the *register* of the question,
and the register tells you whether the questioner is helping or probing.
Both deserve respectful answers; they deserve *different* respectful
answers, and the difference is signaled in the first half-second. Match
the register: a curious question gets a slightly longer, more exploratory
answer; a probing question gets a tighter, more precise one. Mismatching
the register — answering a curious question with hostile precision, or a
hostile question with curious meandering — is a small but real failure
mode, and it accumulates across nine minutes.

**Body language and the room.** Look up, not at the screen, when the
question is being asked. The questioner's posture (leaning in vs. leaning
back), the chair's posture (whether they are about to intervene), and the
other audience members' attention (whether they are nodding along or
visibly disagreeing) all carry information. A question that the *rest of
the room* is also nodding to is a question you need to answer well, because
the room is also waiting for your answer; a question that is idiosyncratic
to one questioner — the rest of the room looks puzzled — can be handled
more briefly and you can offer to follow up afterward.

**The first noun.** The very first noun in a question is usually the
question's *target*. "*Your standard errors…*" — they are attacking the
inference; you need to know which cluster-level you used. "*Your control
group…*" — they are attacking the comparison; you need the design slide
ready. "*The mechanism…*" — they are asking what's behind the result; you
need the narrative answer. "*The relevance to…*" — they are attacking the
contribution; you need the so-what. By the time the rest of the sentence
arrives, your hand should already be at the page in the deck where the
relevant backup slide lives. This is what backup-slide navigation (Ch 9.2
§7) is *for* — the noun tells you where to go, and the deck has the page.

A practical drill, useful in the pre-flight: have a peer ask you ten
questions in rapid succession, and *write down* — before answering — the
first noun in each. Did the noun correctly forecast where the question was
going? After a few sessions, the noun becomes a reflex, and reflexes are
what survive nerves.

A note on the chair. The session chair's job is to manage time and to
intervene if a question becomes abusive — though abusive questions are
rare and almost always inappropriate to a venue where the speakers are
students. Treat the chair as an ally. If a question is genuinely unclear,
look at the chair before answering and the chair will often ask the
questioner to rephrase; if you are running out of time, the chair will
politely close the line. You do not have to manage the room alone. The
chair is in the room for exactly this reason.

## 2. The hostile-question taxonomy

Most empirical questions, hostile or otherwise, fall into a small number of
buckets. Knowing the buckets is what lets you classify a question in the
first second and respond from a script in the next eight. The buckets:

**Bucket A: number-choice questions** ("*did you cluster by industry?*",
"*why winsorize at 1% not 5%?*", "*what if you drop the 2008-09 crisis?*").
These attack a *choice* in the analysis. The right answer is usually a
robustness pointer: *"yes, we ran that on slide 6 / in Appendix B.2; the
result is within 0.3 standard errors of the headline."* These are
**survivable critiques** in the sense of Chapter 8.5 §4 — they ask whether
the result moves under a different choice, and you (almost always, if
Chapter 8.2 was done honestly) have already checked.

**Bucket B: identification-attack questions** ("*your treated and control
groups were diverging before treatment*", "*your instrument plausibly affects
the outcome directly*", "*the same cutoff also switches eligibility for
program X*"). These attack the *comparison*, not the choice. They are
**fatal-if-right**, in the sense that if the questioner is correct, no
robustness check rescues you, because the design itself was never clean.
These are the questions where the threats table from Chapter 7.5 earns its
keep — every Bucket B question is, by construction, a row of that table.
The answer template is: *yes-fair-did-residual*, as in Chapter 9.1 §3.

**Bucket C: mechanism / "why" questions** ("*what's driving the effect?*",
"*through what channel?*", "*is this about [X] or [Y]?*"). These ask you to
narrate the *story* behind the number. They are not attacks; they are
invitations to tell the room what you think is going on. The right answer
is the *mechanism sentence* from your paper's discussion section, said in
plain English: *"the channel we think is operating is [mechanism]; we see
two pieces of evidence consistent with that — [a] and [b]; what we can't
rule out is [alternative channel], and distinguishing the two is what
[future work / a follow-up paper / a different design] would do."* These
questions are gifts — the questioner is interested — and they should be
answered warmly.

**Bucket D: generalization questions** ("*does this hold in [other setting]?*",
"*would you expect this in [other market / period]?*", "*how externally
valid is this?*"). These ask whether your result *extends* beyond your
sample. The right answer is a careful one: *"my data is [X], so I genuinely
can't speak directly to [Y]; the mechanism I identify is [M], and I'd
expect it to matter wherever [precondition] holds, which suggests
[direction] for [Y] — but I wouldn't extend the estimate without re-running
it there."* Notice the structure: *what your data covers, what mechanism
travels, what the prediction would be if you had to make one, what you'd
need to test it*. That is a far better answer than either "yes, of course"
(naive) or "no, only my sample" (defensive).

**Bucket E: motivation / so-what questions** ("*why does this matter?*",
"*what's the policy implication?*", "*who would care about this result?*").
These attack the *contribution* slide, and they are usually asked when the
contribution slide was weak. The right answer is the *stakes sentence*
from your introduction, said with conviction: *"the headline gap is 12
percentage points, which means [concrete consequence]; that's why the
1.8-point reduction we identify matters — it's a sixth of the gap, and
it's identified to a specific policy lever you could in principle adjust."*
If you cannot make the stakes feel like stakes in one sentence, the talk
itself needs more motivation; the question is signaling that the room did
not feel it.

**Bucket F: data-construction questions** ("*how did you handle missing
values?*", "*how did you merge HMDA to lender characteristics?*", "*what's
the unit of observation?*"). These ask about how the dataset was built.
They are not glamorous, but they are *high-information* questions, because
many empirical results live or die at the merge step (Chapter 7.4). The
right answer is *technically precise*: *"we merge HMDA loan-applications
to Call Report lender-quarters by RSSD ID, with about 92% match rate, and
we drop unmatched observations because we cannot identify the lender's
characteristics; we document the merge in Appendix A and the unmatched
observations look [similar / different] on observables."* Precision here
buys credibility everywhere else.

**Bucket G: methodology-curiosity questions** ("*why this estimator and not
that one?*", "*why OLS and not a non-linear model?*", "*did you consider
[fancier method]?*"). These ask about your method choice. The right answer
is the *parsimony argument*: *"we use the simplest estimator that the
design supports, because the load-bearing assumption is the comparison
(slide 3), not the functional form; the result is essentially identical
under [the more complex method], which we report in Appendix B."* This
answer is strong because it foregrounds what actually matters — the
identification — and treats the estimator choice as the second-order
detail it is.

**Bucket H: out-of-scope questions** ("*what about [a completely different
topic]?*", "*did you look at [unrelated outcome]?*"). These are not hostile;
they are *curious-but-mis-targeted*, and they are the most common questions
at a conference because attendees relate your work to their own. The right
answer is *gracious redirection*: *"that's a great question and I haven't
looked at it — my data doesn't have [the variable], and I'd need [the
extension] to do it justice; but [the question] connects nicely to [a
piece of related work the questioner might know], and I'd love to talk
about it after the session."* This answer is honest, brief, and turns the
question into a hallway conversation (Ch 9.4), which is where most of
those questions belong.

A *taxonomy*, then, of eight buckets. Internalize the buckets, and any
question becomes a classification problem first, an answer problem second.
The classification takes one second; the answer follows from the script.

## 3. "I don't know" — the located version

Of the three honest answers, this is the one students fear most and the one
they need most. The fear is that admitting ignorance damages credibility;
the truth is that admitting ignorance *honestly* — that is, *located*,
*structured*, *useful* — *builds* credibility, because the rest of your
"I know"s become more trustworthy by contrast. A speaker who has admitted
"I don't know" once, well, is a speaker the room believes when they then
say "I do know." A speaker who has bluffed once is a speaker the room
suspects everywhere else, because the room is full of professionals who
have spent careers learning to detect bluffs.

The structure of a good "I don't know" has four parts, said in this order.

**Part one: the admission, plain.** "*I don't know*" — said calmly, at the
top of the answer, before any qualifier. The temptation is to lead with the
qualifier — *"well, in some sense the answer is…"* — and the temptation is
to bury the admission. Don't. Lead with the admission. The room hears
*confidence*, paradoxically, when you say "I don't know" first, because it
is the move of a person who is not afraid of the question.

**Part two: the locator.** *"That's outside what my design can speak to,
because [specific reason]."* This sentence is the difference between a
naive shrug and a professional one. The locator says *I do not know, and I
know why I do not know* — which is itself a form of expertise. The reason
is specific: the data does not cover that range, the variation is not in
the sample, the identification assumption breaks for that subgroup, the
mechanism is plausible but I have no test for it. Whatever the reason, it
is *precise*, because the precision tells the room that you have already
thought about the boundary of your claim.

**Part three: the directional guess (optional but powerful).** *"My guess
is [direction], because [reasoning]."* This sentence is what separates "I
don't know" from "I refuse to engage." You are not making a *claim*; you
are making a *prediction* — and you are flagging it as a prediction by
saying "guess." A questioner who asked "*does your fair-lending effect
extend to auto loans?*" is asked, by your willingness to guess, to engage
with your *intuition* about the mechanism, which is exactly the
intellectual exchange the question wanted. Refusing to guess is a way of
shutting down the conversation; guessing — and flagging the guess as a
guess — keeps it open.

**Part four: the resolution.** *"What I'd want to do to actually answer
that is [specific test / data extension / collaboration]."* This sentence
is the constructive close. You are saying *I do not know, and I know what
would resolve the not-knowing*. The room hears that you are *capable* of
extending the work, even if you have not. Sometimes the right next step is
"a follow-up paper"; sometimes it is "data I'm trying to get access to";
sometimes it is "a different design I would partner with [a domain expert]
on." Whatever it is, it makes the not-knowing *productive* rather than
*terminal*.

Maya's "I don't know," on auto loans:

> *"I don't know. My data is mortgages, so I genuinely can't speak to
> auto lending — the mechanism I identify is examiner attention through
> the HMDA disclosure requirement, and auto lending falls under a
> different supervisor with a different disclosure regime, so I have no
> reason to think the mechanism even applies the same way. My guess is
> that the effect would be smaller in autos because the regulatory
> scrutiny is lower — but I wouldn't bet on the sign without re-running
> it there, and the natural next step would be to get the comparable
> auto-loan data through the Consumer Financial Protection Bureau and
> redo the within-lender comparison."*

That answer is 110 words. It is more credible than "yes, I'd expect the
effect to generalize" (a bluff) and more credible than "I can't comment on
that" (a refusal). It is also a small invitation: the room now knows what
data Maya is looking for, and somebody in the room may have a path to it.
The located "I don't know" is, accidentally, a form of the *ask* from
Chapter 9.2 §6.

## 4. "It's in the paper" — the gracious deflection

This is the answer for questions that are perfectly fair but would, if
fully answered live, eat your remaining Q&A budget. The room has thirty
seconds for an answer; the question requires three minutes; the right move
is to *answer briefly and point to the paper*, where the full treatment
lives. This is not deflection in the bad sense — *I refuse to answer* — but
in the good sense — *here is where the answer is, in the right depth, for
the reader who actually wants it*.

The structure has three parts.

**Part one: a one-sentence partial answer.** Give the *headline of the
answer*, not the full answer. The questioner asks "*how do you handle
missing values?*"; you say "*we impute one outcome variable, the rest are
listwise dropped; the imputed variable's results are essentially identical
to listwise — within a tenth of a standard error.*" That is *the answer*,
compressed.

**Part two: the pointer.** "*Appendix B.3 has the full table and the
imputation diagnostics.*" Be *specific* about where in the paper. "It's in
the paper" is a bad pointer; "Appendix B.3, table B6" is a good pointer.
The specificity is what tells the room you actually know where it is,
which tells them you actually did the analysis, which is the point.

**Part three: the open door.** "*I'm happy to walk through it with anyone
afterward.*" This is the invitation to extend the conversation off-stage,
where it belongs. The room hears *I have more to say*; the questioner
hears *I respect your question enough to discuss it properly*; the chair
hears *we can move on without losing anyone*. Three audiences, one
sentence.

The failure mode for this answer is *using it for the wrong questions*.
You cannot say "it's in the paper" for an identification-attack question
(Bucket B), because the question is exactly the thing the audience needs
*you* to defend live; deflecting it reads as evasion. You also cannot say
it for a so-what question (Bucket E), because the question is about
*motivation* and the motivation needs to be made live, in the room, not
silently in the introduction. *Use the deflection for Buckets A (number
choices), F (data construction), G (methodology), and sometimes the
narrower parts of C (mechanism details).* For Buckets B, D, E — defend
live.

Sam, asked "*why don't you control for size?*" during his momentum talk:

> *"We do — column 3 of Table 3 includes size as a Fama-MacBeth control,
> and the intraday-momentum coefficient is unchanged. Table 3 has the
> four common controls (size, book-to-market, momentum, short-term
> reversal) as separate columns, so you can see each in turn. Happy to
> walk through it afterward if helpful."*

Forty-six words. The question is fully answered for the room; the deep dive
is offered for the questioner. The exchange moves to the next hand. Sam
spent thirty seconds of his question-period budget on a question that
deserved exactly that much.

## 5. "Good point" — the prepared sub-answer

This is the third honest answer, and it is what you say when the question
is a Bucket B question (identification attack) and you have the *exact* row
of your threats table ready. "Good point" is *not* a polite pleasantry; it
is a marker that signals to yourself *I am about to read from the matrix*
and to the room *the questioner has found a real concern, which I have
already thought about*.

The structure is the *fair-did-residual-retreat* template from Chapter 9.1
§3. Every column of the matrix surfaces here, in this order, in the live
answer.

**(Fair) "*That's a fair question, because [reason the threat is real]…*"**
This is column 3 of the matrix. The granting of legitimacy. You are not
bluffing your way past the threat; you are acknowledging it.

**(Did) "*…we tested for it two ways: [test 1] is on slide N / in
Appendix M, [test 2] is in the spec curve…*"** This is column 4. The
evidence. You point to specific slides or appendix tables, because
specificity is the form credibility takes.

**(Residual) "*What I still can't fully rule out is [residual concern],
though [bound on how bad it could be]…*"** This is column 5. The honesty
move. Naming the *residual* concern is what makes the answer credible — it
signals that you know exactly where your defense stops.

**(Retreat, only if asked or only if the threat is fatal) "*If [the
identifying assumption] doesn't hold, the strongest claim that survives is
[the descriptive pattern], which is itself a robust finding worth the
attention.*"** This is column 6. You almost never need to deploy the
retreat sentence — but when the threat is genuinely fatal, the retreat is
the difference between conceding gracefully and being defensive about an
indefensible point.

Priya's "Good point," on whether her wildfire result might be picking up
declining property values:

> *"That's a fair question — counties most exposed to fire are also
> disproportionately rural and losing population, and either alone could
> drive insurer exit. We tested that two ways. On the event-study plot,
> slide 5, the pre-treatment coefficients on insurer exit are flat for
> four years before the fire-risk re-rating, so the divergence dates to
> the rating shock, not before. And in Appendix table A.4, we re-estimate
> dropping counties with population decline above 5% per year; the
> coefficient is within 0.2 standard errors of the headline. What I
> still can't fully rule out is a county-specific shock coincident with
> the rating change — a contemporaneous water-rights ruling, or a
> wildfire-prevention bond election, for instance — that I haven't
> observed. If you don't buy the causal reading, the descriptive
> finding — that insurer concentration collapses within two years of a
> regulatory re-rating in high-fire counties — is robust on its own and
> worth the policy attention."*

This is 170 words, longer than the other two answer templates, but it is
exactly the length the Bucket B question deserves, because Bucket B is the
load-bearing question. The structure is *visible* — *fair, did, residual,
retreat* — and that visibility is itself a credibility move; the room
hears that you have a *system* for answering exactly this question, which
is what they want to hear.

A subtle point on tone. The phrase "good point" is overused in academic
exchange, to the point where it can read as a tic. The *function* — the
marker that says *I take this seriously and have prepared an answer* — can
be carried by many phrases: "that's the central worry, and…", "you've put
your finger on the hardest question for this design…", "I'll grant the
premise — what we did about it is…" — pick the version that fits your
speaking style. The phrase is replaceable; the *move* is not.

## 6. When a fatal critique lands and the attacker is right

Chapter 8.5 §4 introduced this case; this chapter has to live with it,
because it is the case Q&A is hardest. A fatal critique is one that, if the
attacker is right, breaks the *identifying assumption itself* — not a
choice in the analysis, but the comparison the whole paper rests on. When
one lands and you privately recognize the attacker is right, you have a
second to decide between two responses, and they are not equally good.

**The wrong response: defend harder.** The instinct under pressure is to
double down, to find a sub-argument, to invoke a robustness check that
doesn't actually address the threat. This is the worst move in academic
exchange, because the room can *see* it, and what they see is a speaker
who knew the critique was right and chose to obscure it. The reputation
cost is not the broken design; it is the broken integrity, and integrity
is the substrate of every future paper you will ever submit.

**The right response: concede at the level of the critique, and retreat
to what survives.** "*You're right that I can't rule that out with this
design. So the honest reading is that this is a strong association
consistent with the mechanism, not the clean causal estimate I'd hoped
for, and that's a real limitation.*" That sentence is hard to say —
genuinely hard, because saying it publicly costs you the polished claim
you came in with — and it is the *single most credibility-building thing
you can say in the question period*. The room watches a person publicly
prefer truth to defense, and that watching is the single most consequential
moment of your conference. Every senior researcher in the room remembers it.

The retreat to what survives is what Chapter 9.1 §3 set up in column 6 of
the matrix. You do not retreat to *nothing*; you retreat to the *descriptive
claim* that the design does cleanly support. Almost every causal paper has
a descriptive sibling — a pattern in the data, robustly documented, that
does not depend on the identifying assumption. The pattern is, often, a
real publishable contribution in its own right, and treating it as one in
the moment converts a damaged paper into a less-damaged one. Maya, if her
within-lender comparison turned out to be confounded by lender entry/exit
in a way she could not test for, would retreat to: *"the descriptive fact
that the denial gap fell 1.8 points after the regulation is robust to all
the cuts we tried, and that pattern alone — independent of whether
algorithmic intensity is the channel — is worth the policy attention."*
That sentence is what Week 10 begins from. (See Chapter 9.5 §3 for how
the conceded critique converts into a revision plan.)

The discipline before you walk in: **know your retreat position for each
Bucket B row of the matrix.** Column 6 exists *exactly* for the case where
you have to retreat under fire. If you fill column 6 in calm, in advance,
the retreat is something you *read*, in stress, rather than *invent*. The
amateur invents under stress and invents badly; the professional reads
from a script they wrote when they were calm.

## 7. Tone, breath, and the physical question period

The verbal content is most of what this chapter has been about; the
physical content matters too, and it is what nervous speakers most
neglect.

**Breath.** When a hard question lands, your instinct is to start
answering immediately, to fill the silence, to defuse the threat with
words. Do not. *Pause*. Take one breath. The pause feels like an eternity
to you and is invisible to the room — they are still parsing the question.
The breath does two things: it gives you the moment to *classify* the
question (Bucket A through H above), and it gives your voice the support
it needs to be steady in the answer. A speaker who answers without
breathing first is a speaker whose voice gives away the panic; a speaker
who breathes is a speaker the room reads as in control. Practice this in
rehearsal: take a question, count "one Mississippi" silently, then answer.
It feels artificial; do it anyway.

**Eye contact.** Look at the questioner when they ask. Look briefly at the
chair when you start to answer (a courtesy). Then look at the *room*, not
just the questioner. Q&A is a conversation between you and the room, with
the questioner as the proximate cause; an answer delivered only to the
questioner is too narrow, because the rest of the room is also evaluating
the answer. End on the questioner — a half-second of eye contact when you
finish — so they know you have addressed them specifically.

**Voice.** The end of every sentence is a *declarative*, not a question.
Nervous speakers' voices rise at the ends of sentences, a *vocal fry* of
uncertainty; the room reads the rise as a lack of confidence. Practice
ending sentences flat or descending. Record yourself; you may not know
you do this. Most people do.

**Hands.** Keep them visible. Keep them still when answering. A speaker
who gestures wildly under pressure reads as nervous; a speaker who
gestures *deliberately* — pointing to a slide, indicating the audience,
opening a palm to acknowledge a point — reads as composed. If in doubt,
hold the clicker in both hands and rest them at waist height; the
clicker becomes a *prop* that gives your hands something to do, and it
solves the gesture problem with no extra mental load.

**Time.** Watch the chair. Conferences run on tight schedules, and a
session that runs over hurts the next speaker, which the room *will* hold
against you. If the chair signals "one more question" and your answer is
getting long, *land it*: "*the short answer is [headline]; happy to
discuss the details afterward.*" Ending on time is itself a credibility
move; the room reads punctuality as professionalism.

## 8. After the last question, before you sit down

The chair says "*let's thank our speaker*." The applause comes. You step
off-stage. Do *not* sit down quietly and check your phone. The thirty
seconds after the talk ends are the highest-value networking minute of the
day, and most students waste them.

**Stay near the front.** People who want to ask follow-up questions —
including the people you most want to talk to — come to the front in the
ninety seconds after a talk. If you have already retreated to the back of
the room, you have missed them. Stand near the podium, gather your laptop
slowly, and be findable.

**The first three approaches are the most important.** Three people
typically come up: one with a *technical question that didn't fit Q&A*
(give them five minutes; they are interested), one with *a paper to
recommend* (write it down, on the *literal* paper notebook you brought for
this purpose, not your phone), and one with *a name and a card / contact*
(say thank you, write the name down with one sentence about why). These
three people are the proximate ROI of the talk. Spend the time.

**Write down what you heard, immediately.** As soon as you are out of the
conversation, before you walk to the next session, *write*. The feedback
ledger from Chapter 9.5 begins right now, not at 5 p.m. The questions
asked, the people who approached, the comments overheard from the
audience as they filed out — all of it. Memory fades within hours; the
pen catches what the memory will lose. Keep the notebook in your pocket
all day.

**The chair will sometimes find you afterward.** Especially at a student
conference, the chair often has a personal observation — about the talk,
about the questions, about which audience member's reaction to watch. This
is privileged information; treat it as such. Write it down too. The chair
sees the whole session and has a perspective you do not; their five
seconds of feedback can shape the rest of your week.

## 9. The arc, from rehearsal to recovery

Step back from the Q&A mechanics and notice the *arc* of what this chapter
has built. You spent Chapter 9.1 hardening the work; you spent Chapter 9.2
hardening the *delivery*. This chapter is hardening the *defense* — the
nine minutes that come after the talk and before the day ends, where the
room finds out whether you actually understand what you presented.

The arc, condensed:

- **Before the talk.** The threats table (Ch 7.5) and the
  anticipated-questions matrix (Ch 9.1 §3) anticipate Bucket B questions in
  *writing*, in calm, with the *fair-did-residual-retreat* structure
  pre-filled. The spec curve (Ch 8.1) and robustness battery (Ch 8.2)
  preempt Bucket A questions on the *threats slide* of the talk (Ch 9.2 §5).
- **During the question.** Read the room (§1) — *who*, *tone*, *first
  noun* — then classify the question into one of eight buckets (§2). The
  bucket dictates the script: A → robustness pointer; B → "Good point" /
  fair-did-residual; C → mechanism narrative; D → "what data / what
  mechanism / what prediction / what test"; E → stakes sentence; F →
  technical precision; G → parsimony; H → gracious redirection. The
  classification takes one second; the answer follows.
- **When you don't know.** The located "I don't know" (§3) — *admit, locate,
  guess (optional), resolve*. Honest, structured, useful. Builds
  credibility, does not damage it.
- **When the question is deep but takes too long.** "It's in the paper"
  (§4) — *one-sentence headline, specific pointer, open door*. Use for
  Buckets A, F, G; never for B, D, E.
- **When the question is fatal.** Concede at the level of the critique
  (§6); retreat to what survives. The retreat sentence was pre-written in
  column 6 of the matrix, so you read it under fire rather than
  improvising it.
- **After the questions.** Stay near the front. Take the three approaches.
  Write down what you heard, on paper, immediately. The feedback ledger
  (Ch 9.5) starts here, not later.

The deeper claim — the one that organizes all of this — is that **the
question period is not adversarial; it is *evaluative*.** A hostile-sounding
question is, almost always, a smart person trying to figure out whether
the result is real. Their job is to probe; your job is to make probing
productive. When the probe finds something — and a good probe usually
does — the credibility move is to *show the room you found it first*.
That is what the matrix is for. That is what the threats table was for.
That is what the whole chain from Chapter 7.5 to here has been preparing.

The talk ended forty-five seconds ago. The first hand went up. You
classify the question (Bucket B), recognize the threat (row 4 of your
matrix), grant the legitimacy ("that's a fair worry, because…"), point
to the evidence ("slide 6, the event-study"), name the residual ("what I
can't rule out is…"). The questioner nods. The next hand goes up. You
classify it (Bucket A), recognize the choice (clustering at the
lender level), give the robustness pointer ("Appendix B.2, within 0.3 SE
of the headline"). Two questions in, the room has decided you know what
you are doing. The next seven questions are graded against that decision.
That is the question period, working.

## Your Turn

The Q&A skill is the one that improves fastest with practice, *because*
the bucket taxonomy makes it diagnosable. Use that.

- **nb9.3 — question-taxonomy classifier.** The notebook lets you, in
  rehearsal, type a hypothetical question and have it classified into one
  of the eight buckets, with the script template for the answer. For
  Bucket B questions, it joins on threat-id to your Ch 7.5 threats table
  and surfaces the row you should answer from. Use it in pair-rehearsal:
  one person asks, the other classifies-then-answers, against the
  notebook's two-second classification deadline.
- **PS 9.3 — hostile-question simulation.** Run a recorded ten-question
  adversarial Q&A against a partner (or two partners — one ally, one
  pretending to be the meanest reviewer they can be). For each of the
  ten questions, the deliverable is the *classification* (which bucket),
  the *answer* (in writing, after the fact, in 100-150 words), and a
  one-sentence reflection on whether the live answer was strong, weak,
  or somewhere in between. Submit the transcript and the reflection.
- **Lab 9, Phase C.** The Phase C lab session is the full adversarial Q&A,
  in front of a small audience, recorded. The grading rubric scores the
  classification speed (under two seconds), the answer structure (does it
  match the bucket's script?), and the use of the three honest answers
  (does each get deployed at the right moment?). Watch the recording
  afterward; you will notice the rises in your voice, the un-paused
  starts, the bluffs you didn't know you were making. Fix them.

### A closing reflection

Pick the single Bucket B question in your matrix that you are most afraid
of — the one where the attacker, if right, breaks the design. Write the
full *fair-did-residual-retreat* answer in long form, paragraph form, on a
piece of paper you keep in your pocket. Then ask yourself: *if I had to
deliver the retreat sentence live, in front of a room, could I do it?*
The question period is judged on whether you can. The chapter is judged on
whether the answer is now closer to yes than it was an hour ago.
