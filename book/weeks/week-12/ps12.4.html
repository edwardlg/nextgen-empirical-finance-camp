PS 12.4 — Conference-Circuit Plan: Three Target Venues for the Next 18 Months, with Deadlines and Eligibility Audited

Course: 8-Week Empirical Finance Camp · Week 12 · Problem Set 12.4 Covers: the move from Chapter 12.4 — the six-tier conference ecosystem (the majors, the majors’ undergraduate tracks, the second-tier nationals, the regionals, the undergraduate-specific venues, the high-school-specific competitions) — applied as a concrete eighteen-month plan with three named target venues, audited deadlines, audited eligibility criteria, and a calendar that the student can give to a parent, an advisor, and a future-application committee. This problem set turns the abstract claim “I will present my paper at conferences” into a Gantt-chart-grade plan with line items, costs, and submission dependencies. PS 12.4 builds on Chapter 12.4 §3 (the FMA Undergraduate Poster), §5 (the regionals), §7 (the AEA Pipeline Program), §8 (the Schar Young Scholars Symposium), and on the conference circuit lessons from the Rainbow of Credits case in Chapter 12.3. It draws no new empirical results; the deliverable is planning, with verification, audit, and contingency. Type: ~30% research (venue lookups, deadline verification, eligibility-criteria audit), ~25% writing (the per-venue application memos, the funding-and-travel plan, the cohort-coordination memo), ~25% planning (the Gantt chart, the dependency graph, the contingency plan), ~20% verification (the deadline-cross-check, the eligibility-cross-check, the budget-cross-check).

Total: 100 points. Point values stated per part. A model deliverable — Sam’s intraday-momentum paper plan, targeting the FMA Undergraduate Poster (October 2026), the Eastern Finance Association annual meeting (April 2027), and the SFS Cavalcade undergraduate poster (May 2027), with all deadlines audited, an eligibility audit that surfaces one disqualifier, and a $2,400 cohort-pooled travel budget — is in Appendix E (E-w12-ps12.4-solutions.md).

A boundary worth holding before you start. PS 12.4 is not “apply to as many conferences as possible.” The most common mistake camp graduates make is applying broadly to every venue they hear about; the cost is time (each application takes 3–8 hours of preparation), travel (each accepted invitation costs ~$400–$1,200), and attention (presenting the same paper four times in six months is exhausting and rarely productive). The discipline of this problem set is selectivity: pick three venues, pick them deliberately against your paper’s stage and your eighteen-month calendar, audit every deadline, audit every eligibility criterion, build a budget, and identify the contingency plan if any one venue declines. Three venues, well-chosen, applied to with care and attended with preparation, will produce more career value than ten venues applied to in haste.

What you submit

A single folder, conference-plan/, committed to your capstone repository, containing:

  1. conference-plan/plan-overview.md — the eighteen-month plan with three named venues, dates, and roles.
  2. conference-plan/venue-1.md — the application memo for venue 1 (typically the first-tier-5 venue, due first).
  3. conference-plan/venue-2.md — the application memo for venue 2 (typically a regional in the spring).
  4. conference-plan/venue-3.md — the application memo for venue 3 (typically a second-tier national or a credentialing program).
  5. conference-plan/gantt.md — a Markdown Gantt chart of the eighteen months with submission deadlines, decision dates, travel dates, and pre-conference preparation tasks.
  6. conference-plan/dependency-graph.md — the graph showing which venue feeds which (e.g., FMA Undergraduate Poster feedback in October 2026 feeds the EFA submission in January 2027 feeds the SFS Cavalcade submission in February 2027).
  7. conference-plan/budget.md — the funding-and-travel plan with line-item costs and the cohort-pooled-budget rationale.
  8. conference-plan/eligibility-audit.md — for each venue, a literal copy-paste of the eligibility criteria from the venue’s published call for papers, with a per-criterion check.
  9. conference-plan/contingency.md — the contingency plan if venue 1 declines, if venue 2 declines, if all three decline.

Part 1 — The plan overview (10 points)

The single sin this part prevents: a plan with too many venues, the wrong sequence of venues, or a sequence that fights the paper’s natural revision rhythm.

(a) Write the plan-overview document (10 pts). The first thing the reader of plan-overview.md sees is a one-paragraph summary naming the three venues, their roles, and the eighteen-month window:

We propose a three-venue conference plan for [paper title] over October 2026 – March 2028. Venue 1 (October 2026) — [name] — is the feedback-first entry, where the paper’s first non-camp audience will surface comments that drive the v1.1 revision in November–December. Venue 2 (April 2027) — [name] — is the credentialing-and-network-expansion presentation, with the revised v2.0 of the paper produced in response to v1.1 feedback. Venue 3 (May 2027 or later) — [name] — is the stretch venue, targeting a higher tier where acceptance is uncertain but the credential is durable. Total budget: $[X]. Total time commitment: [Y] hours across the eighteen months. Decisions: venue 1 accepts by [date]; venue 2 by [date]; venue 3 by [date].

The paragraph names the venues, the roles (feedback / credentialing / stretch), the calendar, the budget, and the time commitment.

(b) State the audience hypothesis (3 pts of the 10). A one-paragraph statement identifying the target audience for each venue and why your paper speaks to them. Example for Maya:

  • Venue 1 (FMA Undergraduate Poster, October 2026, Atlanta). Audience: undergraduate finance researchers and the senior faculty discussants who circulate among posters. The role: feedback. The expected payoff: 2–4 substantive comments per discussant interaction, ~6 interactions in the 90-minute poster session, ~15 actionable comments total.
  • Venue 2 (Eastern Finance Association annual meeting, April 2027, Charleston). Audience: regional-finance faculty, advanced PhD students, and a competitive undergraduate-paper track. The role: credentialing (a Tier 4 venue on the resume) and network-expansion. Expected payoff: one published abstract in conference proceedings (credential), three or four substantive conversations with regional faculty (network).
  • Venue 3 (SFS Cavalcade Undergraduate Poster, May 2027). Audience: research-active finance faculty, the SFS membership. The role: stretch — acceptance rate ~35% means a real possibility of rejection. If accepted, the credential is meaningful (Tier 3); if rejected, the application packet is reusable for the next round.

The audience-hypothesis paragraph forces honesty about why each venue is on the plan.

Part 2 — Venue 1: the feedback-first entry (20 points)

The single sin this part prevents: picking the wrong first venue and burning the first-presentation-of-the-paper credential on a venue that does not produce useful feedback.

For most camp graduates, the right first venue is the FMA Undergraduate Poster Session (Chapter 12.4 §3). The Schar Young Scholars Symposium (an in-house GMU venue) is a complement, not a substitute — the camp’s Week 9 culminating event served the same role and is now in the rear-view mirror. The FMA poster is the first external feedback environment with a real selection process and a real audience.

(a) Audit the FMA Undergraduate Poster Session eligibility (5 pts). From the FMA’s published call for papers (typically issued in March, with a June deadline), confirm each eligibility criterion:

  • Author status. Must be an undergraduate or precollege author at the time of the call (May–June). Precollege authors are eligible per FMA’s expanded undergraduate-research program; verify in the current year’s call.
  • Faculty advisor. A faculty advisor must be named on the submission. Prof. Gao serves this role.
  • Paper type. Empirical, theoretical, or replication — empirical is by far the modal accepted type.
  • Length. Abstract: 500–1,500 words. Full paper: optional but recommended (most accepted submissions include the full paper).
  • Originality. Paper must not have been previously published.
  • Single-blind. Submission is single-blind: reviewers see author names; authors do not see reviewer names.

Copy-paste each eligibility criterion from the FMA’s call into venue-1.md under the eligibility audit section, and mark each as Met / Not Met / Verify. If any criterion is not met, name the disqualifier and either fix it (if fixable) or replace the venue.

(b) Write the application packet (10 pts). The FMA submission requires:

  • Extended abstract. 500–1,500 words. Different from PS 11.1’s abstract: longer, includes more methodological detail, includes one paragraph on the empirical setting. Compose by combining PS 11.1’s chosen abstract + one paragraph from the introduction’s paragraph 2 (setting) + one paragraph from the results section. Word-count audit: target 1,000 words.
  • Full paper. The submitted PDF from PS 12.1’s submission packet, suitable for circulation to reviewers.
  • Faculty advisor letter. Prof. Gao writes a one-page letter endorsing the submission and confirming the camp affiliation. Provide the draft text of the letter (Prof. Gao will polish) — three paragraphs, naming the student, the paper, and the advisor’s endorsement.
  • Submission form fields. Title, abstract (paste from PS 11.1), keywords (paste from PS 12.2), JEL codes (paste from PS 12.2), author info, advisor info.

Compose each component in venue-1.md under labeled sections. Each component is short; the discipline is to assemble them correctly and on time.

(c) Calendar the FMA timeline (5 pts). A calendar in venue-1.md:

  • 2026-05-15. FMA call for papers opens (verify date against current year’s call).
  • 2026-06-25. Submission deadline. Submit no later than two weeks before this date.
  • 2026-07-25. Decision returned.
  • 2026-09-15. If accepted, register and pay the registration fee (typically ~$50 for undergraduates).
  • 2026-10-XX. Conference meeting (date varies by year and city; in 2026 it is in Atlanta).
  • 2026-10-XX (post-conference). Within one week of returning, write the feedback memo capturing what was learned at the poster session.

The calendar is the line items; the timeline is what you put on your wall.

Part 3 — Venue 2: the regional credentialing venue (20 points)

The single sin this part prevents: picking a regional venue without auditing its undergraduate-paper track or its acceptance criteria, and then discovering after submission that the venue does not accept undergraduate work.

For most camp graduates, the right second venue is a regional finance association annual meeting (Chapter 12.4 §5). The Eastern Finance Association, the Midwest Finance Association, the Southwestern Finance Association, and the Eastern Economic Association all maintain undergraduate paper tracks; the specific venue depends on the geographic feasibility and the paper’s topic match.

(a) Choose a regional and audit eligibility (5 pts). Choose one of the major regionals based on (i) date alignment with your paper’s revision schedule (the regional should be at least 3 months after venue 1 to allow v1.1 revisions), (ii) topic-area fit (some regionals have stronger sub-fields than others — e.g., the EFA-East is strong on financial-institutions topics, the MFA on corporate finance, the SWFA on energy and real estate), (iii) geographic feasibility (travel cost).

For Maya, the natural choice is the Eastern Finance Association annual meeting (held in spring, typical April venue in Charleston or similar): the EFA has a strong banking-and-financial-institutions sub-field, the date alignment is ~6 months after the FMA poster, and the venue is in the eastern US. For Devon (crypto), the Midwest Finance Association is a good fit because of its strong microstructure / FinTech sub-field. For Priya (climate-insurance), the EFA or the Southern Finance Association (which has an active environmental-finance track). For Sam (intraday momentum), the EFA or MFA (both have microstructure tracks). For Leah (patents-innovation), the EFA (corporate-finance track) or the MFA.

Document the choice in venue-2.md and audit eligibility against the venue’s published call for papers. The regionals are not undergraduate-only venues; their main programs accept undergraduate work in competition with graduate students and junior faculty, but they typically have a dedicated undergraduate paper session or poster session with a slightly more permissive standard. Verify which track you are submitting to.

(b) Write the application packet (10 pts). The regional submission typically requires:

  • Full paper. The revised v1.1 paper (incorporating FMA feedback from venue 1). PDF.
  • Abstract. Paste from PS 11.1; may be the same as the FMA submission or may be lightly revised if v1.1 changed a result.
  • Author info, including coauthors.
  • Submission fee. Regionals charge $30–$100 per submission; budget for this.
  • Specification of session preference. “Undergraduate paper session” or “general session — finance” or “poster session.” For most camp graduates, target the undergraduate paper session.
  • Discussant suggestion (optional). Suggest one or two faculty members whose work your paper engages with. The regional may use the suggestion or assign its own.

Compose each component in venue-2.md.

(c) Plan the v1.1 revision in advance of venue 2 (5 pts). Between venue 1 (October 2026 feedback) and venue 2 (April 2027 submission), the paper undergoes a v1.1 revision. Plan that revision in venue-2.md:

  • Expected feedback from venue 1. Three to five items the FMA discussant or audience members are likely to raise (e.g., “robustness to alternative clustering,” “heterogeneity by lender type,” “the parallel-trends test”).
  • Revision plan. Per expected feedback item, the change you would make (a new column in Table 2, a new appendix table, an expanded paragraph in the discussion).
  • Revision timeline. November 2026 (data work), December 2026 (writing), January 2027 (advisor review), February 2027 (final polish), March 2027 (submission to venue 2).

The revision plan is a contract with the future-you. Without it, the four months between venues will dissolve into vague intentions; with it, the revision is on the calendar.

Part 4 — Venue 3: the stretch venue (15 points)

The single sin this part prevents: playing only safe venues and missing the stretch credential that compounds for graduate-school applications.

The stretch venue is a Tier 3 (second-tier national) or a credentialing program (AEA Pipeline, NBER summer program affiliate, AFA undergraduate track). Acceptance is uncertain — 20–40% is the modal acceptance rate at this tier — but the credential is durable. Apply, plan as if the application will succeed, and treat the rejection as a non-event (the application is the credential, not the acceptance).

(a) Choose a stretch venue (5 pts). Options:

  • SFS Cavalcade Undergraduate Poster. The Society for Financial Studies hosts an annual Cavalcade in May; the undergraduate poster session is selective (~35% acceptance) and high-quality. Audience: research-active finance faculty. This is the modal stretch venue for camp graduates with strong empirical papers.
  • AEA Pipeline Program. The AEA’s summer program for underrepresented students in economics, with multiple program variants (Pipeline, Mentoring, Summer Training). Application due in winter; cohort selected in spring. Credentialing for students continuing in economics; not strictly a presentation venue, but a year-long program with conference attendance built in.
  • AFA Undergraduate Research Awards. The American Finance Association occasionally runs an undergraduate research-award competition; the credential is significant. Application typically due in fall.
  • Carroll Round Conference (Georgetown). An undergraduate research conference focused on international economic policy. Tier 5 selectivity but Tier 4 quality of audience.

Choose one. Document the choice in venue-3.md. For Maya, the SFS Cavalcade is the natural choice; for Devon, the same. For Priya, the AEA Pipeline (her climate-policy interest aligns with the AEA’s policy focus). For Sam, the SFS Cavalcade. For Leah, the SFS Cavalcade or the Carroll Round.

(b) Audit eligibility and timing (5 pts). Copy the published eligibility criteria from the venue’s website. The Cavalcade’s undergraduate poster has, as of 2026: undergraduate or precollege author at time of submission, faculty advisor signature, full paper required, single-blind review, abstract ≤2,000 words. The AEA Pipeline has: rising junior or senior undergraduate (precollege not eligible at the time of writing — verify in the current year’s call), academic transcript, two letters of recommendation. Note: AEA Pipeline eligibility for precollege students has shifted over time; verify the current criteria, and if you are precollege at submission time, look for the AEA’s summer experience for precollege students track or substitute a different stretch venue.

(c) Write the application packet (5 pts). Compose the application materials for the stretch venue in venue-3.md. For a paper-based venue, this is the v2.0 paper plus a 2,000-word abstract plus author info. For a program-based venue (AEA Pipeline), this is the application form, the personal statement, the transcript, and the letters of recommendation. State each component, its source, and its preparation timeline.

Part 5 — The Gantt chart and dependency graph (15 points)

The single sin this part prevents: a plan that has all three venues on it but no visual representation of how they interact in time, with the result that two deadlines collide and you miss one.

(a) Write the Markdown Gantt chart (10 pts). A month-by-month grid from October 2026 to March 2028:

              | 26-10 | 26-11 | 26-12 | 27-01 | 27-02 | 27-03 | 27-04 | 27-05 | 27-06 | ... |
Venue 1 (FMA) |  [P]  |  [F]  |       |       |       |       |       |       |       |
v1.1 revision |       |  [W]  |  [W]  |  [W]  |  [F]  |       |       |       |       |
Venue 2 (EFA) |       |       |       |  [S]  |       |       |  [P]  |       |       |
v2.0 revision |       |       |       |       |       |  [W]  |  [W]  |  [W]  |  [F]  |
Venue 3 (SFS) |       |       |       |       |  [S]  |       |       |  [P]  |       |

Legend: [S] = submission due, [P] = presentation at venue, [F] = feedback-incorporation deadline, [W] = writing window. Each row is a stream of activity; each column is a month. The chart is a one-page visual that goes on your wall.

(b) Write the dependency graph (5 pts). A small Markdown DAG (directed acyclic graph) showing which venue’s output feeds which:

Venue 1 (FMA poster, Oct 2026)
  ↓ feedback → v1.1 revision (Nov–Feb)
                ↓
Venue 2 (EFA, Apr 2027)
  ↓ feedback → v2.0 revision (Apr–Jun)
                ↓
Venue 3 (SFS Cavalcade, May 2027 or later)

The graph names dependencies. If venue 1 is rejected, venue 2 still goes forward — but the v1.1 revision is now driven by the camp-internal feedback alone, not by FMA-discussant comments. The graph makes the dependencies explicit.

Part 6 — Budget, cohort coordination, and contingency (20 points)

The single sin this part prevents: a plan with no budget, no cost-sharing strategy, no contingency, and therefore no path from “I have a plan” to “I have the resources to execute the plan.”

(a) Write the venue-by-venue budget (8 pts). For each venue, line-item costs in budget.md:

Cost item Venue 1 (FMA) Venue 2 (EFA) Venue 3 (SFS)
Registration $50 (undergrad rate) $75 $50 (undergrad rate)
Submission fee $0 $40 $0
Travel (flight, train, or driving) $250 $200 $400
Lodging (2 nights) $200 (student rate, shared room) $150 $250
Meals (3 days @ $40) $120 $120 $120
Poster printing $60 $0 (paper, not poster) $60
Per-venue total $680 $585 $880

Camp graduates can pursue several funding sources: (i) school-based travel funds (most STEM-magnet HS programs have a research-travel line); (ii) the camp’s own modest travel-stipend program (limited; cohort-pooled, see (b) below); (iii) family contribution; (iv) need-based travel-stipend programs at the host conferences (FMA and the regionals all have these — apply).

(b) Write the cohort-pooled-budget memo (4 pts). For students in the same cohort attending the same venue, pool travel costs: shared lodging, group flights, joint car rentals. Document the pooling logic in budget.md. For venue 1 (FMA in Atlanta), if four students from the cohort are attending, the per-student cost drops from $680 to roughly $480 through shared lodging and one rental car. The cohort discount is real and worth budgeting for explicitly.

(c) Write the contingency plan (8 pts). What if venue 1 declines? What if venue 2 declines? What if all three decline? Write a paragraph for each scenario in contingency.md:

  • Venue 1 declines (October 2026). Acceptance rates for the FMA Undergraduate Poster are ~50–70%, so the decline probability is ~30–50%. Contingency: the camp’s Symposium presentation (Week 9) has already happened; treat that as venue 1 in retrospect, accelerate the EFA submission, and use the FMA-prep work (the extended abstract, the poster) as drafts for venue 2.
  • Venue 2 declines (January 2027). Acceptance rates at EFA undergraduate sessions are ~40–60%. Contingency: substitute the Southern Economic Association (or another regional) whose submission deadline is 2–3 months later, giving you time to revise the application in response to whatever soft-feedback the EFA decision included.
  • Venue 3 declines (April 2027). Acceptance rates at SFS Cavalcade undergraduate poster are ~35%. Contingency: substitute the Carroll Round Conference or the Eastern Economic Association undergraduate session — both more accessible Tier 5 venues. The paper still gets a presentation; the credential is slightly lower-tier but still real.
  • All three decline. Probability is low but nonzero (~5–10% under independence assumptions; lower under positive correlation across venues). Contingency: pivot to the Schar Young Scholars Symposium 2027 (in-house GMU venue, near-certain acceptance for camp alumni), use that as the spring 2027 presentation, and re-apply to all three venues in the fall 2027 cycle with a v2.0 paper that has incorporated a year of additional revision.

The contingency plan is the second-most-important deliverable in PS 12.4 (after the plan itself). Plans that have no contingency tend to collapse on the first rejection.

Submission checklist

How this is graded

Part 1 (overview, 10) on naming three venues with explicit role-assignments (feedback / credentialing / stretch) and an audience hypothesis per venue. Part 2 (Venue 1, 20) on FMA-specific eligibility audit and application packet completeness. Part 3 (Venue 2, 20) on the regional choice with topic-fit and date-alignment rationale, application packet, and v1.1 revision plan. Part 4 (Venue 3, 15) on the stretch venue choice and eligibility audit. Part 5 (Gantt + dependency graph, 15) on visual clarity and dependency naming. Part 6 (budget + contingency, 20) on line-item budget per venue, cohort-pooling rationale, and contingency plans for each rejection scenario.

The single highest-signal check: for each of the three venues, does your eligibility-audit document include a verbatim copy of the venue’s published eligibility criteria with a per-criterion check mark? If yes, you have done the homework. If no, you have a plan based on assumed eligibility — and assumed eligibility is the leading cause of post-submission disqualification.

The bridge to PS 12.5 and beyond

PS 12.4 produces the eighteen-month conference plan. PS 12.5 zooms out further to the five-year research-identity arc — the through-line from your camp paper to three follow-up projects that, by 2031, define a coherent research line. The conference plan is the operational scaffolding for the next eighteen months; the five-year memo is the identity that the conference plan serves. Treat the two as nested: the conference plan is the inner loop, the five-year memo is the outer loop.

End of PS 12.4. Three venues, eighteen months, audited deadlines, named contingencies. Begin with venue 1; the rest flows from the feedback that venue 1 produces. By the end of the week the FMA application is half-drafted, the EFA submission window is on the calendar, and the stretch-venue application is in the planning stage. The path from camp graduate to working researcher runs through these venues, in order.

6. `conference-plan/dependency-graph.md` — the graph showing which venue feeds which (e.g., FMA Undergraduate Poster feedback in October 2026 feeds the EFA submission in January 2027 feeds the SFS Cavalcade submission in February 2027).
7. `conference-plan/budget.md` — the funding-and-travel plan with line-item costs and the cohort-pooled-budget rationale.
8. `conference-plan/eligibility-audit.md` — for each venue, a literal copy-paste of the eligibility criteria from the venue's published call for papers, with a per-criterion check.
9. `conference-plan/contingency.md` — the contingency plan if venue 1 declines, if venue 2 declines, if all three decline.

## Part 1 — The plan overview (10 points)

The single sin this part prevents: **a plan with too many venues, the wrong sequence of venues, or a sequence that fights the paper's natural revision rhythm.**

**(a) Write the plan-overview document (10 pts).** The first thing the reader of `plan-overview.md` sees is a one-paragraph summary naming the three venues, their roles, and the eighteen-month window:

> *We propose a three-venue conference plan for [paper title] over October 2026 – March 2028. Venue 1 (October 2026) — [name] — is the feedback-first entry, where the paper's first non-camp audience will surface comments that drive the v1.1 revision in November–December. Venue 2 (April 2027) — [name] — is the credentialing-and-network-expansion presentation, with the revised v2.0 of the paper produced in response to v1.1 feedback. Venue 3 (May 2027 or later) — [name] — is the stretch venue, targeting a higher tier where acceptance is uncertain but the credential is durable. Total budget: $[X]. Total time commitment: [Y] hours across the eighteen months. Decisions: venue 1 accepts by [date]; venue 2 by [date]; venue 3 by [date].*

The paragraph names the venues, the roles (feedback / credentialing / stretch), the calendar, the budget, and the time commitment.

**(b) State the audience hypothesis (3 pts of the 10).** A one-paragraph statement identifying the *target audience* for each venue and why your paper speaks to them. Example for Maya:

- *Venue 1 (FMA Undergraduate Poster, October 2026, Atlanta).* Audience: undergraduate finance researchers and the senior faculty discussants who circulate among posters. The role: *feedback*. The expected payoff: 2–4 substantive comments per discussant interaction, ~6 interactions in the 90-minute poster session, ~15 actionable comments total.
- *Venue 2 (Eastern Finance Association annual meeting, April 2027, Charleston).* Audience: regional-finance faculty, advanced PhD students, and a competitive undergraduate-paper track. The role: *credentialing* (a Tier 4 venue on the resume) and network-expansion. Expected payoff: one published abstract in conference proceedings (credential), three or four substantive conversations with regional faculty (network).
- *Venue 3 (SFS Cavalcade Undergraduate Poster, May 2027).* Audience: research-active finance faculty, the SFS membership. The role: *stretch* — acceptance rate ~35% means a real possibility of rejection. If accepted, the credential is meaningful (Tier 3); if rejected, the application packet is reusable for the next round.

The audience-hypothesis paragraph forces honesty about why each venue is on the plan.

## Part 2 — Venue 1: the feedback-first entry (20 points)

The single sin this part prevents: **picking the wrong first venue and burning the first-presentation-of-the-paper credential on a venue that does not produce useful feedback.**

For most camp graduates, the right first venue is *the FMA Undergraduate Poster Session* (Chapter 12.4 §3). The Schar Young Scholars Symposium (an in-house GMU venue) is a complement, not a substitute — the camp's Week 9 culminating event served the same role and is now in the rear-view mirror. The FMA poster is the first external feedback environment with a real selection process and a real audience.

**(a) Audit the FMA Undergraduate Poster Session eligibility (5 pts).** From the FMA's published call for papers (typically issued in March, with a June deadline), confirm each eligibility criterion:

- *Author status.* Must be an undergraduate or precollege author at the time of the call (May–June). Precollege authors are eligible per FMA's expanded undergraduate-research program; verify in the current year's call.
- *Faculty advisor.* A faculty advisor must be named on the submission. Prof. Gao serves this role.
- *Paper type.* Empirical, theoretical, or replication — empirical is by far the modal accepted type.
- *Length.* Abstract: 500–1,500 words. Full paper: optional but recommended (most accepted submissions include the full paper).
- *Originality.* Paper must not have been previously published.
- *Single-blind.* Submission is single-blind: reviewers see author names; authors do not see reviewer names.

Copy-paste each eligibility criterion from the FMA's call into `venue-1.md` under the eligibility audit section, and mark each as Met / Not Met / Verify. If any criterion is not met, name the disqualifier and either fix it (if fixable) or replace the venue.

**(b) Write the application packet (10 pts).** The FMA submission requires:

- *Extended abstract.* 500–1,500 words. Different from PS 11.1's abstract: longer, includes more methodological detail, includes one paragraph on the empirical setting. Compose by combining PS 11.1's chosen abstract + one paragraph from the introduction's paragraph 2 (setting) + one paragraph from the results section. Word-count audit: target 1,000 words.
- *Full paper.* The submitted PDF from PS 12.1's submission packet, suitable for circulation to reviewers.
- *Faculty advisor letter.* Prof. Gao writes a one-page letter endorsing the submission and confirming the camp affiliation. Provide the *draft text* of the letter (Prof. Gao will polish) — three paragraphs, naming the student, the paper, and the advisor's endorsement.
- *Submission form fields.* Title, abstract (paste from PS 11.1), keywords (paste from PS 12.2), JEL codes (paste from PS 12.2), author info, advisor info.

Compose each component in `venue-1.md` under labeled sections. Each component is short; the discipline is to assemble them correctly and on time.

**(c) Calendar the FMA timeline (5 pts).** A calendar in `venue-1.md`:

- *2026-05-15.* FMA call for papers opens (verify date against current year's call).
- *2026-06-25.* Submission deadline. Submit no later than two weeks before this date.
- *2026-07-25.* Decision returned.
- *2026-09-15.* If accepted, register and pay the registration fee (typically ~$50 for undergraduates).
- *2026-10-XX.* Conference meeting (date varies by year and city; in 2026 it is in Atlanta).
- *2026-10-XX (post-conference).* Within one week of returning, write the feedback memo capturing what was learned at the poster session.

The calendar is the line items; the timeline is what you put on your wall.

## Part 3 — Venue 2: the regional credentialing venue (20 points)

The single sin this part prevents: **picking a regional venue without auditing its undergraduate-paper track or its acceptance criteria, and then discovering after submission that the venue does not accept undergraduate work.**

For most camp graduates, the right second venue is a *regional finance association annual meeting* (Chapter 12.4 §5). The Eastern Finance Association, the Midwest Finance Association, the Southwestern Finance Association, and the Eastern Economic Association all maintain undergraduate paper tracks; the specific venue depends on the geographic feasibility and the paper's topic match.

**(a) Choose a regional and audit eligibility (5 pts).** Choose one of the major regionals based on (i) date alignment with your paper's revision schedule (the regional should be at least 3 months after venue 1 to allow v1.1 revisions), (ii) topic-area fit (some regionals have stronger sub-fields than others — e.g., the EFA-East is strong on financial-institutions topics, the MFA on corporate finance, the SWFA on energy and real estate), (iii) geographic feasibility (travel cost).

For Maya, the natural choice is the *Eastern Finance Association annual meeting* (held in spring, typical April venue in Charleston or similar): the EFA has a strong banking-and-financial-institutions sub-field, the date alignment is ~6 months after the FMA poster, and the venue is in the eastern US. For Devon (crypto), the *Midwest Finance Association* is a good fit because of its strong microstructure / FinTech sub-field. For Priya (climate-insurance), the EFA or the *Southern Finance Association* (which has an active environmental-finance track). For Sam (intraday momentum), the EFA or MFA (both have microstructure tracks). For Leah (patents-innovation), the EFA (corporate-finance track) or the MFA.

Document the choice in `venue-2.md` and audit eligibility against the venue's published call for papers. The regionals are *not* undergraduate-only venues; their main programs accept undergraduate work in competition with graduate students and junior faculty, but they typically have a dedicated *undergraduate paper session* or *poster session* with a slightly more permissive standard. Verify which track you are submitting to.

**(b) Write the application packet (10 pts).** The regional submission typically requires:

- *Full paper.* The revised v1.1 paper (incorporating FMA feedback from venue 1). PDF.
- *Abstract.* Paste from PS 11.1; may be the same as the FMA submission or may be lightly revised if v1.1 changed a result.
- *Author info, including coauthors.*
- *Submission fee.* Regionals charge $30–$100 per submission; budget for this.
- *Specification of session preference.* "Undergraduate paper session" or "general session — finance" or "poster session." For most camp graduates, target the undergraduate paper session.
- *Discussant suggestion (optional).* Suggest one or two faculty members whose work your paper engages with. The regional may use the suggestion or assign its own.

Compose each component in `venue-2.md`.

**(c) Plan the v1.1 revision in advance of venue 2 (5 pts).** Between venue 1 (October 2026 feedback) and venue 2 (April 2027 submission), the paper undergoes a v1.1 revision. Plan that revision in `venue-2.md`:

- *Expected feedback from venue 1.* Three to five items the FMA discussant or audience members are likely to raise (e.g., "robustness to alternative clustering," "heterogeneity by lender type," "the parallel-trends test").
- *Revision plan.* Per expected feedback item, the change you would make (a new column in Table 2, a new appendix table, an expanded paragraph in the discussion).
- *Revision timeline.* November 2026 (data work), December 2026 (writing), January 2027 (advisor review), February 2027 (final polish), March 2027 (submission to venue 2).

The revision plan is a contract with the future-you. Without it, the four months between venues will dissolve into vague intentions; with it, the revision is on the calendar.

## Part 4 — Venue 3: the stretch venue (15 points)

The single sin this part prevents: **playing only safe venues and missing the stretch credential that compounds for graduate-school applications.**

The stretch venue is a Tier 3 (second-tier national) or a credentialing program (AEA Pipeline, NBER summer program affiliate, AFA undergraduate track). Acceptance is uncertain — 20–40% is the modal acceptance rate at this tier — but the credential is durable. Apply, plan as if the application will succeed, and treat the rejection as a non-event (the application is the credential, not the acceptance).

**(a) Choose a stretch venue (5 pts).** Options:

- *SFS Cavalcade Undergraduate Poster.* The Society for Financial Studies hosts an annual Cavalcade in May; the undergraduate poster session is selective (~35% acceptance) and high-quality. Audience: research-active finance faculty. This is the modal stretch venue for camp graduates with strong empirical papers.
- *AEA Pipeline Program.* The AEA's summer program for underrepresented students in economics, with multiple program variants (Pipeline, Mentoring, Summer Training). Application due in winter; cohort selected in spring. Credentialing for students continuing in economics; not strictly a presentation venue, but a year-long program with conference attendance built in.
- *AFA Undergraduate Research Awards.* The American Finance Association occasionally runs an undergraduate research-award competition; the credential is significant. Application typically due in fall.
- *Carroll Round Conference (Georgetown).* An undergraduate research conference focused on international economic policy. Tier 5 selectivity but Tier 4 quality of audience.

Choose one. Document the choice in `venue-3.md`. For Maya, the SFS Cavalcade is the natural choice; for Devon, the same. For Priya, the AEA Pipeline (her climate-policy interest aligns with the AEA's policy focus). For Sam, the SFS Cavalcade. For Leah, the SFS Cavalcade or the Carroll Round.

**(b) Audit eligibility and timing (5 pts).** Copy the published eligibility criteria from the venue's website. The Cavalcade's undergraduate poster has, as of 2026: undergraduate or precollege author at time of submission, faculty advisor signature, full paper required, single-blind review, abstract ≤2,000 words. The AEA Pipeline has: rising junior or senior undergraduate (precollege not eligible at the time of writing — verify in the current year's call), academic transcript, two letters of recommendation. Note: AEA Pipeline eligibility for precollege students has shifted over time; verify the current criteria, and if you are precollege at submission time, look for the AEA's *summer experience for precollege students* track or substitute a different stretch venue.

**(c) Write the application packet (5 pts).** Compose the application materials for the stretch venue in `venue-3.md`. For a paper-based venue, this is the v2.0 paper plus a 2,000-word abstract plus author info. For a program-based venue (AEA Pipeline), this is the application form, the personal statement, the transcript, and the letters of recommendation. State each component, its source, and its preparation timeline.

## Part 5 — The Gantt chart and dependency graph (15 points)

The single sin this part prevents: **a plan that has all three venues on it but no visual representation of how they interact in time, with the result that two deadlines collide and you miss one.**

**(a) Write the Markdown Gantt chart (10 pts).** A month-by-month grid from October 2026 to March 2028:

```
              | 26-10 | 26-11 | 26-12 | 27-01 | 27-02 | 27-03 | 27-04 | 27-05 | 27-06 | ... |
Venue 1 (FMA) |  [P]  |  [F]  |       |       |       |       |       |       |       |
v1.1 revision |       |  [W]  |  [W]  |  [W]  |  [F]  |       |       |       |       |
Venue 2 (EFA) |       |       |       |  [S]  |       |       |  [P]  |       |       |
v2.0 revision |       |       |       |       |       |  [W]  |  [W]  |  [W]  |  [F]  |
Venue 3 (SFS) |       |       |       |       |  [S]  |       |       |  [P]  |       |
```

Legend: [S] = submission due, [P] = presentation at venue, [F] = feedback-incorporation deadline, [W] = writing window. Each row is a stream of activity; each column is a month. The chart is a one-page visual that goes on your wall.

**(b) Write the dependency graph (5 pts).** A small Markdown DAG (directed acyclic graph) showing which venue's output feeds which:

```
Venue 1 (FMA poster, Oct 2026)
  ↓ feedback → v1.1 revision (Nov–Feb)
                ↓
Venue 2 (EFA, Apr 2027)
  ↓ feedback → v2.0 revision (Apr–Jun)
                ↓
Venue 3 (SFS Cavalcade, May 2027 or later)
```

The graph names dependencies. If venue 1 is rejected, venue 2 still goes forward — but the v1.1 revision is now driven by the camp-internal feedback alone, not by FMA-discussant comments. The graph makes the dependencies explicit.

## Part 6 — Budget, cohort coordination, and contingency (20 points)

The single sin this part prevents: **a plan with no budget, no cost-sharing strategy, no contingency, and therefore no path from "I have a plan" to "I have the resources to execute the plan."**

**(a) Write the venue-by-venue budget (8 pts).** For each venue, line-item costs in `budget.md`:

| Cost item | Venue 1 (FMA) | Venue 2 (EFA) | Venue 3 (SFS) |
|---|---|---|---|
| Registration | $50 (undergrad rate) | $75 | $50 (undergrad rate) |
| Submission fee | $0 | $40 | $0 |
| Travel (flight, train, or driving) | $250 | $200 | $400 |
| Lodging (2 nights) | $200 (student rate, shared room) | $150 | $250 |
| Meals (3 days @ $40) | $120 | $120 | $120 |
| Poster printing | $60 | $0 (paper, not poster) | $60 |
| **Per-venue total** | **$680** | **$585** | **$880** |

Camp graduates can pursue several funding sources: (i) school-based travel funds (most STEM-magnet HS programs have a research-travel line); (ii) the camp's own modest travel-stipend program (limited; cohort-pooled, see (b) below); (iii) family contribution; (iv) need-based travel-stipend programs at the host conferences (FMA and the regionals all have these — apply).

**(b) Write the cohort-pooled-budget memo (4 pts).** For students in the same cohort attending the same venue, pool travel costs: shared lodging, group flights, joint car rentals. Document the pooling logic in `budget.md`. For venue 1 (FMA in Atlanta), if four students from the cohort are attending, the per-student cost drops from $680 to roughly $480 through shared lodging and one rental car. The cohort discount is real and worth budgeting for explicitly.

**(c) Write the contingency plan (8 pts).** What if venue 1 declines? What if venue 2 declines? What if all three decline? Write a paragraph for each scenario in `contingency.md`:

- *Venue 1 declines (October 2026).* Acceptance rates for the FMA Undergraduate Poster are ~50–70%, so the decline probability is ~30–50%. Contingency: the camp's Symposium presentation (Week 9) has already happened; treat that as venue 1 in retrospect, accelerate the EFA submission, and use the FMA-prep work (the extended abstract, the poster) as drafts for venue 2.
- *Venue 2 declines (January 2027).* Acceptance rates at EFA undergraduate sessions are ~40–60%. Contingency: substitute the *Southern Economic Association* (or another regional) whose submission deadline is 2–3 months later, giving you time to revise the application in response to whatever soft-feedback the EFA decision included.
- *Venue 3 declines (April 2027).* Acceptance rates at SFS Cavalcade undergraduate poster are ~35%. Contingency: substitute the *Carroll Round Conference* or the *Eastern Economic Association undergraduate session* — both more accessible Tier 5 venues. The paper still gets a presentation; the credential is slightly lower-tier but still real.
- *All three decline.* Probability is low but nonzero (~5–10% under independence assumptions; lower under positive correlation across venues). Contingency: pivot to the *Schar Young Scholars Symposium 2027* (in-house GMU venue, near-certain acceptance for camp alumni), use that as the spring 2027 presentation, and re-apply to all three venues in the fall 2027 cycle with a v2.0 paper that has incorporated a year of additional revision.

The contingency plan is the second-most-important deliverable in PS 12.4 (after the plan itself). Plans that have no contingency tend to collapse on the first rejection.

## How this is graded

Part 1 (overview, 10) on naming three venues with explicit role-assignments (feedback / credentialing / stretch) *and* an audience hypothesis per venue. Part 2 (Venue 1, 20) on FMA-specific eligibility audit and application packet completeness. Part 3 (Venue 2, 20) on the regional choice with topic-fit and date-alignment rationale, application packet, and v1.1 revision plan. Part 4 (Venue 3, 15) on the stretch venue choice and eligibility audit. Part 5 (Gantt + dependency graph, 15) on visual clarity and dependency naming. Part 6 (budget + contingency, 20) on line-item budget per venue, cohort-pooling rationale, and contingency plans for each rejection scenario.

The single highest-signal check: **for each of the three venues, does your eligibility-audit document include a verbatim copy of the venue's published eligibility criteria with a per-criterion check mark?** If yes, you have done the homework. If no, you have a plan based on assumed eligibility — and assumed eligibility is the leading cause of post-submission disqualification.

## The bridge to PS 12.5 and beyond

PS 12.4 produces the *eighteen-month conference plan*. PS 12.5 zooms out further to the *five-year research-identity arc* — the through-line from your camp paper to three follow-up projects that, by 2031, define a coherent research line. The conference plan is the operational scaffolding for the next eighteen months; the five-year memo is the identity that the conference plan serves. Treat the two as nested: the conference plan is the inner loop, the five-year memo is the outer loop.

*End of PS 12.4. Three venues, eighteen months, audited deadlines, named contingencies. Begin with venue 1; the rest flows from the feedback that venue 1 produces. By the end of the week the FMA application is half-drafted, the EFA submission window is on the calendar, and the stretch-venue application is in the planning stage. The path from camp graduate to working researcher runs through these venues, in order.*
