Ch 12.5 — What Comes After Submission: the Reviewer Wait, the Desk-Reject, the R&R, and How to Build the Five-Year Research Identity That Survives Both

You have submitted to the Schar Young Scholars Journal (Ch 12.1). You have posted to SSRN (Ch 12.2). You understand the decade-long arc (Ch 12.3). You have the eighteen-month conference plan (Ch 12.4). The Week 12 work is, in one sense, done. The paper is in the public record; the next conference is in October. And yet the next eight weeks — between submission and the first editor’s decision — are the weeks that, more than any other stretch in the camp, separate students who continue research from students who quietly stop. This chapter is about those weeks, and about the years that follow, and about how to build a research identity that survives the silences, the rejections, and the slow grinding nature of academic writing.

Here is the move this chapter turns on, before the machinery. The reviewer wait, the desk-reject, the revise-and-resubmit, and the eventual acceptance are not events that happen to your paper; they are events that happen to you, and what makes you a researcher five years from now is how you respond to them. A paper is a single artifact; a research identity is a multi-year posture toward the unknown — toward the question you do not yet know how to answer, the data you do not yet know how to obtain, the writing you do not yet know how to do, the rejection you do not yet know how to absorb, and the longer-than-expected silence you do not yet know how to wait through. The discipline of building a research identity is the discipline of choosing this posture deliberately, against the strong pulls of impatience, ego, and the human desire for quick verdicts. The discipline is what the camp has been training all summer, in different forms, in every week. This chapter assembles the pieces into the practice that, if you sustain it for the next five years, makes you a researcher.

We will work through the post-submission emotional rhythm honestly (§§1–3), the desk-reject as the modal first outcome and how to absorb it (§4), the R&R as a stylized form of editing-by-committee and how to do it well (§§5–7), the longer waits and the longer rejections that mark the journey to publication (§8), the research-identity practices that sustain the long work (§§9–11), and what the five years after the camp can look like if the practices hold (§§12–13). This chapter is more about how to live in the world of academic research than about specific technical maneuvers; it closes the camp by giving you the posture that will determine whether the technical content of Weeks 1–11 stays alive in your life or fades into a single-summer memory.

1. The post-submission silence

The day you submit to a journal, the next thing that happens is silence. The manuscript-number email confirms within an hour that the submission was received. Then nothing, for days, weeks, months.

The silence is structural, not personal. The journal’s editor reads the submission within five business days; she decides whether to assign referees (the desk-decision) or to desk-reject, and that decision typically arrives within three weeks. If the paper is sent to referees, the referees have eight to twelve weeks to return reports. After the reports arrive, the editor synthesizes and writes the decision letter; this takes another two to four weeks. Total time from submission to first decision: 14–22 weeks, median around 16 weeks for the Schar Young Scholars Journal (faster than most adult journals’ 24-week median).

During this silence, you have approximately zero leverage over the process. You cannot speed the editor; you cannot prompt the referees; you cannot improve the paper (the version under review is locked). The only honest action is to wait.

The wait is, however, only superficially passive. The first week of silence is relief — the work is done, the file is sent, the burden has lifted. The second week is curiosity — what is the editor thinking? The third week is mild anxiety — has the editor read it yet? The sixth week is genuine anxiety — is the silence bad news? The tenth week is the temptation to email the editorial office “to check on the status,” which is the move that ScholarOne explicitly recommends against and that editors uniformly mildly resent (the editorial-office assistant deals with twenty such emails per day; he answers them politely but they do not help). The fifteenth week is the resolution of either receiving the decision letter or facing the harder anxiety of “is it taking longer than expected because they hate the paper or because the referees are slow?”

Most experienced researchers describe the post-submission silence in nearly identical terms: emotionally turbulent in the early weeks, then resigned, then turbulent again as the decision approaches. The discipline is to acknowledge the emotional pattern (it is normal, every researcher experiences it) and to not let it become destructive — not to constantly check the ScholarOne portal, not to send the prodding email, not to draft and re-draft the next-submission cover letter in anticipation of needing it.

The healthy practice during the wait is to do other work. Start another project. Refine the next paper. Apply to conferences. Read the literature you should have been reading. The post-submission wait is not a passive interval; it is an opportunity to invest in the rest of the portfolio (Ch 12.3 §13). Maya, in the 14 weeks between her October 2026 submission and her February 2027 first-decision letter, can: (1) prepare the FMA Undergraduate Poster, (2) attend the AEA in January, (3) start drafting the Carroll Round application, (4) begin reading for the next research question (perhaps a follow-up on consumer-credit access more broadly), (5) take care of college applications and senior-year coursework. The 14 weeks pass quickly when filled with other research-adjacent work.

2. The four outcomes — what to expect

The first decision letter contains one of four outcomes. Each demands a different response. Knowing the categories before you receive the letter helps you respond effectively.

Outcome 1 — Acceptance, full or conditional. Rare on first submission (around 5% of submissions at any journal); essentially nonexistent on first submission at top-tier journals. Conditional accept means “we will publish if you address these specific items in the response.” Full accept means “publish as-is.” If you receive either, the work shifts to Phase 6 (copy-editing and proof; Ch 12.3 §8). Celebrate, briefly, and prepare the revisions if conditional.

Outcome 2 — Revise-and-resubmit (R&R). The most desirable common outcome (around 15–25% of submissions). The editor’s letter says “we are interested in this paper, but it needs substantial revision before we can decide.” The letter is accompanied by two or three referee reports (typically 5–25 pages each) and an editor’s synthesis. The R&R is the publication mechanism of empirical finance; most papers that publish go through one to three R&Rs before final acceptance. We discuss the R&R response in §§5–7.

Outcome 3 — Reject after review. A “soft” rejection — the paper went to referees, the referees found problems too substantial for revision, the editor concurs. The decision letter includes the referee reports, which are useful even though the answer is no. About 35–45% of submissions at most journals. The right next step is to absorb the referee comments, revise the paper, and submit to a different journal. The reports are valuable; treat them as you would treat a high-quality discussant.

Outcome 4 — Desk-reject. The “hard” rejection — the editor returned the paper without sending to referees. The most common outcome at top journals (around 40% at JF). The desk-reject letter is short (often one or two paragraphs) and does not include detailed feedback; the editor judged the paper outside the journal’s scope, beneath the journal’s quality bar, or in a topical area the journal does not currently prioritize. About 30–40% of submissions overall.

For Maya’s submission to the Schar Young Scholars Journal, the modal outcome is R&R (the journal’s editorial policy favors revise-and-resubmit decisions over rejection for precollege papers, because the journal’s mission includes developmental support). The realistic outcome distribution is approximately: 50% R&R, 20% conditional accept, 15% reject after review, 15% desk-reject. The expected first-decision outcome is therefore an R&R; Maya should prepare for this.

For papers targeting adult journals — say, the JF target of the Rainbow paper from Ch 12.3 — the outcome distribution is different: 5% conditional accept, 15% R&R, 30% reject after review, 40% desk-reject, 10% other (sometimes “transfer to other journal in the publisher’s family”). The expected first-decision outcome is a desk-reject. This is not a failure of the paper; it is the modal outcome of the system.

3. The desk-reject — how to absorb it

The desk-reject is the most psychologically difficult outcome to process, because the feedback is minimal. The editor’s letter often reads:

“Thank you for submitting your manuscript to the Journal of Finance. After careful consideration, the editorial board has decided that the paper is not a good fit for our journal at this time. We encourage you to submit to a more specialized journal where the paper may find a more appropriate audience. We wish you the best in your continued research.”

That is the entire feedback. Three sentences. No detail on why. The author is left with no information about what was wrong.

The first move on receiving a desk-reject is to not respond emotionally. The desk-reject is structural, not personal; the editor made a triage decision based on a 90-minute read, and that read does not represent the paper’s full quality. The second move is to parse what little information is in the letter. “Not a good fit at this time” is editor code for one of several things: the question is outside the journal’s current priority areas; the empirical strategy is judged unlikely to meet the journal’s identification standards; the topical area is already well-represented in the journal’s pipeline; or the paper’s contribution claim is judged insufficiently differentiated from prior published work. Parsing the letter against the journal’s recent issues can sometimes reveal which interpretation is correct.

The third move is to send the paper to the next venue. Do not delay. Top researchers have a “stretch journal → next-tier journal → safety journal” plan for every paper; the desk-reject moves the paper from the stretch to the next-tier. For the Rainbow paper, the plan is JF → JFE → RFS → JFQA, with each step taking 16 weeks. For Maya’s paper (if it desk-rejects from the Schar Young Scholars Journal, which is unlikely but possible), the plan is the Schar Young Scholars Journal → Issues in Political Economy (an undergraduate-focused economics journal) → the Journal of Student Research (a broader undergraduate journal) → the Princeton Undergraduate Research Journal of Economics. Each step takes 12 weeks. The plan is set in advance, before the first decision; this prevents the desk-reject from triggering decision paralysis.

The fourth move is to learn from the desk-reject in aggregate. A single desk-reject is noise; three desk-rejects in succession is signal. If three different journals desk-reject the paper, the paper has a problem the author has not yet diagnosed. The diagnostic move is to send the paper to two trusted colleagues with a specific request: “I’ve had three desk-rejects on this paper. Can you read the abstract and the introduction and tell me what you think the editors are seeing that I’m not?” The trusted colleagues usually identify the problem within an hour; it is typically either (a) the contribution claim is overstated, (b) the identification strategy has an obvious threat the paper does not address, or (c) the writing is unclear in ways the author cannot see from inside it. Fix the problem, then resume the submission ladder.

The fifth move is to not let the desk-reject change your view of the paper’s value. Many papers that ultimately published at top journals had multiple desk-rejects along the way. Krueger and Mas (2004) on tire recalls was desk-rejected at one of the top economics journals before publishing at QJE. The Bhattacharya, Galpin, and Haslag (2020) high-frequency trading paper was desk-rejected three times before finding a home at RFS. Desk-rejects are part of the process; the discipline is to absorb them without letting them define the paper or the author.

4. The R&R — what an R&R actually is

A revise-and-resubmit decision is the most consequential outcome short of acceptance. The decision letter typically runs 1–3 pages from the editor plus 5–25 pages per referee (typically two or three referees). The letter says, in essence: “We are interested. Address these concerns. Resubmit, and we will likely send the revision to the same referees for second-round review.”

The R&R is not an acceptance; it is an option to continue the process. Most R&Rs go through one revision and a second decision; the second decision is either (a) acceptance, often conditional; (b) another R&R, demanding further revision; or (c) rejection, if the revision did not address the referees’ concerns. About 50% of first-round R&Rs at top journals end in acceptance after one revision; 30% in a second R&R; 20% in rejection after the revision.

The R&R is also not a guarantee. The editor’s invitation to resubmit is genuine, but the resubmission is judged against a stricter standard than the initial submission — the referees have invested time in the paper, and they expect the revision to address their concerns substantively. A revision that does not address a referee’s main critique well will be rejected at the second round; the second-round rejection is the worst outcome short of the desk-reject, because the author has invested another six to twelve months in the paper before learning that the path forward is blocked.

The R&R is also a contract. By accepting the invitation to revise and resubmit, the author commits to the revision. Most journals expect the revision within 6–12 months; some impose explicit deadlines. The revision is a non-trivial labor — typically 4–8 weeks of focused work for an empirical-finance paper, spread over 3–6 months calendar time because the author has other obligations. The R&R is therefore a multi-month project, and the author should plan it accordingly.

5. The response letter — how to write it

The single most important artifact of the R&R revision is the response letter (also called the “response to referees” or the “memo”). The response letter accompanies the revised manuscript and walks the editor and referees through, point by point, how the revision addresses each comment they made. The response letter for a top-finance-journal R&R can run 30–80 pages — sometimes longer than the original manuscript.

The response letter has three audiences: (1) the editor, who skims it for structure and tone; (2) the original referees, who read every point that addressed their specific comment; (3) the future-you of three years from now, who will need to remember why specific revisions were made.

The structure of a publication-grade response letter is:

Section 1 — Cover letter. A one-page summary that thanks the editor and referees, characterizes the revision at a high level (“We have addressed all the concerns raised by the referees and the editor; the revision adds three new robustness analyses, restructures the literature review, and tightens the identification discussion”), and previews the major changes (“The most significant revisions are: (a) the new instrumental-variables analysis in Section 5.3 addressing Referee 2’s identification concern; (b) the heterogeneity analysis in Section 6 addressing the editor’s external-validity concern; (c) the streamlined literature review responding to Referee 1’s organizational comments”).

Section 2 — Response to the Editor. Address each point in the editor’s letter, in order. For each, quote the editor’s point in italic block-quotes, then respond in regular text. Maintain a respectful tone; the editor is the ultimate arbiter.

Section 3 — Response to Referee 1. Number every point Referee 1 made (typically 8–25 points per referee). For each point, quote the referee’s words, describe how the revision addresses the point, name the section of the revised manuscript where the change appears (with page numbers if possible), and — when appropriate — explain why a counter-argument is justified rather than capitulating to a comment you disagree with. Push back when you should; agreement with every comment is a sign of weakness, not strength. The good response letter acknowledges valid critiques, addresses them with substance, and disagrees respectfully with the critiques that are mistaken.

Section 4 — Response to Referee 2 (and Referee 3, if applicable). Same structure as Section 3.

Section 5 — Acknowledgment. A brief paragraph thanking the editor and referees for the careful engagement; noting any third-party feedback (conference discussants, colleagues, etc.) that informed the revision; and committing the authors to any agreed-upon next steps.

A response letter written carefully takes 2–4 weeks of focused work. It is not a short document. It is also not optional; a perfunctory response letter is the single most common reason a second-round R&R outcome is rejection. Referees read response letters with care; they notice when their main critique has been brushed aside.

6. The principles of responding to referees

Beyond the structure, the response letter has a set of principles that distinguish good responses from bad responses.

Principle 1 — quote the referee verbatim. Do not paraphrase the referee’s comment in your response. Quote the exact language. This makes it impossible for the referee to feel that the response missed the point; it also creates a precise record of what was asked and what was answered.

Principle 2 — address the comment, not the surrounding text. If the referee said “the standard errors should be clustered at the state level rather than the county level,” respond to that exact comment with a specific change (“We have re-estimated all tables with state-level clustering; the results are reported in Tables 2 and 3 of the revision. The point estimate is unchanged at 1.4 pp, and the standard error increases from 0.30 to 0.42, narrowing the t-statistic from 4.7 to 3.3 but maintaining statistical significance at the 1% level”). Do not respond by saying “we have addressed the standard-errors concern” without specifying how.

Principle 3 — name the section, page, and table where the change is. This makes the referee’s check fast: she does not have to search the revised manuscript to confirm the change happened. Reference the revision with precision.

Principle 4 — disagree when you should, but disagree carefully. Some referee comments are wrong. The discipline is to identify which ones, and to push back without insulting the referee. The template is: “We respectfully disagree with this characterization. The referee’s concern is X; however, we believe that X is addressed by the discussion in Section 4.2, which establishes that [argument]. We have added a footnote on page 17 making this explicit, so that future readers do not have the same concern.” This response acknowledges the referee’s perspective, names a counter-argument, and adds clarifying text — the referee usually accepts this response even when she is not fully persuaded.

Principle 5 — be honest about what you cannot do. Some referee requests are infeasible (the data does not exist, the method does not work for this setting, the requested analysis would take a separate paper). Acknowledge this honestly: “We agree that an instrumental-variables strategy would strengthen the identification, but the variation the referee proposes is not available in our data. We have added a discussion of this limitation in Section 5.4 and have run a related but feasible robustness analysis (the matched-counties analysis in Section 5.5) that we believe partially addresses the underlying concern.” Honest acknowledgment of limits is more credible than evasive language.

Principle 6 — do not surface bias against the referee. Even if the referee misread the paper, misunderstood the data, or made a comment that you find unfair, the response should be uniformly respectful. The journal’s editor reads the response letter and notes the tone; an antagonistic response damages the paper’s chances regardless of substantive merit.

Principle 7 — keep track of what changed. Maintain a changelog (a separate document, not part of the response letter) that lists every change in the revised manuscript: new tables, new figures, new sections, removed sections, modified paragraphs. The changelog is for your records, not the journal’s; it helps you remember, six months from now in the second round, what you changed and why.

7. The R&R timeline — how to budget

A first-round R&R typically allows 6 months for revision (sometimes 4, sometimes 12). The temptation is to wait until the deadline approaches to start; the discipline is to start within two weeks of receiving the letter.

The pacing for a 6-month R&R is approximately:

  • Weeks 1–2. Read the referee reports carefully, twice. The first read is for understanding; the second is for diagnosis. Make a list of every point each referee raised, organized by referee. Identify the “main critiques” — typically one or two per referee, the points the referee returned to repeatedly. The main critiques are what the revision must address most thoroughly.

  • Weeks 3–4. Triage the points (the same triage discipline from Ch 10.1). For each point: estimate the cost of addressing it (in days of work), estimate the value (how much does it strengthen the paper if addressed?), and identify the threat (does ignoring this comment harm the paper’s chances at the journal?). Build the revision plan: which points get addressed in detail, which get brief acknowledgment, which get respectful disagreement.

  • Weeks 5–12. Do the empirical work. New robustness tests, new heterogeneity analyses, new identification checks. The work is structurally the same as the Week 8 robustness battery — different specifications, new tables, new figures — but scaled to the specific demands of the referee comments.

  • Weeks 13–16. Rewrite the manuscript to integrate the new empirical work. Revise the abstract (Ch 11.1), the tables (Ch 11.2), the figures (Ch 11.3), the introduction (Ch 11.4), and the literature review (Ch 11.5) as needed. The writing discipline of Week 11 is the same.

  • Weeks 17–22. Write the response letter. This is 2–4 weeks of focused work. Use the principles of §6.

  • Weeks 23–24. Final verification. Run the eight-item submission checklist from Ch 12.1 §9. Submit the revision through ScholarOne. Wait for the second-round decision.

The 6-month timeline assumes the revision is one of multiple things the author is doing; it is not 6 months of full-time work. A graduate student or junior faculty member often has two or three R&Rs running simultaneously, plus teaching, plus other obligations. An undergraduate author can carry one R&R during a semester with careful time management.

8. The longer arc — multiple R&Rs and the journal-ladder strategy

We addressed the journal-ladder strategy briefly in §3; here we develop it in detail.

Most papers that publish at top finance journals go through more than one journal’s submission process. The Rainbow paper’s likely trajectory is JF (likely desk-reject or reject) → JFE (likely R&R) → second-round at JFE → eventual acceptance. The total time from first submission to acceptance, for the median top-finance paper, is 28 months across a median of 2.1 journal cycles.

The journal-ladder strategy is the explicit plan for which journals to target in what order. The principles:

Start at the stretch journal. Submit first to the highest-tier journal where the paper might plausibly land. The cost of starting too high (a desk-reject after 12 weeks) is small compared to the cost of starting too low (an acceptance at a tier-too-low journal that forecloses the higher-tier publication). Aim high.

Descend the ladder deliberately. After a rejection, submit to the next-tier journal within four weeks. The four-week delay is to absorb the referee reports (if any) and to revise the paper to address the most important critiques; the discipline of not waiting longer is to maintain momentum.

Do not skip tiers indiscriminately. If JF desk-rejects, the natural next step is JFE (the second-tier within the top three). Skipping to RFS or to a fourth-tier journal sacrifices the credentialing value of the second submission. The exception is when the desk-reject letter explicitly suggests a different journal (“we suggest this paper may be a better fit at the Journal of Banking and Finance”); take the editor’s suggestion seriously.

Know when to stop. Not every paper publishes. Some papers, after three or four rejections, are correctly assessed as below publication threshold and should be retired (or thoroughly rewritten as a different paper). The discipline of stopping is hard; the temptation to keep submitting is real. The signal that stopping is right: when the cumulative referee feedback agrees that the paper has a problem the author cannot or will not address. If three sets of referees say “the identification is not credible” and you cannot improve the identification, the paper will not publish.

Track the ladder, paper by paper. Maintain a spreadsheet with: paper title; current journal; submission date; expected decision date; outcome; next-journal plan if rejected. The spreadsheet is the operational memory of the portfolio (Ch 12.3 §13).

For papers in the precollege/early-undergraduate tier — Maya’s fair-lending paper, Devon’s crypto paper, etc. — the ladder is different in structure but similar in logic. The first venue is the Schar Young Scholars Journal (or an analogous undergraduate journal); the next venues are Issues in Political Economy, the Journal of Student Research, or the Princeton Undergraduate Research Journal of Economics. The ladder runs across undergraduate-friendly venues until either an acceptance is achieved or the paper is retired. The discipline is the same as for adult papers; the venues are different.

9. The five-year research identity — the practices that sustain

We turn now to the larger question: how do you build a research identity that survives the silences and the rejections, not for a year but for five?

The research identity has six practices.

Practice 1 — read three papers a week. Empirical finance has a rapidly evolving research frontier. The papers being published this year are the inputs to next year’s literature reviews. Set aside three hours a week to read three new working papers — typically pulled from the SSRN weekly digest in your subscribed networks, the NBER working-paper feed (if accessible), and the cited literature in papers you are reading for your own projects. Do not try to read more than three per week; the discipline is consistent depth, not volume. Over five years, this practice produces around 750 papers read; over a decade, 1,500. This is the substrate of a research career.

Practice 2 — write 30 minutes a day, almost every day. The discipline of writing is the discipline of producing pages. Empirical-finance papers are 40-to-80-page documents; producing one paper per year requires writing perhaps 500 publishable pages over a career, which decomposes to about 2 pages per week of average output. Thirty minutes a day, six days a week, produces roughly this. The 30 minutes is non-negotiable; it happens whether or not you “feel inspired” (inspiration is unreliable; routine is reliable). Most active researchers describe their writing routine in similar terms: a morning block, before email, before meetings, before anything that competes for attention.

Practice 3 — present at one conference per year, minimum. The conference circuit (Ch 12.4) is the feedback engine of academic research. A researcher who does not present at conferences does not get the feedback that distinguishes a Phase-3 paper from a Phase-4 paper. Plan for one conference per year, minimum; two or three is better.

Practice 4 — maintain the portfolio. At any time, have one paper in late-stage revision (Phase 5), one paper in early-stage circulation (Phase 2), and one paper in Phase 1 (idea). The portfolio practice protects against the failure of any single paper (Ch 12.3 §13); it also ensures that the slow phases of any paper are offset by the active phases of others. The portfolio is what makes a career; no single paper carries the load.

Practice 5 — build relationships. Academic finance is a small world. The senior faculty member who discussed your paper at the SFS Cavalcade in 2027 may become the senior referee on a paper of yours in 2031. The advisor of your undergraduate thesis in 2028 may become a coauthor on a paper in 2033. The graduate student who sat next to you at the AEA poster session in 2027 may become the colleague at a different institution that you collaborate with for two decades. Invest in the relationships. Send the thank-you emails (Ch 12.4 §11). Follow up on coffee invitations. Maintain contact with the camp cohort. Over five years, the relationships compound; over a decade, they are the infrastructure of your career.

Practice 6 — keep a research journal. A research journal is a private notebook (paper, or a private file) where you record: the question you are working on; the result you got today; the surprise you encountered; the literature you read; the conference comments you received; the long-term plan you are working toward. The journal is not for publication; it is for you, to maintain the thread of your thinking across years and across paper portfolios. Most active researchers keep some form of research journal; the formats vary widely, but the function is consistent. The journal is what allows you to remember, five years from now, why you cared about the question you are working on today.

The six practices, sustained, produce a research identity. The identity is what survives the rejections, the desk-rejects, the long R&R cycles, the slow conference acceptances. The identity is what you are still doing when the artifact (the paper) is in the hands of editors and referees, and there is no urgent task in front of you. The identity is the durable thing; the paper is the visible thing.

10. The five-year question — what do you want to be working on?

The harder question than “what paper am I writing now?” is “what do I want to be working on in five years?” This is the question of research identity at the strategic level.

The five-year question has three sub-questions.

Sub-question 1 — what substantive area? The substantive area you choose at age 17 may or may not be the area you work on at age 22. ## 1. The post-submission silence

The day you submit to a journal, the next thing that happens is silence. The manuscript-number email confirms within an hour that the submission was received. Then nothing, for days, weeks, months.

The silence is structural, not personal. The journal's editor reads the submission within five business days; she decides whether to assign referees (the desk-decision) or to desk-reject, and that decision typically arrives within three weeks. If the paper is sent to referees, the referees have eight to twelve weeks to return reports. After the reports arrive, the editor synthesizes and writes the decision letter; this takes another two to four weeks. Total time from submission to first decision: 14–22 weeks, median around 16 weeks for the Schar Young Scholars Journal (faster than most adult journals' 24-week median).

During this silence, you have approximately zero leverage over the process. You cannot speed the editor; you cannot prompt the referees; you cannot improve the paper (the version under review is locked). The only honest action is to wait.

The wait is, however, only superficially passive. The first week of silence is relief — the work is done, the file is sent, the burden has lifted. The second week is curiosity — what is the editor thinking? The third week is mild anxiety — has the editor read it yet? The sixth week is genuine anxiety — is the silence bad news? The tenth week is the temptation to email the editorial office "to check on the status," which is the move that ScholarOne explicitly recommends against and that editors uniformly mildly resent (the editorial-office assistant deals with twenty such emails per day; he answers them politely but they do not help). The fifteenth week is the resolution of either receiving the decision letter or facing the harder anxiety of "is it taking longer than expected because they hate the paper or because the referees are slow?"

Most experienced researchers describe the post-submission silence in nearly identical terms: emotionally turbulent in the early weeks, then resigned, then turbulent again as the decision approaches. The discipline is to acknowledge the emotional pattern (it is normal, every researcher experiences it) and to not let it become destructive — not to constantly check the ScholarOne portal, not to send the prodding email, not to draft and re-draft the next-submission cover letter in anticipation of needing it.

The healthy practice during the wait is to *do other work*. Start another project. Refine the next paper. Apply to conferences. Read the literature you should have been reading. The post-submission wait is not a passive interval; it is an opportunity to invest in the rest of the portfolio (Ch 12.3 §13). Maya, in the 14 weeks between her October 2026 submission and her February 2027 first-decision letter, can: (1) prepare the FMA Undergraduate Poster, (2) attend the AEA in January, (3) start drafting the Carroll Round application, (4) begin reading for the next research question (perhaps a follow-up on consumer-credit access more broadly), (5) take care of college applications and senior-year coursework. The 14 weeks pass quickly when filled with other research-adjacent work.

## 2. The four outcomes — what to expect

The first decision letter contains one of four outcomes. Each demands a different response. Knowing the categories before you receive the letter helps you respond effectively.

**Outcome 1 — Acceptance, full or conditional.** Rare on first submission (around 5% of submissions at any journal); essentially nonexistent on first submission at top-tier journals. Conditional accept means "we will publish if you address these specific items in the response." Full accept means "publish as-is." If you receive either, the work shifts to Phase 6 (copy-editing and proof; Ch 12.3 §8). Celebrate, briefly, and prepare the revisions if conditional.

**Outcome 2 — Revise-and-resubmit (R&R).** The most desirable common outcome (around 15–25% of submissions). The editor's letter says "we are interested in this paper, but it needs substantial revision before we can decide." The letter is accompanied by two or three referee reports (typically 5–25 pages each) and an editor's synthesis. The R&R is *the* publication mechanism of empirical finance; most papers that publish go through one to three R&Rs before final acceptance. We discuss the R&R response in §§5–7.

**Outcome 3 — Reject after review.** A "soft" rejection — the paper went to referees, the referees found problems too substantial for revision, the editor concurs. The decision letter includes the referee reports, which are useful even though the answer is no. About 35–45% of submissions at most journals. The right next step is to absorb the referee comments, revise the paper, and submit to a different journal. The reports are valuable; treat them as you would treat a high-quality discussant.

**Outcome 4 — Desk-reject.** The "hard" rejection — the editor returned the paper without sending to referees. The most common outcome at top journals (around 40% at JF). The desk-reject letter is short (often one or two paragraphs) and does not include detailed feedback; the editor judged the paper outside the journal's scope, beneath the journal's quality bar, or in a topical area the journal does not currently prioritize. About 30–40% of submissions overall.

For Maya's submission to the Schar Young Scholars Journal, the modal outcome is R&R (the journal's editorial policy favors revise-and-resubmit decisions over rejection for precollege papers, because the journal's mission includes developmental support). The realistic outcome distribution is approximately: 50% R&R, 20% conditional accept, 15% reject after review, 15% desk-reject. The expected first-decision outcome is therefore an R&R; Maya should prepare for this.

For papers targeting adult journals — say, the JF target of the Rainbow paper from Ch 12.3 — the outcome distribution is different: 5% conditional accept, 15% R&R, 30% reject after review, 40% desk-reject, 10% other (sometimes "transfer to other journal in the publisher's family"). The expected first-decision outcome is a desk-reject. This is not a failure of the paper; it is the modal outcome of the system.

## 3. The desk-reject — how to absorb it

The desk-reject is the most psychologically difficult outcome to process, because the feedback is minimal. The editor's letter often reads:

> "Thank you for submitting your manuscript to the Journal of Finance. After careful consideration, the editorial board has decided that the paper is not a good fit for our journal at this time. We encourage you to submit to a more specialized journal where the paper may find a more appropriate audience. We wish you the best in your continued research."

That is the entire feedback. Three sentences. No detail on why. The author is left with no information about what was wrong.

The first move on receiving a desk-reject is to *not respond emotionally*. The desk-reject is structural, not personal; the editor made a triage decision based on a 90-minute read, and that read does not represent the paper's full quality. The second move is to *parse what little information is in the letter*. "Not a good fit at this time" is editor code for one of several things: the question is outside the journal's current priority areas; the empirical strategy is judged unlikely to meet the journal's identification standards; the topical area is already well-represented in the journal's pipeline; or the paper's contribution claim is judged insufficiently differentiated from prior published work. Parsing the letter against the journal's recent issues can sometimes reveal which interpretation is correct.

The third move is to *send the paper to the next venue*. Do not delay. Top researchers have a "stretch journal → next-tier journal → safety journal" plan for every paper; the desk-reject moves the paper from the stretch to the next-tier. For the Rainbow paper, the plan is JF → JFE → RFS → JFQA, with each step taking 16 weeks. For Maya's paper (if it desk-rejects from the Schar Young Scholars Journal, which is unlikely but possible), the plan is the Schar Young Scholars Journal → Issues in Political Economy (an undergraduate-focused economics journal) → the Journal of Student Research (a broader undergraduate journal) → the Princeton Undergraduate Research Journal of Economics. Each step takes 12 weeks. The plan is set in advance, before the first decision; this prevents the desk-reject from triggering decision paralysis.

The fourth move is to *learn from the desk-reject in aggregate*. A single desk-reject is noise; three desk-rejects in succession is signal. If three different journals desk-reject the paper, the paper has a problem the author has not yet diagnosed. The diagnostic move is to send the paper to two trusted colleagues with a specific request: "I've had three desk-rejects on this paper. Can you read the abstract and the introduction and tell me what you think the editors are seeing that I'm not?" The trusted colleagues usually identify the problem within an hour; it is typically either (a) the contribution claim is overstated, (b) the identification strategy has an obvious threat the paper does not address, or (c) the writing is unclear in ways the author cannot see from inside it. Fix the problem, then resume the submission ladder.

The fifth move is to *not let the desk-reject change your view of the paper's value*. Many papers that ultimately published at top journals had multiple desk-rejects along the way. Krueger and Mas (2004) on tire recalls was desk-rejected at one of the top economics journals before publishing at QJE. The Bhattacharya, Galpin, and Haslag (2020) high-frequency trading paper was desk-rejected three times before finding a home at RFS. Desk-rejects are part of the process; the discipline is to absorb them without letting them define the paper or the author.

## 4. The R&R — what an R&R actually is

A revise-and-resubmit decision is the most consequential outcome short of acceptance. The decision letter typically runs 1–3 pages from the editor plus 5–25 pages per referee (typically two or three referees). The letter says, in essence: "We are interested. Address these concerns. Resubmit, and we will likely send the revision to the same referees for second-round review."

The R&R is not an acceptance; it is an *option to continue the process*. Most R&Rs go through one revision and a second decision; the second decision is either (a) acceptance, often conditional; (b) another R&R, demanding further revision; or (c) rejection, if the revision did not address the referees' concerns. About 50% of first-round R&Rs at top journals end in acceptance after one revision; 30% in a second R&R; 20% in rejection after the revision.

The R&R is also not a guarantee. The editor's invitation to resubmit is genuine, but the resubmission is judged against a stricter standard than the initial submission — the referees have invested time in the paper, and they expect the revision to address their concerns substantively. A revision that does not address a referee's main critique well will be rejected at the second round; the second-round rejection is the worst outcome short of the desk-reject, because the author has invested another six to twelve months in the paper before learning that the path forward is blocked.

The R&R is also a *contract*. By accepting the invitation to revise and resubmit, the author commits to the revision. Most journals expect the revision within 6–12 months; some impose explicit deadlines. The revision is a non-trivial labor — typically 4–8 weeks of focused work for an empirical-finance paper, spread over 3–6 months calendar time because the author has other obligations. The R&R is therefore a *multi-month project*, and the author should plan it accordingly.

## 5. The response letter — how to write it

The single most important artifact of the R&R revision is the *response letter* (also called the "response to referees" or the "memo"). The response letter accompanies the revised manuscript and walks the editor and referees through, point by point, how the revision addresses each comment they made. The response letter for a top-finance-journal R&R can run 30–80 pages — sometimes longer than the original manuscript.

The response letter has three audiences: (1) the editor, who skims it for structure and tone; (2) the original referees, who read every point that addressed their specific comment; (3) the future-you of three years from now, who will need to remember why specific revisions were made.

The structure of a publication-grade response letter is:

**Section 1 — Cover letter.** A one-page summary that thanks the editor and referees, characterizes the revision at a high level ("We have addressed all the concerns raised by the referees and the editor; the revision adds three new robustness analyses, restructures the literature review, and tightens the identification discussion"), and previews the major changes ("The most significant revisions are: (a) the new instrumental-variables analysis in Section 5.3 addressing Referee 2's identification concern; (b) the heterogeneity analysis in Section 6 addressing the editor's external-validity concern; (c) the streamlined literature review responding to Referee 1's organizational comments").

**Section 2 — Response to the Editor.** Address each point in the editor's letter, in order. For each, quote the editor's point in italic block-quotes, then respond in regular text. Maintain a respectful tone; the editor is the ultimate arbiter.

**Section 3 — Response to Referee 1.** Number every point Referee 1 made (typically 8–25 points per referee). For each point, quote the referee's words, describe how the revision addresses the point, name the section of the revised manuscript where the change appears (with page numbers if possible), and — when appropriate — explain why a counter-argument is justified rather than capitulating to a comment you disagree with. *Push back when you should*; agreement with every comment is a sign of weakness, not strength. The good response letter acknowledges valid critiques, addresses them with substance, and disagrees respectfully with the critiques that are mistaken.

**Section 4 — Response to Referee 2 (and Referee 3, if applicable).** Same structure as Section 3.

**Section 5 — Acknowledgment.** A brief paragraph thanking the editor and referees for the careful engagement; noting any third-party feedback (conference discussants, colleagues, etc.) that informed the revision; and committing the authors to any agreed-upon next steps.

A response letter written carefully takes 2–4 weeks of focused work. It is not a short document. It is also not optional; a perfunctory response letter is the single most common reason a second-round R&R outcome is rejection. Referees read response letters with care; they notice when their main critique has been brushed aside.

## 6. The principles of responding to referees

Beyond the structure, the response letter has a set of principles that distinguish good responses from bad responses.

**Principle 1 — quote the referee verbatim.** Do not paraphrase the referee's comment in your response. Quote the exact language. This makes it impossible for the referee to feel that the response missed the point; it also creates a precise record of what was asked and what was answered.

**Principle 2 — address the comment, not the surrounding text.** If the referee said "the standard errors should be clustered at the state level rather than the county level," respond to that exact comment with a specific change ("We have re-estimated all tables with state-level clustering; the results are reported in Tables 2 and 3 of the revision. The point estimate is unchanged at 1.4 pp, and the standard error increases from 0.30 to 0.42, narrowing the t-statistic from 4.7 to 3.3 but maintaining statistical significance at the 1% level"). Do not respond by saying "we have addressed the standard-errors concern" without specifying how.

**Principle 3 — name the section, page, and table where the change is.** This makes the referee's check fast: she does not have to search the revised manuscript to confirm the change happened. Reference the revision with precision.

**Principle 4 — disagree when you should, but disagree carefully.** Some referee comments are wrong. The discipline is to identify which ones, and to push back without insulting the referee. The template is: "We respectfully disagree with this characterization. The referee's concern is X; however, we believe that X is addressed by the discussion in Section 4.2, which establishes that [argument]. We have added a footnote on page 17 making this explicit, so that future readers do not have the same concern." This response acknowledges the referee's perspective, names a counter-argument, and adds clarifying text — the referee usually accepts this response even when she is not fully persuaded.

**Principle 5 — be honest about what you cannot do.** Some referee requests are infeasible (the data does not exist, the method does not work for this setting, the requested analysis would take a separate paper). Acknowledge this honestly: "We agree that an instrumental-variables strategy would strengthen the identification, but the variation the referee proposes is not available in our data. We have added a discussion of this limitation in Section 5.4 and have run a related but feasible robustness analysis (the matched-counties analysis in Section 5.5) that we believe partially addresses the underlying concern." Honest acknowledgment of limits is more credible than evasive language.

**Principle 6 — do not surface bias against the referee.** Even if the referee misread the paper, misunderstood the data, or made a comment that you find unfair, the response should be uniformly respectful. The journal's editor reads the response letter and notes the tone; an antagonistic response damages the paper's chances regardless of substantive merit.

**Principle 7 — keep track of what changed.** Maintain a changelog (a separate document, not part of the response letter) that lists every change in the revised manuscript: new tables, new figures, new sections, removed sections, modified paragraphs. The changelog is for your records, not the journal's; it helps you remember, six months from now in the second round, what you changed and why.

## 7. The R&R timeline — how to budget

A first-round R&R typically allows 6 months for revision (sometimes 4, sometimes 12). The temptation is to wait until the deadline approaches to start; the discipline is to start within two weeks of receiving the letter.

The pacing for a 6-month R&R is approximately:

- **Weeks 1–2.** Read the referee reports carefully, twice. The first read is for understanding; the second is for diagnosis. Make a list of every point each referee raised, organized by referee. Identify the "main critiques" — typically one or two per referee, the points the referee returned to repeatedly. The main critiques are what the revision must address most thoroughly.

- **Weeks 3–4.** Triage the points (the same triage discipline from Ch 10.1). For each point: estimate the cost of addressing it (in days of work), estimate the value (how much does it strengthen the paper if addressed?), and identify the threat (does ignoring this comment harm the paper's chances at the journal?). Build the revision plan: which points get addressed in detail, which get brief acknowledgment, which get respectful disagreement.

- **Weeks 5–12.** Do the empirical work. New robustness tests, new heterogeneity analyses, new identification checks. The work is structurally the same as the Week 8 robustness battery — different specifications, new tables, new figures — but scaled to the specific demands of the referee comments.

- **Weeks 13–16.** Rewrite the manuscript to integrate the new empirical work. Revise the abstract (Ch 11.1), the tables (Ch 11.2), the figures (Ch 11.3), the introduction (Ch 11.4), and the literature review (Ch 11.5) as needed. The writing discipline of Week 11 is the same.

- **Weeks 17–22.** Write the response letter. This is 2–4 weeks of focused work. Use the principles of §6.

- **Weeks 23–24.** Final verification. Run the eight-item submission checklist from Ch 12.1 §9. Submit the revision through ScholarOne. Wait for the second-round decision.

The 6-month timeline assumes the revision is one of multiple things the author is doing; it is not 6 months of full-time work. A graduate student or junior faculty member often has two or three R&Rs running simultaneously, plus teaching, plus other obligations. An undergraduate author can carry one R&R during a semester with careful time management.

## 8. The longer arc — multiple R&Rs and the journal-ladder strategy

We addressed the journal-ladder strategy briefly in §3; here we develop it in detail.

Most papers that publish at top finance journals go through *more than one* journal's submission process. The Rainbow paper's likely trajectory is JF (likely desk-reject or reject) → JFE (likely R&R) → second-round at JFE → eventual acceptance. The total time from first submission to acceptance, for the median top-finance paper, is 28 months across a median of 2.1 journal cycles.

The journal-ladder strategy is the explicit plan for which journals to target in what order. The principles:

**Start at the stretch journal.** Submit first to the highest-tier journal where the paper might plausibly land. The cost of starting too high (a desk-reject after 12 weeks) is small compared to the cost of starting too low (an acceptance at a tier-too-low journal that forecloses the higher-tier publication). Aim high.

**Descend the ladder deliberately.** After a rejection, submit to the next-tier journal within four weeks. The four-week delay is to absorb the referee reports (if any) and to revise the paper to address the most important critiques; the discipline of not waiting longer is to maintain momentum.

**Do not skip tiers indiscriminately.** If JF desk-rejects, the natural next step is JFE (the second-tier within the top three). Skipping to RFS or to a fourth-tier journal sacrifices the credentialing value of the second submission. The exception is when the desk-reject letter explicitly suggests a different journal ("we suggest this paper may be a better fit at the Journal of Banking and Finance"); take the editor's suggestion seriously.

**Know when to stop.** Not every paper publishes. Some papers, after three or four rejections, are correctly assessed as below publication threshold and should be retired (or thoroughly rewritten as a different paper). The discipline of stopping is hard; the temptation to keep submitting is real. The signal that stopping is right: when the cumulative referee feedback agrees that the paper has a problem the author cannot or will not address. If three sets of referees say "the identification is not credible" and you cannot improve the identification, the paper will not publish.

**Track the ladder, paper by paper.** Maintain a spreadsheet with: paper title; current journal; submission date; expected decision date; outcome; next-journal plan if rejected. The spreadsheet is the operational memory of the portfolio (Ch 12.3 §13).

For papers in the precollege/early-undergraduate tier — Maya's fair-lending paper, Devon's crypto paper, etc. — the ladder is different in structure but similar in logic. The first venue is the Schar Young Scholars Journal (or an analogous undergraduate journal); the next venues are Issues in Political Economy, the Journal of Student Research, or the Princeton Undergraduate Research Journal of Economics. The ladder runs across undergraduate-friendly venues until either an acceptance is achieved or the paper is retired. The discipline is the same as for adult papers; the venues are different.

## 9. The five-year research identity — the practices that sustain

We turn now to the larger question: how do you build a research identity that survives the silences and the rejections, not for a year but for five?

The research identity has six practices.

**Practice 1 — read three papers a week.** Empirical finance has a rapidly evolving research frontier. The papers being published this year are the inputs to next year's literature reviews. Set aside three hours a week to read three new working papers — typically pulled from the SSRN weekly digest in your subscribed networks, the NBER working-paper feed (if accessible), and the cited literature in papers you are reading for your own projects. Do not try to read more than three per week; the discipline is consistent depth, not volume. Over five years, this practice produces around 750 papers read; over a decade, 1,500. This is the substrate of a research career.

**Practice 2 — write 30 minutes a day, almost every day.** The discipline of writing is the discipline of producing pages. Empirical-finance papers are 40-to-80-page documents; producing one paper per year requires writing perhaps 500 publishable pages over a career, which decomposes to about 2 pages per week of average output. Thirty minutes a day, six days a week, produces roughly this. The 30 minutes is non-negotiable; it happens whether or not you "feel inspired" (inspiration is unreliable; routine is reliable). Most active researchers describe their writing routine in similar terms: a morning block, before email, before meetings, before anything that competes for attention.

**Practice 3 — present at one conference per year, minimum.** The conference circuit (Ch 12.4) is the feedback engine of academic research. A researcher who does not present at conferences does not get the feedback that distinguishes a Phase-3 paper from a Phase-4 paper. Plan for one conference per year, minimum; two or three is better.

**Practice 4 — maintain the portfolio.** At any time, have one paper in late-stage revision (Phase 5), one paper in early-stage circulation (Phase 2), and one paper in Phase 1 (idea). The portfolio practice protects against the failure of any single paper (Ch 12.3 §13); it also ensures that the slow phases of any paper are offset by the active phases of others. The portfolio is what makes a career; no single paper carries the load.

**Practice 5 — build relationships.** Academic finance is a small world. The senior faculty member who discussed your paper at the SFS Cavalcade in 2027 may become the senior referee on a paper of yours in 2031. The advisor of your undergraduate thesis in 2028 may become a coauthor on a paper in 2033. The graduate student who sat next to you at the AEA poster session in 2027 may become the colleague at a different institution that you collaborate with for two decades. Invest in the relationships. Send the thank-you emails (Ch 12.4 §11). Follow up on coffee invitations. Maintain contact with the camp cohort. Over five years, the relationships compound; over a decade, they are the infrastructure of your career.

**Practice 6 — keep a research journal.** A research journal is a private notebook (paper, or a private file) where you record: the question you are working on; the result you got today; the surprise you encountered; the literature you read; the conference comments you received; the long-term plan you are working toward. The journal is not for publication; it is for *you*, to maintain the thread of your thinking across years and across paper portfolios. Most active researchers keep some form of research journal; the formats vary widely, but the function is consistent. The journal is what allows you to remember, five years from now, why you cared about the question you are working on today.

The six practices, sustained, produce a research identity. The identity is what survives the rejections, the desk-rejects, the long R&R cycles, the slow conference acceptances. The identity is what you are still doing when the artifact (the paper) is in the hands of editors and referees, and there is no urgent task in front of you. The identity is the durable thing; the paper is the visible thing.

## 10. The five-year question — what do you want to be working on?

The harder question than "what paper am I writing now?" is "what do I want to be working on in five years?" This is the question of research identity at the strategic level.

The five-year question has three sub-questions.

**Sub-question 1 — what substantive area?** The substantive area you choose at age 17 may or may not be the area you work on at age 22. Maya's fair-lending paper is the result of a Week-1-of-camp interest in household-credit access; that interest may evolve toward broader consumer-credit questions, or toward financial-inclusion in international settings, or toward bank-regulation more generally. The substantive evolution is normal; the discipline is to be open to it. The 17-year-old should not commit to a substantive area for life; the substantive area emerges from the questions you find yourself returning to, the conversations you find yourself engaged in, the data you find yourself drawn to.

**Sub-question 2 — what methodological identity?** Empirical finance distinguishes between several methodological communities: the *applied microeconomist* community (Maya's fair-lending paper sits here — staggered DiD, regression discontinuity, careful identification); the *empirical asset-pricing* community (factor models, return predictability, asset-pricing tests — Sam's intraday-momentum paper sits here); the *empirical corporate finance* community (firm-level decisions, governance, capital structure — many camp papers fit here); the *empirical banking* community (financial institutions, intermediaries, regulation — Maya's paper also sits here); the *text-and-data* community (NLP, machine learning, large-scale text analysis — Leah's patent paper sits here). The methodological identity shapes what conferences you go to, what literature you read, what advisors you seek. The 17-year-old chooses tentatively; the 22-year-old is mostly committed.

**Sub-question 3 — what venue identity?** Within the methodological community, you also choose where you publish. Some researchers publish primarily in finance journals (JF, JFE, RFS); others in economics journals (AER, QJE, JPE, Econometrica); others in field journals (Journal of Banking and Finance, Real Estate Economics, etc.); others in interdisciplinary venues (PNAS, Nature, Science). Gao's career, as a worked example, spans finance (Journal of Corporate Finance, JFE, JFQA) and interdisciplinary (PNAS, the Gao and Sun (2019) paper). The venue identity reflects who you want to talk to and who you want to be cited by.

The five-year question is asked, in the camp's language, by Prof. Gao in the final Week 12 lab session: "What do you want to be working on in five years? Tell me a substantive area, a methodological identity, a venue identity. The answer can change every six months; but you should have an answer today." The exercise is to articulate the answer, not to commit to it. Maya's answer in June 2026 is: "I want to be working on consumer credit access, using applied-microeconometric methods, publishing in finance and economics journals — JFE, JF, AER, QJE." This is a reasonable answer for a 17-year-old. Five years from now Maya may discover she actually wants to work on insurance markets, or on cryptocurrency, or on something we have not yet named. The exercise of articulating the five-year plan is what creates the research identity; the specific plan may evolve.

## 11. The role of mentors over five years

The mentor relationship that began in the camp does not end at Week 12. Prof. Gao remains a mentor figure for Maya for at least the next decade — through college applications (when his recommendation letter is the strongest line on Maya's application), through college coursework (when she asks him for advice on which finance and econometrics courses to take), through the next research project (when she runs the idea past him before committing), through graduate-school applications (when his recommendation is again decisive), through the early career (when his network helps her place at her first job). The mentor relationship is multi-year, and the discipline of cultivating it is the discipline of staying in touch.

The cultivation is light-touch but consistent. Send an email twice a year (October and April work well) updating the mentor on progress. The email is short: what paper you are working on, what conference you presented at, what college decision you made, what life event affected your research time. The mentor does not need details; he needs the thread. Without the email, the thread frays; with it, the thread holds.

Build other mentor relationships too. In college, a faculty advisor in your major; a research-experience advisor (if you do an REU or a senior thesis); a postdoctoral mentor (if you do a research assistantship). Each relationship operates at a different temporal and substantive scale; the multi-mentor structure is the norm for high-performing researchers.

The mentor relationship is also reciprocal in the long run. Five years from now, when Maya is in graduate school, she will be a mentor to the new generation of NextGen camp students. The mentor structure is a chain; the camp graduates become the camp mentors; the cycle sustains the research community. Building toward becoming a mentor — by paying close attention to how your own mentors do it — is part of the five-year identity work.

## 14. The commit checklist for the research identity

Apply this checklist at the start of every academic semester for the next five years.

1. **The reading queue has three papers per week scheduled.** Pull them from SSRN, NBER, and the cited literature of your active projects.
2. **The writing block is on the calendar — 30 minutes per day, six days per week.** It is the first hour of the morning, before anything else.
3. **At least one conference is on the calendar for the year.** Submit early; plan the trip; budget the funds.
4. **The portfolio has at least two projects in flight at different phases.** Do not let a single project consume the entire trajectory.
5. **Two mentor emails per year are on the calendar.** October and April work well.
6. **The research journal has been opened in the last seven days.** If you cannot remember the last time you wrote in it, you have lost the thread; restart this week.

The six practices are the framework. The framework is what survives the years.
