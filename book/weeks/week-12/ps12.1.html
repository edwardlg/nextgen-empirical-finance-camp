A boundary worth holding before you start. PS 12.1 is not “write a better paper.” The paper is what you have. The skill this problem set teaches is the discipline of transmitting the paper without losing it in the seams between formats and audiences. Most failed submissions in the modern empirical-finance pipeline fail not on substance but on assembly: a corrupted PDF, a misnamed file, an embargo box checked when it should not have been, a missing CRediT statement, an unattributed funder. The skill is operational, the skill is checkable, and the skill is the difference between a paper that enters the public record cleanly and a paper that bounces back from the editorial office on Monday morning with a procedural rejection note that will cost you two weeks. Treat this problem set with the seriousness you would treat a tax return; the analogy is exact.

  2. submission-packet/cover-letter.pdf — the one-page cover letter.
  3. submission-packet/author-statement.pdf — the CRediT author-contribution statement.
  4. submission-packet/data-code-availability.pdf — the data-and-code availability statement.
  5. submission-packet/conflicts.pdf — the conflicts-of-interest declaration.
  6. submission-packet/replication-package.zip — the code-and-derived-data archive (separately checked against MARS in PS 12.3, but referenced here).

Part 1 — The manuscript PDF and the file-name discipline (15 points)

The single sin this part prevents: a corrupted, badly typeset, or wrongly named manuscript PDF that the editorial-office intake will return without reading.

(a) Compile the manuscript to PDF with publication-standard typesetting (8 pts). Use LaTeX (the recommended workflow) or a Word-to-PDF export that has been visually proofed for: double-spaced 12-point Times or 11-point Computer Modern, one-inch margins, page numbers in the footer, line numbers in the left margin (yes, enable them — referees cite by line number), tables and figures embedded near first reference (not dumped at the end), references in Chicago author-date style. Verify in a PDF reader that no \ref{} placeholders, ?? cross-references, or missing-figure boxes appear anywhere in the document. The PDF must render correctly in Adobe Acrobat Reader, Apple Preview, and a free PDF viewer like Sumatra — open it in two of the three before submission.

(b) Embed all fonts and check the file is not corrupted (4 pts). Run pdffonts manuscript.pdf (from poppler-utils, installable via apt install poppler-utils or brew install poppler) and verify that every font listed has emb yes and sub yes in the appropriate columns. Run pdfinfo manuscript.pdf and verify that Page size, Page rot, and File size are sensible (typical camp manuscript: ~40–80 pages, 800 KB to 2 MB). Run pdftotext manuscript.pdf - and verify that the abstract appears in the extracted text in readable form (if it does not, your PDF has a text-extraction problem and most editorial-office search tools will not find it).

(c) Name the file using the journal’s required convention (3 pts). The Schar Young Scholars Journal requires manuscripts to be named SYSJ-[lastname]-[YYYYMMDD]-[short-slug].pdf. Maya’s file: SYSJ-Patel-20260918-fair-lending.pdf. The slug is hyphen-separated, lowercase, ≤25 characters, no spaces or special characters. Verify the name does not contain spaces, accents, capital letters in the slug, or characters outside [a-zA-Z0-9.-]. Most editorial-office intake scripts choke on Unicode in filenames; the test is whether ls submission-packet/ shows the file with no escaping.

Part 2 — The cover letter (15 points)

The single sin this part prevents: a cover letter that reads like boilerplate and earns the editor’s three-minute scan in the worst possible way — by giving him no reason to advance the paper to assignment.

(a) Write the four-paragraph cover letter (10 pts, 2.5 each). Use the structure from Chapter 12.1 §4: identification → contribution → fit → attestations. Target length: 350–400 words total. Each paragraph does one job and does it in three to five sentences.

  • Paragraph 1 — identification. Name the paper, propose the category (Research Article / Methodological Note / Replication Study / Data Resource Article), name the corresponding author with email, name co-authors with affiliations. Two to three sentences.
  • Paragraph 2 — contribution. One or two sentences only. The elevator pitch from your introduction’s paragraph 5, compressed. Do not rewrite the abstract here; the editor reads the abstract separately. State the question and the headline finding, with the magnitude. Three sentences maximum.
  • Paragraph 3 — fit. One paragraph (three to four sentences) on why this journal is the right venue. Reference the journal’s published scope statement. Name two prior papers in the journal that yours speaks to (verify they exist by checking the journal’s archive). Do not flatter the journal; state the fit.
  • Paragraph 4 — attestations. Four sentences, each attesting to a specific thing: (i) the paper is original and has not been published elsewhere; (ii) the paper is not under consideration elsewhere; (iii) the author list is complete and all listed authors have approved the submission; (iv) any prior conference presentations are disclosed (name them — e.g., “presented at the NextGen 2026 Symposium, August 14, 2026”). Sign with the corresponding author’s typed name and date.

(b) Format the letter as a one-page PDF (3 pts). One page, letterhead optional (the camp provides a template), 12-point font, standard business-letter margins, addressed to “Editor-in-Chief, Schar Young Scholars Journal.” If it spills to a second page, you have written too much; cut.

(c) Suggested reviewers (2 pts). At the end of the cover letter, name two to three suggested reviewers (faculty, postdocs, or advanced PhD students whose work your paper cites). For each, give name, affiliation, email (or a URL where the email is publicly listed), and one sentence of justification. Do not suggest your mentor, your school’s faculty, or anyone who would be ineligible under the journal’s conflict-of-interest policy. The journal is not required to use your suggestions, but the discipline of producing the list is real.

Part 3 — The author statement, data-and-code availability, and conflicts (20 points)

The single sin this part prevents: leaving the three “small documents” as boilerplate, which (per Ch 12.1 §1) is the most common reason for a procedural return-to-author.

(a) Write the CRediT author-contribution statement (8 pts). Use the fourteen-role taxonomy from Chapter 12.1 §2 (Conceptualization, Data curation, Formal analysis, Funding acquisition, Investigation, Methodology, Project administration, Resources, Software, Supervision, Validation, Visualization, Writing — original draft, Writing — review and editing). For each author, list at least one role. For the corresponding author, list explicitly. Do not write “the authors contributed equally”; that is not CRediT-compliant. Use the canonical CRediT phrasing — e.g., “M. Patel: Conceptualization, Formal analysis, Software, Writing — original draft. L. Gao: Conceptualization, Methodology, Supervision, Writing — review and editing.” Verify that every role appears next to at least one author; if “Funding acquisition” does not apply (the camp is grant-funded but you did not personally write the grant), omit that role entirely. The omission is itself information.

(b) Write the data-and-code availability statement (8 pts). One paragraph (~200 words) naming every data source by its proper noun, stating the licensing terms for each (proprietary / restricted / public-domain), stating whether code is available and at what URL, and naming the replication-package handle. Required structure:

  • Data sources. Name each by proper noun (e.g., HMDA-LAR, CRSP, Compustat, FRED-MD, HMDA-PUF). For each, name the licensing regime in one phrase: “public-use file (PUF), available from FFIEC,” “subscription-required, licensed via WRDS to GMU,” etc.
  • Code availability. State whether code is available at a public URL. If yes, name the URL (the GitHub repo or the MARS handle). If no (because the analysis runs on restricted data), state the path by which a future replicator could access the code under a data-use agreement.
  • Replication package. Name the MARS handle (you may write [MARS handle pending] if the deposit is in progress in PS 12.3), the DOI (if any), and the contents (data, code, derived-data, documentation).
  • Embargo / restrictions. State if any data are embargoed and for how long.

“Available upon request” is not acceptable per the DA-RT guidelines (Chapter 12.1 §2). If you cannot share data because it is proprietary, state the restriction and name the data-access pathway (the WRDS subscription, the FFIEC PUF download URL).

(c) Write the conflicts-of-interest declaration (4 pts). A short paragraph. For the precollege author, this is usually two sentences: “I have no financial, professional, or personal interests that could be perceived as conflicting with the integrity of this research.” For the mentor (Prof. Gao), the disclosure must name any consulting, expert-witness, or board roles relevant to the topic — e.g., for the fair-lending paper, Prof. Gao discloses his prior HUD/Fed/Congressional testimony on fair lending; for a crypto paper he would disclose any consulting for crypto firms; etc. The honest version is the only version that survives a careful referee’s check.

Part 4 — The manifest (20 points)

The single sin this part prevents: delivering a zip whose contents you cannot defend three years from now when someone asks “what was in the submission packet?” The manifest is the answer to that future question.

(a) Write the JSON manifest (10 pts). A single manifest.json file in the submission-packet directory. Required fields:

{
  "schema_version": "1.0",
  "packet_name": "SYSJ-Patel-20260918-fair-lending",
  "submission_date": "2026-09-18",
  "corresponding_author": {"name": "Maya Patel", "email": "mpatel@tjhsst.edu", "orcid": "0000-0000-0000-0000"},
  "manuscript_title": "...",
  "files": [
    {
      "filename": "manuscript.pdf",
      "role": "manuscript",
      "size_bytes": 1234567,
      "sha256": "abc123...",
      "content_type": "application/pdf",
      "pages": 47,
      "notes": "..."
    },
    ...
  ]
}

Include one files entry per file in the packet (manuscript, cover letter, author statement, data-code availability, conflicts, replication package). Compute the SHA-256 hash of each file with sha256sum <file> (Linux/WSL) or shasum -a 256 <file> (macOS). Compute the byte size with stat -c %s <file> or wc -c <file>. Verify the JSON parses cleanly with python3 -c "import json; json.load(open('manifest.json'))".

(b) Write the Markdown mirror (4 pts). A human-readable manifest.md with the same content as the JSON, formatted as a table with columns: filename, role, size (KB), SHA-256 (first 16 chars + …), pages (for PDFs), notes. The Markdown is what your future-self will read; the JSON is what an automated intake script will read.

(c) Verify the checksums round-trip (4 pts). Write a one-paragraph note in manifest.md documenting the round-trip check: re-compute the SHA-256 hashes after copying the zip to a different directory, confirm the hashes match the manifest, and confirm unzip -t submission-packet.zip reports no warnings. The point of this check is that the manifest is verifiable — anyone with the zip can re-compute the hashes and confirm the packet is intact.

(d) Include a “what is not in this packet” section (2 pts). A short list at the bottom of manifest.md naming things a reader might expect but that are deliberately not included: e.g., “raw HMDA-LAR data (excluded: 47 GB, available from FFIEC public-use file at [URL]),” “PII-containing intermediates (excluded: contains borrower-level identifiers, not redistributable),” “early-draft versions (excluded: superseded by v3.2 in this packet).” The negative manifest is as informative as the positive one.

Part 5 — The dry-run upload and the near-misses memo (15 points)

The single sin this part prevents: discovering at the upload step that you have the wrong number of files, the wrong file type, or a missing required form field — and discovering it under deadline pressure on Friday afternoon.

(a) Conduct the dry-run upload (10 pts). Log into the ScholarOne portal (or the journal’s submission system) using a test account if available; otherwise, walk through the submission flow without clicking the final “submit.” Take screenshots at each step. Transcribe every form field’s required answer into upload-dry-run.md. Required form fields typically include:

  • Manuscript metadata: title, abstract (paste from the chosen draft, PS 11.1), keywords (3–6), JEL codes (3–6, see PS 12.2), running title (≤60 chars).
  • Author information: every author’s name, affiliation, email, ORCID (required for corresponding author), order on byline, designation as corresponding author.
  • Funding: names of funding sources (e.g., “NextGen 8-Week Camp, GMU Costello College of Business”), grant numbers if applicable.
  • Acknowledgments: a short paragraph (typically 50–100 words).
  • Cover letter: upload PDF.
  • Manuscript file: upload PDF.
  • Supplementary files: upload the replication package zip, the author statement PDF, the data-and-code availability PDF, the conflicts PDF.
  • Attestations: checkboxes confirming originality, exclusive submission, author-list completeness, conflict-of-interest disclosure.
  • Suggested reviewers: the names you generated in PS 12.1 Part 2(c).

For each form field you encounter that you did not anticipate (a journal-specific question, e.g., “what is your IRB protocol number?”), document it and note what answer you would give.

(b) Write the near-misses memo (5 pts). A one-page memo, near-misses.md, listing every error you caught during assembly and corrected before submission. Format as a numbered list, with each entry: what was wrong, when you caught it, how you fixed it. The point of the memo is that the assembly process will generate near-misses (a typo in the title, a missing CRediT role, a stale figure embedded in the PDF, a checksum mismatch), and writing them down is the discipline that makes you catch the next ten. Three to seven near-misses is typical; if you have zero, you have either done this exercise many times before or you have not actually checked carefully.

Part 6 — The packet integrity audit (15 points)

The single sin this part prevents: shipping a packet whose internal references contradict each other — the abstract quotes one number, the introduction quotes another, the cover letter quotes a third.

(a) The number-consistency audit (8 pts). Confirm — by literally grep-ing the packet — that the single headline number of the paper appears identically in five places: the abstract, the introduction’s paragraph 5, Table 2’s column 3, the cover letter’s paragraph 2, and the data-and-code availability statement’s brief description. Use a command like grep -i "1.4 percentage points" submission-packet/*.pdf (after extracting text via pdftotext) or pdfgrep if available. Document the result of the check in a table at the top of near-misses.md showing each location’s quoted number. If the numbers disagree, fix them; the fix is the audit’s whole point.

(b) The author-list consistency audit (3 pts). Confirm the author list appears identically — in spelling, in order, in affiliation — across: the manuscript title page, the cover letter paragraph 1, the author statement, the metadata fields in the dry-run upload. The most common error is an author missing a middle initial in one place but present in another; for ORCID-linked records, this is a serious issue.

(c) The acknowledgments / funding consistency audit (4 pts). Confirm the funding source (“NextGen 8-Week Empirical Finance Camp, Costello College of Business, George Mason University”) appears identically across: the manuscript’s acknowledgments section, the funding metadata field in the dry-run upload, the author statement’s “Funding acquisition” attribution (if any), and the data-and-code availability statement (if the camp’s compute infrastructure is named). Funding-source mismatches are a leading cause of indexed-paper attribution errors three years later.

How this is graded

Part 1 (manuscript PDF, 15) on PDF fidelity (fonts embedded, no broken refs, named per convention). Part 2 (cover letter, 15) on the four-paragraph discipline and the suggested-reviewer list. Part 3 (small documents, 20) on CRediT compliance, DA-RT-compliant data statement, and honest COI disclosure. Part 4 (manifest, 20) on completeness, JSON validity, and the checksum round-trip. Part 5 (dry-run + near-misses, 15) on whether every form field is transcribed and whether the near-misses memo names real errors caught and fixed. Part 6 (integrity audit, 15) on number consistency, author-list consistency, and funding consistency across the packet.

The single highest-signal check: does python3 -c "import json; m = json.load(open('submission-packet/manifest.json')); print(len(m['files']))" print a number equal to the number of files actually in the packet? If the manifest says six files and the packet contains seven, you have shipped an undocumented file; if the manifest says seven and the packet contains six, you have shipped a phantom record. The match is the integrity check.

- *Paragraph 2 — contribution.* One or two sentences only. The elevator pitch from your introduction's paragraph 5, compressed. Do not rewrite the abstract here; the editor reads the abstract separately. State the question and the headline finding, with the magnitude. Three sentences maximum.
- *Paragraph 3 — fit.* One paragraph (three to four sentences) on why this journal is the right venue. Reference the journal's published scope statement. Name two prior papers in the journal that yours speaks to (verify they exist by checking the journal's archive). Do not flatter the journal; state the fit.
- *Paragraph 4 — attestations.* Four sentences, each attesting to a specific thing: (i) the paper is original and has not been published elsewhere; (ii) the paper is not under consideration elsewhere; (iii) the author list is complete and all listed authors have approved the submission; (iv) any prior conference presentations are disclosed (name them — e.g., "presented at the NextGen 2026 Symposium, August 14, 2026"). Sign with the corresponding author's typed name and date.

**(b) Format the letter as a one-page PDF (3 pts).** One page, letterhead optional (the camp provides a template), 12-point font, standard business-letter margins, addressed to "Editor-in-Chief, Schar Young Scholars Journal." If it spills to a second page, you have written too much; cut.

**(c) Suggested reviewers (2 pts).** At the end of the cover letter, name two to three suggested reviewers (faculty, postdocs, or advanced PhD students whose work your paper cites). For each, give name, affiliation, email (or a URL where the email is publicly listed), and one sentence of justification. Do not suggest your mentor, your school's faculty, or anyone who would be ineligible under the journal's conflict-of-interest policy. The journal is not required to use your suggestions, but the discipline of producing the list is real.

## Part 3 — The author statement, data-and-code availability, and conflicts (20 points)

The single sin this part prevents: **leaving the three "small documents" as boilerplate, which (per Ch 12.1 §1) is the most common reason for a procedural return-to-author.**

**(a) Write the CRediT author-contribution statement (8 pts).** Use the fourteen-role taxonomy from Chapter 12.1 §2 (Conceptualization, Data curation, Formal analysis, Funding acquisition, Investigation, Methodology, Project administration, Resources, Software, Supervision, Validation, Visualization, Writing — original draft, Writing — review and editing). For each author, list at least one role. For the corresponding author, list explicitly. Do *not* write "the authors contributed equally"; that is not CRediT-compliant. Use the canonical CRediT phrasing — e.g., "M. Patel: Conceptualization, Formal analysis, Software, Writing — original draft. L. Gao: Conceptualization, Methodology, Supervision, Writing — review and editing." Verify that every role appears next to *at least one* author; if "Funding acquisition" does not apply (the camp is grant-funded but you did not personally write the grant), omit that role entirely. The omission is itself information.

**(b) Write the data-and-code availability statement (8 pts).** One paragraph (~200 words) naming every data source by its proper noun, stating the licensing terms for each (proprietary / restricted / public-domain), stating whether code is available and at what URL, and naming the replication-package handle. Required structure:

- *Data sources.* Name each by proper noun (e.g., HMDA-LAR, CRSP, Compustat, FRED-MD, HMDA-PUF). For each, name the licensing regime in one phrase: "public-use file (PUF), available from FFIEC," "subscription-required, licensed via WRDS to GMU," etc.
- *Code availability.* State whether code is available at a public URL. If yes, name the URL (the GitHub repo or the MARS handle). If no (because the analysis runs on restricted data), state the path by which a future replicator could access the code under a data-use agreement.
- *Replication package.* Name the MARS handle (you may write `[MARS handle pending]` if the deposit is in progress in PS 12.3), the DOI (if any), and the contents (data, code, derived-data, documentation).
- *Embargo / restrictions.* State if any data are embargoed and for how long.

"Available upon request" is not acceptable per the DA-RT guidelines (Chapter 12.1 §2). If you cannot share data because it is proprietary, state the restriction and name the data-access pathway (the WRDS subscription, the FFIEC PUF download URL).

**(c) Write the conflicts-of-interest declaration (4 pts).** A short paragraph. For the precollege author, this is usually two sentences: "I have no financial, professional, or personal interests that could be perceived as conflicting with the integrity of this research." For the mentor (Prof. Gao), the disclosure must name any consulting, expert-witness, or board roles relevant to the topic — e.g., for the fair-lending paper, Prof. Gao discloses his prior HUD/Fed/Congressional testimony on fair lending; for a crypto paper he would disclose any consulting for crypto firms; etc. The honest version is the only version that survives a careful referee's check.

## Part 4 — The manifest (20 points)

The single sin this part prevents: **delivering a zip whose contents you cannot defend three years from now when someone asks "what was in the submission packet?" The manifest is the answer to that future question.**

**(a) Write the JSON manifest (10 pts).** A single `manifest.json` file in the submission-packet directory. Required fields:

```json
{
  "schema_version": "1.0",
  "packet_name": "SYSJ-Patel-20260918-fair-lending",
  "submission_date": "2026-09-18",
  "corresponding_author": {"name": "Maya Patel", "email": "mpatel@tjhsst.edu", "orcid": "0000-0000-0000-0000"},
  "manuscript_title": "...",
  "files": [
    {
      "filename": "manuscript.pdf",
      "role": "manuscript",
      "size_bytes": 1234567,
      "sha256": "abc123...",
      "content_type": "application/pdf",
      "pages": 47,
      "notes": "..."
    },
    ...
  ]
}
```

Include one `files` entry per file in the packet (manuscript, cover letter, author statement, data-code availability, conflicts, replication package). Compute the SHA-256 hash of each file with `sha256sum <file>` (Linux/WSL) or `shasum -a 256 <file>` (macOS). Compute the byte size with `stat -c %s <file>` or `wc -c <file>`. Verify the JSON parses cleanly with `python3 -c "import json; json.load(open('manifest.json'))"`.

**(b) Write the Markdown mirror (4 pts).** A human-readable `manifest.md` with the same content as the JSON, formatted as a table with columns: filename, role, size (KB), SHA-256 (first 16 chars + …), pages (for PDFs), notes. The Markdown is what your future-self will read; the JSON is what an automated intake script will read.

**(c) Verify the checksums round-trip (4 pts).** Write a one-paragraph note in `manifest.md` documenting the round-trip check: re-compute the SHA-256 hashes after copying the zip to a different directory, confirm the hashes match the manifest, and confirm `unzip -t submission-packet.zip` reports no warnings. The point of this check is that the manifest is *verifiable* — anyone with the zip can re-compute the hashes and confirm the packet is intact.

**(d) Include a "what is *not* in this packet" section (2 pts).** A short list at the bottom of `manifest.md` naming things a reader might expect but that are deliberately not included: e.g., "raw HMDA-LAR data (excluded: 47 GB, available from FFIEC public-use file at [URL])," "PII-containing intermediates (excluded: contains borrower-level identifiers, not redistributable)," "early-draft versions (excluded: superseded by v3.2 in this packet)." The negative manifest is as informative as the positive one.

## Part 5 — The dry-run upload and the near-misses memo (15 points)

The single sin this part prevents: **discovering at the upload step that you have the wrong number of files, the wrong file type, or a missing required form field — and discovering it under deadline pressure on Friday afternoon.**

**(a) Conduct the dry-run upload (10 pts).** Log into the ScholarOne portal (or the journal's submission system) using a test account if available; otherwise, walk through the submission flow without clicking the final "submit." Take screenshots at each step. Transcribe every form field's required answer into `upload-dry-run.md`. Required form fields typically include:

- *Manuscript metadata:* title, abstract (paste from the chosen draft, PS 11.1), keywords (3–6), JEL codes (3–6, see PS 12.2), running title (≤60 chars).
- *Author information:* every author's name, affiliation, email, ORCID (required for corresponding author), order on byline, designation as corresponding author.
- *Funding:* names of funding sources (e.g., "NextGen 8-Week Camp, GMU Costello College of Business"), grant numbers if applicable.
- *Acknowledgments:* a short paragraph (typically 50–100 words).
- *Cover letter:* upload PDF.
- *Manuscript file:* upload PDF.
- *Supplementary files:* upload the replication package zip, the author statement PDF, the data-and-code availability PDF, the conflicts PDF.
- *Attestations:* checkboxes confirming originality, exclusive submission, author-list completeness, conflict-of-interest disclosure.
- *Suggested reviewers:* the names you generated in PS 12.1 Part 2(c).

For each form field you encounter that you did *not* anticipate (a journal-specific question, e.g., "what is your IRB protocol number?"), document it and note what answer you would give.

**(b) Write the near-misses memo (5 pts).** A one-page memo, `near-misses.md`, listing every error you caught during assembly and corrected before submission. Format as a numbered list, with each entry: *what was wrong, when you caught it, how you fixed it.* The point of the memo is that the assembly process *will* generate near-misses (a typo in the title, a missing CRediT role, a stale figure embedded in the PDF, a checksum mismatch), and writing them down is the discipline that makes you catch the next ten. Three to seven near-misses is typical; if you have zero, you have either done this exercise many times before or you have not actually checked carefully.

## Part 6 — The packet integrity audit (15 points)

The single sin this part prevents: **shipping a packet whose internal references contradict each other — the abstract quotes one number, the introduction quotes another, the cover letter quotes a third.**

**(a) The number-consistency audit (8 pts).** Confirm — by literally grep-ing the packet — that the *single headline number* of the paper appears identically in five places: the abstract, the introduction's paragraph 5, Table 2's column 3, the cover letter's paragraph 2, and the data-and-code availability statement's brief description. Use a command like `grep -i "1.4 percentage points" submission-packet/*.pdf` (after extracting text via `pdftotext`) or `pdfgrep` if available. Document the result of the check in a table at the top of `near-misses.md` showing each location's quoted number. If the numbers disagree, fix them; the fix is the audit's whole point.

**(b) The author-list consistency audit (3 pts).** Confirm the author list appears identically — in spelling, in order, in affiliation — across: the manuscript title page, the cover letter paragraph 1, the author statement, the metadata fields in the dry-run upload. The most common error is an author missing a middle initial in one place but present in another; for ORCID-linked records, this is a serious issue.

**(c) The acknowledgments / funding consistency audit (4 pts).** Confirm the funding source ("NextGen 8-Week Empirical Finance Camp, Costello College of Business, George Mason University") appears identically across: the manuscript's acknowledgments section, the funding metadata field in the dry-run upload, the author statement's "Funding acquisition" attribution (if any), and the data-and-code availability statement (if the camp's compute infrastructure is named). Funding-source mismatches are a leading cause of indexed-paper attribution errors three years later.

## How this is graded

Part 1 (manuscript PDF, 15) on PDF fidelity (fonts embedded, no broken refs, named per convention). Part 2 (cover letter, 15) on the four-paragraph discipline and the suggested-reviewer list. Part 3 (small documents, 20) on CRediT compliance, DA-RT-compliant data statement, and honest COI disclosure. Part 4 (manifest, 20) on completeness, JSON validity, and the checksum round-trip. Part 5 (dry-run + near-misses, 15) on whether every form field is transcribed *and* whether the near-misses memo names real errors caught and fixed. Part 6 (integrity audit, 15) on number consistency, author-list consistency, and funding consistency across the packet.

The single highest-signal check: **does `python3 -c "import json; m = json.load(open('submission-packet/manifest.json')); print(len(m['files']))"` print a number equal to the number of files actually in the packet?** If the manifest says six files and the packet contains seven, you have shipped an undocumented file; if the manifest says seven and the packet contains six, you have shipped a phantom record. The match is the integrity check.

## The bridge to PS 12.2 and beyond

PS 12.1 produces the *packet that goes to the journal*. PS 12.2 produces the *preprint that goes to SSRN* — same paper, different audience, different metadata discipline, different versioning rules. PS 12.3 produces the *MARS repository deposit* — same paper, archival-permanent version, with metadata fields the journal does not require. PS 12.4 maps the next eighteen months of conference presentations. PS 12.5 zooms out to the five-year arc. The packet you assemble in PS 12.1 is the single most consequential artifact of the camp; treat it accordingly.

*End of PS 12.1. The assembly is operational, the checksums are real, and the manifest is the contract with the future-you who will open this packet in 2029 to write a tenure file, a grad-school application, or a job interview talk. Begin by typesetting the manuscript and naming the file; the rest follows from there. By Friday afternoon the zip will sit on disk, every byte accounted for, every file checked, and the upload will be a button-click rather than a fire-drill.*
