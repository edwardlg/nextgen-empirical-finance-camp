## (a) Pre-read packet — "A paper is not a project. It is a relationship."

*Read this once. One page.*

The camp's twelve weeks taught the *project* side of research: how to assemble a panel, write an abstract, defend an identification claim. That discipline is necessary; without it the rest collapses.

But the project-side discipline is not what determines whether you become a researcher. That is the *relationship-side* discipline — the slow, repeated, year-after-year practice of staying in conversation with a paper that is not yet finished, a literature that does not yet have your sentence in it, and a community that does not yet know your name. **A paper is not a project; a paper is a relationship you maintain with a question for as long as the question deserves to be asked.** Some questions deserve a year. Some deserve ten. Rainbow is on year seven and counting.

The relationship-side discipline is *non-linear* (the inactive years are not failure years — they are when the paper metabolizes feedback that has not yet become a revision) and *socially embedded* (it depends on co-authors, discussants, referees, editors, seminar audiences whose attention you earn and keep over years). None of this is in the chapters. All of it is in the lived experience of every researcher who has ever published in *JF*, *JFE*, or *RFS*. **The camp ends, the work continues, the relationship begins.** Listen to the Rainbow arc not as a war story but as the honest shape of the practice you are now in.

## (c) Five-slide deck (speak-from scaffold)

**Slide 1 — "Year 1: the idea, and the colleague who told me there were three papers."**

- The Rainbow idea began spring 2019 as a side conversation about a curious pattern in the Mergent muni-bond data: counties with similar fiscal fundamentals had wildly different borrowing patterns tracking political composition in ways the existing literature did not explain. The first draft was 30 pages and conflated three empirical questions: borrowing levels, borrowing costs, borrowing composition.
- The Phase-1 breakthrough came when a senior GMU colleague said: "There are three papers here. Pick one." Sicong, Yu, and I picked Question 1 (levels), reserved Question 2 (costs) as a follow-up now in early drafting, and abandoned Question 3 (composition). **The decision to narrow was the single most important Phase-1 move.**
- The lesson: a first draft is usually three papers in a trenchcoat. Identifying the *single most defensible* question and ruthlessly cutting the others is the first discipline of a research career — the Mentor-11 single-gap discipline applied at the question level.

**Slide 2 — "Year 2-3: the four conferences that sharpened identification."**

After the late-2020 first SSRN posting (34 downloads — below average), the paper went on the conference circuit. Four 2022-2023 presentations shaped identification as much as any methodological reading. **EFA April 2022:** the political-composition measure was read as too crude; we built a DW-NOMINATE-style multi-dimensional measure (three months prompted by twenty minutes of Q&A). **FMA October 2022:** the dependent variable was missing institutional features — debt limits, voter approval, refunding composition — so we added a four-level classification (three months). **SFA November 2022:** an eight-page formal discussion said the contribution claim was overstated; we re-engaged Brennan and Eichengreen (2011) and toned the sentence down (two months). **WFA June 2023:** two discussants, one on external validity (Canadian provincial bonds), one on mechanism (cost-of-borrowing vs. demand-for-public-services vs. political-credit-market); we added a Canadian-provincial robustness section and a mechanism decomposition (six months in parallel).

**Slide 3 — "Year 4: the AEA presentation and what it took to get there."**

- AEA January 2024 was the paper's first elite-conference credential. The finance sessions select ~40 slots from ~1,200 submissions (3-4%). Fifteen-minute slot, ten-minute discussant, forty senior researchers in the room — including, for our session, two JF editors. **The discussant's main comment: "The rainbow framing is too cute; you're going to lose the JF editor on the title alone. Either drop it or commit fully with a more careful taxonomic discussion."**
- We chose to commit. Section 1 now defends the rainbow framing on substantive grounds — political composition is genuinely continuous rather than binary, and the rainbow language carries that continuous-spectrum meaning. The title stayed *Rainbow of Credits*.
- The AEA credential is what justifies the JF target. Without it, the paper would go to JFE first, then RFS, then the next tier. *With* it, the JF first-submission is defensible because the paper has been seen by senior researchers at the field's most selective annual venue and the response was substantively engaged. The credential unlocks the journal ladder.

**Slide 4 — "Year 5-6: the response-letter mental model for the imminent JF submission."**

- JF submission is scheduled for September 2026. Desk-reject probability is roughly 40%; review-then-reject roughly 35%; R&R roughly 20%; conditional-accept roughly 5%. **I am submitting expecting a desk-reject and treating any R&R as a bonus.** Not pessimism — base-rate awareness. Top-finance journals desk-reject roughly 80% of submissions for portfolio-fit reasons that have nothing to do with substantive merit.
- I am pre-writing responses to the three most likely referee comments *now*, before any decision arrives. (a) "The political-composition measure has measurement-error problems biasing toward zero; can the authors address with a measurement-error-corrected estimator?" (b) "The Canadian-provincial robustness is unconvincing because U.S. muni bonds and Canadian provincial bonds differ institutionally too much; the external-validity claim should be narrower." (c) "The mechanism-decomposition confounds the political channel with the institutional channel; a sharper test is needed." For each, the response letter has a paragraph already drafted with the argument and a planned new robustness exercise.
- Pre-writing the response is itself a form of revision: it surfaces weaknesses I would not see if I waited. **Three of the most useful AEA-version revisions came from pre-writing referee responses I did not yet have.** That move distinguishes a paper that survives R&R from one that does not.

**Slide 5 — "Year 7+: what comes after, and why it is worth it."**

- Realistic trajectory: JF desk-reject (Sept 2026 → Feb 2027) → JFE submission (March 2027) → JFE R&R (March 2028) → JFE revised submission (March 2029) → JFE conditional accept (Sept 2029) → JFE final acceptance (March 2030) → JFE publication (Fall 2030). **Eleven years from the 2019 origin.**
- Why is it worth it? *First*, the published paper becomes part of the field's permanent record — cited, taught, referenced in policy reports, embedded for decades. The eleven years amortize over a citation life that for a top-finance paper averages 40+ years. *Second*, the work builds relationships — the WFA discussant who now edits a JF special issue, the PhD students who worked on the paper as RAs and now have faculty positions, the Brookings audience that invited the 2024 panel. Relationships are the career; the paper is the artifact around which they cohere. *Third*, the work builds *me* — every year a sharper empirical economist than the year before.
- The disposition: **the paper you submitted this morning is the beginning of a multi-year relationship with a question, a literature, and a community.** Most camp graduates will not stay long enough. Some of you will. Today is year zero.

## (d) Three stretch questions — Gao, Liu & Wang (2025) on the long arc

The constraint we have used since Mentor 7: **reason about how a paper-in-flight builds its case across a decade, not what the paper specifically claims at this moment. Do NOT invent or recite specific magnitudes from the current draft. Frame answers as "how I would approach this kind of arc"; mark anything needing confirmation against a future published version as `[CHECK]`.**

The citation, verbatim per my CV:

> Gao, L., Liu, S., & Wang, Y. (2025). Rainbow of credits: Municipal borrowing and the political economy of state-local fiscal capacity. Working paper presented at the American Economic Association Annual Meeting, January 2025. Target journal: *Journal of Finance*.

1. **The single-author-credit question.** Rainbow has three authors with CRediT roles allocated by primary contribution rather than "all authors contributed equally." (a) Why does naming a *primary* author for each CRediT role matter strategically when a referee asks whether the work is genuinely collaborative? (b) For your own camp paper (one author, one mentor), write the two-sentence CRediT statement as you would submit it to JF in fifteen years; how does it differ from the version you submitted to Schar today? (c) Why does writing the CRediT statement carefully at every submission matter across a paper's lifetime?

2. **The journal-ladder question.** Rainbow is targeted at JF first, with JFE and RFS as the next two rungs. JF's first-decision time is ~8 months, JFE's ~6; desk-reject rates differ by ~5 percentage points; eventual citation impact of JF is ~20% higher than JFE for comparable finance papers. (a) For Rainbow's profile — AEA credential, mature identification, six years of feedback — is JF-first the right play, or is JFE-first more defensible given the 40% desk-reject base rate? Defend your answer. (b) Apply the logic to your own camp paper *if you intended to keep working through college toward a top-tier journal*: what would your ladder look like, and what would you need to add between now and the first submission to make the first target defensible? (c) Why is the journal-ladder choice itself an *intellectual* commitment (not merely strategic)?

3. **The decade-mentor calibration.** If Rainbow publishes at JFE in 2030 the arc will be roughly eleven years. Over that span I will have written ~90 response-letter pages, presented at ~18 conferences, advised ~5 graduate students who worked on the paper as RAs, and built relationships with ~20 senior researchers in the municipal-finance and political-economy literatures. **For each of the four scale dimensions — response-letter pages, conferences, students mentored, senior relationships built — what is your *current* estimate of how many you can sustain over a comparable eleven-year arc starting today?** This is calibration, not goals; honest answers diagnose the kind of research career you are realistically committing to. Your PS-12.5 memo should be consistent with this calibration.
