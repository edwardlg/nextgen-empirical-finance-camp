# Ch 12.4 — The Conference Circuit Next: FMA Undergraduate Poster, AEA Pipeline, SFS Cavalcade — what each is, who attends, and what they reward

Maya's paper is at the Schar Young Scholars Journal, on SSRN, and in MARS. The Rainbow of Credits case study in Ch 12.3 illustrated that the journey from working paper to top journal runs through a long sequence of conference presentations — five to fifteen presentations over two to three years for a typical top-finance paper. The question for a precollege author who is *just starting* the arc is: which conferences should I aim for, and how do I get in?

Here is the move this chapter turns on. **The conference circuit is not one thing; it is a stratified ecosystem of venues with different audiences, different selection standards, and different rewards, and the discipline of building a research career is to enter the ecosystem at the level you are ready for and to climb deliberately.** A precollege author starting in fall 2026 with a polished paper from the NextGen camp is ready to enter at the *undergraduate-friendly tier* — venues that explicitly welcome precollege and early-undergraduate authors, with selection standards calibrated to that audience. The next tier up — the regional finance associations' general sessions — accepts undergraduate authors competitively alongside graduate students and junior faculty. The tier above that — the major national associations' undergraduate-specific tracks and pipeline programs — is selective and credentialing. The tier above that — the major national associations' main programs — is generally not yet accessible. **This chapter walks through each tier in detail, names the canonical venues at each level, explains the selection mechanics, and discusses what each rewards.**

We will work through five venues in depth: the FMA Undergraduate Poster Session (the canonical entry-tier undergraduate venue), the AEA Summer Pipeline Program and the related AEA undergraduate-research initiatives (the canonical credentialing program for underrepresented students), the SFS Cavalcade (the second-tier finance conference with a strong but accessible standard), the regional FMAs (the Eastern, Midwest, Southern, Western FMAs — accessible but rigorous), and the Eastern Economics Association annual meeting (the accessible economics conference with a strong undergraduate session). At each venue we ask: who attends, what is the selection process, what is the format, what does the conference reward, and what does an HS-aged or early-undergrad author get out of attending. By the end of the chapter, Maya, Devon, Priya, Sam, and Leah each have a conference plan for the next eighteen months that fits their paper, their stage, and their career trajectory.

## 1. The reveal: what conferences are *for*

A conference presentation has four functions, and the function-mix shapes which venue is appropriate at which stage.

**Function 1 — feedback.** The Phase-3 conference grind of the Rainbow paper (Ch 12.3 §5) was primarily a feedback-gathering exercise. Five conferences, fifteen substantive comments, paper reshape. For an early-stage paper, the feedback function dominates; the most important question is "will the discussant and audience members give me useful comments?" Venues that select strong discussants and that have audiences with relevant expertise are high-value-feedback venues. Venues with weak discussants or general-audience attendees give thinner feedback.

**Function 2 — credentialing.** A presentation at the AEA, AFA, or WFA — the three "majors" of finance and economics — is a line on the resume that signals quality. The credential value comes from the selection process: the AEA's main program accepts perhaps 30% of submitted papers, and the WFA accepts perhaps 12%, so a paper that gets in has been judged competitively against many others. For a precollege or undergraduate author, the credentialing function shifts to undergraduate-specific tracks where the audience is the credentialer (the program committee, the discussant, the senior faculty in attendance).

**Function 3 — networking.** Conferences are where you meet the other people working on your topic. The 30 minutes you spend in conversation with the discussant after your session, the lunch you share with a junior faculty member working on a related question, the coffee you have with a senior faculty member who later writes you a recommendation letter — these encounters compound over years. The networking function is invisible in the short run and the dominant function in the long run.

**Function 4 — visibility.** A conference presentation puts the paper in front of an audience that might not otherwise find it. Even at a small conference with 80 attendees, the abstract is in the program, the paper is on the conference website, and 20 people in the room saw a 12-minute presentation that established the result. The visibility function is what feeds the citation long tail (Ch 12.3 §8).

The four functions are weighted differently at different career stages. For a precollege author with a polished first paper, the function weights are roughly: feedback (40%), credentialing (30%), visibility (20%), networking (10%). The networking weight rises sharply once you are in college or graduate school; the credentialing weight rises sharply once you have a sequence of papers; the feedback weight stays high throughout. The conference choice should serve the function-mix appropriate to your stage.

## 2. The hierarchy, named

The finance and economics conference ecosystem has roughly six tiers, from most-selective to most-accessible.

**Tier 1 — the majors (general program).** AEA (American Economic Association) annual meeting; AFA (American Finance Association) annual meeting; WFA (Western Finance Association); EFA (European Finance Association). General programs accept 12–30% of submitted papers. Audiences include tenured faculty, junior faculty, advanced PhD students, and (selectively) senior figures from regulators, central banks, and finance practice. Not yet accessible to precollege authors as main-program presenters.

**Tier 2 — the majors (undergraduate / pipeline tracks).** AEA Pipeline Program, AEA CSWEP (Committee on the Status of Women in the Economics Profession) sessions, AFA undergraduate research presentations (occasional), AEA poster sessions with undergraduate participation. Selective but designed for undergraduate participation. Accessible to ambitious precollege and early-undergraduate authors with strong papers.

**Tier 3 — the second-tier nationals.** SFS Cavalcade (Society for Financial Studies); SFA (Southern Finance Association); NBER Summer Institute (mostly by invitation, but the affiliated junior meetings are more accessible); ASSA's allied society sessions (the AEA's annual meeting is the umbrella for many smaller societies' annual meetings, which have lower selectivity than the AEA itself). Selective at the 15–35% range. Audiences are largely the same as Tier 1 but with somewhat more junior faculty. Accessible to strong undergraduate papers.

**Tier 4 — the regionals.** Eastern Finance Association (EFA — distinct from European Finance Association, confusingly); Midwest Finance Association (MFA); Southwestern Finance Association (SWFA); Eastern Economic Association (EEA); Southern Economic Association (SEA); Western Economic Association International (WEAI). Acceptance rates of 40–70%. Audiences are mostly faculty at regional and second-tier institutions, advanced PhD students, and a robust undergraduate-paper presence. *These are the workhorse venues for the conference circuit.* Most early-career economists and finance scholars present at one or two regionals every year.

**Tier 5 — the undergraduate-specific.** FMA Undergraduate Poster Session (annual, fall); CMC (Carroll Round Conference, hosted by Georgetown); ASUEcon (the Eastern Economic Association's undergraduate session); various university-hosted undergraduate research conferences (NYU's Stern Undergraduate Finance Conference, Wharton's Undergraduate Research Symposium, etc.). Acceptance rates of 50–80%; selection by faculty advisors and undergraduate-research committees. *These are the canonical entry-tier venues for precollege and early-undergraduate authors.*

**Tier 6 — the high-school-specific.** Regeneron Science Talent Search (the STS, formerly Intel STS, formerly Westinghouse STS); the Junior Science and Humanities Symposium (JSHS); the Society for Science's Broadcom MASTERS; the various state-level junior academies of science; the Schar Young Scholars Symposium (a sibling venue to the Schar Young Scholars Journal). Selective in the 5–25% range for the top venues (STS) and 30–60% range for the others. Audiences are heavily K-12 educators and science-fair judges, with substantial industry-and-research representation at the top venues. *These are accessible to current HS-aged authors and credential strongly within HS competitions, but typically less weight in finance-and-economics career trajectories than the Tier 5 venues.*

The right plan for a typical NextGen camp graduate is: enter at Tier 5 (FMA Undergraduate Poster) in the fall after the camp; enter at Tier 4 (a regional) in the spring of the freshman year of college; reach Tier 3 (SFS Cavalcade) by junior year of college with the second or third paper; aspire to Tier 2 (AEA Pipeline) by senior year of college; and target Tier 1 (AEA/AFA main programs) in graduate school. The climb is graduated and the timeline is realistic.

For students who want to compete in the HS-specific competition track in parallel — Tier 6 — the path is supplementary, not substitutive. Maya could present at the FMA Undergraduate Poster (Tier 5) *and* submit to Regeneron STS (Tier 6); the two credentials operate in different communities and both have value, but the long-term career-in-research pathway is Tier 5 → Tier 4 → Tier 3 → Tier 2 → Tier 1. We will focus the chapter on the Tier 5 and Tier 4 venues that are the immediate next moves for camp graduates.

## 3. FMA Undergraduate Poster Session

The Financial Management Association International (FMA) is a finance professional association with about 5,000 members, headquartered in Tampa, Florida. The FMA hosts an annual meeting each October (typically in a major US city; recent locations include Chicago, Atlanta, San Diego, New Orleans), with a regular research program (Tier 3 selectivity) and a parallel undergraduate poster session (Tier 5 selectivity). The undergraduate poster session is the canonical entry-tier venue in finance for undergraduate authors.

**Who attends.** The FMA undergraduate poster session is held alongside the FMA annual meeting; the audience is the meeting's attendees — faculty (mostly from teaching-oriented institutions, with a smaller cohort from research-oriented institutions), advanced PhD students, finance-industry practitioners, and a sizable cohort of undergraduate authors. Total meeting attendance is around 1,200; undergraduate poster-session attendance during the dedicated session is around 200, including the poster presenters themselves, judges, faculty advisors, and audience.

**Selection process.** The submission window opens in May, closes in late June. Authors submit an extended abstract (typically 500–1,500 words) plus the manuscript draft. The FMA undergraduate-research committee reviews submissions; selection is based on quality of the abstract, evidence of empirical work, and clarity of the research question. Acceptance rates have run 50–70% in recent years. Decisions arrive in late July or early August. Accepted authors are invited to present a poster at the October meeting.

**Format.** The poster session is a 90-minute block on one afternoon of the conference. Each presenter stands by their poster, talks to audience members one-on-one as they walk through, and responds to questions. Posters are typically 36-by-48 inches (printed at most universities' research-poster printers; the camp's poster-template file from Week 9 can be adapted). The judges circulate during the session and rate posters on a rubric (research question, identification, robustness, presentation quality). Top-rated posters receive awards (the "Best Undergraduate Poster" award, sometimes broken into substantive sub-fields).

**What it rewards.** The FMA undergraduate poster session rewards clear empirical work with a defensible identification strategy, a clean poster, and an articulate presenter. The reward is twofold: an award (if selected as a top poster) and the credential of having presented at the FMA. The credential is meaningful — it is the standard line on undergraduate-research CVs for finance-track students applying to finance PhD programs.

**What you get out of attending.** Beyond the credential, you get: 90 minutes of high-density feedback from 15–20 audience members who stop at your poster; a chance to attend research sessions on your topic (the FMA's regular program runs alongside, and you can attend as a registered participant); a chance to meet faculty members at potential graduate-school programs; a sense of the broader finance research community that is impossible to get from journal articles alone.

**Cost and logistics.** FMA registration for undergraduate authors is reduced (around $150 vs. the $400 faculty rate); the camp typically covers registration for camp graduates presenting their work. Travel and lodging are typically $400–800 depending on the conference location. Camp graduates are eligible for travel grants through the NextGen camp's alumni-support fund (apply early; the fund is limited to roughly six grants per year).

For Maya, the FMA Undergraduate Poster Session in October 2026 is the canonical next venue. Her fair-lending paper fits the FMA's audience (finance scholars with interest in banking, supervision, and policy). The poster format suits her current draft state: she can present the headline result, the identification strategy, the robustness battery, and the policy implication in a 36-by-48-inch format with five panels. Her plan is to submit the extended abstract in mid-June for the October meeting.

## 4. AEA Summer Pipeline Program and AEA undergraduate-research initiatives

The American Economic Association (AEA) runs several programs aimed at building the undergraduate-to-PhD pipeline in economics, with particular emphasis on broadening participation. These programs are separate from the AEA's main annual-meeting program (Tier 1, generally inaccessible to undergraduates) but are tightly affiliated with the AEA's institutional infrastructure.

**The AEA Summer Pipeline Program.** A summer research residency for rising college juniors and seniors interested in economics PhD programs, hosted at one of several affiliated universities (UC Berkeley, NYU, Stanford, MIT, others depending on the year). Selection is competitive (acceptance rate around 5–10%); the program lasts 8 weeks, pairs students with faculty mentors, and includes intensive coursework in mathematics, econometrics, and research methods. Graduates receive a stipend, housing, and substantive faculty mentorship. The program is *the* canonical credentialing program for underrepresented students entering economics PhD programs; alumni have a strikingly high rate of admission to top economics PhD programs (Berkeley, Princeton, MIT, etc.).

For HS-aged authors, the Pipeline Program is not directly accessible (it requires college enrollment), but the *AEA Mentoring Program* and the *CSWEP Mentoring Workshop* are. The AEA Mentoring Program (run through the AEA's Committee on the Status of Minority Groups in the Economics Profession, CSMGEP) connects underrepresented students with faculty mentors throughout their undergraduate years and into graduate school. The mentoring relationships often persist for a decade or more. Applications are submitted in the spring of the student's senior year of high school or freshman year of college.

**AEA poster sessions and selected undergraduate presentations.** The AEA annual meeting (held in early January each year, the largest economics conference in North America with about 12,000 attendees) includes some undergraduate-research presentations through CSWEP and CSMGEP-affiliated sessions. Selection is highly competitive (acceptance rates around 10–15% of undergraduate submissions). For an HS-aged author, the AEA is generally a venue to *attend* (registration is open to anyone; student rates apply) rather than to *present at* in the first paper.

**What it rewards.** The AEA Pipeline and Mentoring programs reward demonstrated research interest (an SSRN paper, an FMA poster, a published thesis), academic preparation in mathematics and economics, and commitment to a research career. Selection is holistic but research evidence is highly weighted.

**What you get out of attending the AEA.** Attending the AEA as a registered student is one of the highest-value uses of a five-day window in a young researcher's calendar. The program includes hundreds of sessions across all fields of economics; the exhibit hall includes booths from every major economics PhD program; the social events bring together junior and senior researchers across institutions. Even without presenting, attending the AEA in January exposes a precollege or early-undergraduate author to the breadth of the field in a way no other venue does.

For Maya: not eligible to present at the AEA in her precollege year, but she should plan to attend the AEA in January 2027 as a registered student. The cost is approximately $200 in registration plus $600–1,000 in travel and lodging. The credential of having attended the AEA before college matriculation is small but real; the substantive value of seeing the field in person is large.

For Priya, who is interested in the climate-and-insurance intersection: there is a parallel set of programs run through the Association of Environmental and Resource Economists (AERE) that operate at similar selectivity to the AEA's pipeline programs but with a sub-field focus. Priya should explore AERE's undergraduate-research initiatives in parallel.

## 5. The SFS Cavalcade

The Society for Financial Studies (SFS) is the scholarly society that publishes the *Review of Financial Studies*, one of the top-three finance journals. The SFS hosts an annual conference, the *SFS Cavalcade*, held in May or June at a major US research university (recent hosts include UNC Chapel Hill, USC, Yale, Carnegie Mellon, and CalTech). The Cavalcade is a Tier 3 venue — selective, but with a strong undergraduate-paper-track that is accessible to ambitious early-undergraduate authors.

**Who attends.** The Cavalcade is a research-faculty conference. Attendance is around 400 — substantially smaller than the FMA's 1,200 or the AFA's 2,500 — but the attendee mix is more concentrated in research-active faculty and advanced PhD students. The discussant pool is strong: many of the discussants are senior research faculty at top-twenty finance programs. The audience for any individual session is around 30–50, dense with expertise.

**Selection process.** Submission window opens in November, closes in mid-January. Authors submit the full manuscript. The SFS program committee selects approximately 30 main-program papers from around 250 submissions (acceptance rate around 12%). The Cavalcade also has a *PhD Student Symposium* and an *Undergraduate Research Symposium* that operate at higher acceptance rates (around 25% and 35% respectively).

**Format.** Main-program sessions are 90 minutes, four papers per session, 22 minutes per paper (15 minutes presentation, 5 minutes discussant, 2 minutes audience Q&A). The undergraduate sessions are typically 60 minutes with three papers, 18 minutes per paper. The format is rigorous: discussants prepare formal slide-deck discussions, and audience questions are sharp.

**What it rewards.** The Cavalcade rewards strong identification, careful empirical work, and contributions that engage the contemporary research frontier in finance. For an undergraduate paper, the bar is high: the paper has to be of a quality that a senior faculty discussant takes seriously. Maya's fair-lending paper, in 2027, with another round of revisions following the FMA poster session, would plausibly be at the Cavalcade undergraduate-symposium level.

**What you get out of attending.** Cavalcade discussant feedback is some of the highest-quality feedback you will get on a paper anywhere. A 5-minute formal discussion by a senior faculty member reshapes the paper. The attendee mix also creates strong networking opportunities; the conference's smaller size makes it easier to meet senior figures than at the AEA or AFA.

For Maya's plan: present the FMA undergraduate poster in October 2026, revise the paper through fall 2026 and winter 2027 based on FMA feedback, submit to the SFS Cavalcade undergraduate symposium in November 2027 (her freshman year of college if she goes to college in fall 2027), present in May 2028 if accepted. This is the canonical climbing path.

## 6. The regional FMAs

The Financial Management Association International, the same organization that runs the FMA Undergraduate Poster Session, also has regional chapters: *Eastern FMA, Midwest FMA, Southwestern FMA, Western FMA*. Each holds an annual meeting (typically in the spring) with a regular research program at Tier 4 selectivity and an undergraduate-research presence.

**Who attends.** Regional FMA attendance is 200–400. The faculty mix is heavily regional (Eastern FMA faculty are mostly from East Coast institutions; Midwest from the Midwest; etc.), with some national presence. Junior faculty and advanced PhD students dominate the regular program. The undergraduate presence is robust at all four regionals.

**Selection process.** Submission deadlines are in December–February (varies by regional). Selection is competitive but accommodating; acceptance rates run 45–65% for the regular program and higher for the undergraduate sessions. The decisions arrive in January–March, with the conferences in March–May.

**Format.** Regular-program sessions are 90 minutes, three to four papers per session, with discussants. Undergraduate sessions are sometimes structured as concurrent paper sessions and sometimes as poster sessions; check the specific regional's format.

**What it rewards.** The regional FMAs reward solid empirical work and the willingness to engage with discussants. They are excellent venues for the second or third presentation of a paper, after the FMA undergraduate poster has refined it.

For Maya: the Eastern FMA in spring 2027 is the natural next venue after the FMA Undergraduate Poster in fall 2026. By spring 2027 her fair-lending paper will be Version 2.0 (or 1.2, depending on revision intensity), and the Eastern FMA's regular-program audience is sophisticated enough to give her substantive feedback at the next level.

## 7. The Eastern Economic Association and other accessible economics venues

The Eastern Economic Association (EEA) annual meeting is the most accessible Tier 4 economics venue. Held in late February in a major Northeast city (recent locations include Boston, Philadelphia, New York), the EEA has a robust undergraduate-paper presence and a low-friction selection process.

**Who attends.** EEA attendance is around 800. The attendee mix leans toward teaching-and-research faculty at liberal-arts colleges and master's-program institutions, with a smaller research-faculty presence than the AEA. Undergraduate authors are welcomed and well-represented.

**Selection process.** Submission deadline in November. Selection is by topic-area chairs (each area — labor, public, finance, urban, etc. — has a chair who organizes sessions). Acceptance rates are high (60–75%); the EEA is more of a "show up and present" venue than a competitive-selection venue.

**Format.** Regular sessions are 90 minutes, three to four papers per session, with discussants. Undergraduate sessions are sometimes integrated into regular sessions and sometimes held separately.

**What it rewards.** The EEA rewards careful work and a willingness to engage with the broader economics community. It is not a credentialing-heavy venue (the line on a CV is modest), but it is a feedback-heavy venue, and the undergraduate-author experience is positive.

The *Southern Economic Association* (SEA, annual meeting in November in a Southern city) and the *Western Economic Association International* (WEAI, annual meeting in June or July in a Western city) operate similarly to the EEA. All three are accessible to precollege and undergraduate authors with solid papers.

For students whose papers fit the economics framing better than the finance framing (Priya's climate-and-insurance paper, for example, is arguably more environmental-economics than finance), the EEA/SEA/WEAI track is a parallel path to the FMA/SFA track. Some camp graduates present at both an FMA regional and an economics regional in the same year; this is fine and even valuable, because the audiences differ.

## 8. The Carroll Round Conference and other university-hosted undergraduate venues

The Carroll Round Conference (CRC), hosted by Georgetown University each April, is the most prestigious dedicated-undergraduate economics-and-finance conference in the United States. The CRC selects approximately 35 undergraduate authors from a national applicant pool of around 300, brings them to Georgetown for a three-day conference, pairs each author with a faculty discussant, and provides intensive mentoring around the paper.

**Selection process.** Submission deadline in early February. Authors submit the full manuscript plus a letter of support from a faculty mentor. Selection is highly competitive (acceptance rate around 12%). The CRC explicitly considers high-school authors with strong research, although the modal admit is a college junior or senior.

**Format.** Sessions are 60 minutes, two papers per session, with formal discussants. The atmosphere is closer to the SFS Cavalcade than to a typical undergraduate session — rigorous, focused, with substantive engagement.

**What it rewards.** The CRC rewards papers that combine clear identification, careful empirical work, and the ability to articulate a contribution. The CRC selection committee values research that engages contemporary policy debates; Maya's fair-lending paper would be a strong fit for the policy-relevance criterion.

Other notable university-hosted undergraduate venues:

- *NYU Stern Undergraduate Finance Research Conference* — November in NYC; finance-focused; accepts about 20 papers per year out of around 150 submissions.
- *Wharton Undergraduate Research Symposium* — April in Philadelphia; broader scope including finance, economics, and business; accepts about 30 papers per year.
- *Harvard Undergraduate Economics Association Conference* — spring in Cambridge; smaller but selective.
- *Columbia Undergraduate Economic Review Symposium* — spring in NYC; tied to the publication.
- *Yale Undergraduate Economic Review Conference* — spring in New Haven; smaller, well-organized.

Many of these accept HS-aged authors with strong papers; check the specific guidelines.

For Maya's plan: target the Carroll Round Conference for the April after her FMA Undergraduate Poster — so April 2027 if she presents at FMA in October 2026. The CRC's national reach and the strength of its discussant pool would make it a high-value presentation. ## 9. The decision tree — when to present where

The student often asks: "I have one paper; which conference should I prioritize?" The decision tree depends on three factors.

**Factor 1 — the paper's stage.** A paper that is still pre-Version 1.0 (rough first draft) should present nowhere; it is not ready. A Version 1.0 paper (the camp manuscript) is ready for Tier 5 venues (FMA Undergraduate Poster, Carroll Round). A Version 2.0 paper (post-FMA-revision) is ready for Tier 4 venues (regional FMAs, EEA). A Version 3.0 paper (post-regional revision) is ready for Tier 3 venues (SFS Cavalcade undergraduate symposium). Climb the tiers as the paper climbs the version numbers.

**Factor 2 — the topic-audience match.** A finance paper goes to FMA venues first; an economics-flavored paper goes to EEA/SEA/WEAI; a methodological paper might go to a methodological conference (the Stata Conference, the useR! conference for R-methodology papers). Match the paper to the audience that will give it the best feedback.

**Factor 3 — the timing of submission.** Conferences have submission deadlines, and the deadlines do not coincide with your readiness to submit. The FMA Undergraduate Poster deadline (late June) and the Carroll Round deadline (early February) mean you need to plan the conference calendar a year ahead. Maya's calendar: late June 2026 submit FMA, October 2026 present FMA, December 2026 revise based on FMA feedback, early February 2027 submit CRC, April 2027 present CRC if accepted, June 2027 submit regional FMA for spring 2028, etc.

For Maya, the eighteen-month conference plan from October 2026 to spring 2028 is:
- *October 2026:* FMA Undergraduate Poster Session (Tier 5).
- *January 2027:* attend the AEA as a registered student, no presentation.
- *February 2027:* attend, possibly present at the Schar Young Scholars Symposium at GMU (in-house Tier 6 venue).
- *March 2027:* present at the Eastern Economic Association undergraduate session if her paper has been revised to Version 1.2 (Tier 4).
- *April 2027:* present at the Carroll Round Conference if accepted (Tier 5/highly selective).
- *October 2027:* return to FMA, possibly in the regular program if her paper is now Version 2.0 (Tier 3).
- *May 2028:* present at SFS Cavalcade undergraduate symposium if accepted (Tier 3).

This is six conferences in eighteen months, which is approximately the pace at which a serious paper accumulates feedback in Phase 3 of the decade arc (Ch 12.3 §5). It is a lot of conferences. The cost is real: each conference is 3 days of travel and approximately $600–1,200 in registration plus travel. The camp's alumni-support fund can underwrite two to three of these for camp graduates; the rest require funding from the student's college institution (most colleges have undergraduate-research-travel funds; apply early) or from the student's family.

The discipline of choosing which conferences to attend, given a limited budget, is to prioritize: the FMA Undergraduate Poster (the must-do credential), the Carroll Round (the must-apply, high-reward), the AEA in January (the must-attend even without presenting), and one regional (the must-do feedback venue). Other venues are nice-to-have; these four are the workhorses.

## 10. The poster vs. paper presentation — what's different

For all camp graduates, the first conference presentation is likely a poster (the FMA Undergraduate Poster, or a poster session at a regional). The poster format differs from the paper-presentation format in ways that matter.

**The poster is a static artifact.** Once printed, it cannot be changed during the conference. The 36-by-48-inch poster has roughly six panels: title and authors; abstract; question and motivation; data and design; results (one or two figures and one table); conclusion and implications. The Tufte data-ink discipline from Ch 11.3 applies fully to posters; sparse, clear, readable from six feet away.

**The presentation is conversational.** The presenter stands beside the poster for 90 minutes. Audience members walk up, the presenter delivers a 90-second elevator pitch, and then the audience member asks questions. Most poster presenters give the same 90-second pitch fifteen to twenty times during a session. Practice the pitch until it is fluid.

**The poster's success depends on the panel.** Audience members glance at the poster for 5–10 seconds before deciding whether to stop. The title and the headline figure must catch attention in those 5 seconds. The headline figure should be your event-study plot or your specification curve (Ch 11.3) — the visualization that summarizes the result.

**Paper-presentation format is different.** At a regional FMA or an SFS Cavalcade undergraduate session, you present a 12–18 minute talk with slides, followed by 4–8 minutes from a discussant and 4–6 minutes of Q&A. The 8-minute talk discipline from Ch 9.2 (in Week 9 of the camp) applies, but scaled up to 12–18 minutes. The discipline is essentially the same: five jobs, one slide per job, with strict time budgeting.

Most camp graduates will move from poster to paper-presentation format around their second year of conference attendance. The two formats are complementary; the poster format is better for early-stage papers and broader audiences, the paper format is better for refined papers and specialist audiences.

## 11. What the conference discussant does, and how to manage them

The discussant is the structural feature of academic conferences that most distinguishes them from other professional gatherings. A discussant is a faculty member (or advanced graduate student) assigned to formally engage with your paper, in writing or in a prepared spoken response, in front of the audience. The discussant has typically read the paper carefully (or at least the abstract, introduction, and tables) and has prepared 4–8 minutes of commentary.

The discussant's commentary follows a standard structure: (1) brief summary of the paper (1 minute), (2) statement of what they liked (1 minute), (3) the main critique (2–3 minutes), (4) secondary critiques (1–2 minutes), (5) suggestions for revision (1 minute). The main critique is the centerpiece; it is the discussant's interpretation of the paper's weakest joint, presented to the audience.

The presenter's job, when the discussant is presenting, is to *listen*. Not to defend, not to interrupt, not to take notes in a way that signals disagreement — to listen carefully and to make a record (in your notebook, not on your face) of every point. After the discussant finishes, the presenter has 1–2 minutes to respond. The response should acknowledge the main critique, agree with what is correct, push back gently on what is mistaken, and commit to following up after the session. Do not try to win the argument; the audience is judging your judgment, not your debating skills.

The discussant's report becomes the most valuable single artifact you receive from the conference. Save the discussant's written comments (request them by email if not provided immediately) and treat them as a referee report. The discussant's main critique often becomes a referee's main critique six months later when the paper is submitted to a journal; addressing it now is two-months-of-work-saved later.

A subtle norm: thank the discussant in writing within 48 hours of the session. The thank-you email is short, specific, and substantive: "Thank you for the careful discussion of the paper today. Your critique about the political-composition measure's endogeneity was the comment I needed; we'll address it in the next revision by [proposed approach]. I appreciated the time you put in." This email is normal professional courtesy and builds a relationship that may matter five years from now when you are sending the same person a paper for review or a recommendation request.

## 12. The discipline of saying no — when to decline a venue

The opposite problem from "which conference should I attend?" is "I'm being invited to too many; which should I decline?" For an active researcher with a strong paper, invitations multiply. The discipline of declining is harder than the discipline of accepting.

The decision rule: present at a conference only if the function-mix justifies the cost. For an early-career researcher, every conference takes 3 days plus prep time (a week of prep for a 15-minute talk is normal), and the cumulative cost of presenting at six conferences in a year is 4–6 weeks of working time. If the conference offers limited feedback (the discussant is weak, the audience is small), limited credentialing (the venue is not on the standard CV list), limited networking (the attendees are not aligned with your sub-field), and limited visibility (the conference's audience does not overlap with the citing literature), then declining is the right move.

For camp graduates: do not feel obligated to present at every conference that accepts you. Triage. Spend energy on the conferences that produce real feedback, real credentialing, real networking, real visibility. The portfolio approach from Ch 12.3 §13 (multiple papers across phases) is the right frame; the discipline is to allocate conference effort across the portfolio, not to present every paper at every venue.

A particular case: presenting *the same paper* at multiple venues in the same year is normal in academic finance — the Rainbow paper was presented at five conferences in one year (Ch 12.3 §5). But each venue should produce a different revision of the paper; you should not present the identical version at conferences 2, 3, and 4 just because you have already prepared the slides. If the paper is not evolving between conferences, you are not learning from the feedback, and the conference time is wasted.

## 13. The commit checklist for a conference presentation

Before submitting an abstract or paper to any conference, run the five-item checklist.

1. **The paper is at the version level the conference rewards.** Tier 5 wants a clean first version; Tier 4 wants a refined second version; Tier 3 wants a near-publication-ready version. If your paper is not at the right level for the tier, climb at your own pace.

2. **The submission deadline is on your calendar with a four-week buffer.** Conference deadlines do not move; missing them costs a year. Set the calendar reminder six weeks out.

3. **The abstract submitted matches the abstract on SSRN, with no quiet changes.** Versioning discipline applies (Ch 12.2 §5).

4. **The faculty mentor (Prof. Gao, or your college advisor once you are in college) has read the abstract and endorses the submission.** The endorsement is not formal; it is the conversation that confirms the paper is ready.

5. **The travel funding is confirmed before submission.** Some conferences ask for institutional-funding confirmation at submission time; even when they don't, you should know how the trip will be paid for before you commit.

Submit. Wait for the decision. If accepted, prepare carefully. If rejected, take the rejection as data — sometimes the conference was wrong for you, sometimes the paper needs more work, sometimes the program committee made a noisy decision and the paper is fine. Re-submit elsewhere.
