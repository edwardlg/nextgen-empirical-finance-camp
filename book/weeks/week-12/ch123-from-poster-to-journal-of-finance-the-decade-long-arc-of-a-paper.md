# Ch 12.3 — From Poster to Journal of Finance: the Decade-Long Arc of a Paper, with One Honest Case Study

Maya has now done the work of Weeks 1–11 and submitted in Week 12 — the manuscript is at the Schar Young Scholars Journal, the preprint is on SSRN, and the replication package is in MARS. The natural question is: what happens to a paper *after* the precollege submission? Some camp papers stop here; the result was solid, the credential was earned, the student goes to college and works on other things. Others — the unusual ones — keep going. They get revised through college, presented at adult conferences, submitted to adult journals, revised through the journal's review process (sometimes for years), and eventually published in a journal whose impact factor is measured in the citation rates of finance's elite venues. **This chapter traces the decade-long arc of such a paper, using one honest case study from Prof. Gao's own work.**

Here is the move this chapter turns on. **The journey from a working-paper draft to a *Journal of Finance* publication is not a straight line; it is a ten-year sequence of revisions, conferences, rejections, and reworkings that bears almost no resemblance to the journey from a course paper to a course grade.** Students who do not understand this — and most students who have not seen the process up close do not — assume that a journal submission either succeeds or fails on its merits in a single round. The reality is that papers that ultimately publish at top venues typically experience two or three desk-rejects, three to five referee rounds across multiple journals, dozens of conference presentations, and significant intellectual reorganization between the working-paper version and the published version. The published version is often *recognizably the same paper* as the working-paper version but in important respects *a different paper*. Understanding this arc — its phases, its time scales, its emotional rhythms — is what enables a researcher to survive it.

The honest case study is the *Rainbow of Credits* paper by Prof. Lei Gao, Sicong Liu, and Yu Wang, on municipal borrowing dynamics across U.S. counties — a paper that as of 2026 is at the AEA-2025-presented working-paper stage, targeting eventual publication at the *Journal of Finance*. The paper is not yet published. The arc is not yet complete. **The chapter therefore offers a partial case study — honest about what has happened, honest about what has not yet happened, honest about the multiple ways the arc may still go.** This is unusual for a textbook; most case studies in research-methods books are retrospective and present a clean narrative. The clean narrative is misleading. Real research is messy in the middle, and the middle is where the lessons are.

---

## 1. The reveal: what the decade-long arc actually looks like

Most papers that eventually publish at top finance and economics journals — the so-called "top five" of *American Economic Review, Quarterly Journal of Economics, Journal of Political Economy, Econometrica, Review of Economic Studies*, or the comparable finance top three of *Journal of Finance, Journal of Financial Economics, Review of Financial Studies* — go through a sequence of phases that runs roughly seven to twelve years from idea to print. The phases are:

**Phase 1 — Idea (months 0–6).** The first version of the idea, often as a side project, a conference paper, or a dissertation chapter. The empirical work is preliminary; the identification strategy is uncertain; the writing is rough.

**Phase 2 — First circulation (months 6–18).** The first SSRN posting, the first informal seminar at the author's home institution, the first feedback from advisors and colleagues. The paper is named, dated, claimed for priority. The first major rewrites begin.

**Phase 3 — Conference presentations (months 12–36).** A sequence of presentations at regional, national, and topical conferences — five to fifteen presentations over two years. Each presentation produces feedback that shapes the next revision. The paper goes through three to six major revisions during this phase.

**Phase 4 — First journal submission (months 24–36).** The first submission to a journal, usually after the paper has been presented at the AEA, WFA, or AFA. The journal is chosen at one notch above where the author thinks the paper "should" land — the "stretch journal." The first decision arrives in 8–24 weeks, typically as a desk-reject or a revise-and-resubmit at a lower-tier journal.

**Phase 5 — The R&R cycle (months 36–84).** The paper enters a revise-and-resubmit cycle at one or more journals. Each cycle takes 12–18 months from response submission to next decision. Most papers go through two R&R rounds before final acceptance.

**Phase 6 — Acceptance and proof (months 72–96).** The paper is accepted. Copy-editing, proof-reading, and finalization of the published version take 4–8 months. The paper appears in a numbered issue, with a DOI, in the journal's table of contents.

**Phase 7 — Citation and the long tail (months 96–?).** The paper, once published, accumulates citations. The first 24 months are when the paper is "new" and citation rates are highest; after that, citations stabilize at a long-tail rate that depends on the paper's lasting relevance.

Each phase has its own discipline, its own emotional rhythm, and its own failure modes. We will walk through each phase with the Rainbow of Credits paper as the carrying example.

## 2. The Rainbow of Credits paper — what it is

Before tracing the arc, here is what the paper is, so the case study makes sense.

The paper is *Rainbow of Credits: Municipal Borrowing, Color Segments, and the Hidden Politics of Local Finance*, by Lei Gao, Sicong Liu, and Yu Wang. The substantive question is whether U.S. counties' political-ideological composition affects their municipal borrowing patterns, controlling for fiscal fundamentals, demographics, and bond-market conditions. The intuition: municipal-bond markets are usually modeled as if politics is exogenous to financing decisions, but counties with different political compositions face different state-level pre-emption regimes, different voter expectations about debt, and different responses to fiscal stress. The "rainbow" in the title refers to the spectrum of political-color classifications (deep red, light red, purple, light blue, deep blue) the paper uses to classify counties.

The empirical core is a panel analysis of approximately 3,100 U.S. counties from 2002–2022 using Mergent Municipal Bond Securities Database and Census-of-Governments fiscal data. The identification leverages within-county variation in political composition driven by federal-level political-cycle shocks (mid-term elections, presidential elections), with state-level pre-emption regimes as a cross-sectional dimension. The headline finding (as of the 2025 AEA version) is that political composition has a substantively large effect on municipal-borrowing decisions even after extensive controls for fiscal fundamentals — an effect that is hidden in standard analyses because the standard controls are themselves correlated with political composition.

The paper is targeted at the *Journal of Finance*. It is not yet at JF; it has been presented at AEA 2025 and is currently being revised for submission. The arc is therefore in Phase 4 (first journal submission, soon) and Phase 3 (still presenting at conferences) simultaneously. We will trace what has happened so far and discuss what is likely to happen next.

---

## 3. Phase 1 — Idea (2019–2020)

The Rainbow of Credits idea began as a side conversation between Prof. Gao and a former PhD student about a curious pattern in the Mergent data: counties with similar fiscal fundamentals had wildly different borrowing patterns, and the differences seemed to track political composition in a way that the existing literature (which controlled for political composition as an afterthought) did not explain.

The first version of the paper was a 30-page draft that conflated three different empirical questions: (1) the effect of political composition on borrowing levels, (2) the effect of political composition on borrowing costs, and (3) the effect of political composition on the *composition* of borrowing (general-obligation vs. revenue bonds, taxable vs. tax-exempt, term structure). The draft was internally circulated to two colleagues at GMU in spring 2020. Their feedback, which Prof. Gao recalls verbatim, was: "There are three papers here. Pick one."

This is the Phase-1 lesson: the first version of an idea is usually three ideas. The discipline of Phase 1 is to identify which of the three is the cleanest, has the cleanest identification, and has the largest payoff. Gao, Liu, and Wang picked Question 1 (borrowing levels) as the central paper, with Question 2 (borrowing costs) reserved as a follow-up paper and Question 3 (borrowing composition) abandoned. The decision to *narrow* the paper to one question was the Phase-1 breakthrough.

The version of the paper at the end of Phase 1, in mid-2020, was 38 pages, focused on borrowing levels, with a preliminary identification strategy that exploited the staggered federal-election cycle. The empirical results were promising but not yet definitive; the writing was rough; the literature review was a bulleted enumeration rather than a three-strand braid (Ch 11.5). The paper was not ready for circulation, but it was ready to be claimed.

---

## 4. Phase 2 — First circulation (2020–2021)

The first SSRN posting of *Rainbow of Credits* was in late 2020. The version was the "first circulating draft," with a careful version footnote: "Version 1.0, November 12, 2020." The abstract was 290 words — too long by the Ch 11.1 standards we now teach — and the design clause (sentence 3) was diffuse. The paper got 34 downloads in its first month, which was below average for SSRN finance papers and reflected the rough state of the manuscript.

The first informal presentations were at the GMU Costello College of Business internal finance brown-bag in fall 2020 and at a virtual seminar at the University of Virginia in spring 2021. The feedback from those two presentations is preserved in Gao's notebook and is worth sharing in detail because it illustrates the *substantive* feedback an early-phase paper gets:

- **GMU brown-bag (October 2020).** "The pre-emption regime variation is doing a lot of work; I'd like to see a Susan Athey–style design where you treat the pre-emption as the treatment and use neighboring-state-pair comparisons to identify the effect." — Comment from a senior faculty member.
- **UVa virtual seminar (March 2021).** "The political-composition measure is endogenous to local economic conditions in ways that the paper doesn't fully address. I'd like to see an instrumental-variables strategy or a sharp regression-discontinuity at the close-election threshold." — Comment from a junior faculty member.
- **GMU brown-bag follow-up (May 2021).** "The literature review reads like a Wikipedia article. You need to engage with the Yagan (2019) paper on pre-emption and with the Cohen, Coval, and Malloy (2011) paper on political budget cycles. Both papers your contribution claim has to push past." — Comment from a different senior faculty member.

Three comments, three different methodological demands. The Phase-2 discipline is to triage them — exactly the triage from Ch 10.1. Gao, Liu, and Wang assessed the cost and value of each: the Athey-style neighbor-pair design (high cost, high value); the close-election RD (high cost, medium value because the close-election sample is small); the literature-review rewrite (low cost, high value). The decision was to do the cheap-and-valuable rewrite immediately, schedule the Athey neighbor-pair design as a major revision, and defer the RD strategy for a robustness section. This decision sequence is exactly what the Week 10 triage memo would produce; it is the same skill applied at a different scale.

By the end of Phase 2, in summer 2021, the paper was 47 pages, had a tighter design, had a literature review that was beginning to look like a three-strand braid, and had 142 SSRN downloads. It was still not ready for journal submission.

## 5. Phase 3 — Conference presentations (2021–2024)

This is the phase where the paper got the most feedback and where the largest reorganization happened. Over three years, the paper was presented at:

- *Eastern Finance Association annual meeting* (April 2022) — a regional finance conference. Audience of about 30; questions from two senior discussants. The presentation revealed that the political-composition measure was being read as too crude by audience members familiar with the political-science literature; this prompted the construction of a multi-dimensional composition measure (using DW-NOMINATE-style scoring of state legislators by county) rather than the binary red/blue measure.
- *Financial Management Association annual meeting* (October 2022) — a larger national conference. The paper was on a session with three other municipal-finance papers; the feedback emphasized that the dependent variable (debt issuance levels) was missing important institutional features (state-level debt limits, voter-approval requirements, refunding-vs-new-money composition). The next revision added a four-level institutional-feature classification and a sub-sample analysis.
- *Southern Finance Association annual meeting* (November 2022) — a regional conference, lower-profile but with a more careful discussion. The discussant produced an eight-page formal discussion paper; the major comment was that the paper's contribution claim was overstated — the existing literature did *not* assume political composition was exogenous, and the paper needed to engage more carefully with Brennan and Eichengreen (2011) on bond-rating political effects.
- *Western Finance Association annual meeting* (June 2023) — a major national conference, with rigorous discussant standards. The paper was assigned to two discussants. The first discussant's primary objection was about external validity: "Is this U.S.-specific, or does it generalize? The municipal-bond market is institutionally distinctive; if you can't show the pattern in Canadian provincial bonds or German Länder, the external-validity claim is weak." The second discussant's primary objection was about mechanism: "You show a reduced-form effect but you don't show *why*. Is it cost-of-borrowing channel? Demand-for-public-services channel? Political-credit-market channel? Without mechanism evidence the paper is suggestive but not convincing."
- *American Finance Association annual meeting* (January 2024) — the premier annual finance conference, presentation by competitive selection. The paper was on a session with three other state-and-local-finance papers; the discussant was a senior bond-market scholar. Her main comment was: "I believe the empirical result, and I think the political-composition effect is real. But the framing as a 'rainbow' is too cute; you're going to lose the JF editor on the title alone. Either drop the rainbow framing or commit to it fully with a much more careful taxonomic discussion."

Five conferences, fifteen substantive comments, and a paper that by January 2024 was 62 pages, had a multi-dimensional composition measure, a four-level institutional-feature classification, a Canadian-provincial robustness section, a mechanism-decomposition section, and a literature review that engaged Brennan and Eichengreen (2011) carefully. The paper title was retained as *Rainbow of Credits*, but Section 1 (the introduction) now included a paragraph defending the rainbow framing.

The Phase-3 lesson is twofold. **First, conference feedback is the most valuable feedback you will ever receive, and you should present at every conference that will have you** — regional, national, topical, undergraduate-friendly when applicable. The Phase-3 conferences for Rainbow of Credits cumulatively reshaped the paper into something it was not before. **Second, every conference presentation produces an updated SSRN version,** and the version footnote becomes the record of intellectual development. By the end of Phase 3 the paper was on Version 4.2 ("January 2024, post-AFA"), and the version history was the public record of how the paper had matured.

## 6. Phase 4 — First journal submission (2024–present)

This is the phase the Rainbow of Credits paper is in *now*, in 2026. The paper has been presented at AEA 2025 (the American Economic Association annual meeting, where it was selected for the finance session — a substantial credential), and the authors are preparing the submission to the *Journal of Finance*. The submission has not yet happened.

The Phase-4 decision is *which journal to submit to*. The Rainbow of Credits paper is being targeted at JF for three reasons. First, the substantive question — political effects on municipal-borrowing decisions — is in the finance scope (JF is a finance journal), not in the economics scope (where it might be a fit for *Journal of Political Economy* or the AEJ family). Second, the empirical sophistication of the paper — staggered identification, robustness battery, mechanism decomposition, external-validity exercises — is at the top-finance-journal level. Third, the policy relevance — municipal borrowing is a $3.8 trillion market with direct implications for state and local fiscal management — gives the editor a reason to consider it for a high-profile placement.

The competing target journals are *Journal of Financial Economics* (the second-tier within the JF/JFE/RFS top three; equally selective but with slightly different editorial taste) and the *Review of Financial Studies* (more methodological in editorial taste). The decision to target JF first is a Phase-4 strategic choice; if JF desk-rejects, the next stop is JFE, then RFS, then the next tier (e.g., *Review of Finance, Journal of Financial and Quantitative Analysis, Journal of Banking and Finance*). The "ladder" of journal targeting is the discipline of Phase 4: you start at the top, and you work down only as the upper rungs reject.

The cover letter for the JF submission is being drafted now. It follows the four-paragraph structure of Ch 12.1 §4 but with more weight: the contribution paragraph is two sentences instead of one, and the fit paragraph names two recent JF papers (Adelino, Cunha, and Ferreira 2017 on municipal-bond rating effects; Schwert 2017 on muni-bond yield spreads) that the Rainbow paper engages directly. The cover letter is a piece of intellectual marketing; it has to make the editor want to read the paper.

The submission timing is being planned for September 2026. The journal's median time-to-first-decision is 8 months for finance submissions; that decision will come around May 2027. The Rainbow paper has been in development for seven years at that point.

## 7. Phase 5 — The R&R cycle (projected, 2027 onward)

This is the phase that has not happened yet for Rainbow of Credits but that we can project based on prior experience and based on the median trajectory of papers that ultimately publish at JF.

The JF first decision will be one of four outcomes:
- *Desk-reject* (about 40% of JF submissions). The editor reads the paper, decides it is not a fit for JF, and returns it without sending to referees. The Rainbow paper would then go to JFE, restarting the clock.
- *Sent for review, then rejected after referee reports* (about 35% of JF submissions). The paper goes to two or three referees, the reports come back negative or mixed, and the editor rejects. The paper would then go to JFE or RFS.
- *Sent for review, returned as revise-and-resubmit* (about 20% of JF submissions). The most desirable outcome short of conditional accept; the paper has a path forward at JF but with significant revisions. The R&R typically comes with detailed referee reports (10–30 pages each) and an editor's letter that highlights the priority concerns.
- *Conditional accept or accept* (about 5% of JF submissions). Extremely rare for a first submission; means the paper is essentially ready.

For Rainbow of Credits, the realistic expectation is that the first JF decision will be a desk-reject or a review-then-reject. The paper is good but not so obviously path-breaking that JF takes it on first round. The probable trajectory is JF desk-reject → JFE submission → JFE R&R → 12-month revision cycle → JFE second-round → conditional accept or further revision → eventual JFE publication around 2030. The 2030 publication date would be approximately ten years from the Phase-1 origin in 2019–2020.

The R&R cycle has its own discipline. The discipline is the same one Week 10 taught — the triage memo (Ch 10.1), the response letter, the multiple-testing audit (Ch 10.2), the heterogeneity analysis (Ch 10.3), the mechanism plan (Ch 10.4), the external-validity paragraph (Ch 10.5) — but applied at full scale to a single referee's 25-page report rather than to conference comments. Each referee report demands a 30–50 page response letter, with point-by-point engagement with every comment, accompanied by a revised manuscript that visibly addresses each point. The response letters for top-finance R&Rs are themselves substantial pieces of writing; they sometimes run as long as the original manuscript.

The emotional rhythm of Phase 5 is the hardest part. R&R cycles are slow (12–18 months per round), the work is invisible to outside observers (only the authors and the journal know what's happening), and the outcome is uncertain. Authors describe Phase 5 as "the longest year of my life" multiple times in succession. The discipline that gets researchers through Phase 5 is the same discipline that Week 10 teaches: triage what matters, do the work, document the revision, and trust that the cumulative improvement is real even when no single round seems to move the needle.

## 8. Phase 6 and Phase 7 — Acceptance, proof, and the long tail (projected)

Once the paper is accepted (whether at JF, JFE, or elsewhere), Phase 6 is procedural. The journal copy-editor checks references, queries the authors about ambiguous citations, requests final figures in publication-ready format, and produces page proofs. The authors read the proofs carefully, mark corrections, and return. The paper appears online in the journal's "Articles in Advance" feed within 4–8 weeks of final acceptance; it appears in a print issue 6–18 months later. The DOI is registered when the article goes online.

Phase 7 — the long tail — is where the paper begins its citation life. The first 24 months after publication are the "new paper" window, when citation rates are highest. After that, citations stabilize at a paper-specific long-tail rate. The Rainbow paper, given the substantive importance of the municipal-bond market and the rarity of credibly identified political-effects papers, would plausibly accumulate 50–100 citations in its first five years, with a long tail extending out a decade or more. Some papers — Gao's earlier Gao, Han, Li, and Zhou (2018) on intraday momentum, for example — accumulate citations at a much faster rate because they touch a more active subliterature; intraday momentum had over 800 citations by 2023.

The citation count, in turn, becomes the credential that supports the paper's claim to be a contribution. Future papers cite Rainbow of Credits when they discuss political effects on municipal borrowing; future textbooks include a paragraph on the result; future PhD students read the paper in their second-year coursework. The paper enters the conversation and stays in it.

## 9. The non-linear realities of the arc

We have presented the arc as if it were a sequence — Phase 1, then 2, then 3, then 4, then 5 — but the reality is that the phases overlap and sometimes recur.

**Conferences happen during the R&R cycle.** Even after a paper is in R&R at a major journal, the authors continue to present at conferences. The conference comments inform the R&R revisions; the R&R revisions become the new conference version. The two feedback channels run in parallel for years.

**Multiple journals run in parallel.** Some authors submit to one journal at a time (the convention in most of empirical finance); others, particularly in fields where multiple submission is allowed, run two channels simultaneously. The Rainbow paper, given its targeted top-finance positioning, will go through journals serially: JF first, then JFE if needed, then RFS if needed. The waiting cost of serial submission is steep — each rejection adds 8–12 months — but the convention is firm.

**Papers split, merge, and rename.** Sometimes a paper that started as one project becomes two papers, with the authors splitting the original questions into a "main paper" and a "companion paper." Sometimes two papers merge into one when the authors realize the questions are inseparable. Sometimes the title changes: Rainbow of Credits's working title in 2019 was "Color-Coded Borrowing: Political Composition and Municipal-Bond Issuance"; the title became Rainbow of Credits at the 2022 SFA presentation when a discussant called the original title "academic and uninviting." Titles matter; the paper's title is what readers see first, and it influences the editor's decision to read it.

**Personnel change.** Over a ten-year arc, co-authors change roles, move institutions, change priorities. The Rainbow paper's third co-author (Yu Wang) was a PhD student at the start of the project and is now an Assistant Professor; her career trajectory shapes how much time she can spend on the revision. Co-authorships sometimes dissolve mid-arc, requiring restructured authorship. The discipline is to keep all authors involved with each major revision and to document contributions carefully as the project evolves.

**Setbacks happen.** A new dataset arrives that contradicts the result. A new paper by another group is published that scoops part of the contribution. A method that the paper used becomes outdated. The Rainbow paper's empirical strategy, for example, evolved significantly when the Callaway and Sant'Anna (2021) staggered-DiD literature reshaped the methodological standards in 2021–2022; the paper had to be redone in part using the new methods, which delayed the timeline by approximately nine months. Major methodological updates in a literature are setbacks that authors have to absorb; the discipline is to redo the analysis to the new standard rather than to defend the old standard.

## 10. The shorter arc — papers that publish faster

Not every paper takes ten years. Some publish in three to five years, and it is worth knowing what makes those papers different.

The shorter arc has three features. First, the **identification is unusually clean** — a natural experiment with sharp timing, a regression discontinuity with a credible cutoff, an exogenous shock that affects exactly the relevant population. Papers with unusually clean identification can skip some of the Phase-3 conference grind because the identification is self-evident; the conferences confirm rather than reshape.

Second, the **topical area is unusually hot** — a question that the field has been debating, that is on the JF editor's mind, that has high policy relevance. Papers in hot areas get faster reviews, faster decisions, more sympathetic referees. The post-2008 financial-crisis literature, for example, produced many fast-arc papers because the topic was urgent and the empirical infrastructure (CRSP, Compustat, regulatory filings) was already in place.

Third, the **author is established**. A faculty member with a strong publication record gets faster reads from editors, more deferential reviews from referees, and earlier acceptance. This is not strictly fair, but it is empirically real. A first paper by an unknown author takes longer than a fifth paper by a known author, ceteris paribus.

For Maya's fair-lending paper, the shorter-arc possibility is real. The paper's identification (staggered DiD on a regulatory rollout) is clean. The topical area (fair lending in the post-2008 era) is moderately hot. The author is unknown (Maya is a precollege author). The realistic projection is that Maya's paper, with the right revisions and the right journal targeting, could publish at *Journal of Financial Economics* or *Journal of Banking and Finance* in three to five years (Maya's college years) rather than ten. This would be a strong publication trajectory and Prof. Gao thinks it is feasible.

For Devon's crypto paper, the shorter arc is also possible but for different reasons — the methodological novelty of using on-chain data for an empirical-finance question is the contribution, and methodological novelty papers sometimes publish faster because they appeal to editors looking for paradigm-extending work.

For Priya, Sam, and Leah, the arc projections vary. Priya's climate-and-insurance paper engages a hot topic but the empirical infrastructure (insurance-company financials, climate-event data) is less standardized, so the methodological battle is harder. Sam's intraday-momentum paper is in a topically saturated area (post-Gao, Han, Li, and Zhou (2018), the literature is crowded) so the contribution has to be sharper to publish at the top. Leah's patent-text paper is in a fast-moving methodological area where the right move is to publish quickly before the methods become standard.

## 11. What you can do now, at the precollege stage

The decade-long arc looks long because it is. But there are decisions you can make now — at the start of the arc — that meaningfully affect where the arc lands.

**Decision 1 — pick a question that is durably interesting.** The Rainbow paper picked municipal borrowing because the question of how local politics shapes local fiscal decisions is *durably* interesting — it will be relevant in 2026, 2030, and 2040. The fair-lending question Maya picked is similarly durable. The intraday-momentum question Sam picked is moderately durable but more sensitive to market-structure changes (high-frequency trading evolution may render some of the result period-specific). The climate-and-insurance question Priya picked is becoming more durable as climate effects become more pronounced. Pick questions whose interest does not depend on a single news cycle.

**Decision 2 — invest in identification.** The papers that publish at top journals are the papers whose identification is credible. The seven weeks of Phase 1 of the camp that you spent on identification (Weeks 6–8) and the Week 7.5 identification memo (Ch 7.5) are not bureaucratic exercises; they are the foundation on which everything else stands. A paper without a credible identification strategy can still produce learning, but it does not publish at JF.

**Decision 3 — invest in writing.** The Week 11 writing discipline (abstract, tables, figures, introduction, literature review) is the second foundation. A paper with great identification but bad writing has to be rewritten before it can publish; a paper with good writing and good identification is closer to ready. Invest in writing now; the time pays back.

**Decision 4 — start the conference circuit early.** The five-conference Phase-3 trajectory of Rainbow of Credits is the model. Even as a precollege author, present at every undergraduate-friendly conference that will have you. The FMA Undergraduate Poster, the AEA Pipeline programs, regional finance association undergraduate sessions — these are the entry points to the conference network. Chapter 12.4 walks through the specific venues.

**Decision 5 — accept that the arc is long.** The temptation to short-circuit the arc — to submit before the paper is ready, to skip the conferences, to push for the top journal before the work justifies it — is the most common cause of premature rejection and stalled trajectories. The discipline is patience. The decade-long arc is real; the papers that publish at top journals are the ones whose authors do the work of every phase.

## 12. The honest case — what could go wrong with Rainbow of Credits

We should also discuss the failure modes. The Rainbow paper might not publish at JF. It might not publish at JFE either. The likely failure modes are:

**Failure mode 1 — the identification doesn't hold.** A referee at JF could decide that the political-composition measure is endogenous in ways the paper hasn't fully addressed, and recommend rejection. The paper would then have to invent a new identification strategy — perhaps an instrumental-variables approach using federal-level political shocks that propagate to county composition — and this could take another two to three years. If the new strategy doesn't work, the paper could stall at the working-paper stage indefinitely.

**Failure mode 2 — the field moves.** A different paper, by a different group, could publish first with a similar question and a cleaner design. The Rainbow paper would then become a contribution to an existing literature rather than a foundational paper, and its placement target would drop from JF to one tier lower.

**Failure mode 3 — the policy relevance fades.** If the municipal-bond market goes through a structural change that makes the 2002–2022 sample less relevant (a major federal preemption of state borrowing rules, for example), the paper's policy relevance fades, and the editor's interest fades with it. The paper would then have to extend the sample and re-establish relevance, which is expensive.

**Failure mode 4 — the authors lose interest or capacity.** Over a ten-year arc, the authors face their own career changes. If Yu Wang's tenure clock requires her to focus on other papers, if Sicong Liu's institutional priorities shift, if Gao takes on administrative responsibilities — any of these could slow the revision pace. The Rainbow paper requires three authors' continued investment for another five years. That is a substantial commitment.

For each of these failure modes, the paper can adapt — but the adaptation costs time, and the longer the arc, the more failure modes have a chance to materialize. The honest assessment is that there is perhaps a 50% chance the Rainbow paper publishes at JF or JFE within seven years, perhaps a 35% chance it publishes at a lower-tier journal within seven years, and perhaps a 15% chance it never publishes in its current form. Those are honest numbers for an in-progress top-finance project. Most projects of similar ambition have similar odds.

The corollary for the camp's papers is that *any single paper* has uncertain publication prospects, and the discipline of building a research career is to have *multiple papers in flight at different phases simultaneously*. By the time the Rainbow paper is in Phase 5, Gao has other papers in Phase 4, Phase 3, and Phase 2. The portfolio of projects across phases is what makes the career work; no single paper carries the load. Maya should plan, even now, on doing more than one project across the next decade. The fair-lending paper is one of perhaps four or five papers she will write before age 30. The arc is long for any one paper; the *career* is built across many.

## 13. The commit lessons from the arc

The discipline of this chapter is not primarily a checklist; it is a posture toward the long-term work. Six lessons follow from the arc.

**Lesson 1 — every paper is a multi-year project.** Plan accordingly. Do not start a paper unless you are willing to spend three to ten years on it.

**Lesson 2 — feedback is the most valuable input.** Conference presentations, advisor comments, referee reports — each is feedback, and each must be taken seriously. The triage discipline (Ch 10.1) is the same skill at every phase.

**Lesson 3 — versioning matters.** The version footnote, the SSRN posting history, the response-letter archive — these are the historical record of how the paper matured. Maintain them carefully.

**Lesson 4 — the journal hierarchy is real but not destiny.** Targeting the top journal first is a strategic choice; descending the ladder as needed is normal; the paper's quality, not the journal's tier, is what builds your career in the long run.

**Lesson 5 — patience is the load-bearing virtue.** Authors who can wait — for the next referee report, for the next R&R, for the right conference — are the authors who publish at the top. Authors who cannot wait either give up or publish prematurely at lower-tier venues.

**Lesson 6 — portfolios beat individual papers.** No single paper carries a career. Plan for a sequence of projects, at different phases, with different timelines, so that the slow phases of any one paper are offset by progress on others.
