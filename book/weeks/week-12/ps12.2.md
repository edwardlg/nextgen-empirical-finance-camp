# PS 12.2 — SSRN Pre-Print Draft with JEL Codes, Keywords, and Versioning Plan

**Course:** 8-Week Empirical Finance Camp · Week 12 · Problem Set 12.2
**Covers:** the move from Chapter 12.2 — the SSRN preprint as a time-stamped public record, with the network-selection, JEL-classification, keyword-discipline, and versioning machinery that turn a manuscript PDF into a discoverable working paper. This problem set is the parallel deposit channel to the Schar Young Scholars Journal submission you assembled in PS 12.1; same paper, different audience, different metadata norms, and a versioning plan that has to live for years. PS 12.2 builds on the JEL-code conversation in Chapter 12.2 §5, the network-taxonomy table in §6, and the versioning-discipline argument in §7. It also leans on PS 11.1 (the abstract is your SSRN abstract verbatim) and PS 11.4 (the introduction's paragraph 5 is the source of the contribution sentence in your SSRN metadata).
**Type:** ~30% metadata research (JEL codes, networks, keywords), ~30% writing (the SSRN-specific cover blurb, the version footnote, the abstract polish), ~25% versioning planning (the version-numbering scheme, the change-log discipline, the citation-recommendation text), ~15% verification (the SSRN posting checklist, the cross-reference check between SSRN and MARS).

**Total: 100 points.** Point values stated per part. A model deliverable — Devon's stablecoin paper SSRN posting, with JEL codes G14 / G18 / G23, keywords chosen for FEN/BankN discovery, a v1.0 footer dated October 2026, and a versioning plan that anticipates v2.0 after the November conference comments — is in Appendix E (`E-w12-ps12.2-solutions.md`).

A boundary worth holding before you start. PS 12.2 is **not** "post to SSRN." The literal posting takes 60 minutes once the metadata is right; the discipline this problem set teaches is the *metadata work that has to be done before the form is opened*. A poorly classified paper on SSRN is a paper that downloads at one-tenth the rate of a well-classified one, because the SSRN discovery infrastructure — the FEN and BankN email digests, the topic-area search, the recommender — runs on the JEL codes and keywords you choose. A poorly versioned paper on SSRN is a paper that, three years from now, a citing scholar cannot find the version of, because the "v1" you posted in October 2026 has been silently replaced by "v3.7" with no change-log and no archived prior version. The metadata discipline is the discovery discipline; the versioning discipline is the citation discipline. Both compound over years.

## Part 1 — JEL codes (15 points)

The single sin this part prevents: **classifying your paper with the wrong JEL codes, which sends it to the wrong SSRN topic networks, which sends it to the wrong scholars' inboxes, which produces the silent failure mode of "great paper, nobody downloaded it."**

The JEL classification — published by the *Journal of Economic Literature* and maintained by the American Economic Association — is the canonical subject taxonomy of economics. Codes are alphanumeric (e.g., G14, G18, G21) and organized hierarchically: top-level letters (A–Z) name broad fields, two-digit codes (G2) name sub-fields (financial institutions), three-character codes (G21) name finer categories (banks, depository institutions). SSRN, RepEc, NBER, and most economics journals use JEL codes as the primary subject index.

**(a) Select three to six JEL codes (10 pts, ~2 each up to 5).** Choose codes at the *three-character* level (G14, G21, not G2). Each code must be defensible — your paper has to engage substantively with the literature in that JEL area. Do not over-classify; six codes on a single paper signals that the author does not know what the paper is. The modal published empirical-finance paper has three to four JEL codes.

For each code you select, write one sentence justifying the choice with reference to *what in your paper* matches the JEL definition. Example for Maya's fair-lending paper:

- *G21 — Banks; Depository Institutions; Micro Finance Institutions; Mortgages.* "The paper studies mortgage-denial behavior by OCC-supervised depository institutions, the canonical G21 subject."
- *G28 — Government Policy and Regulation (Financial Institutions).* "The paper estimates the causal effect of a federal supervisory program, the canonical G28 subject."
- *J15 — Economics of Minorities, Races, Indigenous Peoples, and Immigrants; Non-labor Discrimination.* "The outcome is the Black–White denial-rate gap, which is the canonical J15 subject for non-labor-market discrimination."
- *G18 — Financial Markets and the Macroeconomy: Government Policy and Regulation.* "Optional fourth code; the paper engages secondarily with macroprudential supervision literature."

Maya picks three JEL codes (G21, G28, J15) with one optional fourth (G18). Devon (algorithmic stablecoins) picks G14, G18, G23. Priya (climate-insurance) picks G22, Q54, R52. Sam (intraday momentum) picks G12, G14, G17. Leah (patents-innovation-text) picks G14, O34, M13.

**(b) Audit the code selection against the JEL hierarchy (5 pts).** Look up each of your selected codes on the official JEL classification page at `https://www.aeaweb.org/econlit/jelCodes.php`. Verify that the *full text* of the code's definition matches your paper. Document the audit in `jel-codes.md` as a short table: code, official definition (paraphrased), your paper's match. If any code's definition does not actually match, replace it.

## Part 2 — Keywords (15 points)

The single sin this part prevents: **choosing keywords that are too generic (and lose to the 10,000 other papers using the same keyword) or too narrow (and have zero search volume).**

SSRN allows up to six keywords per paper. Keywords are free text — there is no controlled vocabulary — which is both an opportunity (you can choose the exact phrase a scholar would search for) and a hazard (you can choose a phrase nobody searches for). The discipline is *Goldilocks search volume*: a keyword that returns 500–5,000 hits on Google Scholar is in the right band; a keyword that returns 5 hits is too narrow; a keyword that returns 200,000 hits is too broad.

**(a) Select four to six keywords (10 pts).** Each keyword should be a phrase a researcher familiar with your area would actually type into a search bar. Use lowercase, no punctuation other than hyphens, two to four words per phrase. Examples for Maya:

- *fair lending.* Broad-but-discoverable; ~30,000 Google Scholar hits in the past five years.
- *mortgage discrimination.* Sharper than "fair lending"; ~12,000 hits.
- *OCC supervision.* Institutional-specific; ~1,800 hits — narrow enough to surface to specialists.
- *staggered difference-in-differences.* Method-specific; ~6,500 hits — surfaces to method-focused readers.
- *HMDA.* Data-specific; ~9,000 hits — surfaces to data-aware readers.
- *examination intensity.* Treatment-specific; ~900 hits — narrow but matches the contribution.

The mix matters: include one broad (fair lending), one medium (mortgage discrimination), one institution-specific (OCC), one method (staggered DiD), one data (HMDA), and one treatment (examination intensity). The mix maximizes the chance of being found by scholars approaching the paper from any of those angles.

**(b) Run the search-volume audit (5 pts).** For each keyword, search Google Scholar for `"<keyword>" finance 2020..2026` and record the approximate result count. Tabulate in `keywords.md` with columns: keyword, Google Scholar count (band), inclusion rationale, expected reader-type. Cut any keyword whose count is below ~100 (too narrow to discover) or above ~100,000 (too broad to differentiate).

A small subtlety: SSRN's own search uses prefix matching and stemming, so "fair lending" and "fair-lending" return the same hits, but "fair lendings" (plural) does not. Use the singular noun.

## Part 3 — SSRN network selection (15 points)

The single sin this part prevents: **cross-posting to too many networks (which reads as desperate and gets fewer downloads) or to the wrong networks (which sends the paper to scholars who will not read it).**

SSRN is organized by *network*. Each network is a topical community with its own subscriber list and weekly email digest. When you post a paper, you cross-post to one to ten networks; each network's subscribers receive the abstract in the next digest. The canonical advice is: choose *three to five* networks that match your paper's primary topics. Six or more reads as cross-posting spam; one or two reads as under-marketing.

**(a) Select three to five SSRN networks (10 pts, ~2 each).** Reference the SSRN network taxonomy (browseable at `https://www.ssrn.com/index.cfm/en/Networks/`) and select networks whose subject areas match your paper. For each selected network, write one sentence justifying the choice. Examples for Maya:

- *Financial Economics Network (FEN), Banking eJournal.* "Paper's primary venue: it studies bank-supervisor behavior and bank-level mortgage outcomes."
- *Financial Economics Network (FEN), Real Estate eJournal.* "Mortgages and housing-finance audience."
- *Banking Network (BankN), Bank Regulation eJournal.* "Direct fit: a federal-supervision-program causal effect."
- *Labor and Employment Law eJournal* (optional — only if the paper's discrimination angle is the lead). For Maya the discrimination angle is secondary to the supervision angle, so this one is borderline.

Maya picks four networks (FEN-Banking, FEN-Real-Estate, BankN-Bank-Regulation, FEN-Government-and-Financial-Markets). Devon picks three (FEN-Capital-Markets, FEN-Microstructure-eJournal, FEN-FinTech-eJournal). Priya picks four (FEN-Insurance, FEN-Real-Estate, ERN-Environment, FEN-Climate-Finance). Sam picks three (FEN-Capital-Markets, FEN-Microstructure, BehaviorN-Behavioral-Finance). Leah picks three (FEN-Innovation, IPRN-Intellectual-Property, FEN-Corporate-Governance).

**(b) Justify the network choices and the omissions (5 pts).** A one-paragraph rationale, ~150 words, naming *which networks you considered and rejected* and why. The rejection list is as informative as the selection list. Example: "We considered SSRN's Behavioral and Experimental Economics eJournal but rejected it because the paper does not test a behavioral hypothesis; we considered ERN's Public Economics eJournal but rejected it because the paper's policy framing is supervisory, not fiscal." The audit demonstrates that you have *looked at* the network taxonomy rather than guessing.

## Part 4 — The SSRN-specific cover blurb and abstract polish (15 points)

The single sin this part prevents: **pasting the journal cover letter into SSRN, where the editor-as-reader is not the audience. SSRN has a different reader and a different writing format.**

The SSRN posting interface asks for two text fields in addition to the metadata: the *abstract* (which is the same abstract from PS 11.1, with the version footnote appended) and the *paper info / cover blurb* (a 100–250-word descriptive paragraph that appears on the abstract page above the download button). The cover blurb is *not* the journal cover letter; it is a marketing document aimed at the abstract-page visitor who is deciding whether to download the PDF. It is read by a different audience than either the journal editor or the manuscript referee.

**(a) Paste the chosen abstract from PS 11.1, with the version footnote (5 pts).** The abstract is exactly the version you chose in PS 11.1 — verbatim, no editing. At the end of the abstract, append a version footnote on its own line:

> *Version 1.0, October 7, 2026. Forthcoming in the Schar Young Scholars Journal (under review). For the current version, see https://ssrn.com/abstract=[number].*

The footnote does three jobs: (i) anchors the priority date, (ii) signals that a peer-reviewed venue is in the pipeline, (iii) directs readers to the canonical URL.

**(b) Write the cover blurb (10 pts).** A 150–250-word paragraph for the SSRN abstract page. The cover blurb tells the abstract-page visitor: what the paper is, why it matters now, and what they will learn by reading it. Different from the journal cover letter (which is for the editor) and different from the abstract (which is the formal one-paragraph summary).

Three things the cover blurb must do:

- *Open with the contribution in plain language.* Not the abstract's academic phrasing; the cover blurb's first sentence is for a smart reader who is not yet sure whether to download. E.g., "Federal mortgage regulators began intensifying fair-lending examinations in 2017. This paper asks whether that change moved the Black–White denial-rate gap, and finds that it did — by a measurable 1.4 percentage points in the counties where examinations intensified."
- *Name the data and design in one sentence.* "Using HMDA loan-level data for 47 million originations across 614 OCC-supervised counties from 2014–2020, we exploit the staggered rollout of intensified examinations in a difference-in-differences design."
- *Close with the call to action.* "The paper is the first to provide a credibly identified causal estimate of supervisory intensity on disparate-impact outcomes. Read on for the heterogeneity, the placebo tests, and the implications for the 2017 reform's policy debate."

The cover blurb is the friendliest of the three texts (abstract, journal cover letter, SSRN blurb) and the only one that earns the click-to-download.

## Part 5 — The versioning plan (20 points)

The single sin this part prevents: **silently replacing v1.0 with v3.7 over time with no change-log, so that a citing scholar three years from now cannot tell which version of your paper they are citing.**

The versioning discipline of working papers is a real, contested topic in academic finance. The canonical approach — used by the NBER, by most senior empirical-finance researchers, and recommended by Prof. Gao — is the following: *every posted version of a paper has a number, a date, and a public change-log; the SSRN abstract page lists the version history; the recommended citation specifies the version.*

**(a) Specify the version-numbering scheme (5 pts).** Choose one of:

- *Semantic versioning (MAJOR.MINOR.PATCH).* v1.0.0 = first SSRN posting. MINOR bump (v1.1.0) for substantive revisions that change a result or add a section; PATCH bump (v1.0.1) for typos and bibliographic fixes that do not change a result. MAJOR bump (v2.0.0) for a fundamental rewrite (a new identification strategy, a new sample, a new headline finding).
- *Date-based versioning.* "Version of October 7, 2026." The version *is* the date. Simpler; some papers prefer this.

For the camp, use semantic versioning. The discipline of distinguishing MINOR from PATCH from MAJOR forces you to *think* about whether a revision is a polish or a re-publication.

State your scheme in `version-plan.md` with two sentences and the v1.0.0 designation for your first SSRN posting.

**(b) Write the change-log template (5 pts).** A Markdown file at the top of your repository, `CHANGELOG.md`, with the structure used in most software projects:

```
# Changelog

## v1.0.0 — 2026-10-07 (first SSRN posting)
- Initial public version.
- Submitted to Schar Young Scholars Journal on 2026-09-18 (manuscript number SYSJ-2026-0089).
- Presented at NextGen Symposium on 2026-08-14.

## v1.1.0 — [planned, ~2026-12-01]
- Incorporate comments from FMA Undergraduate Poster discussant (October 2026).
- Add robustness check requested by NextGen Symposium audience: cluster at the lender level.
- Re-run main tables with extended sample through 2024Q4 (HMDA-LAR 2024 release).

## v2.0.0 — [planned, ~2027-06-01]
- Submit revised version to second-round journal in light of Schar Young Scholars Journal R&R or decision.
- Major restructuring of literature review (Strand 3).
```

Maintain the change-log going forward; every version bump gets a dated entry.

**(c) Write the recommended-citation sentence (5 pts).** A one-sentence recommended citation that appears in your SSRN abstract page and in your manuscript's title-page footnote. Format:

> *Cite as: [LastName1], [Initial1], [LastName2], [Initial2], and [LastName3], [Initial3]. ([Year]). "[Title]." Version [X.Y.Z], [Date]. Available at SSRN: https://ssrn.com/abstract=[number]. https://dx.doi.org/[doi-if-any].*

Including the version number in the recommended citation is the discipline that makes citing scholars version-aware.

**(d) Identify the v2.0 revision trigger (5 pts).** A single sentence naming the event that will trigger your v2.0 release. For a paper that is also under journal review, the natural v2.0 trigger is the *first R&R decision*: when the editor returns the first round of referee reports, you respond, and the response document plus revised manuscript is v2.0. For a paper that is preprint-only, the trigger might be *first conference round with substantive comments* (e.g., the FMA poster session). Naming the trigger in advance is a forcing function for honest revision.

## Part 6 — The posting checklist and cross-reference audit (20 points)

The single sin this part prevents: **discrepancies between the SSRN metadata, the journal submission, and the MARS deposit — three records of the same paper that, ten years from now, look like three different papers.**

**(a) Write the click-by-click SSRN posting checklist (10 pts).** A step-by-step ordered list naming every action you would take in the SSRN submission interface. Required sections:

- *Sign in / create account.* (ORCID required; see Ch 12.2 Step 1.)
- *Start new submission.* "My Papers" → "Submit a Paper."
- *Enter manuscript metadata.* Title, authors (with ORCIDs), abstract (PS 12.2 Part 4), JEL codes (Part 1), keywords (Part 2), networks (Part 3), recommended citation (Part 5).
- *Upload the PDF.* The same PDF from the journal submission, with the version footnote on the title page.
- *Set posting options.* Public posting (not embargo). Allow downloads (yes). Allow citations (yes). Open-access (yes).
- *Set recommended-version and version history.* v1.0.0 designation.
- *Review and submit.* Final review of metadata; submit.
- *Confirm posting.* Within 24 hours, SSRN approves the submission; the abstract page is live. Document the abstract URL.

**(b) Cross-reference the SSRN metadata against the Schar Young Scholars Journal submission (PS 12.1) and the MARS deposit (PS 12.3) (10 pts).** A table in `cross-reference.md` with columns: *field, SSRN value, Schar value, MARS value, match?* Cover at minimum: title, author list (order), abstract, keywords, JEL codes (Schar/MARS may use different taxonomies — note the mapping), funding source, embargo status, recommended citation.

Where the three records disagree, document the *intentional* disagreements (e.g., MARS does not use JEL codes; it uses Library-of-Congress Subject Headings — that is not a discrepancy, that is a different vocabulary). Where the disagreements are *accidental* (a typo in one record, a stale author affiliation in another), fix them before posting. The cross-reference is the single highest-yield integrity check for the public-record version of the paper.

## How this is graded

Part 1 (JEL codes, 15) on whether each code is defensible against the official JEL definition. Part 2 (keywords, 15) on the Goldilocks search-volume discipline and the mix of broad/medium/narrow. Part 3 (networks, 15) on selecting three to five matched networks *and* naming the rejected ones with reasons. Part 4 (cover blurb + abstract polish, 15) on whether the cover blurb is different from both the journal cover letter and the abstract, and whether the version footnote anchors the priority date. Part 5 (versioning plan, 20) on the scheme (semantic versioning is preferred), the change-log template, the recommended-citation sentence including the version number, and the named v2.0 trigger. Part 6 (posting checklist + cross-reference, 20) on the completeness of the checklist *and* the audit that flags real discrepancies across the three public records.

The single highest-signal check: **does the cross-reference table flag any *intentional* disagreement between SSRN and MARS metadata (and explain it) while having *zero* accidental disagreements?** The intentional disagreements demonstrate that you understand the three records have different vocabularies; the absence of accidental ones demonstrates that you ran the audit.
