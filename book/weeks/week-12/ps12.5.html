PS 12.5 — Five-Year Research-Identity Memo: the Through-Line from This Paper to Three Follow-Ups

Course: 8-Week Empirical Finance Camp · Week 12 · Problem Set 12.5 Covers: the move from Chapter 12.3 — the decade-long arc of a paper, with phases of idea, circulation, conference, journal submission, R&R cycle, acceptance, and citation — applied as a forward-looking exercise that asks the student to articulate, in writing, what kind of researcher she expects to be by 2031. PS 12.5 is the capstone deliverable of the camp: not another piece of code, not another table, but a memo that names the through-line connecting your camp paper to three follow-up projects that, taken together, define a coherent research line. The memo is the document that future you will hand to a graduate-school admissions committee in 2028, to a fellowship committee in 2029, to a postdoctoral search committee in 2031. The discipline is to name the through-line now, while it is fresh, while the camp paper is the most recent thing you have produced, and while the next three projects are still possibilities rather than commitments. PS 12.5 builds on PS 12.1–12.4 (the public-record artifacts), on Chapter 12.3’s case study of the Rainbow of Credits paper’s evolution, and on Mentor 12’s discussion of building an identity rather than building a CV. Type: ~70% writing (the memo is a 1,800–2,200-word document with five sections), ~15% research (verifying that the three follow-up project ideas are actually open questions in the literature, not already solved), ~10% planning (the dependency graph of how the three follow-ups relate to the camp paper), ~5% reflection (a closing paragraph naming what you have learned about yourself as a researcher over the eight weeks of camp).

Total: 100 points. Point values stated per part. A model deliverable — Leah’s five-year research-identity memo, naming her camp paper on patent text and innovation as the entry into a research line on textual measurement of unobserved economic constructs, with three named follow-up projects spanning patent abandonment text, regulatory filing tone, and earnings-call language — is in Appendix E (E-w12-ps12.5-solutions.md).

A boundary worth holding before you start. PS 12.5 is not an essay about your goals. The most common failure mode is to write a personal-statement-style document in which the student names her interests in finance, her admiration for Prof. Gao, and her excitement about graduate school. That is not the deliverable. The deliverable is a research-identity memo in the sense that senior researchers write research identity statements when they are up for tenure, for major grants, or for journal-editor appointments: a statement that says here is the central question I have been answering for ten years; here are the four or five papers that answer it from different angles; here is what comes next. You are too early in the arc to have ten years of papers, but the discipline is the same — name the central question, name the angle the camp paper takes, and name the three angles the follow-up papers will take. The result is a document that, in five years, you can hand to a search committee with the same structure and substantially updated content.

What you submit

A single document, research-identity-memo.md, committed to the root of your capstone repository, containing the five sections specified below (~2,000 words total). Plus three supporting documents:

  1. research-identity-memo.md — the memo itself.
  2. follow-up-1.md — a one-page sketch of follow-up project 1 (the closest sibling of the camp paper).
  3. follow-up-2.md — a one-page sketch of follow-up project 2 (the methodological cousin).
  4. follow-up-3.md — a one-page sketch of follow-up project 3 (the long-range stretch).
  5. through-line-graph.md — a Markdown DAG showing how the camp paper and the three follow-ups connect through the central research question.

Part 1 — The central question (20 points)

The single sin this part prevents: naming a topic (e.g., “fair lending”) instead of a question (e.g., “how do measurement frictions in protected-class definitions affect the estimation of disparate-impact effects?”). Topics are infinite; questions are the unit of research-identity.

(a) Name the central research question in one sentence (10 pts). A single sentence, ~25–40 words long, that names the question your camp paper takes one angle on and your three follow-ups take three more angles on. The question is broader than the camp paper’s question but narrower than the field. It should fit on one printed line.

Examples from each cast character’s plausible identity:

  • Maya — fair lending. “How does the institutional design of fair-lending supervision — examination intensity, definition of protected classes, and the assignment of enforcement responsibilities across agencies — affect the realized disparate-impact gap in U.S. consumer credit markets?” This is the central question; the camp paper takes the examination-intensity angle. Follow-ups would take the protected-class-definition angle, the enforcement-allocation angle, and the consumer-credit-product variation angle.
  • Devon — crypto / FinTech. “How do market-microstructure features of decentralized financial systems — algorithmic stability mechanisms, oracle architectures, cross-chain bridges — affect the propagation of liquidity shocks across crypto markets?” The camp paper takes the algorithmic-stability angle (the May 2022 TerraUSD de-peg).
  • Priya — climate / insurance. “How do informational frictions between climate-risk producers (modelers, satellite providers, federal agencies) and climate-risk consumers (insurers, homeowners, lenders) affect the pricing and take-up of climate insurance?” The camp paper takes the FEMA-flood-map angle.
  • Sam — momentum / markets. “How do intraday patterns in liquidity provision and order-flow imbalance generate predictable short-horizon return patterns in equity markets, and how does the supply of high-frequency trading capital respond?” The camp paper takes the intraday-momentum angle.
  • Leah — patents / innovation / text. “How can textual data — patents, filings, earnings calls, regulatory disclosures — be used to measure economic constructs (innovation, uncertainty, strategic disclosure) that are otherwise unobservable to outside observers?” The camp paper takes the patent-text-and-innovation angle.

Write the central question in research-identity-memo.md under the heading “Central Question.” Be honest. The first draft is often too broad; rewrite until the question fits on one line and names a real research line.

(b) Justify the question against the literature (5 pts). A ~150-word paragraph naming three or four papers in the literature that share the central question (each from a different angle), with the citations correctly formatted. The point is to show that the question exists — other researchers are also answering it. If you cannot name three papers that share the question, the question is either too narrow (genuinely unstudied) or too vague (not crisp enough to be claimed). The exercise is to find the right granularity.

For Maya, the literature might include Gao and Sun (2019) on Black–White mortgage discrimination (the question-from-protected-class-definition angle), Bartlett et al. (2022) on FinTech-versus-bank denial gaps (the question-from-technology angle), Bhutta and Hizmo (2021) on supervisor effects on credit access. For Leah, the literature includes Kogan, Papanikolaou, Seru, and Stoffman (2017) on patent text and innovation, Hoberg and Phillips (2016) on 10-K text and product-market relationships, Loughran and McDonald (2011) on tone in financial text.

(c) Name the question’s standing in the field (5 pts). A ~75-word paragraph identifying whether the question is crowded (many researchers working on it, contributions need to be incremental and clever), emerging (small number of researchers working on it, contributions can be broader), or underused (the question has been articulated but the empirical infrastructure to answer it is just maturing). Different positioning has different career implications: crowded fields are unforgiving but produce many citations to good papers; emerging fields are easier to enter but riskier for citation accumulation; underused fields are the highest-payoff bets but require building empirical infrastructure that may not pay off for years.

Part 2 — The camp paper’s angle on the question (15 points)

The single sin this part prevents: describing the camp paper without locating it within the central question. The paper has to belong to the research identity, not stand alone.

(a) Name the camp paper’s specific contribution to the central question (8 pts). A ~200-word paragraph that does two things: (i) restates the camp paper’s headline finding in one sentence, and (ii) locates that finding within the central question in three to five sentences. The location-paragraph is the work; it forces you to name what your paper adds to the broader question that the literature has not yet added.

For Maya: “The camp paper’s headline finding is that the 2017 intensification of OCC fair-lending examinations reduced the Black–White denial-rate gap by 1.4 percentage points in covered counties — a 23% reduction. Located within the central question (the effect of supervisory design on the realized disparate-impact gap), the paper contributes a clean causal estimate of examination intensity, one of three institutional levers identified in the central question (the others being protected-class definition and enforcement-agency allocation). The paper is silent on the other two levers; that silence is the entry point for the three follow-ups. Bartlett et al. (2022) studies a different lever (FinTech-versus-bank technology); Gao and Sun (2019) studies the steady-state gap rather than a specific institutional change. The camp paper fills a gap in the literature: a causally identified estimate of supervisory intensity per se.”

(b) Name what the camp paper does not answer (4 pts). A ~100-word paragraph naming three or four open questions left after the camp paper closes. Each should be a real question that further research could answer. For Maya: (i) does the effect persist beyond the three-year post-treatment window the camp paper observes? (ii) does it operate through bank-level deterrence or through borrower-level learning? (iii) does it interact with the CFPB’s parallel non-bank examination program? (iv) does it close other dimensions of the disparate-impact gap (Hispanic, Asian, etc.)? The named open questions are the next-paper space.

(c) Identify the camp paper’s research-identity role (3 pts). A one-sentence statement of what role the camp paper plays in the broader identity: is it the foundational paper (the first paper, the one that defines the question), the replication-and-extension paper (a paper that confirms a prior finding in a new setting), or the methodological-innovation paper (a paper whose contribution is a new tool)? For most camp papers, the answer is “foundational” — the first paper in a still-to-be-built research line. The discipline is to name the role honestly.

Part 3 — Three follow-up projects (40 points)

The single sin this part prevents: naming follow-ups that are also the camp paper — incremental extensions that do not add a new angle on the central question.

For each of three follow-up projects, write a one-page sketch in a separate file (follow-up-1.md, follow-up-2.md, follow-up-3.md) plus a one-paragraph summary in the main memo. The three follow-ups should differ from each other in angle on the central question, not just in data or setting.

(a) Follow-up 1 — the closest sibling (12 pts). A natural next paper that takes a related but distinct angle on the central question. The closest sibling shares the camp paper’s empirical infrastructure (same data sources, same general method) but answers a different question within the central research line. Distance from the camp paper: low. Time horizon: 12–24 months from the camp paper. Suitable as an early-undergraduate or honors-thesis paper.

For Maya, follow-up 1 could be: “Protected-Class Definition and the Realized Disparate-Impact Gap: Evidence from the CFPB’s 2020 Definition Expansion.” Same HMDA data, same difference-in-differences method, but a different institutional lever (definition rather than intensity). For Leah, follow-up 1 could be: “Beyond Patent Text: Measuring Innovation Effort Through Patent-Abandonment Filings.” Same NLP infrastructure, same patent corpus, but a different text source (abandonment filings rather than granted patents).

Each follow-up sketch should include: (i) the working title, (ii) the research question in one sentence, (iii) the proposed data source(s), (iv) the proposed identification strategy, (v) the expected timeline, (vi) the closest prior paper in the literature, (vii) the named open question the follow-up answers from Part 2(b).

(b) Follow-up 2 — the methodological cousin (14 pts). A follow-up that takes a new methodological angle on the central question. The methodological cousin keeps the substantive question in the same neighborhood but switches the empirical method — e.g., from difference-in-differences to regression discontinuity, from quasi-experimental causal identification to a field experiment, from cross-sectional regression to a structural model. Distance from the camp paper: medium. Time horizon: 24–48 months. Suitable as a senior-thesis or first-year-PhD paper.

For Maya, follow-up 2 could be: “A Structural Model of Bank Examination Choice and Lending Outcomes.” Same fair-lending question, but a structural model of bank behavior rather than a reduced-form treatment estimate — answers a deeper question about the mechanism through which examinations affect lending. For Leah, follow-up 2 could be: “A Topic-Model Approach to Measuring Innovation Heterogeneity Across Technologies and Time.” Same patent-text data but a topic-modeling method rather than the camp paper’s keyword-extraction approach.

The methodological cousin is the paper that demonstrates you are a method-builder, not just a method-consumer.

(c) Follow-up 3 — the long-range stretch (14 pts). A follow-up that takes a radically new angle on the central question — a different country, a different period, a different sub-field — that requires significant new empirical infrastructure. Distance from the camp paper: high. Time horizon: 36–72 months. Suitable as a second-year-PhD or junior-faculty paper. The long-range stretch is the paper that shows you are not a one-paper researcher.

For Maya, follow-up 3 could be: “International Evidence on Supervisory Intensity and Discrimination: A Cross-Country Comparison of Mortgage Lending in the UK, Canada, and Australia.” Same central question, but a different institutional setting, requiring new data and a new identification strategy. For Leah, follow-up 3 could be: “Textual Measurement of Strategic Disclosure in Earnings Calls: Evidence from Forced-Response Sequences.” Same central question (textual measurement of unobservable constructs), but a new text corpus (earnings calls), a new construct (strategic disclosure), and a new methodological tool (sequence modeling).

The long-range stretch is not required to be feasible right now; it is required to be envisionable. A researcher’s identity is partly about what she imagines herself doing in five years. The follow-up sketch documents the imagination.

Part 5 — Self-reflection on the eight weeks (15 points)

The single sin this part prevents: closing the memo on the projects without closing it on the person who has changed over the eight weeks of camp.

(a) Write a ~250-word reflection (10 pts). Three connected paragraphs. First paragraph: what you knew when the camp started (write honestly — not what you wish you had known, but what you actually knew, including what was wrong). Second paragraph: what shifted over the eight weeks — the moment you understood that empirical finance is not a series of regressions but a discipline of answering specific questions with specific designs, or whichever moment the camp produced for you. Third paragraph: what kind of researcher you expect to be in five years — not job title, not journal placement, but how you expect to behave when faced with a new dataset or a new question.

Examples of moves: a student who arrived believing that “more data = better paper” and learned over the camp that more identification is the right currency. The example from the model deliverable: “I came into this camp wanting to be the kind of student who finishes a project; I leave it wanting to be the kind of researcher who finishes a question.” The closing line is the line that, in 2031 when you read the memo again, will tell you whether you became the person the 2026 memo imagined.

Submission checklist

How this is graded

Part 1 (central question, 20) on the question’s specificity (a question, not a topic), on three-paper literature justification, and on honest positioning (crowded / emerging / underused). Part 2 (camp paper’s angle, 15) on locating the paper within the question and on naming what the paper does not answer. Part 3 (three follow-ups, 40) on the distance between the three (closest sibling, methodological cousin, long-range stretch — three genuinely different angles) and on each sketch’s completeness. Part 4 (through-line graph, 10) on the visual demonstration of shared investments. Part 5 (reflection, 15) on the honesty of the eight-week reflection and on the closing line.

The single highest-signal check: if the three follow-up sketches’ titles were stripped of their author name and shown to a senior empirical-finance researcher, would she be able to identify that they belong to a single research line? If yes, the through-line is real. If no, the follow-ups are just three projects.

The bridge from PS 12.5 to your career

There is no PS 12.6. PS 12.5 is the last problem set of the camp. The deliverable is the document that, in October 2027, you will revise lightly and use as the research-statement attachment to your college applications; in May 2028, you will use as the research-interests section of your honors-thesis proposal; in November 2030, as the personal statement for graduate school; in May 2032, as the research statement for your first faculty job. The document evolves — the central question may sharpen, the three follow-ups may rotate, the reflection may deepen — but the structure is the same. You are writing the first version of a document that you will own for the rest of your research career.

End of PS 12.5. End of Week 12. End of the camp.

Begin with the central question. The rest of the memo follows from it. Be honest about what you are building toward; the future-you will be grateful for the truth. By the end of the week the memo will sit in your capstone repository alongside the journal submission packet, the SSRN preprint posting, the MARS deposit, and the conference plan — the five artifacts that close the camp, and the first five chapters of a research identity that, in 2031, will have many more chapters.

## Part 2 — The camp paper's angle on the question (15 points)

**(a) Name the camp paper's specific contribution to the central question (8 pts).** A ~200-word paragraph that does two things: (i) restates the camp paper's headline finding in one sentence, and (ii) locates that finding within the central question in three to five sentences. The location-paragraph is the work; it forces you to name *what your paper adds to the broader question that the literature has not yet added*.

For Maya: "The camp paper's headline finding is that the 2017 intensification of OCC fair-lending examinations reduced the Black–White denial-rate gap by 1.4 percentage points in covered counties — a 23% reduction. Located within the central question (the effect of supervisory design on the realized disparate-impact gap), the paper contributes a clean causal estimate of *examination intensity*, one of three institutional levers identified in the central question (the others being protected-class definition and enforcement-agency allocation). The paper is silent on the other two levers; that silence is the entry point for the three follow-ups. Bartlett et al. (2022) studies a different lever (FinTech-versus-bank technology); Gao and Sun (2019) studies the steady-state gap rather than a specific institutional change. The camp paper fills a gap in the literature: a causally identified estimate of supervisory intensity per se."

**(b) Name what the camp paper *does not* answer (4 pts).** A ~100-word paragraph naming three or four open questions left after the camp paper closes. Each should be a real question that further research could answer. For Maya: (i) does the effect persist beyond the three-year post-treatment window the camp paper observes? (ii) does it operate through bank-level deterrence or through borrower-level learning? (iii) does it interact with the CFPB's parallel non-bank examination program? (iv) does it close other dimensions of the disparate-impact gap (Hispanic, Asian, etc.)? The named open questions are the next-paper space.

**(c) Identify the camp paper's research-identity role (3 pts).** A one-sentence statement of *what role the camp paper plays in the broader identity*: is it the *foundational* paper (the first paper, the one that defines the question), the *replication-and-extension* paper (a paper that confirms a prior finding in a new setting), or the *methodological-innovation* paper (a paper whose contribution is a new tool)? For most camp papers, the answer is "foundational" — the first paper in a still-to-be-built research line. The discipline is to name the role honestly.

## Part 3 — Three follow-up projects (40 points)

The single sin this part prevents: **naming follow-ups that are *also* the camp paper — incremental extensions that do not add a new angle on the central question.**

For each of three follow-up projects, write a one-page sketch in a separate file (`follow-up-1.md`, `follow-up-2.md`, `follow-up-3.md`) plus a one-paragraph summary in the main memo. The three follow-ups should differ from each other in *angle on the central question*, not just in data or setting.

**(a) Follow-up 1 — the closest sibling (12 pts).** A natural next paper that takes a *related but distinct* angle on the central question. The closest sibling shares the camp paper's empirical infrastructure (same data sources, same general method) but answers a different question within the central research line. Distance from the camp paper: low. Time horizon: 12–24 months from the camp paper. Suitable as an early-undergraduate or honors-thesis paper.

For Maya, follow-up 1 could be: *"Protected-Class Definition and the Realized Disparate-Impact Gap: Evidence from the CFPB's 2020 Definition Expansion."* Same HMDA data, same difference-in-differences method, but a different institutional lever (definition rather than intensity). For Leah, follow-up 1 could be: *"Beyond Patent Text: Measuring Innovation Effort Through Patent-Abandonment Filings."* Same NLP infrastructure, same patent corpus, but a different text source (abandonment filings rather than granted patents).

Each follow-up sketch should include: (i) the working title, (ii) the research question in one sentence, (iii) the proposed data source(s), (iv) the proposed identification strategy, (v) the expected timeline, (vi) the closest prior paper in the literature, (vii) the named open question the follow-up answers from Part 2(b).

**(b) Follow-up 2 — the methodological cousin (14 pts).** A follow-up that takes a *new methodological angle* on the central question. The methodological cousin keeps the substantive question in the same neighborhood but switches the empirical method — e.g., from difference-in-differences to regression discontinuity, from quasi-experimental causal identification to a field experiment, from cross-sectional regression to a structural model. Distance from the camp paper: medium. Time horizon: 24–48 months. Suitable as a senior-thesis or first-year-PhD paper.

For Maya, follow-up 2 could be: *"A Structural Model of Bank Examination Choice and Lending Outcomes."* Same fair-lending question, but a structural model of bank behavior rather than a reduced-form treatment estimate — answers a deeper question about the *mechanism* through which examinations affect lending. For Leah, follow-up 2 could be: *"A Topic-Model Approach to Measuring Innovation Heterogeneity Across Technologies and Time."* Same patent-text data but a topic-modeling method rather than the camp paper's keyword-extraction approach.

The methodological cousin is the paper that demonstrates you are a *method-builder*, not just a method-consumer.

**(c) Follow-up 3 — the long-range stretch (14 pts).** A follow-up that takes a *radically new angle* on the central question — a different country, a different period, a different sub-field — that requires significant new empirical infrastructure. Distance from the camp paper: high. Time horizon: 36–72 months. Suitable as a second-year-PhD or junior-faculty paper. The long-range stretch is the paper that shows you are not a one-paper researcher.

For Maya, follow-up 3 could be: *"International Evidence on Supervisory Intensity and Discrimination: A Cross-Country Comparison of Mortgage Lending in the UK, Canada, and Australia."* Same central question, but a different institutional setting, requiring new data and a new identification strategy. For Leah, follow-up 3 could be: *"Textual Measurement of Strategic Disclosure in Earnings Calls: Evidence from Forced-Response Sequences."* Same central question (textual measurement of unobservable constructs), but a new text corpus (earnings calls), a new construct (strategic disclosure), and a new methodological tool (sequence modeling).

The long-range stretch is *not* required to be feasible right now; it is required to be *envisionable*. A researcher's identity is partly about what she imagines herself doing in five years. The follow-up sketch documents the imagination.

## Part 4 — The through-line graph (10 points)

The single sin this part prevents: **naming three follow-ups that do not actually share a research line, just three separate ideas.**

**(a) Draw the through-line graph (8 pts).** A Markdown DAG in `through-line-graph.md` showing the central question at the top, the camp paper and the three follow-ups as nodes, and arrows showing which methodological tools, data sources, and intellectual investments are shared across nodes. ## Part 5 — Self-reflection on the eight weeks (15 points)

The single sin this part prevents: **closing the memo on the projects without closing it on the *person* who has changed over the eight weeks of camp.**

**(a) Write a ~250-word reflection (10 pts).** Three connected paragraphs. First paragraph: what you knew when the camp started (write honestly — not what you wish you had known, but what you actually knew, including what was wrong). Second paragraph: what shifted over the eight weeks — the moment you understood that empirical finance is not a series of regressions but a discipline of *answering specific questions with specific designs*, or whichever moment the camp produced for you. Third paragraph: what kind of researcher you expect to be in five years — not job title, not journal placement, but *how you expect to behave* when faced with a new dataset or a new question.

Examples of moves: a student who arrived believing that "more data = better paper" and learned over the camp that *more identification* is the right currency. A student who arrived believing she wanted to study crypto and discovered, through her Week 6 lab, that the questions that excite her are about *institutional design* rather than *technology* — and that the crypto angle is one application of an institutional-design identity. A student who arrived believing that mentorship is about being told what to do and learned (through Mentor 12) that mentorship is about being *seen* by a senior researcher who will name your strengths.

The reflection is honest writing, not performance. The grade is on the honesty.

**(b) Write the closing line (5 pts).** A single sentence — the closing line of the memo — that the future-you will recognize as your own. It can be any tone: declarative, aspirational, dry, ironic. The example from the model deliverable: "I came into this camp wanting to be the kind of student who finishes a project; I leave it wanting to be the kind of researcher who finishes a question." The closing line is the line that, in 2031 when you read the memo again, will tell you whether you became the person the 2026 memo imagined.

## How this is graded

Part 1 (central question, 20) on the question's specificity (a question, not a topic), on three-paper literature justification, and on honest positioning (crowded / emerging / underused). Part 2 (camp paper's angle, 15) on locating the paper within the question and on naming what the paper does *not* answer. Part 3 (three follow-ups, 40) on the *distance* between the three (closest sibling, methodological cousin, long-range stretch — three genuinely different angles) and on each sketch's completeness. Part 4 (through-line graph, 10) on the visual demonstration of shared investments. Part 5 (reflection, 15) on the honesty of the eight-week reflection and on the closing line.

The single highest-signal check: **if the three follow-up sketches' titles were stripped of their author name and shown to a senior empirical-finance researcher, would she be able to identify that they belong to a single research line?** If yes, the through-line is real. If no, the follow-ups are just three projects.

## The bridge from PS 12.5 to your career

There is no PS 12.6. PS 12.5 is the last problem set of the camp. The deliverable is the document that, in October 2027, you will revise lightly and use as the research-statement attachment to your college applications; in May 2028, you will use as the research-interests section of your honors-thesis proposal; in November 2030, as the personal statement for graduate school; in May 2032, as the research statement for your first faculty job. The document evolves — the central question may sharpen, the three follow-ups may rotate, the reflection may deepen — but the structure is the same. You are writing the first version of a document that you will own for the rest of your research career.

*End of PS 12.5. End of Week 12. End of the camp.*

*Begin with the central question. The rest of the memo follows from it. Be honest about what you are building toward; the future-you will be grateful for the truth. By the end of the week the memo will sit in your capstone repository alongside the journal submission packet, the SSRN preprint posting, the MARS deposit, and the conference plan — the five artifacts that close the camp, and the first five chapters of a research identity that, in 2031, will have many more chapters.*
