### 1. The opening boundary-defense paragraph (~155 words)

> *We engage three literatures. The first documents the racial gap in mortgage approval as a phenomenon; the second examines the effects of regulatory supervisory intensity on bank behavior more broadly; the third evaluates the causal effects of anti-discrimination interventions. ### 2. Strand 1 — racial gaps in mortgage approval (~700 words)

> *The first strand of work this paper engages is the literature on racial gaps in mortgage approval, which documents persistent disparities in denial rates that are not fully explained by observable creditworthiness. The strand has, since Munnell, Tootell, Browne, and McEneaney (1996), established a series of robust findings: gaps persist across data sources (HMDA, proprietary lender data), gaps persist across time, gaps persist after controlling for an increasingly rich set of observable creditworthiness variables, and gaps vary across geography and lender type. We summarize the strand by engaging with four landmark papers, in chronological order, then state our contribution.*
>
> *Munnell, Tootell, Browne, and McEneaney (1996) — the foundational paper of this literature — combined the publicly available HMDA application data with a richer survey of underwriting variables from Boston lenders, finding that the Black-White denial-rate gap shrank from roughly 60% to 30% after the full controls were added, but that a substantial residual gap remained. The Munnell et al. study has been replicated, criticized, and re-examined many times, and the surviving consensus is that some portion of the gap reflects measurement error in creditworthiness, some portion reflects lender discretion, and the remainder reflects mechanisms — including structural credit-score features and within-lender heterogeneity — that subsequent work has continued to disentangle. Our paper takes the post-control residual gap as the *phenomenon* and asks whether a specific supervisory tool can affect it.*
>
> *Bayer, Ferreira, and Ross (2016) examined the high-cost-mortgage gap during the subprime period and decomposed it into between-lender and within-lender components, finding that within-lender variation explained the larger share. Their decomposition is the key methodological contribution: by showing that the racial gap is not primarily a sorting-into-lenders story but a within-lender story, they redirected the literature toward lender-level interventions as the natural lever. We follow them in clustering at the lender level for inference and in emphasizing that supervisory tools that target lender behavior — as ours does — operate on the dimension where the action is.*
>
> *Bartlett, Morse, Stanton, and Wallace (2022) brought the literature into the algorithmic-underwriting era, finding that FinTech lenders display narrower but still substantial gaps relative to bank lenders. Their paper extends the strand in two ways: it documents that algorithmic underwriting does not eliminate the gap, and it documents that the gap differs by lender type. Our paper restricts attention to the bank lenders that are subject to OCC supervision; the FinTech segment is outside our identification (and outside our 2017 reform's scope), and we note in §7's external-validity memo that our estimates do not transport to the FinTech segment without further assumptions.*
>
> *Gao and Sun (2019) demonstrated that race-matching between loan officer and borrower reduces denial-rate gaps by roughly half of the unadjusted disparity, suggesting that lender-level interventions can move the gap. Their identification — exploiting variation in the racial composition of loan-officer assignments — is distinct from ours, but the magnitude estimate (roughly 3 pp on the conditional gap) is in the same order of magnitude as our headline (1.4 pp). The two papers together suggest two complementary channels through which lender behavior is shaped: from below (the staffing of the loan-officer pool, Gao and Sun's lever) and from above (the intensity of supervisory examination, our lever).*
>
> *Our paper adds to this strand by isolating the *causal* effect of a specific *regulatory* tool — the 2017 OCC examination intensification — on the documented disparate-impact gap, complementing the within-lender decomposition of Bayer et al. (2016), the FinTech-vs-bank-lender contrast of Bartlett et al. (2022), and the lender-staffing-level evidence of Gao and Sun (2019) with the first credibly-causal estimate of a supervisory-program-level intervention on the disparate-impact outcome the literature has long documented.*

Word count: 607.

### 3. Strand 2 — regulatory supervisory intensity (~720 words)

> *The second strand on regulatory supervisory intensity has produced a body of evidence on how examination effort, examination scope, and examination consequences affect supervised institutions' behavior — but rarely on disparate-impact outcomes. The strand asks how the intensity with which a regulator scrutinizes a bank affects what the bank does, with most prior work focusing on prudential outcomes (capital ratios, lending growth, risk-taking) rather than on the disparate-impact outcomes our paper targets. We engage with three papers in chronological order, then state our contribution.*
>
> *Agarwal, Lucca, Seru, and Trebbi (2014) — the foundational paper for the modern supervisory-intensity literature — exploited within-bank-across-supervisor variation in the strictness with which OCC and FDIC supervisors classified bank loans. They found that loan classifications differed substantially across supervisors examining the same bank, and that the differences mapped onto downstream lending behavior. Their identification design — comparing the same bank under different supervisors — is the cleanest within-bank causal estimate of supervisory intensity in the literature; our identification differs (we exploit across-county variation in the rollout of one program) but the spirit is the same: isolating the effect of supervisory variation from the behavior of the supervised institution.*
>
> *Granja and Leuz (2024) extended the strand by exploiting the closure of the Office of Thrift Supervision (OTS) and the migration of its supervised institutions to OCC supervision. They found that the migrated banks experienced increased supervisory strictness and reduced lending growth in response. Their identification — the agency-closure event — is the cleanest large-N supervisory-intensity event in recent literature, and their finding establishes that supervisory intensity does affect aggregate lending. Our paper differs in two ways: we examine a different outcome (disparate-impact denial gaps rather than aggregate lending) and a different identification source (within-OCC staggered rollout of a specific program rather than between-OTS-and-OCC agency migration). The two papers are complementary: theirs estimates the lending-volume effect of supervisor change, ours estimates the disparate-impact effect of supervisory-program change.*
>
> *Bhattacharya, Gabrieli, and Nieves (2024) [CHECK — citation to be verified against final journal] examined the heterogeneity of supervisory intensity effects across bank size and lending-portfolio composition, finding that smaller and more-concentrated banks respond more strongly to intensity changes. Their heterogeneity findings inform our heterogeneity analysis in §5.3 of the paper: we examine whether the intensification effect on the denial gap is heterogeneous across counties with different minority-population shares and across lenders with different examination-finding histories. Our heterogeneity findings (concentrated in above-median-minority counties and above-median examination-finding lenders) are consistent with the broader Bhattacharya et al. pattern that supervisory intensity acts more strongly where there is more for the supervisor to find.*
>
> *Beyond these three papers, the strand includes a broader body of work on bank supervisory enforcement actions (Delis, Hasan, and Tsionas 2014 on supervisory ratings and risk-taking; Doerr 2021 on stress-test publication and bank behavior; Frame, Steiner, and Zimmermann 2023 on supervisory pressure and bank consolidation). We do not engage with each individually in this strand because the disparate-impact outcome our paper targets is sufficiently distinct from the prudential and risk-taking outcomes those papers examine that the engagement would not earn the citation cost.*
>
> *Our paper adds to this strand by extending the supervisory-intensity outcome variable from prudential measures (capital ratios, lending growth, risk-taking) to disparate-impact measures (denial-rate gaps), and by exploiting the staggered 2017 rollout of the OCC fair-lending examination intensification as a clean source of identifying variation that is not confounded with the supervisor-pairing identification of Agarwal et al. (2014) or with the agency-closure event used by Granja and Leuz (2024). The contribution lies in the *outcome*, not the *method*: the staggered-DiD design we use is now standard in the supervisory-intensity literature, but applying it to a disparate-impact outcome is — to our knowledge — first done in this paper.*

Word count: 623.

### 4. Strand 3 — causal evaluation of anti-discrimination interventions (~680 words)

> *The third strand on the causal evaluation of anti-discrimination interventions is smaller than the first two, in part because credible identification of such interventions is rare. The strand estimates the effect of laws, programs, and tools on disparate-impact outcomes in markets and labor pools, with most credibly-identified estimates coming from audit studies (the gold standard for paired comparisons of similar applicants of different races) rather than from regulatory-program rollouts. We engage with three papers, then state our contribution.*
>
> *Ross, Turner, Godfrey, and Smith (2008) reported on the Mortgage Lending in Chicago and Los Angeles paired-testing study, in which matched testers of different races approached the same lenders for the same product. They found systematic differences in treatment by lender staff that mapped onto eventual application outcomes — informational steering, differential disclosure of product options, differential follow-up frequency. The Ross et al. study is the cleanest causal estimate in the anti-discrimination literature for the lender-staff-behavior channel; our paper's intervention (intensified examination by supervisors) operates upstream of staff behavior, on the lender's internal compliance and risk-management process. Our results are complementary: their evidence establishes that staff-level differences exist; ours establishes that supervisory intensification reduces the gap, presumably by changing the lender's incentives to control staff-level discretion.*
>
> *Munnell, Browne, McEneaney, and Tootell (1996) — already cited in strand 1 — appears here because its policy-implications section was an early statement of the case that regulatory tools (CRA assessment, fair-lending examinations) might be effective in reducing the gap. The 1996 paper was, in essence, the theoretical motivation for the 2017 OCC reform; our paper estimates whether the prediction was right. We are direct that the answer is "partially yes": the 2017 intensification reduces the gap by 1.4 pp (23% of the pre-treatment gap), which is meaningful but does not close the gap entirely. The prediction was directionally correct; the magnitude is less than what an enthusiast of supervisory tools would have hoped for in 1996.*
>
> *Recent work on the impact of the Consumer Financial Protection Bureau (CFPB) [CHECK — Calderon and Steinmetz 2024 or related] and the impact of the 2018 HMDA disclosure-rule change on lender behavior [CHECK — citations to be added once primary literature is settled] would inform this strand but we cite them lightly because the identification in those papers is contested. We summarize: the CFPB literature offers correlational evidence that consumer-side disclosure affects lender behavior; the HMDA-rule-change literature offers evidence that increased disclosure granularity affects lender care in application-data reporting. Neither is directly comparable to our supervisory-intensification identification.*
>
> *Our paper contributes to this strand by providing a magnitude estimate — roughly one-quarter of the persistent national gap, closed by the 2017 intensification in covered counties — that informs the design choice between supervisory tools and other anti-discrimination levers (community reinvestment requirements, lender-staffing interventions, audit-study disclosure). The magnitude estimate is, to our knowledge, the first credibly-identified estimate of a supervisory-program-level lever on disparate-impact denial rates, and it suggests that supervisory intensification is a *complementary* rather than *substitute* tool — meaningful in size but not a complete solution — to the staff-behavior, lender-staffing, and audit-study levers documented in the rest of the strand.*

Word count: 521.

## Instructor grading notes — what an A looks like

**Each strand has its own opening sentence naming the strand.** Maya's strand 1 opens "The first strand of work this paper engages is the literature on racial gaps in mortgage approval, which documents persistent disparities in denial rates that are not fully explained by observable creditworthiness." This sentence names the strand in two clauses and lets the reader anchor the strand against the others. Students who launch directly into citations without naming the strand confuse the reader about which conversation is being entered.

**Each cited paper does specific work.** Maya engages with four landmark papers in strand 1; each has one or two sentences explaining what it added and a sentence connecting to her own paper. A 24-citation literature review where every citation does work is more credible than a 60-citation review where half are decoration. Maya cites approximately 12–15 papers total across the three strands, all with explicit roles.

**Each contribution sentence names the closest comparison and the specific dimension.** Maya's strand-1 contribution sentence names Bayer et al. (2016), Bartlett et al. (2022), and Gao and Sun (2019) as the closest comparisons, and the dimension of differentiation as "first credibly-causal estimate of a supervisory-program-level intervention." Generic "we contribute" sentences are graded down.

**The contribution-reveal mini-paragraph is consistent with PS 11.4 paragraph 5.** The three strand-contribution sentences from PS 11.5 are essentially the same content as PS 11.4's paragraph 5 (the introduction's contribution paragraph), at slightly more detail. The two must agree on the headline claims; if they contradict, fix the intro (paragraph 5 was written under less information).

**The unifying summary sentence is the bottom line.** Maya's: "Taken together, our findings demonstrate that supervisory intensity is a tool that can move a long-documented disparate-impact outcome, that the magnitude is one-quarter of the persistent gap in covered counties, and that the result is robust to the multiple-testing, robustness-battery, and external-validity disciplines we apply." ## Common student errors

1. **The three strands overlap.** "Racial gaps," "discrimination in financial markets," "fair-lending policy" are essentially the same strand. Fix: make the three intellectually distinct (phenomenon-documentation vs. method-development vs. policy-evaluation).
2. **The contribution sentence is generic.** "We contribute to this literature" — does not name the closest comparison or the specific dimension. Fix: name two or three papers as the closest comparisons and name the dimension of differentiation.
3. **Cited papers are listed without engagement.** "Smith (2024), Jones (2025), and Lee (2026) have also examined this." Each citation should do one sentence of work. Fix: rewrite so each citation has a verb describing what the paper did.
4. **The bibliography has unverified citations not marked `[CHECK]`.** Maya marks two citations `[CHECK]`; students who fabricate citations or guess at volumes/issues are graded down hard. The convention is unambiguous: verified or `[CHECK]`.
5. **The contribution-reveal contradicts PS 11.4 paragraph 5.** Most often because PS 11.4 was written under less information; the fix is to update PS 11.4, not the literature review. Verify the consistency.
6. **The third strand is much shorter than the first two.** Sometimes acceptable (Maya's third strand is shorter because the policy-evaluation literature is genuinely thinner), but flagged in the verification log. If the third strand is under 400 words, consider whether it is *really* a strand or whether the paper has only two strands and the policy-evaluation discussion belongs in §7 rather than the lit review.
