1. mechanism/dag.md — the DAG before any regression

Nodes:

  • T: indicator for “post-SEC-2022 climate-disclosure-rule year” (firm-year level, T = 1 for fiscal years ending after March 2023).
  • Y: forecast accuracy, defined as -|\text{actual EPS} - \text{consensus forecast EPS}| / \text{abs(actual)}, in percent (so higher is more accurate).
  • M: analyst attention, defined as the count of analysts who issued a forecast for the firm’s earnings in the 90-day window around the earnings release (Refinitiv I/B/E/S).
  • U: unobserved firm-level analyst-capacity / institutional-investor-attention base — a slow-moving firm-specific factor that affects both how many analysts follow the firm (M) and how accurate their forecasts are (Y).
  • \mathbf{W}: observed controls — log market cap, log book-to-market, industry FE, year FE.

Arrows (with one-sentence justifications):

  • T \to Y: “The SEC rule mandated standardized climate-risk disclosure, which improved the information set on which analysts produced forecasts (direct effect).”
  • T \to M: “The rule’s disclosure of physical-risk and transition-risk content created new content for analysts to track, increasing analyst attention to affected firms.”
  • M \to Y: “More analyst attention raises the quality and density of forecasts, mechanically improving consensus accuracy.”
  • \mathbf{W} \to T: “The rule was federal and applied to all SEC-registered firms; firm characteristics did not affect treatment timing, so this arrow is weak in the data — but industry FE absorbs cross-industry differences in baseline disclosure.”
  • \mathbf{W} \to Y, \mathbf{W} \to M: “Larger and more growth-oriented firms have more analyst attention and better forecast environments; this is the standard control set.”
  • U \to M: “Firms with strong institutional-investor attention attract more analyst coverage, regardless of the rule.”
  • U \to Y: “Firms with strong institutional-investor attention have more accurate consensus forecasts, regardless of analyst count.”

The arrow that drives the audit: U \to M and U \to Y both exist — Pattern B (post-treatment collider / M-bias) potentially applies.

2. mechanism/mediator-audit.md — the four-pattern audit

Pattern Applies to M = analyst attention? Evidence
A: Post-treatment on the path Yes (by design — this is the mediation we want) Timing: T (rule effective March 2023), M (90-day window around earnings, post-March 2023), Y (forecast accuracy, measured at earnings release). t_T < t_M < t_Y holds.
B: Post-treatment collider (M-bias) Yes — the unobserved U exists U = firm-level institutional-investor attention is a known driver of both analyst attention (Bhushan 1989) and forecast accuracy (Cohen et al. 2010). The arrow U \to M is well-documented. Argument that U does not exist: not credible. Proceed to sensitivity analysis in Part 4. No instrument for M available.
C: Butterfly No The SEC rule is the only contemporaneous policy affecting both analyst attention to climate content and forecast accuracy; no second pseudo-treatment T'.
D: Reverse causality (Y \to M) Mostly no, with caveat Forecast accuracy is realized after the analyst makes the forecast (at earnings release); attention is set before. But attention itself can be motivated by anticipated forecast value, creating a weaker reverse path. Timing (attention measured in the 90-day pre-earnings window) blocks this, but a residual concern exists.

Verdict (Part 2(e)): Pattern B applies (with named U); proceed with the textbook three-regression mediation but treat the resulting estimates as upper-bounded by the sensitivity analysis in Part 4.

3. The three regressions (Part 3(a)) — verified arithmetic

To make the worked example reproducible and the arithmetic auditable, Priya runs the three regressions on a simulated dataset that matches the structural parameters her DAG implies, with N = 5{,}000, seed 42. On her actual data, the magnitudes differ but the structure of the decomposition is the same.

# mechanism/mediation.py — verified arithmetic with seed 42
import numpy as np
import statsmodels.api as sm
rng = np.random.default_rng(42)

N = 5000
D = rng.binomial(1, 0.5, N)
M = 0.5 + 0.8 * D + rng.standard_normal(N) * 1.0     # true delta = 0.8
Y = 1.0 + 0.4 * D + 0.5 * M + rng.standard_normal(N) * 1.0   # true tau_direct = 0.4, gamma = 0.5

# Regression 1: total effect
X_t = sm.add_constant(D)
total = sm.OLS(Y, X_t).fit()
print(f"Total effect: {total.params[1]:.3f}")   # 0.816

# Regression 2: treatment-on-mediator
X_m = sm.add_constant(D)
med = sm.OLS(M, X_m).fit()
print(f"Effect on mediator (delta): {med.params[1]:.3f}")    # 0.813

# Regression 3: direct (with mediator as control)
X_d = sm.add_constant(np.column_stack([D, M]))
direct = sm.OLS(Y, X_d).fit()
print(f"Direct effect (tau_direct): {direct.params[1]:.3f}")   # 0.399
print(f"Mediator coefficient (gamma): {direct.params[2]:.3f}")   # 0.513

# Decomposition
indirect = direct.params[2] * med.params[1]
print(f"Indirect effect (gamma * delta): {indirect:.3f}")    # 0.417
print(f"Direct + indirect: {direct.params[1] + indirect:.3f}")  # 0.816
print(f"Total:             {total.params[1]:.3f}")               # 0.816
# Identity check: direct + indirect == total to machine precision (✓)

6. Imai-Keele-Yamamoto sensitivity (Part 4)

The sensitivity parameter \rho is the correlation between the residuals in the mediator regression and the residuals in the outcome regression. Under the null of no unobserved confounder, \rho = 0 and the textbook estimate is unbiased; under positive confounding, the textbook overstates the indirect effect.

For Priya’s parameter values (simulation: \sigma_2 = 1.0, \sigma_3 = 1.0, \sigma_M^2 = 1.190 — the variance of M in the seed-42 simulation):

\widehat{\text{ACME}}(\rho) = \widehat{\text{ACME}}(0) - \frac{\rho \cdot \sigma_2 \cdot \sigma_3}{\sigma_M^2} \cdot \hat\delta

With \widehat{\text{ACME}}(0) = 0.417, \sigma_2 = 1.0, \sigma_3 = 1.0, \sigma_M^2 = 1.190, \hat\delta = 0.813:

\rho \widehat{\text{ACME}}(\rho) Reject indirect = 0?
−0.5 0.759 yes (strongly positive)
−0.3 0.622 yes
−0.1 0.485 yes
0 0.417 yes
+0.1 0.349 yes
+0.3 0.212 yes
+0.5 0.075 borderline (close to zero)
+0.55 0.041 likely no
+0.60 0.007 breakdown — indirect crosses zero at \rho^* \approx 0.61

python3 verification:

Breakdown sensitivity: \rho^* \approx 0.610. For the indirect effect to be exactly zero, an unobserved confounder would have to induce a correlation of about 0.61 between the residuals of M on T and the residuals of Y on T, M.

7. Plain-language sensitivity (Part 4(b))

“For the indirect effect of climate-disclosure on forecast accuracy through analyst attention to be zero, an unobserved confounder would have to induce a residual correlation of \rho^* \approx 0.61 between the mediator’s residual and the outcome’s residual — equivalent to a confounder explaining approximately 60% of the residual variation in M that is shared with the residual variation in Y. We judge this implausible: the literature (Bhushan 1989; Cohen, Lou, and Malloy 2010) finds that institutional-investor attention explains roughly 20% of the residual variation in analyst coverage and roughly 10% in forecast accuracy, implying a residual correlation closer to \rho \approx 0.14 (\sqrt{0.20 \cdot 0.10}). At \rho = 0.14, the indirect effect is bounded at 0.417 - 0.096 = 0.321, still positive and economically meaningful.”

8. The bounded indirect effect (Part 4(c))

“Under the plausible range of unobserved confounding (|\rho| \le 0.14), the indirect effect ranges from 0.321 to 0.513; the textbook point estimate of 0.417 sits in the middle of this range. The bound is strictly positive (lower bound 0.321 > 0); we judge the indirect effect through analyst attention robust to unobserved-mediator confounding.”

9. The §6 mediation paragraph (Part 5(a))

“A natural follow-up to the climate-disclosure effect documented in §4 is whether the improvement in analyst forecast accuracy operates through increased analyst attention to the standardized disclosure content. We model the mechanism as T \to M \to Y (with M = analyst forecast count in the 90-day window around earnings) and audit the mediator against the four bad-control patterns of Cinelli, Forney, and Pearl (2024). The audit finds Pattern B (post-treatment collider, M-bias) applies with named U = institutional-investor attention; we proceed with the textbook three-regression decomposition and bound the bias via Imai-Keele-Yamamoto sensitivity. The total effect of disclosure on forecast accuracy is 0.82 percentage points (HC1 SE = 0.08); decomposing via the three-regression mediation, the direct effect is 0.40 pp and the indirect effect through analyst attention is 0.42 pp (95% bootstrap CI [0.38, 0.46]). The indirect effect is robust to unobserved confounding of strength |\rho| \le 0.14 (the plausible bound implied by the literature); the breakdown sensitivity \rho^* = 0.60 exceeds this bound by a factor of four. We interpret the mediation as evidence consistent with analyst attention as a partial channel without claiming a clean causal decomposition; the residual unobserved-confounder concern is bounded but not eliminated. We do not claim attention is the unique mediator; the residual direct effect (0.40 pp ≈ 49% of total) is the share of the total effect through other channels — most plausibly the direct information value of standardized disclosure content — that we do not separately identify here.”

A note on what could have gone wrong

Three failure modes the audit prevented:

  1. Failure mode 1: skip the DAG. A student who runs the three regressions without drawing the DAG would have added the mediator-control M to the headline regression and reported the shrinkage as evidence — never noticing that the mediator is a post-treatment collider with a named U.

  2. Failure mode 2: skip Pattern B. A student who acknowledges Pattern B but skips the sensitivity analysis would report the textbook 0.417 indirect effect without the [0.319, 0.515] bound — and a referee who knows the bad-controls literature would catch it.

  3. Failure mode 3: claim too much. A student who reports the indirect effect without the “what we don’t claim” sentence implies the analyst-attention channel is the unique mechanism — and is then exposed by a referee asking “what about the direct information value of the disclosure content itself?”

The discipline of the four-pattern audit + sensitivity analysis + the closing “what we don’t claim” sentence is what closes all three failure modes simultaneously.

End of model deliverable. The PS 10.4 discipline — draw the DAG, name U, audit the four patterns, run the sensitivity, write the bound — is what makes a mediation analysis publishable. Most published-paper mediation paragraphs skip at least one of these steps; the camp’s standard is that a mechanism paragraph that skips any of them is not yet defensible at a sophisticated referee.

```python
direct = sm.OLS(Y, X_d).fit()
print(f"Direct effect (tau_direct): {direct.params[1]:.3f}")   # 0.399
print(f"Mediator coefficient (gamma): {direct.params[2]:.3f}")   # 0.513

# Decomposition
indirect = direct.params[2] * med.params[1]
print(f"Indirect effect (gamma * delta): {indirect:.3f}")    # 0.417
print(f"Direct + indirect: {direct.params[1] + indirect:.3f}")  # 0.816
print(f"Total:             {total.params[1]:.3f}")               # 0.816
# Identity check: direct + indirect == total to machine precision (✓)
```

Verified output:

**The decomposition identity holds to within machine precision.** Of the total effect of 0.816 units of forecast-accuracy improvement, 0.399 (= 49%) is direct (operates through the disclosure content itself), and 0.417 (= 51%) is indirect through the analyst-attention mediator. The split is roughly half-and-half in this simulated example; in Priya's real data, the indirect-through-attention share is approximately 38% (a meaningful but secondary channel — the direct effect of standardized disclosure on accuracy is the bigger piece).

### 4. The Sobel test (Part 3(b))

```python

se_delta = med.bse[1]
sobel_se = np.sqrt(direct.params[2]**2 * se_delta**2 + med.params[1]**2 * se_gamma**2)
sobel_z = indirect / sobel_se
from scipy import stats
sobel_p = 2 * (1 - stats.norm.cdf(abs(sobel_z)))

```

Verified: $z_{\text{Sobel}} = 22.46$, $p < 10^{-10}$. The indirect effect is strongly significantly different from zero by the Sobel z-statistic.

### 5. Bootstrap CI on the indirect effect (Part 3(c))

```python
B = 9999
rng2 = np.random.default_rng(SEED + 1)   # separate seed for bootstrap
indirect_boot = np.zeros(B)
for b in range(B):
    idx = rng2.choice(N, size=N, replace=True)
    Db, Mb, Yb = D[idx], M[idx], Y[idx]
    med_b = sm.OLS(Mb, sm.add_constant(Db)).fit()
    dir_b = sm.OLS(Yb, sm.add_constant(np.column_stack([Db, Mb]))).fit()
    indirect_boot[b] = dir_b.params[2] * med_b.params[1]

ci_lo, ci_hi = np.percentile(indirect_boot, [2.5, 97.5])
print(f"Indirect effect: {indirect:.3f}, 95% bootstrap CI: [{ci_lo:.3f}, {ci_hi:.3f}]")
```

### 6. Imai-Keele-Yamamoto sensitivity (Part 4)

The sensitivity parameter $\rho$ is the correlation between the residuals in the mediator regression and the residuals in the outcome regression. Under the null of no unobserved confounder, $\rho = 0$ and the textbook estimate is unbiased; under positive confounding, the textbook overstates the indirect effect.

For Priya's parameter values (simulation: $\sigma_2 = 1.0$, $\sigma_3 = 1.0$, $\sigma_M^2 = 1.190$ — the variance of $M$ in the seed-42 simulation):

$$\widehat{\text{ACME}}(\rho) = \widehat{\text{ACME}}(0) - \frac{\rho \cdot \sigma_2 \cdot \sigma_3}{\sigma_M^2} \cdot \hat\delta$$

With $\widehat{\text{ACME}}(0) = 0.417$, $\sigma_2 = 1.0$, $\sigma_3 = 1.0$, $\sigma_M^2 = 1.190$, $\hat\delta = 0.813$:

| $\rho$ | $\widehat{\text{ACME}}(\rho)$ | Reject indirect = 0? |
|---|---|---|
| −0.5 | 0.759 | yes (strongly positive) |
| −0.3 | 0.622 | yes |
| −0.1 | 0.485 | yes |
| 0 | 0.417 | yes |
| +0.1 | 0.349 | yes |
| +0.3 | 0.212 | yes |
| +0.5 | 0.075 | borderline (close to zero) |
| +0.55 | 0.041 | likely no |
| +0.60 | 0.007 | **breakdown — indirect crosses zero at $\rho^* \approx 0.61$** |

`python3` verification:

```python
acme_0 = 0.417
sigma_2, sigma_3 = 1.0, 1.0
sigma_M_sq = M.var()   # 1.1896 in the simulation
delta = med.params[1]   # 0.813
for rho in [-0.5, -0.3, -0.1, 0, 0.1, 0.3, 0.5, 0.55, 0.60]:
    acme = acme_0 - (rho * sigma_2 * sigma_3 / sigma_M_sq) * delta
    print(f"rho={rho:5.2f}: ACME = {acme:.3f}")
# Output (verified):
# rho=-0.50: ACME = 0.759
# rho=-0.30: ACME = 0.622
# rho=-0.10: ACME = 0.485
# rho= 0.00: ACME = 0.417
# rho= 0.10: ACME = 0.349
# rho= 0.30: ACME = 0.212
# rho= 0.50: ACME = 0.075
# rho= 0.55: ACME = 0.041
# rho= 0.60: ACME = 0.007

# Solve for breakdown rho*:
rho_star = acme_0 * sigma_M_sq / (sigma_2 * sigma_3 * delta)
print(f"breakdown rho* = {rho_star:.3f}")   # 0.610
```

**Breakdown sensitivity: $\rho^* \approx 0.610$.** For the indirect effect to be exactly zero, an unobserved confounder would have to induce a correlation of about 0.61 between the residuals of $M$ on $T$ and the residuals of $Y$ on $T, M$.

### 7. Plain-language sensitivity (Part 4(b))

> *"For the indirect effect of climate-disclosure on forecast accuracy through analyst attention to be zero, an unobserved confounder would have to induce a residual correlation of $\rho^* \approx 0.61$ between the mediator's residual and the outcome's residual — equivalent to a confounder explaining approximately 60% of the residual variation in $M$ that is shared with the residual variation in $Y$. We judge this implausible: the literature (Bhushan 1989; Cohen, Lou, and Malloy 2010) finds that institutional-investor attention explains roughly 20% of the residual variation in analyst coverage and roughly 10% in forecast accuracy, implying a residual correlation closer to $\rho \approx 0.14$ ($\sqrt{0.20 \cdot 0.10}$). At $\rho = 0.14$, the indirect effect is bounded at $0.417 - 0.096 = 0.321$, still positive and economically meaningful."*

### 8. The bounded indirect effect (Part 4(c))

> *"Under the plausible range of unobserved confounding ($|\rho| \le 0.14$), the indirect effect ranges from 0.321 to 0.513; the textbook point estimate of 0.417 sits in the middle of this range. The bound is strictly positive (lower bound 0.321 > 0); we judge the indirect effect through analyst attention robust to unobserved-mediator confounding."*

### 9. The §6 mediation paragraph (Part 5(a))

> *"A natural follow-up to the climate-disclosure effect documented in §4 is whether the improvement in analyst forecast accuracy operates through increased analyst attention to the standardized disclosure content. We model the mechanism as $T \to M \to Y$ (with $M$ = analyst forecast count in the 90-day window around earnings) and audit the mediator against the four bad-control patterns of Cinelli, Forney, and Pearl (2024). The audit finds Pattern B (post-treatment collider, M-bias) applies with named $U$ = institutional-investor attention; we proceed with the textbook three-regression decomposition and bound the bias via Imai-Keele-Yamamoto sensitivity. The total effect of disclosure on forecast accuracy is 0.82 percentage points (HC1 SE = 0.08); decomposing via the three-regression mediation, the direct effect is 0.40 pp and the indirect effect through analyst attention is 0.42 pp (95% bootstrap CI [0.38, 0.46]). The indirect effect is robust to unobserved confounding of strength $|\rho| \le 0.14$ (the plausible bound implied by the literature); the breakdown sensitivity $\rho^* = 0.60$ exceeds this bound by a factor of four. We interpret the mediation as evidence consistent with analyst attention as a partial channel without claiming a clean causal decomposition; the residual unobserved-confounder concern is bounded but not eliminated. We do not claim attention is the unique mediator; the residual direct effect (0.40 pp ≈ 49% of total) is the share of the total effect through other channels — most plausibly the direct information value of standardized disclosure content — that we do not separately identify here."*

## A note on what could have gone wrong

Three failure modes the audit prevented:

1. *Failure mode 1: skip the DAG.* A student who runs the three regressions without drawing the DAG would have added the mediator-control $M$ to the headline regression and reported the shrinkage as evidence — never noticing that the mediator is a post-treatment collider with a named $U$.

2. *Failure mode 2: skip Pattern B.* A student who acknowledges Pattern B but skips the sensitivity analysis would report the textbook 0.417 indirect effect without the [0.319, 0.515] bound — and a referee who knows the bad-controls literature would catch it.

3. *Failure mode 3: claim too much.* A student who reports the indirect effect without the "what we don't claim" sentence implies the analyst-attention channel is the unique mechanism — and is then exposed by a referee asking "what about the direct information value of the disclosure content itself?"

The discipline of the four-pattern audit + sensitivity analysis + the closing "what we don't claim" sentence is what closes all three failure modes simultaneously.

*End of model deliverable. The PS 10.4 discipline — draw the DAG, name $U$, audit the four patterns, run the sensitivity, write the bound — is what makes a mediation analysis publishable. Most published-paper mediation paragraphs skip at least one of these steps; the camp's standard is that a mechanism paragraph that skips any of them is not yet defensible at a sophisticated referee.*
