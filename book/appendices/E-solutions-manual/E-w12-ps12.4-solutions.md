## A subtle point — feedback-tier sequencing matters

The order of the three venues matters as much as the choice. The natural sequence is:

1. **Highest-feedback venue first** (Tier 5, FMA Undergraduate Poster). The first non-camp audience gives the highest information-per-comment density: every comment is from someone seeing the paper fresh.
2. **Credentialing venue second** (Tier 4, EFA). After the v1.1 revision, the paper is in better shape to earn a regional credential.
3. **Stretch venue last** (Tier 3, SFS Cavalcade). The stretch venue evaluates the best version of the paper.

The wrong order — say, applying to SFS first because "stretch is exciting" — burns the best version of the paper on the lowest-probability venue and leaves you presenting the post-feedback v1.1 at the easier FMA. The sequence is *feedback → credential → stretch*; running it in any other order is suboptimal.

```python
# sequence_expected_value.py — illustrative
# Expected credential value, summed across three venues, depends on order.
import itertools

# Tier values (rough credential weights):
tier_value = {"FMA": 1.0, "EFA": 2.0, "SFS": 3.5}
# Acceptance probabilities given paper version:
def p_accept(venue, version):
    base = {"FMA": 0.65, "EFA": 0.55, "SFS": 0.35}
    bonus = {"v1.0": 0.0, "v1.1": 0.05, "v2.0": 0.10}  # better version → higher accept
    return min(0.95, base[venue] + bonus[version])

# In the recommended sequence: FMA gets v1.0, EFA gets v1.1, SFS gets v2.0.
seq_recommended = [("FMA", "v1.0"), ("EFA", "v1.1"), ("SFS", "v2.0")]
ev_recommended = sum(tier_value[v] * p_accept(v, ver) for v, ver in seq_recommended)
print(f"EV (recommended sequence): {ev_recommended:.2f}")

# In the wrong sequence (SFS first, FMA last): SFS gets v1.0, FMA gets v2.0.
seq_wrong = [("SFS", "v1.0"), ("EFA", "v1.1"), ("FMA", "v2.0")]
ev_wrong = sum(tier_value[v] * p_accept(v, ver) for v, ver in seq_wrong)
print(f"EV (wrong sequence): {ev_wrong:.2f}")
```

Verified output:

```
EV (recommended sequence): 3.42
EV (wrong sequence): 3.17
```

The recommended sequence's expected value is roughly 8% higher than the wrong sequence's (3.42 vs. 3.17), because the stretch venue gets the best version of the paper and the highest-acceptance venue gets the earliest version where the feedback marginal-value is highest. The illustrative model abstracts heavily, but even with a small per-version acceptance bonus, the ordering payoff is positive and the qualitative direction is robust. Larger version-quality effects (i.e., a paper that improves meaningfully through revision) increase the gap further.

## Common student errors

1. **Four or more venues.** Each application is 3–8 hours; four applications is 16–32 hours, plus the travel, plus the v1.1 and v2.0 revisions. Three is the right ceiling.
2. **The stretch venue is in the wrong sequence.** Submitting SFS first burns v1.0 on the stretch and leaves v2.0 for the easier venue. Reorder.
3. **No eligibility audit — just venue names.** Many calls have eligibility surprises. Audit explicitly.
4. **The Gantt chart does not flag the SFS-submission-before-EFA-presentation timing.** The dependency exists; the chart should surface it.
5. **The contingency plan says "we'll figure it out."** The contingency is the plan; without it, the first rejection becomes a crisis.
6. **The budget is "approximately $2,000" with no line items.** Specific costs are checkable; vague estimates are not.
