1. external-validity/sample-target.md

Sample (Ethereum mainnet forks):

Unit: a documented mainnet fork event of Ethereum. Inclusion criteria: any planned-or-emergency mainnet fork between July 2016 (the DAO fork) and December 2024 with at least 5% of full nodes activating the alternative chain at t = 0. Exclusion criteria: testnet forks; rollback “forks” that are technically reorganizations rather than protocol changes. Sample size N_S = 14. Time window: 2016-07-20 to 2024-12-15. Selection mechanism: observational; the sample is the set of forks that actually happened. There is no randomization. Selection is not endogenous to the outcome we measure (time-to-fair-value) in any direct sense — forks happen for protocol-governance reasons that precede the outcome — but Ethereum-specific structural features (PoW until 2022, PoS after) shape what we observe.

Target (large-blockchain fork population):

Unit: any mainnet fork on a “large” smart-contract-or-payments blockchain (operationalized as one of: Ethereum, Bitcoin, Solana, Avalanche, Cardano, BNB Chain). Inclusion criteria: same as sample (≥ 5% node activation), but across all six chains. Exclusion criteria: same as sample. Hypothetical/projected size N_T \approx 200 over the next decade, projecting forward each chain’s historical fork rate (Bitcoin: ~1 per 3 years; Ethereum: ~2 per year; Solana: ~1 per year; etc.). Time window: hypothetical 2025–2034. Selection mechanism: hypothetical — there is no actual list of 200 forks. The target distribution is projected using the observed Ethereum sample’s moderator distribution as a baseline and adjusting for known cross-chain differences (Bitcoin and Solana have lower TVL by Ethereum’s standards; Solana has higher volatility; Cardano has a more centralized governance structure).

The implicit-target audit: Devon’s draft abstract reads “contentious forks in large blockchains take longer to reach fair value” — implying the cross-chain target above. The data is Ethereum-only; the abstract’s target is the cross-chain population. The audit finds the abstract overclaims if we cannot defend the transport. The transport in Part 3 below is what we use to either defend the abstract or rewrite it.

3. IPSW weights and effective sample size (Part 2(d))

>>> from sklearn.linear_model import LogisticRegression
>>> X_all = np.vstack([X_s, X_t])
>>> S = np.concatenate([np.ones(n_s), np.zeros(n_t)])
>>> lr = LogisticRegression(max_iter=1000).fit(X_all, S)
>>> ps_s = lr.predict_proba(X_s)[:, 1]
>>> w = (1 - ps_s) / ps_s
>>> print(f"Weight range: [{w.min():.3f}, {w.max():.3f}]")
>>> ESS = w.sum()**2 / (w**2).sum()
>>> print(f"Effective sample size: {ESS:.1f} (out of n_s={n_s})")
Weight range: [0.4, 92.1]
Effective sample size: 9.1 (out of n_s=14)

The effective sample size is 9.1 out of 14 — substantial weight concentration. The Ethereum forks that look most “target-like” (lowest TVL, highest volatility) are upweighted; the most “sample-like” forks are downweighted. ESS = 9.1 < N_S/4 = 3.5 is technically not below the worrying threshold, but it is below the more permissive N_S/2 = 7 — so the IPSW transport is borderline-stable. Devon documents this in the memo: “IPSW weights show substantial concentration (ESS = 9.1 / 14); the transport is implementable but the bootstrap CI in Part 3(c) is wider than it would be on a less-shifted sample-target pair.”

4. The transported ATE (Part 3)

The simulated true CATE function used to compute the “true” target ATE for verification: \tau(\mathbf{x}) = 0.20 + 0.05 \cdot \text{log-TVL} - 0.30 \cdot \text{vol}. Devon’s actual data uses the estimated CATE from a causal forest (PS 10.3) for the g-computation step; the IPSW transport is purely a reweighting of the observed outcomes.

Quantity Value Interpretation
Sample ATE \widehat{\text{ATE}}_S 0.51 Ethereum-fork average treatment effect (in scaled units; corresponds to ~18 hours longer time-to-fair-value for contentious forks on Devon’s actual data)
Naive target ATE (if we just sample-averaged) 0.37 Computed from the simulated tau on the target moderator distribution
Transported ATE \widehat{\text{ATE}}_T (IPSW) 0.46 Reweighted Ethereum sample to look like target
Residual transport bias +0.08 Transported - true target; non-zero because N_S = 14 is small
Transport correction 0.46 - 0.51 = -0.05 About 10% of the sample ATE; meaningful

The IPSW transport closed roughly half of the naive transport gap (from 0.14 to 0.08). The residual 0.08 gap is the sampling error from a 14-fork sample, not a fundamental bias — at larger N_S, the gap would shrink toward zero under the assumption of CATE-function invariance.

6. Sensitivity to unobserved moderator shift (Part 4)

Devon names three candidate unobserved U’s for sensitivity:

  1. Governance centralization — the degree to which the fork decision was made by a centralized foundation versus by distributed coordination. Ethereum is in the middle; Bitcoin is highly decentralized; Solana is centralized. SMD between sample and target: plausibly large (|\Delta_U| \approx 0.50).

  2. PoS vs. PoW — the consensus mechanism. Ethereum is PoS post-2022 (most of Devon’s sample is pre-2022 PoW). Bitcoin is PoW; Solana is PoS-like. SMD: |\Delta_U| \approx 0.30.

  3. Stablecoin TVL share — what fraction of TVL is in stablecoins (more institutional, less reactive to forks). Ethereum has a lot; Bitcoin has essentially none; Solana is in between. SMD: |\Delta_U| \approx 0.40.

Using |\beta_U \cdot s_U| scaled to the strongest BLP moderator (TVL effect of 0.05 per log unit, multiplied by the sample SD of log-TVL = 1.5, gives \beta_U \cdot s_U \approx 0.075 as a comparable per-moderator effect), the bias from an unobserved moderator with |\Delta_U| = 0.50 is bounded:

\text{Bias}_U \le 0.075 \cdot 0.50 = 0.038

For |\Delta_U| = 0.30 and |\Delta_U| = 0.40:

\text{Bias}_U \le 0.075 \cdot 0.30 = 0.023, \quad 0.075 \cdot 0.40 = 0.030

Combining the three plausible unobserved moderators (assuming independent bias contributions, which is conservative if they correlate in the same direction):

\text{Total bias bound} \le \sqrt{0.038^2 + 0.023^2 + 0.030^2} \approx 0.053

The transported ATE of 0.46, bounded by \pm 0.05 for unobserved moderator shift, ranges from approximately 0.41 to 0.51 under the plausible unobserved-shift sensitivity. The bootstrap CI [0.21, 0.78] is wider than this sensitivity bound; the dominant source of uncertainty in Devon’s transport is the small sample size, not the unobserved moderator shift.

7. The §7 external-validity memo (Part 5(a))

“The paper’s headline ATE of 0.51 (≈ 18 hours longer time-to-fair-value for contentious forks) is estimated on a sample of 14 Ethereum mainnet forks 2016–2024; we now ask whether it transports to a target population of large-blockchain forks (Ethereum, Bitcoin, Solana, Avalanche, Cardano, BNB Chain) over a projected next decade.

On the moderators we pre-specified in §5 (the PS 10.3 PAP), the sample and target differ substantially: the sample has higher log-TVL (SMD = +0.92, i.e., Ethereum forks happen on a higher-TVL chain than the cross-chain average) and lower pre-fork volatility (SMD = −1.69, i.e., Ethereum-fork environments are calmer than the cross-chain average).

Reweighting the sample via inverse-probability-of-sampling-weighting (IPSW) on the pre-specified moderators yields a transported ATE of 0.46 (95% bootstrap CI [0.21, 0.78]) — a 10% downward adjustment from the sample ATE.

Under plausible unobserved-moderator shifts (governance centralization, PoS vs. PoW, stablecoin TVL share; |\Delta_U| \le 0.50 each), the transported estimate is robust to bias up to \pm 0.05; the dominant source of uncertainty is the small sample (N_S = 14, effective sample size \approx 9), reflected in the wide bootstrap CI.

We claim our findings apply to the target population of large-blockchain forks under the assumption that the conditional treatment-effect function \tau(\mathbf{x}) is invariant across blockchain platforms on the observed moderators; we do not claim they apply to small-cap or appchain forks (which the inclusion criterion excluded) or to forks driven by quantum-cryptographic breaks (an unobserved structural change that would shift \tau entirely).

The IPSW transport assumes the CATE function is invariant across sample and target; we cannot rule out CATE shifts driven by Bitcoin’s specific institutional culture or Solana’s specific outage history.

A direct test of external validity — re-estimating the analysis on Bitcoin or Solana fork data as those events occur — would refine the bound substantially; we encourage replication on those chains as forks happen.

The fifth and sixth sentences are the load-bearing pair. The fifth names what the paper claims; the sixth names what the paper cannot rule out. The seventh names the future-work pathway.

A note on what could have gone wrong

Three failure modes the memo prevents:

  1. Failure mode 1: handwave. A student who writes only “results may not generalize to other blockchains” makes no claim a reader can evaluate. Devon’s memo names the target (six specific chains), characterizes the shift (SMDs on two moderators), and bounds the transport (CI plus sensitivity).

  2. Failure mode 2: claim too much. A student who reports the sample ATE without the transport correction lets the abstract claim generality the data does not support. The implicit-target audit (Part 1(c)) catches this; the IPSW correction either supports the claim (with the transported number replacing the sample number in the abstract) or forces a rewrite of the abstract to match the narrower target the transport can defend.

  3. Failure mode 3: hide the small-N uncertainty. A student who reports the transport point estimate without the bootstrap CI implies a precision the data does not have. Devon’s CI of [0.21, 0.78] is wide and reported honestly; the memo identifies the bootstrap-CI width (not the unobserved-moderator bound) as the dominant source of uncertainty.

A note on the alternative: g-computation

If Devon’s PS 10.3 causal forest produced per-unit CATE predictions \hat\tau(\mathbf{x}_i), the g-computation transport is the integral of \hat\tau over the target distribution:

\widehat{\text{ATE}}_T^{\text{g}} = \frac{1}{N_T} \sum_{i \in \text{target}} \hat\tau(\mathbf{x}_i)

In Devon’s case, this requires running the trained forest’s effect method on the target moderator vectors:

# After fitting forest on the sample with PS 10.3's protocol
tau_at_target = forest.effect(X_t)
ate_transported_g = tau_at_target.mean()

Under correct specification, g-computation and IPSW give numerically similar transported ATEs. If they disagree by more than the bootstrap CI width, one of them is misspecified — most likely the IPSW logistic regression (because the linear logit can fail to capture all the moderator shift). The disagreement diagnostic is worth reporting: “IPSW and g-computation transport agree on the point estimate to within 0.03 units, increasing our confidence in the transport bound.”

End of model deliverable. PS 10.5’s discipline — name target, compute SMDs, IPSW or g-computation, sensitivity, bound, abstract rewrite — turns vague “may not generalize” hedges into auditable claims about scope. Devon’s deliverable is what the camp aims at; the discipline applies to every empirical paper where the abstract makes a claim broader than the sample, which is approximately every empirical paper.

The effective sample size is 9.1 out of 14 — substantial weight concentration. The Ethereum forks that look most "target-like" (lowest TVL, highest volatility) are upweighted; the most "sample-like" forks are downweighted. **ESS = 9.1 < $N_S/4 = 3.5$** is technically *not* below the worrying threshold, but it is below the more permissive $N_S/2 = 7$ — so the IPSW transport is borderline-stable. Devon documents this in the memo: *"IPSW weights show substantial concentration (ESS = 9.1 / 14); the transport is implementable but the bootstrap CI in Part 3(c) is wider than it would be on a less-shifted sample-target pair."*

### 4. The transported ATE (Part 3)

The simulated true CATE function used to compute the "true" target ATE for verification: $\tau(\mathbf{x}) = 0.20 + 0.05 \cdot \text{log-TVL} - 0.30 \cdot \text{vol}$. Devon's actual data uses the estimated CATE from a causal forest (PS 10.3) for the g-computation step; the IPSW transport is purely a reweighting of the observed outcomes.

| Quantity | Value | Interpretation |
|---|---|---|
| Sample ATE $\widehat{\text{ATE}}_S$ | 0.51 | Ethereum-fork average treatment effect (in scaled units; corresponds to ~18 hours longer time-to-fair-value for contentious forks on Devon's actual data) |
| Naive target ATE (if we just sample-averaged) | 0.37 | Computed from the simulated tau on the target moderator distribution |
| Transported ATE $\widehat{\text{ATE}}_T$ (IPSW) | **0.46** | Reweighted Ethereum sample to look like target |
| Residual transport bias | +0.08 | Transported - true target; non-zero because $N_S = 14$ is small |
| Transport correction | $0.46 - 0.51 = -0.05$ | About 10% of the sample ATE; meaningful |

The IPSW transport closed roughly half of the naive transport gap (from 0.14 to 0.08). The residual 0.08 gap is the *sampling error from a 14-fork sample*, not a fundamental bias — at larger $N_S$, the gap would shrink toward zero under the assumption of CATE-function invariance.

### 5. Bootstrap CI on the transported ATE (Part 3(c))

```python
B = 9999
rng2 = np.random.default_rng(43)
transported_boot = np.zeros(B)
for b in range(B):
    idx_s = rng2.choice(n_s, size=n_s, replace=True)
    Xb_s = X_s[idx_s]
    X_all_b = np.vstack([Xb_s, X_t])
    S_b = np.concatenate([np.ones(n_s), np.zeros(n_t)])
    lr_b = LogisticRegression(max_iter=1000).fit(X_all_b, S_b)
    ps_b = lr_b.predict_proba(Xb_s)[:, 1]
    w_b = (1 - ps_b) / ps_b
    transported_boot[b] = np.average(true_tau(Xb_s), weights=w_b)

ci_lo, ci_hi = np.percentile(transported_boot, [2.5, 97.5])
print(f"Transported ATE: {ATE_transported:.3f}, 95% bootstrap CI: [{ci_lo:.3f}, {ci_hi:.3f}]")
# Output (illustrative): 0.456, 95% CI [0.21, 0.78]
```

Wide CI. This is the consequence of $N_S = 14$: the transport is *point-estimable* but uncertain. A real Devon student would report the wide CI honestly and note that as more Ethereum forks accumulate (or as Bitcoin/Solana forks happen, allowing direct estimation rather than transport), the bound would tighten.

### 6. Sensitivity to unobserved moderator shift (Part 4)

Devon names three candidate unobserved $U$'s for sensitivity:

1. *Governance centralization* — the degree to which the fork decision was made by a centralized foundation versus by distributed coordination. Ethereum is in the middle; Bitcoin is highly decentralized; Solana is centralized. SMD between sample and target: plausibly large ($|\Delta_U| \approx 0.50$).

2. *PoS vs. PoW* — the consensus mechanism. Ethereum is PoS post-2022 (most of Devon's sample is pre-2022 PoW). Bitcoin is PoW; Solana is PoS-like. SMD: $|\Delta_U| \approx 0.30$.

3. *Stablecoin TVL share* — what fraction of TVL is in stablecoins (more institutional, less reactive to forks). Ethereum has a lot; Bitcoin has essentially none; Solana is in between. SMD: $|\Delta_U| \approx 0.40$.

Using $|\beta_U \cdot s_U|$ scaled to the strongest BLP moderator (TVL effect of 0.05 per log unit, multiplied by the sample SD of log-TVL = 1.5, gives $\beta_U \cdot s_U \approx 0.075$ as a comparable per-moderator effect), the bias from an unobserved moderator with $|\Delta_U| = 0.50$ is bounded:

$$\text{Bias}_U \le 0.075 \cdot 0.50 = 0.038$$

For $|\Delta_U| = 0.30$ and $|\Delta_U| = 0.40$:

$$\text{Bias}_U \le 0.075 \cdot 0.30 = 0.023, \quad 0.075 \cdot 0.40 = 0.030$$

Combining the three plausible unobserved moderators (assuming independent bias contributions, which is conservative if they correlate in the same direction):

$$\text{Total bias bound} \le \sqrt{0.038^2 + 0.023^2 + 0.030^2} \approx 0.053$$

**The transported ATE of 0.46, bounded by $\pm 0.05$ for unobserved moderator shift, ranges from approximately 0.41 to 0.51 under the plausible unobserved-shift sensitivity.** The bootstrap CI [0.21, 0.78] is wider than this sensitivity bound; the *dominant* source of uncertainty in Devon's transport is the small sample size, not the unobserved moderator shift.

## A note on what could have gone wrong

Three failure modes the memo prevents:

1. *Failure mode 1: handwave.* A student who writes only "results may not generalize to other blockchains" makes no claim a reader can evaluate. Devon's memo *names* the target (six specific chains), *characterizes* the shift (SMDs on two moderators), and *bounds* the transport (CI plus sensitivity).

2. *Failure mode 2: claim too much.* A student who reports the sample ATE without the transport correction lets the abstract claim generality the data does not support. The implicit-target audit (Part 1(c)) catches this; the IPSW correction either *supports* the claim (with the transported number replacing the sample number in the abstract) or *forces a rewrite* of the abstract to match the narrower target the transport can defend.

3. *Failure mode 3: hide the small-N uncertainty.* A student who reports the transport point estimate without the bootstrap CI implies a precision the data does not have. Devon's CI of [0.21, 0.78] is wide and reported honestly; the memo identifies the bootstrap-CI width (not the unobserved-moderator bound) as the dominant source of uncertainty.

## A note on the alternative: g-computation

If Devon's PS 10.3 causal forest produced per-unit CATE predictions $\hat\tau(\mathbf{x}_i)$, the g-computation transport is the integral of $\hat\tau$ over the target distribution:

$$\widehat{\text{ATE}}_T^{\text{g}} = \frac{1}{N_T} \sum_{i \in \text{target}} \hat\tau(\mathbf{x}_i)$$

In Devon's case, this requires running the trained forest's `effect` method on the *target moderator vectors*:

```python
# After fitting forest on the sample with PS 10.3's protocol
tau_at_target = forest.effect(X_t)
ate_transported_g = tau_at_target.mean()
```

Under correct specification, g-computation and IPSW give numerically similar transported ATEs. If they disagree by more than the bootstrap CI width, *one* of them is misspecified — most likely the IPSW logistic regression (because the linear logit can fail to capture all the moderator shift). The disagreement diagnostic is worth reporting: *"IPSW and g-computation transport agree on the point estimate to within 0.03 units, increasing our confidence in the transport bound."*

Devon's deliverable is what the camp aims at; the discipline applies to every empirical paper where the abstract makes a claim broader than the sample, which is approximately every empirical paper.*
