PS 9.4 has no numeric answer key: it is a project deliverable, and what is graded is whether the 48×36-inch poster is legible at three feet, self-contained for a hallway viewer, and reproducible by `make poster`. So this appendix entry is not a worked solution but a **model deliverable**: a complete, A-grade `poster.pdf` (described in full text, since a PDF cannot be inlined), its `poster.py` build script, and its 200-word `poster.md` build memo, for one cast project (Leah's CEO-political-ideology forward-disclosure text-NLP poster), with instructor grading notes keyed to the rubric.

The project is **Leah's CEO-political-ideology forward-disclosure poster** — a replication-and-extension that draws on Elnahas, Gao, Hossain & Kim (2024), *JFQA* 59(8):3671–3707, *"CEO Political Ideology and Voluntary Forward-Looking Disclosure."* Leah's question: *do firms with more politically conservative CEOs (measured by personal-donation patterns) issue fewer and shorter forward-looking statements in their 10-K MD&A sections?* The primary estimate is the coefficient on a CEO-conservatism score in a panel regression of forward-statement count, with firm and year fixed effects, clustered by firm. Every magnitude below is **illustrative**, presented as a worked *poster* example to show what A-grade poster compression and a reproducible matplotlib build script look like, not as a claimed empirical finding (the published numbers belong to Elnahas et al. 2024, and Leah's extension is the text-NLP scoring of forward-statement *content* in addition to count); a student running `make poster` on the real pinned data gets the real numbers.

## THE MODEL DELIVERABLE

### Part A — The poster (`paper/poster/poster.pdf`), described

The poster is a single 48-inch-wide × 36-inch-tall landscape vector PDF. The layout follows the 3×3 grid from PS 9.4 Part 1(a) with the center cell oversized:

**Top row (height ratio 1):**

- **Top-left + top-center (spanning):** Title bar. *"Conservative CEOs Issue Fewer Forward-Looking Statements: Evidence from a Decade of 10-K MD&A Text"* in 72-point bold Helvetica, dark navy. Author line *"Leah W., GMU Camp 2026 · Replication-and-extension of Elnahas, Gao, Hossain & Kim (JFQA 2024)"* in 36-point regular underneath.
- **Top-right:** A 3×3-inch QR code (generated by the `qrcode` Python package, encoding `github.com/CAMP-ORG/capstone-2026-leah`) plus the URL underneath in 24-point monospace. Beside the QR, a small grey-on-white "scan to clone the packet" instruction in 18 pt.

**Middle row (height ratio 3, the visual core):**

- **Middle-left:** The *design / identification figure* (Leah's one-figure defense from PS 9.3): a coefficient plot of the CEO-conservatism slope across five sequentially-added control sets (raw → +firm-size → +leverage → +industry-year FE → +CEO-tenure controls). Each control set is a row; each estimate is a dot with a 95% CI bar. The five dots cluster between $-0.18$ and $-0.22$ — the coefficient does not drift toward zero as controls are added, which is the visual evidence for identification in a panel-with-controls design. A reference line at zero, a vertical line at the primary specification's estimate ($-0.20$), and one annotation: *"coefficient stable across sequentially-added controls → not driven by an obvious confound."*
- **Middle-center (oversized, the headline figure):** The *headline result* — a coefficient plot showing the CEO-conservatism slope on three outcomes: (1) forward-statement *count*, (2) forward-statement *length* (in words), (3) forward-statement *tone* (Loughran-McDonald sentiment score). All three outcomes show negative coefficients with CIs excluding zero — count $-0.20$ [$-0.26, -0.14$], length $-0.18$ [$-0.25, -0.11$], tone $-0.09$ [$-0.14, -0.04$]. The plot is wider than the others and uses 28-point axis labels. Annotation: *"Conservative CEOs issue fewer, shorter, less optimistic forward-looking statements."*
- **Middle-right:** The *robustness figure* (Leah's spec curve from PS 8.1): a sorted point-and-CI plot of 96 specifications (4 outcome definitions × 3 sample-period choices × 2 clustering × 4 control-set choices). All 96 dots are negative, sorted from $-0.30$ on the left to $-0.10$ on the right; the pre-registered primary ($-0.20$) is marked with a red diamond near the median. Annotation: *"96 of 96 specifications negative; sign is robust."*

**Bottom row (height ratio 1, the text panels):**

- **Bottom-left:** Two short text panels stacked: **Question** ("Do conservative CEOs disclose less forward-looking information?") and **Why it matters** ("Forward-looking statements are how investors learn what management expects; if disclosure is ideologically patterned, market information is too. The MD&A average is 142 forward statements; a 1-SD increase in CEO conservatism predicts about 14 fewer.").
- **Bottom-center:** **Contribution** ("We extend Elnahas et al. (2024) from forward-statement *count* to *content*: not only do conservative CEOs issue fewer forward statements, the ones they do issue are shorter and less optimistic. We do not show causality from ideology to disclosure — the design controls but does not exogenously vary CEO type — but the pattern survives sequentially-added controls and 96 robustness specifications.").
- **Bottom-right:** **Data + replication note** ("Sample: 2010–2022 S&P 1500 firms; CEO-donation data from FEC; 10-K text from EDGAR. All figures regenerate from raw data via `make all` on the packet at the QR-code URL.").

**Color discipline:** dark navy (`#1f2d5a`) for the main series in all three figures; medium grey (`#666`) for confidence bands and reference lines; light grey (`#bbb`) for placebo / secondary series. No red except the pre-registered diamond marker on the middle-right spec curve. No traffic-light palette. White space margins of 1 inch between panels.

**Typography (set once via `matplotlib.rcParams` at the top of `poster.py`):**

- Title: 72 pt bold Helvetica
- Section headers ("Question", "Why it matters", "Contribution", "Data"): 48 pt bold
- Body text: 24 pt regular
- Figure axis labels: 28 pt
- Figure tick labels: 22 pt

**Total prose word count across all body panels:** 192 words (Question 18 + Why-it-matters 38 + Contribution 75 + Data 22 + figure annotations 39). Within the 200-word cap from PS 9.4 Part 1(c).

### Part B — The build script (`src/poster.py`)

```python
# poster.py — 48x36-inch conference poster (CEO conservatism × forward disclosure)
# Builds paper/poster/poster.pdf from the analysis dataset and existing figure
# functions; regenerated by `make poster`; byte-identical on rebuild.

import matplotlib
matplotlib.use("Agg")
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt
import matplotlib.gridspec as gridspec
import numpy as np
import pandas as pd
from pathlib import Path
import qrcode

from config import SEED   # the single named project seed

# Reuse the figure functions from the existing figure scripts (Lab 7 / PS 9.3).
from one_figure import draw_control_stability_plot     # design/identification
from headline_figure import draw_outcomes_panel        # headline
from spec_curve import draw_spec_curve                 # robustness

rng = np.random.default_rng(SEED)

# ---- Poster-scale matplotlib defaults -----------------------------------
matplotlib.rcParams.update({
    "pdf.fonttype": 42,         # TrueType embedding (system-independent)
    "font.family": "Helvetica",
    "axes.linewidth": 1.2,
    "axes.spines.top": False,
    "axes.spines.right": False,
})

# Color palette (two-color rule + grey)
NAVY = "#1f2d5a"
GREY_DARK = "#666666"
GREY_LIGHT = "#bbbbbb"

# ---- Load the analysis dataset ------------------------------------------
df = pd.read_parquet("data/processed/analysis.parquet")
spec_df = pd.read_parquet("data/processed/spec-curve.parquet")
outcomes_df = pd.read_parquet("data/processed/outcomes.parquet")

# ---- Build the 48x36-inch canvas with the 3x3 grid ----------------------
fig = plt.figure(figsize=(48, 36), dpi=150)

gs = gridspec.GridSpec(
    3, 3, figure=fig,
    height_ratios=[1, 3, 1],          # top thin, middle big, bottom thin
    width_ratios=[1.2, 1.6, 1.2],     # center wider than columns
    hspace=0.20, wspace=0.12,
    left=0.03, right=0.98, top=0.97, bottom=0.03,
)

# ---- Top row: title bar (spans cols 0-1) + QR (col 2) -------------------
ax_title = fig.add_subplot(gs[0, 0:2])
ax_title.text(
    0.0, 0.62,
    "Conservative CEOs Issue Fewer Forward-Looking Statements:",
    fontsize=72, fontweight="bold", va="center", color=NAVY,
)
ax_title.text(
    0.0, 0.30,
    "Evidence from a Decade of 10-K MD&A Text",
    fontsize=72, fontweight="bold", va="center", color=NAVY,
)
ax_title.text(
    0.0, 0.05,
    "Leah W., GMU Camp 2026 · Replication-and-extension of Elnahas, Gao, Hossain & Kim (JFQA 2024)",
    fontsize=36, va="center", color=GREY_DARK,
)
ax_title.axis("off")

# QR code
ax_qr = fig.add_subplot(gs[0, 2])
qr_url = "https://github.com/CAMP-ORG/capstone-2026-leah"
qr_img = qrcode.make(qr_url)
ax_qr.imshow(qr_img, cmap="gray_r")
ax_qr.text(
    0.5, -0.08, qr_url,
    fontsize=24, family="monospace", ha="center", va="top",
    transform=ax_qr.transAxes,
)
ax_qr.text(
    0.5, -0.18, "scan to clone the packet",
    fontsize=18, ha="center", va="top",
    color=GREY_DARK, transform=ax_qr.transAxes,
)
ax_qr.axis("off")

# ---- Middle row: three figures -----------------------------------------
ax_design = fig.add_subplot(gs[1, 0])
draw_control_stability_plot(ax_design, df, color=NAVY, font_size=22)
ax_design.set_title("Design: Coefficient Stable Across Controls",
                    fontsize=36, fontweight="bold", color=NAVY, pad=15)

ax_headline = fig.add_subplot(gs[1, 1])
draw_outcomes_panel(ax_headline, outcomes_df, color=NAVY, font_size=28)
ax_headline.set_title("Headline: Fewer, Shorter, Less Optimistic",
                      fontsize=44, fontweight="bold", color=NAVY, pad=20)

ax_robust = fig.add_subplot(gs[1, 2])
draw_spec_curve(ax_robust, spec_df, color=NAVY, font_size=22)
ax_robust.set_title("Robustness: 96 of 96 Negative",
                    fontsize=36, fontweight="bold", color=NAVY, pad=15)

# ---- Bottom row: three text panels --------------------------------------
def text_panel(ax, header, body, header_fs=48, body_fs=24):
    ax.text(0.02, 0.92, header, fontsize=header_fs, fontweight="bold",
            color=NAVY, va="top")
    ax.text(0.02, 0.72, body, fontsize=body_fs, va="top", wrap=True)
    ax.axis("off")

ax_bl = fig.add_subplot(gs[2, 0])
text_panel(
    ax_bl,
    "Question",
    "Do conservative CEOs disclose less forward-looking information?\n\n"
    "Why it matters: forward statements are how investors learn what management\n"
    "expects. If disclosure is ideologically patterned, market information is too.\n"
    "MD&A average: 142 forward statements; +1 SD CEO conservatism → ~14 fewer.",
)

ax_bc = fig.add_subplot(gs[2, 1])
text_panel(
    ax_bc,
    "Contribution",
    "We extend Elnahas, Gao, Hossain & Kim (2024) from forward-statement count\n"
    "to content: conservative CEOs issue fewer forward statements, AND the ones\n"
    "they do issue are shorter and less optimistic. We do not show causality from\n"
    "ideology to disclosure — the design controls but does not exogenously vary\n"
    "CEO type — but the pattern survives 96 robustness specifications.",
)

ax_br = fig.add_subplot(gs[2, 2])
text_panel(
    ax_br,
    "Data & Replication",
    "Sample: 2010–2022 S&P 1500 firms.\n"
    "CEO-donation data: FEC individual contributions.\n"
    "10-K text: EDGAR.\n\n"
    "All figures regenerate from raw data via `make all` on the packet.",
)

# ---- Save -----------------------------------------------------------------
Path("paper/poster").mkdir(parents=True, exist_ok=True)
fig.savefig("paper/poster/poster.pdf", bbox_inches="tight",
            metadata={"CreationDate": None})  # strip timestamp for byte-identical rebuild
plt.close(fig)

print("poster.pdf rebuilt at 48x36 inches; total prose word count = 192")
```

Key reproducibility features:

- `matplotlib.use("Agg")` for headless rendering.
- `pdf.fonttype = 42` for system-independent TrueType embedding.
- `metadata={"CreationDate": None}` in `savefig` strips the timestamp that would otherwise change the SHA-256 on every rebuild.
- Figure functions imported from `one_figure.py`, `headline_figure.py`, and `spec_curve.py` — the same functions that draw the slide-3, slide-4, and slide-5 figures in the deck. The poster's middle-center panel and slide 4 of the deck are *literally the same code drawing the same data*; they cannot drift.
- The 192-word total is printed at the end as a sanity check (the actual word count is computed at runtime in the production version).

### Part D — Pipeline integration

The `Makefile` rule:

```makefile
paper/poster/poster.pdf: data/processed/analysis.parquet \
                        data/processed/spec-curve.parquet \
                        data/processed/outcomes.parquet \
                        src/poster.py \
                        src/one_figure.py \
                        src/headline_figure.py \
                        src/spec_curve.py
	python src/poster.py

poster: paper/poster/poster.pdf

all: figures paper/paper.pdf poster
```

The dependency list explicitly enumerates the three derived parquets and the three figure scripts whose functions `poster.py` imports — any upstream change triggers a poster rebuild.

The byte-identical rebuild check:

```bash
$ shasum -a 256 paper/poster/poster.pdf
c4a8e7f3b1d9c2…d2f193  paper/poster/poster.pdf
$ make clean && make poster && shasum -a 256 paper/poster/poster.pdf
c4a8e7f3b1d9c2…d2f193  paper/poster/poster.pdf
```

Identical hash on rebuild. The `metadata={"CreationDate": None}` line in `savefig` is the critical detail — matplotlib's PDF backend writes a creation timestamp by default, which changes the hash on every rebuild even when the visual content is identical. Stripping the metadata makes the build deterministic.

## INSTRUCTOR GRADING NOTES

### Where this deliverable earns full marks

- **Part 1 (grid and content compression, 25/25).** Default 3×3 grid with oversized center; title is a full-sentence claim; total prose ≤ 200 words (192 actual); each panel respects its sub-budget; exactly three figures (no fourth-figure overcrowding).
- **Part 2 (matplotlib build, 35/35 — the heaviest part).** Single `poster.py` script, no design software in the chain. The script imports figure functions from the existing scripts so the poster cannot drift from the paper or deck. Determinism is achieved via `pdf.fonttype = 42` *and* `metadata={"CreationDate": None}` — the second detail is where most students lose points. Typography at poster scale (title 72 pt, body 24 pt, axis labels 28 pt) is set once via `rcParams.update`. Color palette is two-color-plus-grey. QR code is generated programmatically and embedded as an image, not a hand-pasted PNG.
- **Part 3 (hallway readability, 15/15).** All three tests run with results recorded; the walk-by produced a question-and-headline match in 12 words; the 60-second test produced a contribution-paragraph match.
- **Part 4 (pipeline integration, 15/15).** `Makefile` rule lists *all* upstream dependencies (3 parquets + 3 figure scripts). Byte-identical rebuild produces the same SHA-256. Print-shop send-off documented with proof-hash confirmation.
- **Part 5 (build memo, 10/10).** 195 words, four paragraphs as specified.

### What loses points (illustrative)

- A poster built in PowerPoint with figures pasted in as PNGs. The slide-figure-poster chain is broken: a re-run of the analysis updates the figures but the poster does not change. The rubric penalizes this heavily because the whole point of the assignment is the *reproducibility* of the poster, not its appearance.
- A `poster.py` that does not strip the PDF creation timestamp. The rebuild "works" visually but the hash drifts on every build, failing the byte-identical check in Part 4(b).
- A poster with 250 words of body text. The discipline is the cap; exceeding it produces a hallway-unreadable object, which the three-feet test would catch (the body becomes too dense at 24-point font on a 36-inch-tall poster) but which the student often overrides by shrinking the font, which then fails the three-feet test in a different way. The fix is to cut words, not cut font size.
- A QR code that does not actually scan. Test it from your phone before committing; print shops have rendered QR codes too small to scan on at least one famous occasion.
- A poster with four figures because the student could not decide which to drop. The fourth figure is decoration; the rubric counts it against the compression discipline. Drop it.

### The highest-signal single check

**Does the 60-second test produce a one-paragraph summary that matches the paper's abstract?** Leah's deliverable: Devon's summary matched the contribution panel almost verbatim. A `Y` says the poster is doing its job — a hallway viewer leaves with the question, the design, and the contribution in mind, exactly as you would write them. A `N` means one of the panels is failing: usually the contribution panel is too vague or the figure panels are too crowded; revise and re-test.

*End of model deliverable. The poster, script, and memo above are one shape PS 9.4 can take; the structure (3×3 grid, single matplotlib script, three figures, ≤200 words, three readability tests) is required of any submission, but the specific design (panel regression with sequentially-added controls here, DiD or RD or synthetic control elsewhere) is paper-specific. The point of reading the exemplar is to calibrate the reproducibility-by-default standard and the compression discipline, not to copy the panel-regression layout into a project that needs a different one.*
