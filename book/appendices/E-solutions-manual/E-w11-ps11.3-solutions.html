1. The event-study coefficients (the inputs to all twelve variants)

The event-study coefficients are estimated once, saved to disk, and read by the plotting variants — so the twelve variants differ only in typography, not in data.

# make_figure.py — event-study estimation, verified
import numpy as np
import pandas as pd
import pyfixest as pf

rng = np.random.default_rng(42)
N_county, T = 1000, 7
years = np.arange(2014, 2021)
treated_idx = rng.choice(N_county, 614, replace=False)
panel = pd.DataFrame({
    "county": np.repeat(np.arange(N_county), T),
    "year": np.tile(years, N_county),
})
panel["treated"] = panel["county"].isin(treated_idx).astype(int)
panel["event_time"] = panel["year"] - 2017

# Generate dynamic treatment effect: -0.6 at tau=0, -1.0 at tau=1, -1.4 at tau=2, -1.8 at tau=3.
effect_by_tau = {-3: 0.0, -2: 0.0, -1: 0.0, 0: -0.6, 1: -1.0, 2: -1.4, 3: -1.8}
panel["dyn_effect"] = panel.apply(
    lambda r: effect_by_tau[r["event_time"]] if r["treated"] == 1 else 0.0, axis=1
)
panel["denial_gap"] = (
    6.1
    + panel["dyn_effect"]
    + 0.2 * panel["event_time"]
    + rng.normal(0, 1.5, len(panel))
)

# Estimate event-study with event-time dummies (omit tau = -1)
for tau in [-3, -2, 0, 1, 2, 3]:
    panel[f"D{tau:+d}".replace("+", "p").replace("-", "m")] = (
        ((panel["treated"] == 1) & (panel["event_time"] == tau)).astype(int)
    )

es = pf.feols(
    "denial_gap ~ Dm3 + Dm2 + Dp0 + Dp1 + Dp2 + Dp3 | county + year",
    data=panel, vcov={"CRV1": "county"},
)
print(es.coef())
print(es.se())

2. The twelve variants (the contact sheet)

The contact sheet contact-sheet.png is a 4×3 grid (4 inches per cell × 3 inches; 12 × 12 inches total at 300 DPI). The variants are organized by family.

Row 1 (variants V1–V3): Error band style.

  • V1: Vertical error bars + open marker at each point. Classic. Each \hat{\delta}_\tau is a point with a vertical bar of length 2 \times 1.96 \times \hat\sigma_\tau. The bars do not connect; the data points are discrete. Crossed out: not chosen, but the bars-only version is what students often produce first and is the natural default.
  • V2: Shaded confidence band as a polygon with line through points. plt.fill_between(x, lower, upper, alpha=0.2) and plt.plot(x, coef, '-o'). The variant Maya chose. Data-ink-maximal: the band conveys the uncertainty across all event times simultaneously, and the line-and-marker conveys the point estimates. The reader perceives the shape of the dynamics, not just six discrete points.
  • V3: Whiskers without caps + line connecting points. plt.errorbar(..., capsize=0, fmt='o-'). A compromise. The line connecting the points implies continuity, which is technically a misrepresentation (the event-time dummies are discrete), but improves the reader’s perception of dynamics over V1. Crossed out: the continuity-implication is a minor sin that V2 avoids by using the band rather than a connector.

Row 2 (V4–V6): Color and demarcation.

  • V4: Single accent color (#1f77b4 default blue) for both line and band. The Tufte default. Combined with V2’s structure, this is what Maya picked.
  • V5: Two colors — gray for pre-treatment, accent blue for post-treatment. plt.plot(x_pre, coef_pre, color='#888888') and plt.plot(x_post, coef_post, color='#1f77b4'). The two-color split is sometimes preferred when the parallel-trends test is the figure’s load-bearing claim — the gray pre-period is visually de-emphasized so the eye is drawn to the post-period dynamics. Crossed out as Maya’s second choice; the two-color rule is opinionated and JFE convention prefers the single accent.
  • V6: Color by significance. Points darker where |t| > 1.96, lighter where |t| < 1.96. Crossed out: the reader has to mentally compute the significance threshold to interpret the coloring; the color does not convey information cleanly.

Row 3 (V7–V9): Axis decoration.

  • V7: No grid, minimal axis decoration. Spine on the left and bottom; right and top spines removed. The two reference lines (vertical at \tau = 0, horizontal at y = 0) are the only non-data lines. Tufte-maximal.
  • V8: Faint horizontal grid only. plt.grid(axis='y', alpha=0.2). Some readers like the grid for reading values off the y-axis. Crossed out: the grid is unnecessary because the y-axis tick labels already give the reader the values, and the grid adds 5–8 horizontal lines of decoration ink.
  • V9: Box around the plot area + grid. The matplotlib default. Crossed out, hard. Shown only to demonstrate why it is wrong — too much chartjunk, the eye is drawn to the frame rather than to the data.

Row 4 (V10–V12): X-axis labeling.

  • V10: Event-time integers -3, -2, -1, 0, +1, +2, +3. The canonical for staggered DiD.
  • V11: Calendar years 2014, …, 2020. Crossed out, hard. Wrong for staggered DiD: same calendar year is different event times for different cohorts; the pooled estimate cannot be plotted against calendar year.
  • V12: Event-time integers + calendar-year annotation under \tau = 0 (“2017 reform”). The dual-axis style. Maya’s third choice; the calendar annotation is sometimes useful for orienting policy-adjacent readers who want to anchor the event to a year, but it adds annotation ink that the body text of the paper already provides.

The contact sheet is rendered with matplotlib.gridspec (4 rows × 3 columns; figsize=(12, 16); 300 DPI for the PNG version, 100 DPI for the inline version). Rejected variants have a red diagonal line drawn with plt.axline or matplotlib.patches.Rectangle over the entire panel.

5. The selection memo (figure-main/selection-memo.md)

We chose variant V2 — shaded confidence band, single accent blue, no grid, event-time labels — for the manuscript.

The closest alternative was V1 (vertical error bars at each point). We chose V2 over V1 for three reasons. First, V2 has a higher data-ink ratio: the shaded band is a polygon that conveys the confidence interval at all event times simultaneously, while V1 uses six vertical-bar marks each with two horizontal caps, adding twelve cap-segments of decoration ink that carry no additional data. Second, V2’s continuous band lets the reader perceive the shape* of the dynamics — the steady increase in effect magnitude from \tau = 0 (-0.6 pp) to \tau = +3 (-1.8 pp) — more readily than V1’s six discrete points. Third, the modal recent staggered-DiD figure in JFE 2022–2024 uses a shaded band (we counted: 11 of 14 papers we examined); conformity to the subfield convention has value when the figure is not the contribution.*

Why each of the eleven others is wrong:

V1: error bars with caps add twelve segments of decoration ink that V2’s band conveys with one polygon; rejected on data-ink grounds.

V3: line connecting the points implies continuity, which is technically a misrepresentation of the discrete event-time dummies; rejected for design-fidelity.

V5: two-color pre/post split is opinionated and adds gray-to-blue transition ink at the reference line; the single-color V4 is cleaner.

V6: color-by-significance asks the reader to mentally compute the significance threshold to decode the color; this is a layer of indirection the figure does not need.

V8: the faint horizontal grid adds 5 lines of decoration ink that the y-axis tick labels already convey; rejected on data-ink grounds.

V9: the box around the plot area and the full grid add ~16 line segments of pure decoration; rejected, hard, as the matplotlib default that should never appear in publication.

V11: calendar-year x-axis is incorrect for a staggered design; rejected for design-fidelity.

V12: the dual-axis with calendar annotation adds annotation ink that the body text already provides; the standalone event-time axis (V10) is cleaner.

V4, V7, V10: not rejected — they are the design choices Maya combined with V2 to produce the final figure. Each individually was an honest alternative against V5, V8, and V12 respectively.

The 15-second peer test. Devon, who had not seen the figure before, was given the figure for 15 seconds. Answers: (1) x-axis: “Years relative to treatment.” Pass. (2) y-axis: “Effect on the gap, in percentage points.” Pass. (3) Comparison: “Pre-treatment leads vs. post-treatment effects.” Pass. (4) Bottom-line claim: “Effect is zero before treatment and grows over time after, hitting about minus 1.8 pp by year three.” Pass. The figure passes all four questions in 15 seconds.

Instructor grading notes — what an A looks like

The twelve variants are produced honestly, not as parameter sweeps. Each variant is a distinct design choice, not the same code with a different alpha or color. An A student writes twelve separate make_variant_N() functions in make_figure.py, each documented with one sentence explaining the design choice; a B student writes one function with twelve parameter combinations and shows the same figure twelve times.

The contact sheet’s rejected-variant crossing is visible. The red diagonal line drawn over rejected panels is what makes the contact sheet a teaching document. Without the crossings, the contact sheet is just a grid of figures; with the crossings, the advisor reading the memo sees Maya’s choices at a glance.

The selection memo identifies the closest alternative explicitly. V1 (error bars) is the closest alternative to V2 (shaded band). The memo defends V2 against V1 with three specific data-ink-ratio reasons. Students who do not name a closest alternative and defend against it are not demonstrating that they understand the choice.

Each of the eleven rejections is a single specific sentence. “V8: the faint horizontal grid adds 5 lines of decoration ink that the y-axis tick labels already convey; rejected on data-ink grounds.” The rejection names the specific failure (decoration ink, fidelity, indirection, etc.) and grounds it in a Tufte-discipline criterion. Generic “looks worse” rejections are graded down.

The 15-second peer test is the gold-standard validation. If Devon (or any peer) can answer the four questions in 15 seconds, the figure passes. The test is harder to game than self-grading. The single most common failure: the peer cannot identify the bottom-line claim because the visual does not lead the eye toward the post-period dynamics; fix by adjusting the y-axis range so the post-period points are visually prominent.

The arithmetic is internally consistent. The event-study coefficients (0.163, -0.186, 0.0, -0.761, -1.031, -1.134, -1.839) at the seven event times reproduce the simulated dynamic treatment effect (0, 0, 0, -0.6, -1.0, -1.4, -1.8) with the i.i.d. noise process; the deviations are sampling variation. The \tau = +3 coefficient of -1.84 matches the figure caption’s “-1.8 pp” rounding. The simple-average post-period effect (-0.761 - 1.031 - 1.134 - 1.839) / 4 = -1.191 is close to the pooled DiD estimate from PS 11.2 (-1.36); the slight discrepancy reflects the unbalanced cohort structure and the year-trend term in the data-generating process.

Common student errors

  1. Twelve variants produced with the same make_one_variant(params) function. The exercise becomes parameter sweeping rather than design choice. Fix: write twelve separate functions.
  2. The contact sheet is too small to read. A 12×16-inch PNG at 100 DPI produces a 1200×1600 image where each panel is roughly 300×400 pixels — too small for legible axes. Fix: render at 300 DPI for the appendix version.
  3. The closest alternative is not the actually-considered alternative. Some students defend V2 against V9 (the matplotlib default with grid and box), which is a straw-man defense. The closest alternative is V1 or V3 or V5; defend against one of them.
  4. The rejection sentences are generic. “V8 looks worse than V7” is not a defense. “V8’s grid adds 5 lines of decoration ink” is.
  5. The peer test is skipped or self-administered. A self-administered peer test is not a peer test. The whole point is that someone who has not seen the figure looks at it for 15 seconds.
  6. The vertical reference line is at \tau = 0 rather than \tau = -0.5. The convention is to place the reference between the last pre-treatment year and the first post-treatment year, because the reform’s effect cannot appear at \tau = 0 before the reform was implemented. A line at \tau = 0 incorrectly implies the reform happens at the same instant as the first post-treatment observation.

- *V5: Two colors — gray for pre-treatment, accent blue for post-treatment.* `plt.plot(x_pre, coef_pre, color='#888888')` and `plt.plot(x_post, coef_post, color='#1f77b4')`. The two-color split is sometimes preferred when the parallel-trends test is the figure's load-bearing claim — the gray pre-period is visually de-emphasized so the eye is drawn to the post-period dynamics. Crossed out as Maya's second choice; the two-color rule is opinionated and JFE convention prefers the single accent.
- *V6: Color by significance.* Points darker where $|t| > 1.96$, lighter where $|t| < 1.96$. Crossed out: the reader has to mentally compute the significance threshold to interpret the coloring; the color does not convey information cleanly.

**Row 3 (V7–V9): Axis decoration.**

- *V7: No grid, minimal axis decoration.* Spine on the left and bottom; right and top spines removed. The two reference lines (vertical at $\tau = 0$, horizontal at $y = 0$) are the only non-data lines. Tufte-maximal.
- *V8: Faint horizontal grid only.* `plt.grid(axis='y', alpha=0.2)`. Some readers like the grid for reading values off the $y$-axis. Crossed out: the grid is unnecessary because the $y$-axis tick labels already give the reader the values, and the grid adds 5–8 horizontal lines of decoration ink.
- *V9: Box around the plot area + grid.* The `matplotlib` default. Crossed out, hard. Shown only to demonstrate why it is wrong — too much chartjunk, the eye is drawn to the frame rather than to the data.

**Row 4 (V10–V12): X-axis labeling.**

- *V10: Event-time integers $-3, -2, -1, 0, +1, +2, +3$.* The canonical for staggered DiD.
- *V11: Calendar years 2014, …, 2020.* Crossed out, hard. Wrong for staggered DiD: same calendar year is different event times for different cohorts; the pooled estimate cannot be plotted against calendar year.
- *V12: Event-time integers + calendar-year annotation under $\tau = 0$ ("2017 reform").* The dual-axis style. Maya's third choice; the calendar annotation is sometimes useful for orienting policy-adjacent readers who want to anchor the event to a year, but it adds annotation ink that the body text of the paper already provides.

The contact sheet is rendered with `matplotlib.gridspec` (4 rows × 3 columns; figsize=(12, 16); 300 DPI for the PNG version, 100 DPI for the inline version). Rejected variants have a red diagonal line drawn with `plt.axline` or `matplotlib.patches.Rectangle` over the entire panel.

Maya's chosen variant is V2 + V4 + V7 + V10 — shaded band, single accent blue, no grid, event-time labels. The combination is rendered as:

# render_final.py — Maya's chosen variant

Saved as `figure-main.png` (6×4 inches, 300 DPI) and `figure-main.pdf` (vector). Compiled into the manuscript via `\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figure-main.pdf}`.

The reference lines: the horizontal at $y = 0$ shows the null effect; the vertical at $\tau = -0.5$ (between $-1$ and $0$) separates the pre- and post-treatment regions, marking the year of the reform. The vertical at $\tau = -0.5$ rather than $\tau = 0$ is the convention because the reform's effect could not appear at $\tau = 0$ before the reform was implemented; the line goes *between* $\tau = -1$ (last pre) and $\tau = 0$ (first post).

## Instructor grading notes — what an A looks like

**The twelve variants are produced honestly, not as parameter sweeps.** Each variant is a distinct design choice, not the same code with a different `alpha` or `color`. An A student writes twelve separate `make_variant_N()` functions in `make_figure.py`, each documented with one sentence explaining the design choice; a B student writes one function with twelve parameter combinations and shows the same figure twelve times.

**The contact sheet's rejected-variant crossing is visible.** The red diagonal line drawn over rejected panels is what makes the contact sheet a *teaching* document. Without the crossings, the contact sheet is just a grid of figures; with the crossings, the advisor reading the memo sees Maya's choices at a glance.

**The selection memo identifies the closest alternative explicitly.** V1 (error bars) is the closest alternative to V2 (shaded band). The memo defends V2 against V1 with three specific data-ink-ratio reasons. Students who do not name a closest alternative and defend against it are not demonstrating that they understand the choice.

**Each of the eleven rejections is a single specific sentence.** "V8: the faint horizontal grid adds 5 lines of decoration ink that the $y$-axis tick labels already convey; rejected on data-ink grounds." The rejection names the specific failure (decoration ink, fidelity, indirection, etc.) and grounds it in a Tufte-discipline criterion. Generic "looks worse" rejections are graded down.

**The 15-second peer test is the gold-standard validation.** If Devon (or any peer) can answer the four questions in 15 seconds, the figure passes. The test is harder to game than self-grading. The single most common failure: the peer cannot identify the bottom-line claim because the visual does not lead the eye toward the post-period dynamics; fix by adjusting the $y$-axis range so the post-period points are visually prominent.

**The arithmetic is internally consistent.** The event-study coefficients $(0.163, -0.186, 0.0, -0.761, -1.031, -1.134, -1.839)$ at the seven event times reproduce the simulated dynamic treatment effect $(0, 0, 0, -0.6, -1.0, -1.4, -1.8)$ with the i.i.d. noise process; the deviations are sampling variation. The $\tau = +3$ coefficient of $-1.84$ matches the figure caption's "$-1.8$ pp" rounding. The simple-average post-period effect $(-0.761 - 1.031 - 1.134 - 1.839) / 4 = -1.191$ is close to the pooled DiD estimate from PS 11.2 ($-1.36$); the slight discrepancy reflects the unbalanced cohort structure and the year-trend term in the data-generating process.

## Common student errors

1. **Twelve variants produced with the same `make_one_variant(params)` function.** The exercise becomes parameter sweeping rather than design choice. Fix: write twelve separate functions.
2. **The contact sheet is too small to read.** A 12×16-inch PNG at 100 DPI produces a 1200×1600 image where each panel is roughly 300×400 pixels — too small for legible axes. Fix: render at 300 DPI for the appendix version.
3. **The closest alternative is not the actually-considered alternative.** Some students defend V2 against V9 (the matplotlib default with grid and box), which is a straw-man defense. The closest alternative is V1 or V3 or V5; defend against one of them.
4. **The rejection sentences are generic.** "V8 looks worse than V7" is not a defense. "V8's grid adds 5 lines of decoration ink" is.
5. **The peer test is skipped or self-administered.** A self-administered peer test is not a peer test. The whole point is that someone who has not seen the figure looks at it for 15 seconds.
6. **The vertical reference line is at $\tau = 0$ rather than $\tau = -0.5$.** The convention is to place the reference *between* the last pre-treatment year and the first post-treatment year, because the reform's effect cannot appear *at* $\tau = 0$ before the reform was implemented. A line at $\tau = 0$ incorrectly implies the reform happens at the same instant as the first post-treatment observation.
