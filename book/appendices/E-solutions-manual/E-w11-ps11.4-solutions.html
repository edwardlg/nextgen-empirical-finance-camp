Paragraph 2 — Question (276 words):

This paper estimates the average treatment effect of the 2017 intensified fair-lending examination program on the county-year-level Black-White conventional-mortgage denial-rate gap, in the population of 614 OCC-supervised counties that experienced the rollout between 2017 and 2020. The question matters because the existing literature gives partial answers. A large descriptive literature documents persistent racial gaps in mortgage approval, beginning with Munnell, Tootell, Browne, and McEneaney (1996) and continuing through Bayer, Ferreira, and Ross (2016), Bartlett, Morse, Stanton, and Wallace (2022), and Gao and Sun (2019); this literature establishes the phenomenon but rarely identifies causal* interventions that move the phenomenon. A separate literature examines the effect of regulatory supervisory intensity on bank behavior more broadly (Agarwal, Lucca, Seru, and Trebbi 2014; Granja and Leuz 2024) but rarely focuses on disparate-impact outcomes — typically estimating effects on prudential measures such as capital ratios, lending growth, and risk-taking. A third, smaller literature evaluates anti-discrimination interventions (Ross, Turner, Godfrey, and Smith 2008) but most credibly-identified estimates come from audit studies rather than from regulatory-program rollouts. We bring these three conversations together by estimating, for a specific supervisory program and a specific disparate-impact outcome, the causal effect that the prior work either documents in correlation or examines for a different outcome. Answering this question requires a research design that isolates the effect of the 2017 intensification from other policy and market changes affecting the same counties over the same period; we now describe such a design.*

Word count: 240. Names the question specifically (treatment, outcome, unit, population), engages three literatures at the level of partial-overlap-not-empty-space, and bridges to paragraph 3 with a one-sentence transition.

Paragraph 3 — What-We-Do (319 words):

Paragraph 4 — Findings (393 words):

The estimate is stable across the four saturation choices reported in Table (the coefficient varies between -1.36 and -1.41 across columns) and across the tight-sample restriction in column 4. Effects emerge in the year of treatment and grow in magnitude over the first three post-treatment years (Figure ); the \tau = +3 coefficient is -1.8 pp (95% CI: -2.4 to -1.2), implying a cumulative effect of roughly one-quarter of the pre-treatment gap. The dynamics are inconsistent with the intensification reflecting a contemporaneous shock; the year-by-year buildup suggests a behavioral response by lenders that compounds over examination cycles. Heterogeneity is concentrated in counties with above-median minority-population shares (interaction coefficient: -1.1 pp, BH-FDR-adjusted p = 0.012) and in lenders with above-median examination-finding histories (interaction coefficient: -0.9 pp, BH-FDR-adjusted p = 0.028); the remaining heterogeneity cuts we pre-specified do not survive the multiple-testing adjustment and are reported as exploratory (Appendix Table A-2). The headline estimate survives the staggered-DiD-alternative estimators we apply (Callaway-Sant’Anna, Sun-Abraham, de Chaisemartin-D’Haultfœuille), the sample restrictions, and the placebo events (Appendix Table A-3 and Figure A-1). Word count: 279. Headline number, dynamic interpretation, heterogeneity disciplined by BH-FDR, robustness anchor, and external-validity transport. The headline matches the abstract’s sentence 4 and Table 2’s column-3 cell.

Paragraph 5 — Contribution (366 words):

Second, we add to the literature on regulatory supervisory intensity (Agarwal et al. 2014; Granja and Leuz 2024) by extending the supervisory-intensity outcome variable from prudential measures (capital ratios, lending growth, risk-taking) to disparate-impact measures (denial-rate gaps), and by exploiting the staggered 2017 rollout as a clean source of variation that is not confounded with the supervisor-pairing identification used by Agarwal et al. (2014) or with the agency-death event used by Granja and Leuz (2024). Instructor grading notes — what an A looks like

The five-paragraph rewrite is exactly five paragraphs. Count them. If your rewrite has six, you have absorbed only partly. If four, you have skipped one of the five jobs. Five, no more, no less.

The hook anchors in a specific number, place, and intervention. Maya’s anchor: 2019, Atlanta, 1.7×, 2017 OCC. Any reader can picture this. The literature-review opening that the original ¶1 used cannot be pictured.

The what-we-do paragraph names the assumption AND the test AND the bound. Maya names parallel trends, reports F = 1.04, p = 0.39, and reports Oster δ = 1.7. All three. Students who name the assumption but not the test get the design half-right; the referee will assume the test was not run. Students who name the test but not the bound miss the Oster sensitivity that PS 7.5 produced.

The findings paragraph’s first sentence is the headline. Maya’s first sentence: “We find that the 2017 intensification reduced the county-year Black-White denial-rate gap by 1.4 percentage points.” The headline is not buried. Compare to the original ¶7’s structure where the same number appeared in sentence 5 — buried behind caveats — and the referee’s eye glides over it. The first-sentence position is the discipline.

The contribution paragraph has three specific what-was-added sentences. Maya’s strand-1 contribution: “we isolate the causal effect of a specific regulatory tool against the documented disparate-impact gap, complementing… Gao and Sun (2019)… and… Bartlett et al. (2022).” Names the closest comparisons. Names what is novel. Compare to the original ¶10’s chest-thumping “three contributions to the literature on…” which does neither.

Common student errors

  1. The rewrite has six or seven paragraphs. The most common failure. Usually because one of the five jobs (often Findings or Contribution) is split across two paragraphs. Fix: merge.
  2. The hook is generic. “Fair lending is an important policy issue…” Generic openings fail the concreteness test. Fix: name a specific year, place, magnitude, intervention.
  3. The what-we-do paragraph names the assumption but not the test. “We use a staggered DiD that requires parallel trends.” Fix: add “we test parallel trends with F = X, p = Y on the pre-period leads.”
  4. The findings paragraph’s first sentence is not the headline. A common pathology: the first sentence rehashes the design, the second sentence is the magnitude. Fix: invert; the magnitude is sentence 1.
  5. The contribution paragraph is chest-thumping. “This paper makes three contributions: first, … second, … third, …” Fix: replace each “we contribute” with a specific what-was-added sentence naming the closest comparison.
  6. The tracked-changes file is hand-waved. A student who claims “I cut a lot” without showing the diff has not demonstrated the discipline. Fix: produce the actual ~~deleted~~ and added markup.
  7. The shrink ratio is below 0.30. Means the student expanded rather than rewrote. Diagnosis: probably did not delete the original paragraphs; only added new prose on top. Fix: start over with the original paragraphs explicitly marked for deletion or for merging.

A large descriptive literature documents persistent racial gaps in mortgage approval, beginning with Munnell, Tootell, Browne, and McEneaney (1996) and continuing through Bayer, Ferreira, and Ross (2016), Bartlett, Morse, Stanton, and Wallace (2022), and Gao and Sun (2019); this literature establishes the phenomenon but rarely identifies *causal* interventions that move the phenomenon. A separate literature examines the effect of regulatory supervisory intensity on bank behavior more broadly (Agarwal, Lucca, Seru, and Trebbi 2014; Granja and Leuz 2024) but rarely focuses on disparate-impact outcomes — typically estimating effects on prudential measures such as capital ratios, lending growth, and risk-taking. A third, smaller literature evaluates anti-discrimination interventions (Ross, Turner, Godfrey, and Smith 2008) but most credibly-identified estimates come from audit studies rather than from regulatory-program rollouts. We bring these three conversations together by estimating, for a specific supervisory program and a specific disparate-impact outcome, the causal effect that the prior work either documents in correlation or examines for a different outcome. Answering this question requires a research design that isolates the effect of the 2017 intensification from other policy and market changes affecting the same counties over the same period; we now describe such a design.*

Word count: 240. Names the question specifically (treatment, outcome, unit, population), engages three literatures at the level of partial-overlap-not-empty-space, and bridges to paragraph 3 with a one-sentence transition.

**Paragraph 3 — What-We-Do (319 words):**

> *We implement a staggered difference-in-differences estimator with cohort-specific event-study leads and lags, in the framework of Callaway and Sant'Anna (2021) and Sun and Abraham (2021), which is robust to the negative-weight problem identified by Goodman-Bacon (2021) for two-way fixed-effects estimators in staggered designs. The data are HMDA's Loan Application Register, 2014–2020, covering 47 million conventional mortgage originations across 47,128 zip codes in 3,134 counties, which we aggregate to the county-year panel of 21,938 observations. Identification rests on the assumption that, absent the 2017 intensification, the denial-rate gap in treated counties would have followed the same time path as the gap in untreated counties — the parallel-trends assumption. We test this assumption with pre-period event-study leads (Figure \ref{fig:event-study}); the leads are statistically indistinguishable from zero ($\hat{\delta}_{-3} = 0.06$ pp, 95% CI: $-0.42$ to $0.54$; $\hat{\delta}_{-2} = -0.11$ pp, 95% CI: $-0.59$ to $0.37$) and the F-statistic on their joint significance is 1.04 ($p = 0.39$). We probe robustness to unobservable selection using Oster's (2019) δ; the resulting bound of 1.7 implies that the unobservable selection driving the residual variation would have to be 70% stronger than the observable selection driving the explained variation to explain away the headline estimate. We also report results using alternative staggered estimators (de Chaisemartin and D'Haultfœuille 2020; Borusyak, Jaravel, and Spiess 2024), alternative saturation choices, and alternative sample restrictions, all of which deliver coefficients within the 95% confidence interval of the preferred specification (Table \ref{tab:main}, Appendix Table A-3). The cross-specification stability is the empirical defense of the identifying assumption.*

Word count: 251. Names design family (staggered DiD), variant (Callaway-Sant'Anna, Sun-Abraham), data (HMDA, year range, sample size), assumption (parallel trends), test (F = 1.04, p = 0.39), bound (Oster δ = 1.7), and references both the figure and the table by label.

**Paragraph 4 — Findings (393 words):**

> *We find that the 2017 intensification reduced the county-year Black-White denial-rate gap by 1.4 percentage points (Table \ref{tab:main}, column 3; 95% CI: 0.8 to 2.0), a 23% reduction relative to the pre-treatment mean of 6.1 points. The estimate is stable across the four saturation choices reported in Table \ref{tab:main} (the coefficient varies between $-1.36$ and $-1.41$ across columns) and across the tight-sample restriction in column 4. Effects emerge in the year of treatment and grow in magnitude over the first three post-treatment years (Figure \ref{fig:event-study}); the $\tau = +3$ coefficient is $-1.8$ pp (95% CI: $-2.4$ to $-1.2$), implying a cumulative effect of roughly one-quarter of the pre-treatment gap. The dynamics are inconsistent with the intensification reflecting a contemporaneous shock; the year-by-year buildup suggests a behavioral response by lenders that compounds over examination cycles. Heterogeneity is concentrated in counties with above-median minority-population shares (interaction coefficient: $-1.1$ pp, BH-FDR-adjusted $p = 0.012$) and in lenders with above-median examination-finding histories (interaction coefficient: $-0.9$ pp, BH-FDR-adjusted $p = 0.028$); the remaining heterogeneity cuts we pre-specified do not survive the multiple-testing adjustment and are reported as exploratory (Appendix Table A-2). The headline estimate survives the staggered-DiD-alternative estimators we apply (Callaway-Sant'Anna, Sun-Abraham, de Chaisemartin-D'Haultfœuille), the sample restrictions, and the placebo events (Appendix Table A-3 and Figure A-1). Transported to the broader OCC-supervised population using inverse-probability-of-sampling-weighting on the pre-specified moderators (§7), the implied ATE is $-1.3$ pp (95% bootstrap CI: $-1.9$ to $-0.7$), robust to plausible unobserved-moderator shifts of magnitude $|\Delta_U| \le 0.25$ — supporting the claim that the estimate generalizes within the population the rollout reached, though we are explicit in §7 that the estimate does not transport to FinTech lenders (outside the 2017 reform's scope) or to non-OCC-supervised counties.*

Word count: 279. Headline number, dynamic interpretation, heterogeneity disciplined by BH-FDR, robustness anchor, and external-validity transport. The headline matches the abstract's sentence 4 and Table 2's column-3 cell.

## Common student errors

1. **The rewrite has six or seven paragraphs.** The most common failure. Usually because one of the five jobs (often Findings or Contribution) is split across two paragraphs. Fix: merge.
2. **The hook is generic.** "Fair lending is an important policy issue…" Generic openings fail the concreteness test. Fix: name a specific year, place, magnitude, intervention.
3. **The what-we-do paragraph names the assumption but not the test.** "We use a staggered DiD that requires parallel trends." Fix: add "we test parallel trends with F = X, p = Y on the pre-period leads."
4. **The findings paragraph's first sentence is not the headline.** A common pathology: the first sentence rehashes the design, the second sentence is the magnitude. Fix: invert; the magnitude is sentence 1.
5. **The contribution paragraph is chest-thumping.** "This paper makes three contributions: first, … second, … third, …" Fix: replace each "we contribute" with a specific what-was-added sentence naming the closest comparison.
6. **The tracked-changes file is hand-waved.** A student who claims "I cut a lot" without showing the diff has not demonstrated the discipline. Fix: produce the actual `~~deleted~~` and **added** markup.
7. **The shrink ratio is below 0.30.** Means the student expanded rather than rewrote. Diagnosis: probably did not delete the original paragraphs; only added new prose on top. Fix: start over with the original paragraphs explicitly marked for deletion or for merging.
