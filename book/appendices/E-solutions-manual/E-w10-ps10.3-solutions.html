# PAP-extract — CATE analysis of patent-diversification effects on stock returns

## Moderators (pre-specified, $p = 6$)

1. `industry_fe`: SIC 2-digit industry indicator (categorical, 47 levels).
   Justification: industries with longer innovation cycles (pharmaceuticals, semiconductors)
   may differ from those with shorter cycles in how diversification translates into returns.

2. `mkt_cap`: log market capitalization at year-start (continuous, CRSP).
   Justification: smaller firms may face more idiosyncratic risk and may benefit more from
   portfolio diversification; larger firms may already be diversified by scale.

3. `rd_intensity`: R&D expenditures / total assets (continuous, Compustat XRD/AT).
   Justification: high-R&D firms have more patents to diversify *from*; the marginal value of
   diversification may be larger in their portfolios.

4. `patent_stock_age`: median age of the firm's patent portfolio in years (continuous, USPTO grants).
   Justification: older portfolios may be more vulnerable to obsolescence, raising the value
   of within-portfolio diversification.

5. `citation_count`: log of cumulative citations to the firm's patents (continuous, USPTO + Bessen-NBER linkage).
   Justification: more-cited portfolios contain more economically important patents, where
   diversification across applications matters more.

6. `litigation_history`: indicator for being an NPE-litigation defendant in the past 5 years
   (binary, Stanford NPE database).
   Justification: prior litigation experience may shape the firm's strategic use of its
   portfolio, including how it diversifies.

## Sample, treatment, outcome

- Unit: firm-year.
- Sample: CRSP-Compustat-merged firms with at least 10 USPTO patents granted by year-start,
  fiscal-year ending in calendar year 2010-2023 (14 years of panel data, 13,420 firm-years
  after winsorizing returns at 1/99 and dropping firms with missing R&D for two consecutive years).
- Treatment $T_{it}$: 1 if firm $i$'s patent-portfolio Herfindahl (across USPTO 4-digit classes)
  in year $t$ is below the cross-sectional median (i.e., "diversified"); 0 otherwise.
- Outcome $Y_{it}$: firm's CRSP-adjusted return from January 1 to December 31 of year $t$,
  in percent.

## Cross-validation / honesty protocol

1. 5-fold cross-fitting at the firm level: each firm's predictions are computed by a forest
   trained on a fold not containing that firm.
2. Within each fold: causal forest with `honest=True`, `min_samples_leaf=50`,
   `n_estimators=2000`, leaf-honest split.
3. Clustering: cross-fitting folds formed by whole firms (not firm-years), so the same firm
   never appears in both train and test partitions.
4. Nuisance regression: random forest for $\mathbb{E}[T|X, W]$ and $\mathbb{E}[Y|X, W]$, each
   with `n_estimators=200`, `random_state=SEED=42`.

2. cate/forest.py (the script that produced the BLP)

# cate/forest.py — causal forest with cross-fitting and honest splits.
# Verified deterministic with SEED=42, python=3.11, econml=0.15.0.
import numpy as np
import pandas as pd
import statsmodels.api as sm
from econml.dml import CausalForestDML
from sklearn.ensemble import RandomForestRegressor, RandomForestClassifier
from sklearn.preprocessing import OneHotEncoder
SEED = 42
rng = np.random.default_rng(SEED)

df = pd.read_parquet("data/processed/analysis.parquet")
# Encode industry FE as one-hot (47 levels → 47 dummies, drop first)
ohe = OneHotEncoder(drop="first", sparse_output=False)
ind_dum = ohe.fit_transform(df[["industry_sic2"]])

X_cont = df[["mkt_cap", "rd_intensity", "patent_stock_age",
             "citation_count", "litigation_history"]].to_numpy()
X = np.hstack([ind_dum, X_cont])              # 51 moderator dimensions
W = df[["size_decile", "btm", "momentum"]].to_numpy()   # control vars
T = df["treat_diversified"].to_numpy()
Y = df["ret_pct"].to_numpy()
firms = df["gvkey"].to_numpy()                # cluster id

forest = CausalForestDML(
    model_t=RandomForestClassifier(n_estimators=200, random_state=SEED, n_jobs=-1),
    model_y=RandomForestRegressor(n_estimators=200, random_state=SEED, n_jobs=-1),
    n_estimators=2000,
    min_samples_leaf=50,
    discrete_treatment=True,
    honest=True,
    cv=5,
    random_state=SEED,
)
forest.fit(Y=Y, T=T, X=X, W=W, groups=firms)

# Per-unit CATE with variance
inf = forest.effect_inference(X)
tau_hat = inf.pred
se_hat = np.sqrt(inf.pred_dist.var(axis=0))

# Overall ATE from the forest (should match headline within ~1 SE)
ate_inf = forest.ate_inference(X)
print(f"Forest ATE = {ate_inf.mean_point:.3f} pp, SE = {ate_inf.stderr_mean:.3f}")
# Output: Forest ATE = 1.74 pp, SE = 0.81  (vs. headline ATE = 1.8, HC1 SE = 0.7)

# Save CATE predictions
pd.DataFrame({"tau_hat": tau_hat, "se_hat": se_hat,
              "mkt_cap": X_cont[:, 0], "rd_intensity": X_cont[:, 1],
              "patent_stock_age": X_cont[:, 2], "citation_count": X_cont[:, 3],
              "litigation_history": X_cont[:, 4]}).to_parquet(
    "cate/cate-predictions.parquet")

# --- Honest Best-Linear-Projection (BLP) on the cross-fitted CATE ---
X_blp = sm.add_constant(X_cont)                      # 5 continuous moderators only
blp = sm.OLS(tau_hat, X_blp).fit(cov_type="HC1")
print(blp.summary())
with open("cate/blp.txt", "w") as fh:
    fh.write(str(blp.summary()))

# F-test for joint heterogeneity (all moderator coefficients zero)
A = np.eye(X_blp.shape[1])[1:]                       # exclude constant
joint = blp.f_test(A)
print(f"Joint heterogeneity F = {joint.fvalue.item():.3f}, p = {joint.pvalue.item():.6f}")

A note on the negative case (no heterogeneity)

What if the forest had found no heterogeneity? The F-test would have a p > 0.10, the CATE histogram would be narrower than the ATE CI, and Leah’s paragraph would be the Case 2 version:

“The pre-specified moderators do not produce detectable heterogeneity in the diversification effect (F(5, N) = X.XX, p = 0.YY). The CATE histogram (Figure C1) is concentrated near the ATE (1.80 pp), consistent with a constant treatment effect across the moderator space we pre-specified. We report this null result honestly: the diversification effect we document in Section 4 is, by the standard of the moderator dimensions theory and our PAP identified, not heterogeneous along these directions.”

The null is a finding. A pre-registered moderator list that produces a null F-test is more persuasive than a post-hoc moderator list that produces a starred F-test, because the pre-registration removes the searching-for-significance freedom.

A note on what the BLP cannot tell you

The BLP is a linear projection of a possibly-non-linear CATE function. If the true CATE is non-linear in a moderator (e.g., U-shaped in firm size, with the smallest and largest firms benefiting most), the BLP coefficient on mkt_cap will be approximately zero — and the BLP will report “no heterogeneity in firm size” when in fact the heterogeneity is just non-linear. The CATE-vs-mkt_cap scatter from Part 5 is the diagnostic against this: a flat LOESS despite a significant BLP coefficient flags trouble; a U-shaped LOESS despite a near-zero BLP coefficient flags the non-linearity the BLP missed.

In Leah’s data, the LOESS is monotone-decreasing in mkt_cap, so the BLP is approximately correct. If Leah’s LOESS had been U-shaped, she would have added a mkt_cap² term to the BLP and re-run — but only as a robustness, not as the headline (because the squared term was not pre-registered). The robustness check would go in a paragraph noting “the BLP-linear approximation is adequate for the smooth heterogeneity we find in firm size; for completeness, including mkt_cap² does not change the joint F-test conclusion.”

```python
df = pd.read_parquet("data/processed/analysis.parquet")
# Encode industry FE as one-hot (47 levels → 47 dummies, drop first)
ohe = OneHotEncoder(drop="first", sparse_output=False)
ind_dum = ohe.fit_transform(df[["industry_sic2"]])

X_cont = df[["mkt_cap", "rd_intensity", "patent_stock_age",
             "citation_count", "litigation_history"]].to_numpy()
X = np.hstack([ind_dum, X_cont])              # 51 moderator dimensions
W = df[["size_decile", "btm", "momentum"]].to_numpy()   # control vars
T = df["treat_diversified"].to_numpy()
Y = df["ret_pct"].to_numpy()
firms = df["gvkey"].to_numpy()                # cluster id

forest = CausalForestDML(
    model_t=RandomForestClassifier(n_estimators=200, random_state=SEED, n_jobs=-1),
    model_y=RandomForestRegressor(n_estimators=200, random_state=SEED, n_jobs=-1),
    n_estimators=2000,
    min_samples_leaf=50,
    discrete_treatment=True,
    honest=True,
    cv=5,
    random_state=SEED,
)
forest.fit(Y=Y, T=T, X=X, W=W, groups=firms)

# Per-unit CATE with variance
inf = forest.effect_inference(X)
tau_hat = inf.pred
se_hat = np.sqrt(inf.pred_dist.var(axis=0))

# Overall ATE from the forest (should match headline within ~1 SE)
ate_inf = forest.ate_inference(X)
print(f"Forest ATE = {ate_inf.mean_point:.3f} pp, SE = {ate_inf.stderr_mean:.3f}")
# Output: Forest ATE = 1.74 pp, SE = 0.81  (vs. headline ATE = 1.8, HC1 SE = 0.7)

# Save CATE predictions
pd.DataFrame({"tau_hat": tau_hat, "se_hat": se_hat,
              "mkt_cap": X_cont[:, 0], "rd_intensity": X_cont[:, 1],
              "patent_stock_age": X_cont[:, 2], "citation_count": X_cont[:, 3],
              "litigation_history": X_cont[:, 4]}).to_parquet(
    "cate/cate-predictions.parquet")

# --- Honest Best-Linear-Projection (BLP) on the cross-fitted CATE ---
X_blp = sm.add_constant(X_cont)                      # 5 continuous moderators only
blp = sm.OLS(tau_hat, X_blp).fit(cov_type="HC1")
print(blp.summary())
with open("cate/blp.txt", "w") as fh:
    fh.write(str(blp.summary()))

# F-test for joint heterogeneity (all moderator coefficients zero)
A = np.eye(X_blp.shape[1])[1:]                       # exclude constant
joint = blp.f_test(A)
print(f"Joint heterogeneity F = {joint.fvalue.item():.3f}, p = {joint.pvalue.item():.6f}")
```

## A note on the negative case (no heterogeneity)

What if the forest had found no heterogeneity? The F-test would have a p > 0.10, the CATE histogram would be narrower than the ATE CI, and Leah's paragraph would be the *Case 2* version:

> *"The pre-specified moderators do not produce detectable heterogeneity in the diversification effect ($F(5, N) = X.XX$, $p = 0.YY$). The CATE histogram (Figure C1) is concentrated near the ATE (1.80 pp), consistent with a constant treatment effect across the moderator space we pre-specified. We report this null result honestly: the diversification effect we document in Section 4 is, by the standard of the moderator dimensions theory and our PAP identified, not heterogeneous along these directions."*

The null is a finding. A pre-registered moderator list that produces a null F-test is *more* persuasive than a post-hoc moderator list that produces a starred F-test, because the pre-registration removes the searching-for-significance freedom.

## A note on what the BLP cannot tell you

The BLP is a *linear projection* of a possibly-non-linear CATE function. If the true CATE is non-linear in a moderator (e.g., U-shaped in firm size, with the smallest and largest firms benefiting most), the BLP coefficient on `mkt_cap` will be approximately zero — and the BLP will report "no heterogeneity in firm size" when in fact the heterogeneity is just non-linear. The CATE-vs-`mkt_cap` scatter from Part 5 is the diagnostic against this: a flat LOESS despite a significant BLP coefficient flags trouble; a U-shaped LOESS despite a near-zero BLP coefficient flags the non-linearity the BLP missed.

In Leah's data, the LOESS is monotone-decreasing in `mkt_cap`, so the BLP is approximately correct. If Leah's LOESS had been U-shaped, she would have added a `mkt_cap²` term to the BLP and re-run — but only as a *robustness*, not as the headline (because the squared term was not pre-registered). The robustness check would go in a paragraph noting "the BLP-linear approximation is adequate for the smooth heterogeneity we find in firm size; for completeness, including `mkt_cap²` does not change the joint F-test conclusion."
