## The model deliverable — `research-identity-memo.md`

### 1. The central question (Part 1)

**Section 1 — Central Question.**

> *How can textual data — patents, regulatory filings, earnings calls, and other dated documents produced by economic actors — be used to measure economic constructs (innovation effort, strategic disclosure, uncertainty, fraud, competitive positioning) that are otherwise unobservable to outside observers, and how do those text-derived measurements rewrite the standard empirical-finance accounts of the constructs they measure?*

The question is 51 words, fits on two lines, names: the data class (text), the construct class (unobserved economic variables), the verb pair (measure, rewrite), and the scope (empirical-finance accounts). It is broader than my camp paper (which addresses only innovation effort, measured from patent text) and narrower than my field (textual analysis in finance, which would also include sentiment in news articles, FOMC statements, social media, etc.).

**Literature anchoring (~140 words):**

> *Four papers in the literature share this question from different angles. Kogan, Papanikolaou, Seru, and Stoffman (2017, Quarterly Journal of Economics) measures the market value of innovation from patent text and patent-stock-market reactions, using text to recover an economic construct (innovation value) that is not directly observable. Hoberg and Phillips (2016, Review of Financial Studies) uses 10-K filings to construct a text-based measure of product-market competition, again recovering an unobservable construct (which firms compete with which) from text. Loughran and McDonald (2011, Journal of Finance) constructs the canonical financial-text sentiment lexicon, demonstrating that text-based tone measurements explain return patterns that price-based measures do not. Bybee, Kelly, Manela, and Xiu (2024, Review of Financial Studies, forthcoming) extends text-based measurement to a topic-modeling framework over newspaper articles. Each paper sits at a different point in the (text-source × construct × method) space; my central question asks how to keep moving through that space.*

**Positioning (~75 words):**

> *The question is emerging (not crowded). Textual measurement in finance was a small subfield in 2010; it has grown substantially since but remains less than 5% of empirical-finance papers as measured by JEL-classification searches. The infrastructure is maturing — large language models, sentence embeddings, and topic-modeling libraries are now standard — and the pace of construct-creation is accelerating. Entry now is well-timed: enough community to peer-review, not so much that incremental contributions are unwelcome.*

### 2. The camp paper's angle on the question (Part 2)

**Section 2 — The Camp Paper's Angle.**

> *My camp paper's headline finding is that patents with semantically distinctive language relative to their technological-class peers are associated with 22% higher 36-month forward-looking return volatility, after controlling for citations, claims, and class fixed effects (95% CI: 14% to 30%). The paper uses cosine-distance between USPTO-published patent text and the average text within the patent's CPC sub-class as the language-distinctiveness measure, applied to a 380,000-patent panel from 2008–2020. Located within the central question, the paper contributes a measurement of one specific construct (innovation effort heterogeneity, recovered from patent text) from one specific source (USPTO-published patents, full-text). The paper takes a single (text-source × construct × method) point in the design space. The literature has done patent-text-based measurement before (Kogan et al. 2017) but with a different methodological frame; the paper's contribution is the use of within-class semantic distinctiveness as a finer measurement of innovation effort than aggregate patent count or citation impact.*

**Open questions left after the paper (~140 words):**

> *The camp paper leaves several questions unanswered. First: does the relationship between semantic distinctiveness and forward-looking volatility reverse for non-granted patents (the patent-abandonment text), or is it specific to granted patents? The answer would clarify whether the construct measures innovation effort (which abandonments also reflect) or innovation success (which only grants reflect). Second: do the patent-text-derived measurements correlate with other text-derived measurements of innovation (e.g., 10-K innovation-section text, earnings-call mentions of R&D efforts) in a way that suggests a common construct, or are they measuring different things? Third: how does the measurement perform under selection — patents that file but are never granted, patents that the firm chooses not to file at all? Each question is the seed of a follow-up project.*

**Research-identity role of the camp paper (~30 words):**

> *The camp paper is the foundational paper. It defines my entry into the research line and demonstrates the (text-source × construct × method) framework that the follow-ups will use.*

### 3. Three follow-up projects (Part 3)

**Follow-up 1 (closest sibling) — see `follow-up-1.md` for the full one-pager:**

*Working title:* "Patent Abandonment Text and the Selection Margin in Innovation Measurement."

*Question:* When firms abandon patent applications before grant — typically by failing to respond to USPTO office actions — what does the abandoned-application's text reveal about the *type* of innovation effort the firm was making, and does this innovation-effort dimension predict subsequent firm-level outcomes (R&D persistence, product introductions, market-value adjustments)?

*Data:* USPTO Patent Application Information Retrieval (PAIR) system. Abandoned applications 2014–2022. ~120,000 abandonments. Linked to firm-level outcomes via USPTO assignee field → Compustat GVKEY crosswalk.

*Method:* Same cosine-distance methodology as the camp paper but applied to abandoned-application text. Run as a difference-in-differences comparing granted vs. abandoned patents with similar semantic distinctiveness profiles.

*Closest prior paper:* Mowery, Sampat, and Ziedonis (2002, RAND Journal of Economics) on university patent abandonment; Hegde, Ljungqvist, and Raj (2022, Journal of Finance) on patent grant uncertainty.

*Timeline:* 18 months from start; suitable as an honors-thesis paper in my senior year of college (2030).

**Follow-up 2 (methodological cousin) — see `follow-up-2.md`:**

*Working title:* "Regulatory-Filing Tone and Strategic Disclosure: A Sequence-Modeling Approach."

*Question:* When public firms file 10-K, 10-Q, and 8-K with the SEC, how does the *sequential structure* of the filings — the way disclosures are ordered, which sections elaborate on which, which topics are mentioned in conjunction — reveal strategic-disclosure choices that are invisible in bag-of-words text measurements?

*Data:* SEC EDGAR full-text filings 2010–2024. ~1.2 million 10-K and 10-Q filings.

*Method:* New methodology — sequence models (transformer-based) trained to predict next-paragraph content. Strategic disclosure is identified as paragraphs that deviate from sequence predictions (the "surprise" in filing structure). This is methodologically distinct from the camp paper (which uses cosine distance over bag-of-words text) and from the standard tone-of-text literature (which uses lexicons).

*Closest prior paper:* Cohen, Malloy, and Nguyen (2020, Journal of Finance) on annual-report changes as a disclosure signal; Lopez-Lira (2023) on GPT-based filing analysis.

*Timeline:* 36 months from start; suitable as a first-year PhD paper.

**Follow-up 3 (long-range stretch) — see `follow-up-3.md`:**

*Working title:* "Earnings-Call Linguistic Asymmetry and the Information Content of Management Reluctance."

*Question:* When public-company executives are reluctant to answer earnings-call questions — measured by linguistic features (hedging, deflection, topic-switching, response latency) — does the reluctance reveal information about firm-level outcomes (subsequent earnings surprises, executive turnover, fraud disclosure) that is not in the verbatim transcript?

*Data:* Refinitiv StreetEvents earnings-call transcripts 2010–2024 (~400,000 calls), linked to audio recordings where available (~150,000 with audio).

*Method:* Multi-modal sequence modeling combining text and audio features. Builds infrastructure shared across the research line: transcript-tokenization pipelines, executive-identity tracking, firm-event linkage tables.

*Closest prior paper:* Mayew and Venkatachalam (2012, Journal of Finance) on vocal cues in earnings calls; Bochkay, Hales, and Chava (2020, Journal of Financial Economics) on language tone in calls.

*Timeline:* 60+ months from start; suitable as a second-year-PhD or junior-faculty paper. Long-range, capital-intensive (audio processing requires significant compute), and high-payoff (the construct — "what executives don't want to say" — is highly economic).

## Instructor grading notes — what an A looks like

**The central question fits on one line and names a real research line.** Leah's question is 51 words, names data, construct, verbs, and scope. It is broader than the camp paper but narrower than the field. Students whose central questions are 8 words ("how do firms innovate?") have written a topic, not a question; students whose central questions are 100 words have written a paragraph, not a question.

**The literature anchoring names four papers from different angles.** Kogan-Papanikolaou-Seru-Stoffman (patent value), Hoberg-Phillips (text-and-competition), Loughran-McDonald (sentiment), Bybee-Kelly-Manela-Xiu (topic models). Each addresses the central question from a different (text-source × construct × method) point. The four papers *together* trace the question's boundaries.

## A subtle point — the through-line is a *productivity* argument, not just an *identity* argument

The research-identity memo is sometimes framed as an emotional or philosophical exercise — *who are you as a researcher?* It is also an *economic* exercise: a coherent research line produces papers more efficiently than scattered projects. The arithmetic:

```python
# productivity_check.py — illustrative
# Per-paper empirical infrastructure cost (hours):
infra_cost_scratch = 200       # if you build from scratch every paper
infra_cost_reuse = 50          # if you build once and reuse

n_papers = 4
total_scratch = infra_cost_scratch * n_papers
total_reuse = infra_cost_scratch + infra_cost_reuse * (n_papers - 1)
ratio = total_reuse / total_scratch
print(f"Scratch: {total_scratch} hours total")
print(f"Reuse:   {total_reuse} hours total")
print(f"Reuse ratio: {ratio:.2%}")
```

Verified output:

```
Scratch: 800 hours total
Reuse:   350 hours total
Reuse ratio: 43.75%
```

A researcher with a coherent line spends about 44% of the empirical-infrastructure hours that a researcher without one spends, for the same four papers. The 56% savings is the research-identity dividend.

This arithmetic is illustrative — actual ratios vary by subfield, by data complexity, and by the degree of methodological evolution between papers — but the direction is robust. The research-identity memo is partly identity, partly philosophy, and partly an *operating discipline* that makes the next four papers cheaper to write than the first one.

## Common student errors

1. **The "central question" is a topic.** "Fair lending" is a topic; "how does institutional design affect realized disparate-impact outcomes?" is a question. Topics are infinite; questions are claimable.
2. **The three follow-ups are three sequels of the camp paper.** "More patent text" is not a research line; "patent text + filings text + earnings text" is. The follow-ups should span the design space.
3. **The reflection performs depth instead of being honest.** "This camp transformed my life" is a performance; "I came in believing that more data means a better paper and left believing that more identification matters more" is honest.
4. **The closing line is a quote from someone else.** It must be the student's own line; it has to be the line the future-self recognizes.
5. **The through-line graph has nodes but no arrows.** Without the arrows showing shared investments, the graph is a list of projects, not a research line.
